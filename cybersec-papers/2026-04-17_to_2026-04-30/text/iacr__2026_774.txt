Boolean Masking via Composable Conversion
Jaeseung Han
and Dong-Guk Han

Abstract. Masking is a representative side-channel countermeasure that provides
provable security. Among masking schemes, Boolean masking (BM) is widely adopted
due to its simple sharing structure, while inner product masking (IPM) and code-based
masking (CM) have been studied as alternatives that achieve a higher security order
with the same number of shares—a property known as security order amplification
in the bit-probing model. Recent work by Gaspoz and Dhooghe (TCHES 2025)
proposed an IPM multiplication gadget and CM gadgets with provable bit-level
security; however, the overhead of CM gadgets for linear operations, the overhead
of IPM multiplication, and the lack of a complete provably secure implementation
exploiting IPM security order amplification remain open challenges.
In this paper, we address all three challenges. First, we propose BM-to-IPM and
IPM-to-BM conversion gadgets satisfying bit t-MIMO-SNI in the bit-probing model,
enabling composable and provably secure interoperation between the two masking
domains. Second, we optimize the TCHES 2025 IPM multiplication gadget via Row
Packing and Reduction in Rows, reducing the fresh random bit requirement from

2t(n2 −1)k2(k + 1) to tk(n −1)(kn + W) bits with a proportional reduction in XOR
gates, while maintaining bit t-SNI security. Third, we present a hybrid IPM-BM
framework in which multiplications are performed in IPM with fewer shares and all
Boolean linear operations are handled share-wise in BM at no additional randomness
cost, and show that this hybrid approach requires significantly fewer gates and random
bits than a pure CM approach. As a concrete instantiation, we implement a second-
order masked AES-128 with a 2-share IPM / 3-share BM hybrid architecture, prove
that the implementation satisfies bit 2-PINI, and evaluate its practical side-channel
security via first- and second-order TVLA on an ARM Cortex-M4 with up to one
million traces. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first end-to-end cryptographic
implementation that provably preserves IPM’s security order amplification in the
bit-probing model.

Introduction
Cryptographic implementations are vulnerable to side-channel attacks such as Differential
Power Analysis (DPA) [KJJ99] and Correlation Power Analysis (CPA) [BCO04], which
exploit physical leakage from a device to recover secret keys independently of the underlying
algorithm’s mathematical strength. Masking is one of the most widely studied and deployed
countermeasures against such attacks: it randomizes the computation by splitting each
sensitive variable into n shares. If any subset of at most t shares is statistically independent
of the secret, then the masking achieves t-th order security.
Among masking schemes, Boolean masking (BM) [CJRR99, GP99] is the predominant
choice in practice due to its simple structure and seamless applicability to binary-field oper-

ations. In addition to BM, there are various masking methods depending on purpose such
as multiplicative masking [GT03], affine masking [FMPR11], polynomial masking [PR11,
GM11], inner product masking (IPM) [BFG15, WSY+16, BFG+17], direct sum masking
(DSM) [BCC+14, PGS+17], and code-based masking (CM) [WYS19, WMCS20, WCG+22].
BM achieves only a probing security order of t = n −1, meaning that an adversary placing
t + 1 probes suffices to recover the secret. To achieve higher security at a fixed share count,
researchers have investigated DSM, CM and, in particular, IPM. In an ¯L-IPM encoding, a
secret x is represented as shares ¯x = (x0, . . . , xn−1) satisfying x = Pn−1
i=0 Lixi for a public
vector ¯L = (L0, L1, . . . , Ln−1). Poussier et al. [PGS+17] formalized the key advantage of
IPM by introducing the bit-probing model and showing that, under linear leakage, IPM
achieves a strictly higher security order than BM with the same number of shares—a
phenomenon called security order amplification. For example, a 2-share IPM over F24 with
an optimally chosen ¯L attains second-order bit-probing security, matching the protection
level of a 3-share BM implementation. This property is particularly attractive in scenarios
where the number of shares is constrained by hardware resources (e.g., register pressure in
embedded processors), as IPM can provide a higher security order without increasing the
share count.
Prior work and remaining challenges.
Wang et al. [WSY+16] proposed Boolean matrix
product masking, a hybrid encoding in which the first share is multiplied by a public
non-singular Boolean matrix while the remaining shares follow standard BM. They proved
probing security in the word-level model and observed security order amplification for linear
leakage functions. However, the matrix structure is embedded in the encoding itself, so all
gadgets—including linear ones—must account for the matrix product. Afterwards, Wang
et al. [WMCS20] proposed gadgets for both linear and nonlinear operations under CM;
however, their constructions were designed and proven secure in the word-level probing
model and do not preserve security order amplification at the bit level. This was confirmed
experimentally by Wu et al. [WCG+22], who showed that higher-order IPM gadgets
built on Wang et al.’s method suffer a measurable reduction in practical security order.
Gaspoz and Dhooghe [GD25a] addressed this by proposing the first IPM multiplication
gadget proven bit t-SNI secure, empirically confirming that security order amplification is
maintained during multiplication. In a companion work, Gaspoz and Dhooghe [GD25b]
further proposed basic CM gadgets proven bit t-PINI, including Toffoli, bit-swap, and
bit-XOR operations, enabling bit t-PINI secure CM implementations of arbitrary circuits.
Nevertheless, three challenges remain open after [GD25a, GD25b]:
1. Overhead of linear operations in IPM. In BM, Boolean linear operations such
as bit-swap or bit-XOR can be computed share-wise without any fresh randomness,
trivially satisfying probing security. By contrast, the CM gadgets of [GD25b] require
fresh randomness for such basic operations, incurring significant overhead that exceeds
a simple share-wise repetition.
2. Overhead of IPM multiplication. The secure IPM multiplication of [GD25a] in-
cluding preprocessing gadget requires fresh random bits and XOR gates substantially
more than the BM baseline.
3. Provably secure block cipher using IPM security. No complete, provably
secure cryptographic implementation exploiting IPM’s security order amplification
end-to-end has been demonstrated.
Our contributions.
We resolve all three challenges and present a provably and practically
secure hybrid IPM-BM masking.
1. Composable IPM-BM conversion (Section 3). We propose BM-to-IPM and
IPM-to-BM conversion gadgets that satisfy bit t-MIMO-SNI in the bit-probing model.

all gadgets to operate under the modified encoding, our approach separates the IPM
and BM domains entirely through composable conversion gadgets proven secure in the
bit-probing model. This domain separation allows each masking scheme to be used where
it is most efficient, without altering the encoding structure of either scheme. As a concrete
instantiation, we design a second-order bit-probing secure masked AES-128 with a 2-share

(AddRoundKey, ShiftRows, MixColumns, and the field isomorphism in SubBytes) are
handled share-wise in the 3-share BM. We prove that the complete S-box satisfies bit

further show that our hybrid approach requires significantly fewer gates and random bits
than a pure code-based masking approach using the CM gadgets of [GD25b], quantitatively
justifying the hybrid design.
4. Practical leakage evaluation (Section 6). We evaluate the second-order hybrid
masked AES on an ARM Cortex-M4 using non-specific fixed-vs-random TVLA [GJJR11]

with up to 1 million traces, while the unmasked (RNG-off) baseline leaks immediately,
confirming both the soundness of the measurement setup and the practical effectiveness of
the proposed scheme. In addition, we report the cost and breakdown of the second-order
hybrid IPM-BM AES.

2.2
Security Model for Masking
We recall the security models and notions used throughout this paper. We recall the
probing model at both the word level and the bit level, and then introduce composable
security notions that guarantee probing security under composition.
Probing model.
The probing security model was introduced by Ishai, Sahai, and Wag-
ner [ISW03].
Definition 1 (Probing model). A t-th order probing adversary can observe the values
of at most t wires (called probes) in a circuit. A circuit is t-probing secure if the joint
distribution of any t probed wires is independent of the secret inputs.
Word-level and bit-level probes.
We distinguish between two levels of granularity for
probes.
Definition 2 (Word-level probe [BFG+17]). A word-level probe reveals an entire element
of F2k.
Definition 3 (Bit-level probe [PGS+17]). A bit-level probe (bit-probe) reveals a single
bit regardless of the field in which shares are encoded.
The security order of a masking scheme may differ between the word-level and bit-level
models. In the word-level model, an n-share BM or IPM achieves security order tw = n−1.
In the bit-level model, IPM can achieve a strictly higher security order tb > n −1, as
formalized in Subsection 2.4.
Composable security.
Barthe et al. [BBD+16] introduced the NI, SNI notions to enable
provably secure composition of gadgets.
Definition 4 (t-NI [BBD+16]). A gadget is t-NI if for any set of t1 internal probes such
that t1 ≤t, all probes can be perfectly simulated using at most t1 shares of each input.
Definition 5 (t-SNI [BBD+16]). A gadget is t-SNI if for any set of t1 internal probes and
any set of t2 output probes such that t1 + t2 ≤t, all probes can be perfectly simulated
using at most t1 shares of each input.
As a result, SNI gadgets support secure composition; in particular, a composition of
t-SNI gadgets where each sharing feeds into at most one gadget is again t-SNI [BBD+16].
However, an arbitrary composition of SNI gadgets is not guaranteed to be probing secure.
The following security notion guarantees probing security for arbitrary compositions.
Cassiers and Standaert [CS20] introduced Probe Isolating Non-Interference (PINI),
which permits trivial composition: any composition of PINI gadgets is again PINI, without
requiring intermediate refresh gadgets even for linear operations. In the following, xi,∗
and yi,∗denote all input bits and all output bits, respectively, belonging to share index i.
Definition 6 (t-PINI [CS20]). A gadget is t-PINI if for any set P of t1 internal probes
and any set A of t2 share indices (for output probes) such that t1 + t2 ≤t, there exists a
set B of at most t1 share indices such that P ∪yA,∗can be simulated using xA∪B,∗.

Proposition 3 ([CS20]). A composite gadget G is t-MIMO-SNI if it satisfies the three
following conditions. (i) G is SP-NIB. (ii) For any pair of output nodes u1, u2 there is no
node v such that there is a path from v to u1 and a path from v to u2. (iii) For any pair
of input nodes u1, u2 there is no node v such that there is a path from u1 to v and a path
from u2 to v.
The original definitions of composable security generally use word-level probes. Their
bit-level counterparts are obtained by replacing each word-level unit—probes, inputs, and
outputs—with individual bits. For example, in bit t-SNI, ‘t1 internal probes’ refers to t1
individual bits of internal wires, and ‘each input’ refers to each input bit.

Composable Conversion Between Inner Product and
Boolean Masking
This section presents our construction of bit t-MIMO-SNI conversion gadgets between
IPM and BM. We first explain the design rationale and the structural differences that the
conversion gadget must accommodate. We then describe the detailed algorithms for each
direction, establish their correctness, and prove bit t-MIMO-SNI security.
3.1
Design Overview
There have already been conversion algorithms between different types of masking. Notably,
Goubin proposed a conversion between BM and arithmetic masking [Gou01], and Coron et
al. extended this conversion to an arbitrary higher security order [CGV14]. A conversion
between CM and BM was also indirectly addressed in [WMCS20] through an algorithm
called CodeL. CodeL is a generalized gadget for evaluating Boolean-linear functions under
CM, and is structured in three parts: Part A decodes the CM into an additive sharing
(BM), Part B applies the Boolean-linear function on the BM shares, and Part C re-encodes
the result back into a CM codeword. However, since this construction was designed and
proven secure in the word-level probing model, it does not preserve the security order
amplification of IPM as characterized by the bit-probing model—and therefore provides no
additional bit-level security (e.g., third-order bit security with two shares). This limitation
is noted in Section 4.1 of [WMCS20], and was confirmed in practice in [WCG+22], where
applying the CodeL-based approach to IPM was shown to break the order amplification.
In this work, we design a conversion gadget that is secure in the bit-probing model and
thereby preserves the security order amplification of IPM throughout computation.
Before describing the construction, we highlight two structural differences between
IPM and BM gadgets in the bit-probing model that the conversion must account for: the
number of shares and the number of gadget inputs and outputs.
Number of shares.
Compared to BM, IPM achieves a higher security order for the same
number of shares. Consequently, for a fixed target security order, the BM and IPM require
different numbers of shares. To ensure efficiency of both the conversion gadget and the
overall algorithm, we design the conversion so that the security order is identical on both
sides, while the number of shares differs between IPM and BM accordingly.
Number of gadget inputs and outputs.
In the bit-probing model, an element of F2k is
represented as k independent bits. This independence carries over to BM: since each bit of
the secret is shared independently, a BM sharing of an element of F2k decomposes into k
independent groups of bit shares, which can be treated as k independent inputs or outputs
in a masking gadget. In contrast, for a non-trivial IPM (i.e., one that is not equivalent to
BM), the shares of different bits are not independent. As a concrete example, consider
F22 = F2[X]/(X2 + X + 1) with L0 = 1, L1 = X, and a 2-share encoding of x ∈F22:
2-share BM:
z0[0] = x[0] ⊕r[0], z0[1] = x[1] ⊕r[1], z1[0] = r[0], z1[1] = r[1].
2-share IPM:
z0[0] = x[0] ⊕r[1], z0[1] = x[1] ⊕r[0] ⊕r[1], z1[0] = r[0], z1[1] = r[1].
In BM, the pair (x0[0], x1[0]) depends only on r[0], and (x0[1], x1[1]) only on r[1], so
the two bits’ shares are independent and can be treated as separate gadget inputs or
outputs. In IPM, however, z0[1] depends on both r[0] and r[1], coupling it to z0[0], z1[0],
and z1[1]. Hence the bit shares of an IPM encoding cannot be treated as independent
inputs or outputs.
Based on this observation, we define the conversion gadget interface as follows: an IPM
sharing counts as one input or output unit, while a BM sharing counts as k independent
input or output units.

Field selection and operation classification.
To apply the hybrid IPM-BM to AES, we
first select the field over which IPM operates and classify operations by domain. We choose
the 4-bit field F24. XOR operations between bits of the same field index are linear even in
IPM (since Li is a field element, same-index XOR corresponds to adding two sharings),
so they can be handled share-wise in IPM and trivially satisfy PINI. Accordingly, IPM
handles multiplications and same-index XOR operations. All remaining Boolean linear
operations—including XOR between bits at different field indices, bit permutations, bit
shifts, and byte-level linear maps—are performed in BM.
AES round function decomposition.
AES consists of AddRoundKey, SubBytes, ShiftRows,
and MixColumns. All operations except SubBytes are Boolean linear. AddRoundKey
applies XOR with the round key, which involves same-field-index XOR and could in
principle be performed in IPM; however, since SubBytes begins with a field isomorphism
that maps from the AES field to the composite field F(24)2, performing AddRoundKey
in IPM would require a BM-to-IPM conversion immediately thereafter. To avoid this
overhead, we assign AddRoundKey, ShiftRows, and MixColumns to BM, while SubBytes
takes BM as its input and output with internal multiplications computed in IPM.

verify the three conditions of Proposition 3 for this partial gadget.
Condition (i): the partial gadget is SP-NIB by construction, since every internal gadget
is NI or better, every multiplication input is SNI-refreshed, and the simplified computation
graph contains at most one path between any pair of vertices.
Condition (ii): the back-end conversion gadgets (IPM-to-BM) produce independent
BM outputs from disjoint sub-circuit branches; hence no pair of output nodes shares a
common predecessor.
Condition (iii): similarly, the front-end conversion gadgets (BM-to-IPM) receive in-
dependent BM inputs, so no pair of input nodes shares a common successor within the
partial gadget.
Since all three conditions are satisfied, the partial gadget is t-MIMO-SNI by Proposi-
tion 3. Consequently, the partial gadget is bit t-PINI by Proposition 2. Since the outermost
isomorphism and inverse isomorphism are trivial bit t-PINI share-wise operations, the
whole S-box is bit t-PINI by Proposition 1.

Conclusion
We have addressed three open challenges in applying inner product masking to full
cryptographic implementations.
First, we proposed BM-to-IPM and IPM-to-BM conversion gadgets satisfying bit
t-MIMO-SNI in the bit-probing model, enabling provably secure composition between
IPM and BM while preserving IPM’s security order amplification. Second, we introduced
two optimizations—Row Packing and Reduction in Rows—to the IPM multiplication
gadget [GD25a], reducing its random bit consumption from 1
2t(n2 −1)k2(k + 1) to tk(n −
1)(kn + W) bits and proportionally reducing the XOR gate count, while maintaining bit
t-SNI security. Third, we proposed the hybrid IPM-BM framework and demonstrated
its applicability to AES: we implemented a hybrid 2-share IPM / 3-share BM masked
AES-128 proven to satisfy bit 2-PINI. As summarized in Table 4, across the Boolean linear
operations in the S-box, the hybrid approach reduces the XOR gate count by 3–9× and
the random bit consumption by 7–28× compared to the pure CM construction of [GD25b],
with the advantage widening for more complex operations such as the field isomorphism and
the combined inverse-isomorphism and affine transform. We evaluated the practical second-
order security on an ARM Cortex-M4 via first- and second-order TVLA over 1 000 000
traces with no detectable leakage at either order. A per-component cycle breakdown of the
hybrid SubBytes revealed that the IPM multiplication preprocessing (Gadget-1) accounts
for approximately 60% of the total cost and over 80% of the randomness, identifying it
as the dominant bottleneck. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first end-to-end
cryptographic implementation that provably preserves IPM’s security order amplification
in the bit-probing model.
Several directions remain for future work. First, the Gadget-1 preprocessing is the
primary source of the performance gap between the hybrid construction and optimized
BM-only implementations (approximately 2.58× in SubBytes cycle count). Reducing this
overhead through structural improvements is the most impactful open problem for making
IPM-based masking competitive in practice. Second, we expect the proposed conversion
gadgets to extend naturally to arbitrary code-based masking beyond the IPM family, and
that the hybrid framework can likewise be generalized to hybrid CM-BM constructions.
Finally, extending the hybrid IPM-BM framework to hardware implementations, where
glitch-resistant security notions are required, is a natural next step.
