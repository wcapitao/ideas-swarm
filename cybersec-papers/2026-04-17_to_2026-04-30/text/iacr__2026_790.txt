Abstract
Ongoing advances in quantum computing represent a growing risk to modern cryptography (po-
tentially threatening both asymmetric and symmetric encryption protocols), thereby challenging
the foundations of digital security. In response, global cybersecurity communities, led by stan-
dardization bodies such as NIST and ETSI, launched initiatives to establish migration pathways
toward post-quantum cryptography (PQC).
However, the migration of legacy systems to quantum-safe cryptography presents many chal-
lenges that have not yet been addressed due to their limited cryptographic agility, outdated infras-
tructure, and regulatory constraints. These legacy environments, even though they rely on aging
technologies and constrained hardware, are still vital to major sectors (such as finance, energy,
healthcare, and government).
This paper explores some obstacles to the implementation of PQC in these environments, such
as hard-coded cryptographic functions, outdated programming languages, hardware limitations,
vendor lock-in, interoperability constraints, and certification issues. This shows that, in contrast
to contemporary systems, legacy systems cannot be readily modified or easily re-engineered.
A critical review of existing standards and academic publications revealed key limitations: their
focus on algorithms specifications, the abstract guidance provided without operational depth, the
lack of empirical validations, and the insufficient risk modeling and attention to legacy constraints.
These gaps prevent effective planning and secure execution of the PQC migration in legacy systems.
Consequently, this position paper argues that existing deliverables remain insufficient to address
the specific challenges of PQC migration in legacy systems. It proposes the elaboration of a field-
informed risk-based framework for PQC Migration in Legacy Systems to guide this transition. This
proposed framework combines three interdepedent layers: a diagnostic characterization of legacy
system constraints, a qualitative risk assessment grounded in those constraints, and a quantitative
evaluation of migration options through an ROI-based analysis to support decision-making. Unlike
existing approaches that treat legacy as generic labels, this framework begins by exploring what
makes each system legacy in its specific context before applying the risk model. Its development
is informed by an empirical survey conducted among large organizations across critical sectors,
ensuring relevance beyond theoretical assumptions.
Future work will focus on elaborating the framework through applied research, tool develop-
ment, and real-world case studies in collaboration with financial institutions and critical infras-
tructure operators. In addition, continued engagement with cyber authorities and standardization
bodies will help us ensure alignment with emerging regulations.

Introduction
The rise of the quantum cyber threat is a direct consequence of the rapid advances of quantum
computing. Since public and private actors are heavily invested in the development of increasingly
powerful quantum computers [1], a growing consensus is forming around the fact that these machines,
once sufficiently mature, could break a large portion of the asymmetric encryption protocols currently
in use and even threaten certain weaker symmetric protocols [2]. This threat arises because asymmetric
schemes like RSA [3], DSA [4], ElGamal [5], and ECC [6], rely on difficult mathematical problems such
as integer factorization and discrete logarithms, which can be efficiently solved by Shor’s algorithm
[7] on a sufficiently powerful quantum computer. Despite the fact that symmetric encryptions are
comparatively more resistant, they are not immune to quantum attacks: Grover’s algorithm [8] enables
a quadratic speedup in brute-force key searches, effectively reducing the security levels of symmetric
schemes such as AES [9] by half (for example, AES-256 would provide only ∼128 bit security against
quantum attacks).
Moreover, the field of quantum computing is witnessing continuous breakthroughs [10], both in
hardware and algorithmic optimization, which may accelerate the timeline of the anticipated threat,
initially projected between 5 to 10 years
[11]. These risks are already being taken very seriously
by the main cybersecurity authorities around the world, such as NIST (United States) [12], ANSSI
(France) [13], BSI (Germany) [14], and NIS Cooperation Group (Europe) [15], all of which advocate
for immediate anticipation of these threats.
One of the most feared attacks is known as ’Harvest Now, Decrypt Later’ [16], which involves
intercepting and storing data encrypted with classical algorithms today, in anticipation of having
quantum computing power in the future to decrypt them. This scenario makes migration to post-
quantum cryptography (PQC) all the more urgent today in order to protect long-term communications,
sensitive data, and critical infrastructures. Therefore, several international initiatives (most notably the
NIST PQC Standardization Program [12]) are working to select robust PQC protocols and gradually
publishing migration guidelines to help organizations plan this transition [17].
Despite these efforts, many challenges remain with respect to PQC migration.
These include
uncertainties regarding the precise timeline of the quantum threat, doubts surrounding the maturity
and resilience of proposed PQC protocols, and the technical and operational complexities of hybrid
implementations [18]. However, a particularly critical and underexplored challenge lies in the difficulty
of migrating legacy systems, especially within large and complex IT infrastructures [19].
Legacy environments [20] represent a fundamental aspect of the IT and operational infrastructure
of many public and private organizations. These systems (exemplified in Table 1), though often based
on decades-old technologies, continue to support mission-critical functions in sectors such as banking,
healthcare, energy, transportation and government [21]. Their longevity is explained by their stability,
deep integration into business processes, and the high cost or risk of replacement [22].

2.1
Cryptographic Rigidity and Lack of Agility
One major challenge in legacy environments is their inherent lack of cryptographic agility [29]. Unlike
modern systems, which are built with modularity in mind, legacy infrastructures often have deeply
embedded cryptographic functions within their firmware. This leads to hard-coded algorithms, static
key handling, and rigid protocol stacks that make it difficult to integrate new cryptographic primitives
without substantial re-engineering [30]. Newly proposed PQC protocols, which feature different key
sizes, distinctive signature formats, and computational requirements, cannot simply be plugged into
these systems [31].
Retrofitting them usually requires extensive redevelopment, which introduces
significant risks to system integrity and business continuity [32].
2.2
Resource Constraints and Performance Limitations
PQC protocols usually require more intensive resources than current classical encryptions [33]. Many
legacy systems run on aged hardware, with limited CPU capacity and memory bandwidth, or lacking
the required cryptographic acceleration needed to handle PQC operations efficiently. Lattice-based
key encryption mechanisms (KEM) and hash-based signature schemes [34] can significantly increase
the latency or the throughput requirements, which could be problematic for real-time systems or
high-availability environments [35]. As a result, resource limitations not only jeopardize easy imple-
mentation, but may also require hardware enhancement, which may not be possible due to technical
or financial considerations [22].
3

2.3
Interoperability Issues and Vendor Dependencies
Legacy infrastructures often include multiple components from different vendors, many of whom no
longer support updates or lack PQC-compliant roadmaps [22]. In these environments, interoperability
becomes an important issue [36]. A single cryptographic upgrade can trigger widespread incidents if not
properly coordinated in related information systems [29]. Furthermore, legacy APIs and proprietary
stacks often resist modification, creating vendor lock-in situations that prevent progress [32]. In the
absence of active industry engagement and regulatory pressure, many vendors would likely delay
PQC adoption, leaving organizations exposed to extended periods of vulnerability to quantum cyber
threats [31].
2.4
Regulatory and Certification Barriers
Many legacy systems are present in highly regulated sectors (e.g., finance, healthcare, transporta-
tion) with critical infrastructure, in which any major change to cryptographic mechanisms can trigger
compliance audits or recertifications [32]. PQC protocols, while progressing through standardization,
are not yet well recognized in global certification frameworks (e.g., FIPS [37], Common Criteria [38],
eIDAS [39]) [40].
Moreover, the shift towards hybrid PQC implementation adds legal ambiguity,
requiring organizations to carefully balance the urgency of cryptographic upgrades with regulatory
restrictions [41].
2.5
Operational Risks and System Fragility
Legacy systems are often considered mission critical and fragile, operating in environments where even
minor modifications can result in cascading failures, unplanned downtime, or weak performance [42].
Cryptographic transitions are by nature systemic and sensitive, can introduce bugs, disrupt integra-
tions, or alter authentication workflows [43]. In systems with poor documentation or in the absence of
original developers, upgrades are prone to significant risks. The lack of structured planning, testing,
rollback and contingency strategies, can expose organizations to greater harm from the migration effort
than from quantum cyber threat [29].
2.6
Organizational Barriers and Structural Constraints
In addition to technical limitations, the migration of legacy systems to quantum safe protocols can
be obstructed by budget limitations, undefined ownership of the system, or other organizational fac-
tors [42]. These systems were deployed sometimes decades ago under outdated structural and op-
erational conditions, which may affect the required governance structure and stakeholder alignment
needed to support this massive transformation. Also, these organizational challenges are closely tied
to the maturity of internal processes such as IT asset management [44], lifecycle management [45],
obsolescence management [46], and IT debt management [47]. The weaker these processes are, the
more complex and risky the migration becomes, while robust, well-integrated practices in these areas
can significantly ease the transition and mitigate associated risks [29].
2.7
Programming Language Barriers
Legacy systems sometimes depend on outdated programming languages such as COBOL, early versions
of C, or even assembly. These languages do not naturally support modern security features such as
strong encryption or safe memory management. On the other hand, PQC protocols are generally built
and optimized in modern programming languages [48] such as Rust, Go, or C++, offering memory
safety features, concurrency handling, and cryptographic libraries. Hence, integrating these algorithms
into legacy systems may induce issues such as language incompatibility, runtime limitations [49], and
the lack of compilers for secure cross-language integration. This represents real technical setbacks to
the adoption of PQC in legacy environments.
4

2.8
Implications for Migration Strategy
For a successful PQC migration strategy [33] in legacy systems, cryptographic dependencies need to
be identified, and cryptographic upgrades need to be planned and secured [29]. Also, performance lim-
itations need to be quantified and factored into the broader risk analysis, ensuring realistic migration
timelines and priorities [32]. This strategy needs also to account for cross-system compatibility, vendor
coordination, and fallback mechanisms, in order to avoid service disruption. Potential regulatory re-
quirements, such as certification timelines and jurisdictional requirements, reinforce the need for a risk
framework that integrates compliance impacts [40]. More broadly, successful migration depends on a
gradual and risk-informed dedicated approach, based on system-specific impact assessments and guided
by a governance-aware methodology, that addresses not only technical threats, but also structural and
organizational barriers that can delay or obstruct a timely response [22].
3
The Gaps in Current Approaches
Despite the increased awareness towards quantum cyber threats and the drive behind PQC standard-
ization, current landscape of migration guidance and research displays many critical gaps, particu-
larly regarding legacy environments. Most existing initiatives, either led by standardization bodies
or academic researchers, concentrate on algorithmic design, protocol integration, and macro strategic
planning. Therefore, they often fail to account for the operational realities, technical rigidity, and
governance constraints specific to legacy systems.
These gaps can be summarized across five key
limitations:
3.1
Crypto-Focused Standards
Leading standardization initiatives such as the NIST PQC Program [50], ETSI ISG QSC[33], and ISO
SC 27[51] are instrumental in advancing quantum-resistant protocols and promoting best practices for
their integration. Their focus today is on the selection and specification of quantum-safe cryptographic
primitives. However, these specifications are not generally tailored to outdated architectures or systems
with low crypto-agility. Consequently, organizations with legacy environments are left without practical
methodologies to secure the deployment of PQC in these critical scopes.
The dedicated approach
needed for PQC migration in legacy systems has to extend beyond cryptographic substitution and
address the whole transition process with all its constraints.
3.2
Abstract Migration Guidelines
Many regulatory bodies and tech authorities published high-level migration strategies [52], often recom-
mending inventorying assets, classifying cryptographic dependencies, and adopting hybrid models [15].
However, these guidelines remain too generic to be effective in complex technological environments.
They tend to assume that the IT architectures concerned by the migration are modern and modular,
without providing clear procedures to prioritize efforts, mitigate service disruption, or navigate reg-
ulatory recertification in sectors with critical infrastructures [49]. Legacy systems require granular,
context-aware, and risk-driven migration roadmaps, which are currently lacking in most frameworks.
The dedicated approach needed for PQC migration in legacy systems has to be grounded in operational
reality, and provide practical and actionable guidance, instead of abstract recommendations.
3.3
Experimental Limitations
Much empirical work on the evaluation of PQC protocols has been conducted in controlled labo-
ratory settings, focusing on performance benchmarking, bandwidth requirements, or cryptographic
correctness [53]. Although informative, these experimentations rarely capture the integration friction,
lifecycle constraints, or governance challenges typical of operational legacy systems [49]. As a result,
their findings are not often transferable to production-grade environments where PQC must co-exist
with outdated components, hard-coded protocols, and real-time availability requirements [54]. The
dedicated approach needed for PQC migration in legacy systems has to be informed by real-life data
and field experience to ensure relevance and applicability.
5

3.4
Limited Risk Frameworks
Recent academic initiatives made promising progress in developing risk-based approaches to PQC
migration. These models are still in their early stages and often overlook the operational complexities
of IT environments [43]. As a result, they offer valuable theoretical insights but fall short of providing
actionable guidance for real-world deployments in resource-constrained rigid infrastructures.
Most
frameworks fail to account for sector-specific compliance obligations, interoperability risks, or the
financial implications of cryptographic transformation [55]. Also, few propose practical decision-making
tools, such as readiness indicators, system-specific risk scores, or migration playbooks, creating major
disconnects between cryptographic innovation and secure operations. The dedicated approach needed
for PQC migration in legacy systems has to integrate a comprehensive context-aware risk management
framework to support informed prioritization and sustainable execution.
3.5
Legacy Systems as a Blind Spot
Despite being widely recognized by cybersecurity officials as one of the most difficult aspects of the
transition, migrating legacy systems is still frequently overlooked in technical documentation, pilot
initiatives, and policy frameworks. Their unique characteristics, whether architectural, regulatory, or
organizational, demand targeted solutions that cannot simply be addressed through generic crypto-
graphic transition models. Without a dedicated approach, legacy environments risk becoming the
critical weak point in the post-quantum security chain.

Position: The Need for a Field-Informed, Risk-Based Frame-
work for PQC Migration in Legacy Systems
This position paper addresses the gaps illustrated in Table 3, proposing a comprehensive risk-based
framework tailored to the constraints of legacy systems undergoing post-quantum cryptography (PQC)
migration.
This framework is structured around three interdependent layers: a diagnostic assessment of legacy
system nature and constraints, a qualitative risk assessment grounded in those constraints, and a
quantitative ROI-based evaluation to support migration decision-making.
The following subsections present the key components of the proposed framework, which collectively
form the foundation of a holistic, secure, scalable, and context-aware strategy for PQC migration in
legacy environments.
7

4.1
Diagnostic characterization of legacy system constraints
A foundational element of the proposed methodology is a diagnostic assessment of legacy system nature
and constraints, ensuring that the following risk models are grounded in real-life conditions rather than
in abstract assumptions.
Unlike existing approaches that treat legacy as a generic label, this layer begins by understanding
what makes each system legacy in its specific context before any risk assessment is applied.
This diagnostic layer is informed by an empirical survey conducted among large organizations across
critical sectors. The survey captures field data on legacy system composition, cryptographic usage
patterns, and migration constraints across diverse operational environments, providing the empirical
foundation needed to identify and classify legacy systems according to a structured taxonomy aligned
with recognized governance practices (e.g., ITIL [44], ITAM [64], CIS Controls [65]).
The resulting characterization translates the complexity of legacy environments into actionable
insight by structuring them into a format that can feed directly into the qualitative and quantitative
risk models that follow.
4.2
Qualitative Risk Models to Assess Legacy PQC Migration
The second core component of the proposed methodology is the elaboration of qualitative risk models
dedicated to PQC migration in legacy systems that ensure that the migration strategy relies on strong
risk management foundations while responding to the constraints of these systems (e.g., architectural
rigidity, resource constraints, operational dependencies).
The proposed models need to be inspired by recognized risk frameworks such as ISO 31000 [66], ISO
27005 [58], NIST RMF [67], and STRIDE [64], to cover a wide range of risk categories (e.g., technical,
operational, organizational, regulatory), capturing critical factors outside cryptographic vulnerabilities
and service disruption (vendor lock-in, documentation gaps, system interoperability, recertification
impacts).
In order for these models to reflect real-life behavior, a strong collaboration between cybersecurity,
IT, and transversal functions is required, ensuring the alignment between the migration process and
the technical and organizational realities of organizations.
4.3
Quantitative Risk Models to Evaluate Legacy PQC Migration
To ensure the holistic nature of the proposed framework, the third component introduces quantitative
risk models [68] tailored to PQC migration in legacy systems. These models help organizations move
beyond qualitative assessments by assigning measurable values to risk factors, allowing more objective
data-driven decision making.
Inpired by established approaches such as FAIR [69] (Factor Analysis of Information Risk), the
methodology quantifies a wide range of legacy-specific risks, such as performance degradation, resource
constraints, upgrade costs, and downtime probabilities. It plays a key role in identifying systems in
which the adoption of PQC can exceed technical limits or trigger significant operational and compliance
burdens.
A key output of this quantitative layer is an ROI-based analysis of migration options, enabling
organizations to evaluate the financial and operational trade-offs of each migration path, ensuring
that decisions are not only technically justified, but also strategically and financially grounded [70].
When combined with qualitative insights and governance input, this layer ensures that PQC migra-
tion decisions translate into actionable, resource-aware roadmaps adapted to the realities of legacy
infrastructures.

Conclusion and Future Works
The transition to post-quantum cryptography (PQC) constitutes an important shift in digital secu-
rity with long-term implications regarding cryptographic resilience, data protection, and critical in-
frastructure integrity. Standardization bodies made significant advances regarding quantum-resistant
protocols selection and migration guidance elaboration. However, many practical challenges related
to implementing these changes are not yet adequately addressed, especially in legacy systems. These
environments frequently inflexible, resource-poor and strongly embedded in operational and regulatory
processes require more specific attention.
This paper argued for a comprehensive risk-based framework, tailored to PQC migration in legacy
systems. This suggested approach incorporates incorporates a diagnostic assessment of legacy system
nature and challenges, qualitative and quantitative risk modeling, and migration planning within a
holistic context-aware methodology. It intends to provide organizations with actionable, scalable tools
to achieve cryptographic goals despite real-world constraints of architecture, governance, and sector-
specific regulations.
The proposed position will be substantiated further though a dedicated research program that
merges theoretical guidance with applied experimentation.
Future work will produce risk models,
validate prioritization criteria, and develop practical tools, that can support real-world implementation.
These efforts will be supported by practical case studies within financial institutions and critical
infrastructure operators, providing insight across diverse technical and organizational settings.
Finally, our continued engagement with standardization bodies and regulatory stakeholders will
ensure that the framework evolves in accordance with emerging regulatory requirements and sector-
specific expectations.
The primary objective being the deliverance of a rigorous yet operationally
grounded reference model for transitioning legacy environments into post-quantum readiness.
