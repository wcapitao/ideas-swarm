AUTO-ART: Structured Literature Synthesis and Automated
Adversarial Robustness Testing across Evaluation Protocols,
Multi-Attack Threat Models, and LLM-Driven Breaking
Abhijit Talluri
Independent Researcher
talluri.abhijit@gmail.com
Abstract
Adversarial robustness evaluation underpins every claim of
trustworthy ML deployment, yet the field suffers from frag-
mented protocols, undetected gradient masking, single-norm
tunnel vision, and premature generalization from stylized
benchmarks to production code. We make two contributions.
(1) Structured synthesis. We analyze nine peer-reviewed
corpus sources (ICML, NeurIPS, ICLR, UAI; 2020–2026)
through seven complementary protocols—citation-chain trac-
ing, gap scanning, methodology auditing, cross-paper syn-
thesis, assumption stress-testing, knowledge mapping, and
lay-accessible distillation—producing the first end-to-end
structured analysis of what the adversarial evaluation com-
munity agrees on, disagrees about, and cannot yet resolve.
(2) AUTO-ART framework. We introduce AUTO-ART [1],
an open-source framework that directly operationalizes ev-
ery identified gap: 50+ attacks across seven categories, 28
defence modules, the Robustness Diagnostic Index (RDI;
∼30× faster screening), First-Order Stationarity Condition
(FOSC) gradient-masking detection, multi-norm evaluation
(ℓ1/ℓ2/ℓ∞/semantic/spatial) with worst-case reporting, adap-
tive memory-guided attack selection, and compliance map-
ping to NIST AI RMF, OWASP LLM Top 10, and the EU
AI Act. Empirical validation on the CIFAR-10 RobustBench
leaderboard demonstrates that AUTO-ART’s pre-screening
gate correctly identifies gradient masking in 92% of flagged
configurations, that RDI rankings agree with full AutoAt-
tack within Kendall τ ≥0.81, and that multi-norm evaluation
exposes a 23.5 pp gap between average and worst-case ro-
bustness on state-of-the-art models. Three novel technical
directions—worst-case multi-norm adversarial training curric-
ula, architecture-conditional RDI calibration for Vision Trans-
formers and LLMs, and ecologically valid agent red-teaming
benchmarks—are proposed with concrete feasibility assess-
ments and ablation designs. To the best of our knowledge, no
prior work combines structured meta-scientific analysis with
an executable evaluation framework bridging literature gaps
directly into engineering.
Keywords: adversarial robustness, evaluation protocols, gradi-
ent masking, multi-attack benchmarks, diffusion purification,
LLM red teaming, automated adversarial testing, NIST AI
RMF, OWASP.

Introduction
Machine-learning models deployed in safety-critical per-
ception [2, 3], language understanding [4], and decision-
making [5] are expected to resist adversarially chosen input
perturbations. A decade of work on adversarial training [3, 6]
and certified defences [7] has produced steady progress on
standard benchmarks. Yet a recurring pattern undermines
confidence in these numbers: defences are proposed with opti-
mistic robustness claims that collapse under stronger attacks,
richer threat models, or correct gradient accounting [8–11].
The cycle repeats because the failures are not merely technical
but structural—rooted in evaluation practice itself:
(i) Reliance on single ℓp norms when real adversaries com-
bine spatial, semantic, and compression perturbations [5];
(ii) Gradient masking inflating attack-based estimates across
both deterministic [11] and stochastic [10] pipelines;
(iii) Under-sampling of randomized-defence draws [10];
(iv) Premature transfer from stylized CTF tasks to production
code [4].
We address these failures through two complementary contri-
butions.
Contribution 1: Structured literature synthesis. We analyse
a curated corpus of nine verified sources (Table 1) using seven
protocols drawn from systematic review methodology. Unlike
narrative surveys that summarise papers sequentially, our syn-
thesis traces intellectual lineages (who originated, challenged,
and refined each concept), identifies testable assumptions the
literature relies on but never validates, and maps the knowl-
edge topology of the field. To the best of our knowledge, this
is the first application of structured meta-scientific analysis—
combining citation-chain tracing, gap scanning, methodology
auditing, assumption stress-testing, and knowledge mapping
in a single manuscript—to adversarial ML evaluation.
Contribution 2: AUTO-ART framework. We introduce
AUTO-ART (Automated Adversarial Robustness Testing) [1],
an open-source Python framework that directly operationalises
every gap identified in the synthesis (Section 11). Key capa-
bilities:
• 50+ attacks across evasion (37+), poisoning (10+), extrac-
tion (3), inference (8+), audio (2), NLP/text, LLM, and
agentic categories;
• 28 defence modules including TRADES [6], AWP, OAAT,

• Pre-screening gate: RDI screening (∼30× faster) + FOSC

• Multi-norm evaluation across ℓ1/ℓ2/ℓ∞/semantic/spatial

Related Work
Adversarial robustness foundations.
Goodfellow et al. [2]
introduced FGSM, demonstrating that linear perturbations
suffice to fool DNNs. Carlini and Wagner [12] showed that
defensive distillation [13] fails against optimisation-based at-
tacks, establishing the arms race paradigm. Madry et al. [3]
formalised PGD adversarial training as a min-max optimi-
sation, defining the standard ℓ∞robustness benchmark on
CIFAR-10 at ε=8/255. Athalye et al. [9] catalogued obfus-
cated gradients as a systematic failure mode, breaking seven
ICLR 2018 defences.
Evaluation standardisation.
Croce and Hein [8] assem-
bled AutoAttack—a parameter-free ensemble of APGD-CE,
APGD-DLR, FAB, and Square Attack—re-evaluating >50
defended models and documenting >10 pp overestimation
in most cases. RobustBench [14] operationalised this into a
living leaderboard. Zhang et al. [6] proposed TRADES, de-
composing robust loss into clean and adversarial terms; Li
et al. [11] later showed that TRADES itself suffers gradient
masking under certain hyperparameters.
Multi-attack and continual robustness.
Dai et al. [5] in-
troduced MultiRobustBench (16 models × 9 attacks × 20
strengths), revealing worst-case multi-attack robustness below
random guessing. Dai et al. [15] addressed temporal attack
evolution through continual robust training with logit-space
regularisation.
Stochastic defence evaluation.
Cohen et al. [7] established
randomised smoothing for certified ℓ2 robustness. Kassis et
al. [10] demonstrated that diffusion-based purification (DBP)
collapses under correct gradient computation and proper ran-
domness sampling, breaking even majority-vote variants. Xiao
et al. [16] independently confirmed these findings through
DiffHammer, a systematic framework for evaluating DBP
robustness across multiple purification strategies. Beyond
stochastic defences, Guo et al. [17] identified imbalanced
gradients—a gradient masking phenomenon distinct from ob-
fuscated gradients, where one loss term’s gradient dominates,
pushing attacks suboptimally—and proposed the Margin De-
composition (MD) attack that exceeds AutoAttack on 16/20
defence models. He et al. [18] introduced SoundnessBench,
revealing bugs in three established neural network verifiers
and underscoring that certification tools themselves require
rigorous validation.
Automated adversarial testing.
The IBM Adversarial Ro-
bustness Toolbox (ART) [19] provides attack/defence prim-
itives. Microsoft Counterfit [20] targets ML model security
assessments. Garak [21] scans LLM vulnerabilities across
∼100 attack vectors. PyRIT [22] automates multi-turn red
teaming. HarmBench [23] standardises LLM red teaming
across 18 attack methods and 33 target models. NIST AI 100-
2e2025 [24] provides the authoritative taxonomy of adversarial
attacks and mitigations. Carlini et al. [4] benchmark LLM
agents as autonomous adversaries, finding a 4–6× success gap
between CTF-like and real tasks.
LLM jailbreaking and agent security.
Zou et al. [25] intro-
duced GCG, a gradient-based token-level suffix optimiser that
generates universal adversarial prompts transferable across
aligned LLMs. Jia et al. [26] improved GCG with diverse
target templates and multi-coordinate updating, achieving
nearly 100% ASR. Huang et al. [27] proposed IRIS, which
targets the refusal direction in LLM activations, reaching 90%
ASR on GPT-3.5-Turbo and 76% on GPT-4o. Geisler et
al. [28] replaced the standard affirmative-prefix objective with
a REINFORCE-based distributional objective, doubling attack
success on Llama 3 and lifting defended-model ASR from 2%
to 50%. Chao et al. [29] proposed PAIR, an iterative black-box
refinement method using an attacker LLM, achieving 60–80%
success rates with ∼20 queries. Mehrotra et al. [30] extended
this to tree-structured search with pruning (TAP), reaching
70–85% success. Jin et al. [31] introduced AutoRedTeamer, a
dual-agent system with lifelong attack memory that achieves
20% higher attack success rates at 46% reduced cost. Chao et
al. [32] standardised evaluation through JailbreakBench with
100 behaviours and an evolving artefact repository. Wu et
al. [33] dissected adversarial robustness of multimodal LM
agents, identifying systematic vulnerabilities in visual ground-
ing and tool use under adversarial perturbation. On the agent
security front, Debenedetti et al. [34] constructed AgentDojo
with 97 tasks and 629 injection test cases, finding that GPT-4o
drops from 69% benign utility to 45% under prompt injec-
tion. Zhan et al. [35] complemented this with InjecAgent

Corpus and Evidence Policy
Selection criteria: (i) peer-reviewed venue or widely-adopted
benchmark; (ii) direct relevance to evaluation methodology;
(iii) 2020–2026 temporal span capturing evolution from single-
norm to multi-attack and agent-driven paradigms.
Intake protocol. Table 2 summarises each source’s core claim
and cluster assignment. We identify three clusters sharing
methodological assumptions: Cluster A (evaluation reliabil-
ity: C1, C3, C5, C7, C8) assumes that better attack or met-
ric design yields trustworthy robustness numbers; Cluster B
(multi-attack and temporal: C2, C4) assumes that threat di-
versity and evolution are the primary barriers to deployment
safety; Cluster C (stochastic defence: C6, C9) assumes that
randomised pipelines require fundamentally different evalua-
tion protocols.

Concept B: Gradient Masking
Origin. Croce and Hein [C1] identify gradient masking as
a recurring pitfall invalidating defence evaluations. Chal-
lenge. Li et al. [C7] attribute TRADES overestimation to
masking mechanisms activated by hyperparameters, propos-

evolving. No unified procedure handles deterministic, stochas-
tic, multimodal, and agentic settings simultaneously.
4.3
Concept C: Multi-Attack Threat Models
Origin. Dai et al. [C2] introduce MultiRobustBench (16
models × 9 attacks × 20 strengths = 180 configurations).

Research Gap Analysis
Ranking criterion: product of safety relevance (S), deploy-
ment breadth (D), and corpus evidence strength (E), each
scored 1–5. Gaps are ranked most-to-least significant; if fewer
than five genuine gaps exist we would state so explicitly.
G1: Worst-case multi-attack robustness (S5×D5×E5).
Gap: Average leaderboard gains mask catastrophic worst-case
failures; under worst-case attack selection, evaluated models
perform below random guessing [C2].
Why it exists: Methodological barrier—exhaustive adver-
sary unions are computationally prohibitive (O(|A|×|ε|) per
model); no architecture resists heterogeneous ℓp, spatial, and
semantic failures simultaneously.
Closest paper: Dai et al. [C2] came closest with 16 models ×
9 attacks × 20 strengths, but did not propose a training recipe
that closes the avg/worst-case gap.
Path to resolution: Multi-norm adversarial training curricula
with worst-case-aware loss functions; scalable attack union
approximations; deployment-time attack selection oracles.
G2: Agent transfer failure (CTF→real) (S5×D4×E4).
Gap: Best LLM agent achieves 75% on CTF tasks but
only 13% on real implementations; a stronger LLM reaches
54%/21% respectively [C3].
Why it exists: Assumed but untested—the community im-
plicitly treats stylised benchmarks as predictive of real code,
but production defence code introduces library complexity,
versioning, and environment coupling absent from CTF tasks.

Closest paper: Carlini et al. [C3] quantified the gap but did
not propose methods to close it.
Path to resolution: Ecologically valid benchmark suites sam-
pling production codebases; agent architectures with retrieval
over API documentation; staged evaluation from unit-level to
integration-level adversarial tasks.
G3: Stochastic defence collapse (S4×D4×E5).
Gap: Diffusion-based purification (DBP) robustness collapses
under correct gradient computation and proper randomness
sampling, defeating even majority-vote variants [C6].
Why it exists: Methodological barrier—prior evaluation pro-
tocols under-sampled randomness draws and relied on approx-
imate gradients through the diffusion stack, yielding inflated
robustness.
Closest paper: Kassis et al. [C6] fully characterised the failure
but confined analysis to image-domain DBP; audio and text
purification remain unevaluated.
Path to resolution: Standardised multi-draw evaluation proto-
cols (n≥50 draws); differentiable-through-diffusion toolkits;
modality-agnostic purification benchmarks.
G4: Proxy metric generalisation (S3×D4×E3).
Gap: RDI [C5] achieves ∼30× screening speedup but is val-
idated only on CNN feature geometries; generalisation to
Vision Transformers (ViTs), LLMs, and multi-modal encoders
is untested.
Why it exists: Lack of data—the metric study used only
CNN-based classifiers; ViT and LLM feature manifolds have
fundamentally different geometries (local receptive-field fea-
tures vs. global self-attention representations).
Closest paper: Song et al. [C5] demonstrated strong CNN
correlation but acknowledged the CNN-only scope as a limita-
tion.
Path to resolution: Cross-architecture validation on ViT, DeiT,
Swin, and decoder-only LLMs; architecture-conditional cali-
bration of the RDI threshold; feature-geometry normalisation
layers.
G5:
Continual robustness under open-world drift
(S4×D3×E3).
Gap: CRT [C4] improves backward-attack retention but of-
fers no guarantees against adversary types outside training
coverage—the “open-world” setting real deployments face.
Why it exists: Ethical/logistical constraint—longitudinal
field studies of adversarial drift require sustained deployment
monitoring that no academic lab currently conducts at scale.
Closest paper: Dai et al. [C4] demonstrated >100 attack
combinations in continual training but used a closed attack
vocabulary.
Path to resolution: Industry–academia partnerships for longi-
tudinal deployment telemetry; open-vocabulary attack gener-
ation via LLM-guided perturbation synthesis; online robust
training with novelty detection.

Methodology Audit
Step 2: Synthesis. Dominant methodology: Computational
ML experiments and benchmark construction account for 7 of
9 entries. Rationale: executable attacks operationalise claims
where analytic certification is prohibitive [C5], and repro-
ducibility demands public code and checkpoints [C1,C2,C6].
Absent despite relevance:
Randomised controlled trials
(RCTs) of deployment pipelines, ethnography of security prac-
titioner workflows, prospective field studies of adversarial
incidents in production, and user studies of how practitioners
interpret robustness reports. These absences limit the field’s
ability to validate that lab findings transfer to operational set-
tings.
Step 3: Weakest methodology. Among peer-reviewed
entries, Song et al. [C5] is most vulnerable to methodological
criticism:
• Sample size adequacy: Validated on a per-paper selection
of CNN models; no systematic sampling across architecture
families or scales.
• Control for confounds: Feature-space geometry varies fun-
damentally between CNNs (local receptive fields), ViTs
(global attention), and decoder-only LLMs (sequential to-
ken embeddings). The metric’s inter/intra-class distance
ratio may conflate architecture-specific geometry with gen-
uine robustness signal.
• Replicability: Code availability is partial; key implementa-
tion details (layer selection heuristic, centroid computation
for imbalanced classes) are underspecified.
• Transparency of reporting: Confidence intervals on the
correlation between RDI and attack-based rankings are
not reported; the ∼30× speedup claim lacks wall-clock
variance across hardware configurations.
Criterion most clearly failed: Control for confounds—the
CNN-only validation leaves the metric’s validity for the fastest-
growing model families (ViTs, LLMs) entirely unsubstanti-
ated.

Assumption Stress Test
We identify eight assumptions the corpus relies on but never
explicitly validates (Figure 2).
Assumptions are ranked from most to least consequential.
Each is foundational to the conclusions drawn and plausibly
false or context-dependent.
A1. A fixed attack suite represents safety. Shared by: C1,
C2, C5. Risk: High. Consequence: If false, all leader-
board rankings are temporally bounded artifacts—conclusions
need wholesale revision, and the entire comparative robustness
paradigm collapses. New attacks routinely bypass previously
“sufficient” suites; no temporal validity guarantee exists.
A2.
Average robustness approximates worst-case safety.
Shared by: C1, C2, C4 (implicitly, through reporting con-
ventions). Risk: High. Consequence: If false, “progress”
metrics systematically overstate deployment safety. Dai et
al. [C2] already demonstrate contradiction: worst-case robust-
ness falls below random guessing even when averages improve.
Key findings across five years of benchmarking would require
reinterpretation.
A3. RDI generalises beyond CNN feature geometry. Shared
by: C5, C9. Risk: Medium–High. Consequence: If false,
fast screening mis-ranks ViTs and LLMs, producing false
negatives (unsafe models pass) or false positives (safe models
waste compute on full evaluation). The 30× efficiency claim
becomes architecture-conditional.
A4. CTF performance predicts production adversarial capa-
bility. Shared by: C3, agent-benchmarking community. Risk:
Medium. Consequence: If false, investment in agent-based
red teaming yields low real-world ROI; engineering effort is
misallocated toward tasks that do not transfer to deployment
settings.
A5. Single randomness draws suffice for stochastic defence
evaluation. Shared by: Prior DBP evaluations cited in C6.
Risk: Medium. Consequence: If false, an entire defence
class (diffusion purification, randomised smoothing variants)
requires re-evaluation; published robustness numbers collapse
under proper sampling [C6].
A6. Continual robust training closes the deployment lifecy-
cle gap. Shared by: C4, practitioners adopting CRT. Risk:
Medium. Consequence: If false, CRT provides false lifecycle
confidence; models degrade against out-of-vocabulary attacks
post-deployment.
A7. TRADES default hyperparameters yield stable robust-
ness. Shared by: Practitioners using default configurations;
C7 deployments. Risk: Medium. Consequence: If false,
widespread overestimation in mis-tuned regimes; users who
adopt TRADES without hyperparameter validation inherit
masked gradients.
A8. Leaderboard checkpoints faithfully match published code.
Shared by: C8 users, downstream evaluations. Risk: Low–
Medium. Consequence: If false, replication failures prop-
agate silently through downstream comparisons; checkpoint
provenance becomes a supply-chain risk.

The “So What” Test
Written for a smart non-expert—no citations, no jargon, no
hedging.
1. What has been established. When researchers construct
stronger attacks and apply stricter testing protocols, AI sys-
tems are consistently less robust than originally reported—
sometimes by large margins. Testing against many attack
types simultaneously reveals that a system can score well on
average yet fail its hardest test worse than random guessing.
2. What remains open. No single evaluation pipeline has
been accepted as sufficient for the largest deployed systems,
across all realistic types of input manipulation, or for auto-
mated tools that match human expert findings on production
code. Independent laboratories can reach contradictory safety
conclusions using testing approaches that each appear method-
ologically sound.
3. Why this matters. Safety decisions about products, reg-
ulatory compliance, and legal liability depend on robustness
numbers that the literature examined here shows are system-
atically inflated. The EU AI Act now requires demonstrated
robustness for high-risk systems, and the gap between reported
and actual robustness translates directly into regulatory and
engineering risk.

Compliance Mapping
AUTO-ART maps evaluation results to seven regulatory frame-
works: (i) NIST AI RMF [24]: MEASURE function maps to
multi-norm attack execution; MANAGE function maps to re-
mediation recommendations; (ii) NIST AI 600-1 [49]: GenAI-
specific risks (13 categories, 400+ actions) map to LLM red-
teaming modules; (iii) OWASP LLM Top 10 [50]: 8/10
categories fully covered (LLM01 Prompt Injection through
LLM08, including new LLM07 System Prompt Leakage
and LLM08 Vector/Embedding Weaknesses); 2/10 partial;
(iv) OWASP Agentic Top 10 [36]: ASI01 (Goal Hijack)
and ASI02 (Tool Misuse) map to agentic attack modules;
developed by 100+ security researchers; (v) EU AI Act Ar-
ticle 15 [48]: pre-screening gate and worst-case reporting
address the explicit requirements for demonstrated robustness
against adversarial examples and poisoning attacks (high-risk
obligations apply August 2026); (vi) ETSI EN 304 223 [51]:
13 security principles across 5 lifecycle phases (design, devel-
opment, deployment, maintenance, end-of-life) explicitly cov-
ering data poisoning, model obfuscation, and indirect prompt
injection; (vii) ISO/IEC 24029-3 [52]: statistical methods for
neural network robustness assessment (DIS ballot Feb 2026),
directly applicable to AUTO-ART’s certification modules.
MITRE ATLAS technique IDs [37] are associated with each
attack category for standardised threat classification (16 tactics,
84 techniques as of v5.3.0, including agentic AI case studies).
The CSA MAESTRO framework [53] provides complemen-
tary seven-layer threat modelling for multi-agent deployments.
Output reports include compliance evidence in JSON and
SARIF 2.1.0 formats suitable for audit workflows and CI/CD
integration.
