Failure of proximity gaps close to capacity⋆
Dmitry Krachun1, Stepan Kazanin2, and Ulrich Hab¨ock3
1 Princeton University
2 Stanford University
3 StarkWare Industries
Abstract. We give a simple counterexample which shows that, for Reed–
Solomon codes over multiplicative subgroups of prime fields, proximity
gaps do not hold near capacity, at least not as conjectured by Ben-Sasson,
et al., in [BCI+20]. For relative distance θ = 1 −ρ −η, where ρ is the
rate of the code, and positive η = Θρ(1/ log n), where n is the length
of the code, we construct an affine line that is not entirely θ-close to
the code but still contains 2Ωρ(1/η) such points. The same construction
gives a slightly stronger list-decoding lower bound. The proof uses a new
additive-combinatorics lemma on sums of roots of unity.

Introduction
Let F be a finite field, and D be a finite subset of F of size n, and let C =
RS[F, D, k] denote the Reed–Solomon code obtained by evaluating polynomials
of degree at most k on D. For any two u, v ∈FD, ∆(u, v) denotes the relative
Hamming distance. Given an affine line in FD, spanned by two words u0, u1 ∈FD,
where u1 ̸= 0,
L = {u0 + λu1 : λ ∈F} ,
and parameter θ ∈(0, 1), the proximity-gap question asks whether L can contain
many words u with relative distance ∆(u, C) = minc∈C ∆(u, c) ≤θ, without
being entirely contained in the θ-neighborhood of C. That is, one asks for an
upper bound on the number of θ-close points
|{λ ∈F : ∆(u0 + λu1, C) ≤θ}| ,
over all lines which are not entirely θ-close. This upper bound is called soundness
error of the proximity gap at distance θ.
Proximity gaps play a key role in the construction of interactive oracle proofs
[BCS16], also called probabilistic checkable interactive proofs [RRR16], and in
particular in the celebrated FRI proof of proximity to Reed–Solomon codes
[BSBHR18], which is widely used in practice. A convenient close-to-capacity
version of the conjectural picture is the following:

Overview.
This note is a polished and extended version of a previous draft, which has
been shared with the Ethereum Foundation in regards to their 1 Million Dollar
Proximity Prize [Fou25]. We fill in some details and elaborate on list-decodability
(Item 1 above), an aspect that has not been discussed in the draft. The writeup
is organized as follows:
– Section 2 covers the original proof of the proximity gap Theorem 1, Item 2,
for a slightly larger bound on β than stated above. The construction goes
along the same lines as in [BCH+25, Section 7], and is based on vanishing
polynomials of large cosets of the domain. However, instead of relying on a
conjecture on the sum-sets of multiplicative subgroups, we prove a weaker
variant of their conjecture, which is yet sufficient to yield the claimed prox-
imity gap.
– Section 3 then shows how to easily read off the claimed list-decoding center
(Item 1 above) from the same construction.
– In Appendix A, we supply a simpler proof of the theorem, based on the
above mentioned quotienting construction from [CS25] and [BCH+25]. That
construction is generic and highlights the important role of list-decodability.
Somewhat surprisingly we are able to yield even slightly better concrete
bounds than with the direct approach from Section 2.
We conclude by noting that Theorem 1 affects the parameters of practical
interactive oracle proofs more significantly than suggested by Fenzi and Sanso
[FS25]. For a detailed discussion of the issue we refer to the whitepaper of Car-
mon, et al. [CGH+26].
