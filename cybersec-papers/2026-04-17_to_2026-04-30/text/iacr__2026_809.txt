Abstract. The resistance of provably secure masked circuits to physical attacks
depends in part on the underlying algebraic group and recombination function.
Masking over finite fields of odd prime order has been demonstrated, both in theory
and in practice, to provide increased natural resistance to side-channel and fault
attacks. Its instantiation with a simple additive encoding and implementation-friendly
prime modulus was suggested to lead to favorable tradeoffs between security and
performance in prior works. To most efficiently leverage these advantages, a family
of lightweight Tweakable Block Ciphers (TBCs) called Feistel for Prime Masking
(FPM) has been introduced by Grassi et al. at Eurocrypt’24, together with a first
hardware-oriented instance called small-pSquare. Yet, barriers for the use and further
development of prime-field masking continue to exist and include the lack of automated
verification tools compatible with arithmetic over Fp, as well as efficient methods
for constant-time generation of uniformly distributed randomness over the field. In
this work we tackle these barriers and present our findings from formally verifying,
securely integrating and physically evaluating higher-order masked implementations
of small-pSquare as an exemplary case study. Our integration includes the tape-out of
an Application-Specific Integrated Circuit (ASIC) manufactured in 65 nm technology
and a custom Printed Circuit Board (PCB). We demonstrate how to securely verify
prime-field masked circuits with existing tools such as SILVER, MATCHI and PROLEAD
and certify the glitch+transition robustness of our concrete implementations. Along
the way we discover and solve a 0-issue originating from incomplete modulo reductions
which is present in public source codes of masked prime-field ciphers but has never
been discussed. We also introduce Privium, a Bivium-inspired primitive, to efficiently
produce random values uniformly distributed over Fp without the need for rejection
sampling. We then describe our efficient serialized pipelined small-pSquare architecture
enabling an attractive tradeoff between area and latency and compare its pre- and
post-layout implementation figures. Finally, we experimentally demonstrate the
strong leakage resistance of our formally verified circuits on real silicon.

Introduction
Historically, the design of secure cryptographic algorithms and the study of their imple-
mentation security have been largely independent research areas. Algorithms would be
selected based on their back-box security and implementation efficiency in software and/or
hardware. Their potential protection against physical adversaries was most commonly
an afterthought – supposed to be figured out by a different branch of the cryptographic
research community. Over the years it has become increasingly apparent that i) physical

security can be important in more contexts than traditionally assumed (see e.g. remote
side-channel [MDB21] or fault attacks [GMM16]) and ii) considering the suitability of
algorithms for the efficient application of the relevant countermeasures as early in the
design process as possible can save a lot of headache later and lead to substantially
better tradeoffs between security and performance once physical security matters. PI-
CARO [PRC12], Zorro [GGNS13], Fides [BBK+13] and Robin/Fantomas [GLSV14] are
early examples of symmetric ciphers deliberately designed for the efficient application
of the masking countermeasure. Even recently standardized general-purpose primitives
like the Ascon family of lightweight cryptographic algorithms [DEMS21] have made
specific design choices (in this case a non-linear layer of low algebraic degree) to stay
competitive in settings where the application of masking is a must. Such design goals are
more frequently made explicit for symmetric cryptographic algorithms, but examples also
exist for asymmetric (post-quantum) cryptography, notably the Raccoon digital signature
scheme [dPPRS23, dPKPR24]. The differences between primitive-level and mode-level
techniques to provide security in presence of leakage are discussed in [BBC+20].
With respect to fault attacks, the tweakable block cipher CRAFT [BLMR19] roots its
design decisions in the goal of facilitating the efficient application of countermeasures against
Differential Fault Analysis (DFA) – arguably the most powerful class of fault attacks. The
authenticated encryption scheme Friet [SBD+20] and the block cipher DEFAULT [BBB+21]
made use of cipher-level design techniques to inherently detect or prevent fault attacks,
but eventually succumbed to vulnerabilities [WFH+22, NDE22, JKP24].
In summary, all of the aforementioned primitives make deliberate decisions within their
design space to aid the application of side-channel and/or fault countermeasures, commonly
observing improved tradeoffs when physical security is needed. While the majority of
designs is based on bit-level operations, a number of recent works decided to leave the
comfort of this established design space to let physical security considerations fully guide
the underlying algebraic setting. This has opened up a new direction for masking-friendly
cipher design based on arithmetic over odd prime-order fields which we focus on in this
work. First we extend existing formal and simulation-based verification methods to this
algebraic setting. Then we integrate and evaluate a concrete primitive in a case study.
Prime Ciphers for Physical Security.
After it had been discovered that masking over
groups of odd prime order generally comes with improved noise resistance [DFS16], the
authors of [MMMS23] performed an information theoretic analysis of different masked
encodings to find that equivalent recombination functions provide stronger security for
a given number of shares when defined over prime-order fields regardless of the noise
level (although the gap to binary-field masking is still most pronounced at lower noise
levels). This prompted the design of an exemplary AES-like cipher operating over Fp
with p = 27 −1 = 127, a small Mersenne prime. The most straightforward choice for
masking-friendly non-linear functions over prime fields is power maps of low degree, where
exponentiation can be realized from a few squarings and/or multiplications. Contrary to the
common binary-field setting, the squaring operation is non-linear in odd prime fields. Thus,
corresponding provably secure and composable gadgets needed to be introduced [CMM+23].
These masked gadgets proved to be almost twice as efficient as corresponding masked
multiplications in terms of partial products to compute while providing the same algebraic
degree, which immediately made them an interesting component for masking-friendly
cipher design. This observation provided the necessary foundation for the introduction
of the Feistel for Prime Masking (FPM) family of lightweight Tweakable Block Ciphers
(TBCs) [GMM+24] which uses squaring as the only non-linear component inside a Feistel
structure. A hardware-oriented instance of the family is called small-pSquare and also
based on the Mersenne prime p = 27 −1 = 127. A software-oriented instance has been
proposed a year later under the name mid-pSquare [BGM+25] and operates over F231−1.

Masking Verification.
Masking has been proposed more than 25 years ago as a sound
countermeasure against side-channel attacks [CJRR99]. Relevant formalizations since then
include the probing model [ISW03], the robust probing model [FGP+18] as well as different
composability notions based on non-interference [BBD+16, CS20]. These formalizations
enable designers to not only prove the local security of gadgets for atomic operations
(like multiplication or squaring) under physical defaults, but also their secure composition.
While these notions make the path towards a securely masked cipher implementation
significantly less rocky, maintaining robust probing security through optimizations and
composition of multiple parts of a larger algorithm still remains extremely brittle and
error-prone. For that reason, a large number of automated tools for masking security
verification applicable at different stages of the design process has been introduced over
the past years [BBD+16, BGI+18, BGR18, BBC+19, KSM20, GHP+21, HB21, CGLS21,
BMRT22, MM22, RFSG22, ZMM23, ZCF25, BFG+25].
Randomness.
Masking schemes consume randomness – at least for the initial sharing of
the inputs, but most commonly also during each processing of a non-linear gadget. Excep-
tions to the latter are usually not generalizable to arbitrary orders in an efficient manner.
Depending on the concrete number of shares and the size of the cryptographic algorithm
to mask, the number of random elements consumed per clock cycle can be very significant,
especially in hardware where parallel computation is common. A multitude of solutions
exists for concurrent randomness generation for masked implementations [UHIM24], but
the vast majority of them focuses on generating uniformly random bits, i.e., elements of F2.
This also includes the recent study suggesting that in hardware implementation, unrolled
Trivium-like stream ciphers lead to great efficiency for masking randomness generation
and sufficient leakage resilience to avoid state recovery [CMM+24].

Our Contributions
In this work we provide a comprehensive end-to-end security verification and full integration
of masked small-pSquare implementations for multiple protection orders on actual silicon,
including a physical evaluation of the final circuits on the manufactured chip. To the best
of our knowledge this work is the first example of such a complete end-to-end study. As a
general contribution we detail how existing formal and simulation-based verification tools,
in particular SILVER1 [KSM20], MATCHI2 [Cas25] and PROLEAD3 [MM22], can be used
to correctly verify circuits operating over Fp elements for their robust probing security
and trivial composability. During the verification we notice that existing source codes
for Fp squaring gadgets use incomplete modulo reductions in a very liberal, potentially
dangerous, manner. We discuss and provide efficient workarounds for this issue and
verify their correctness with multiple tools. We then introduce Privium, a Bivium-inspired
constant-time generator of uniformly distributed field elements without the need for re-
jection sampling. The concrete instance proposed is specific to F27−1, but its underlying
design principles are generalizable to other Mersenne primes. While we do not provide
any black-box security analysis of the Privium primitive in this work, we have tested the
quality of its randomness output using common statistical test suites and conjecture its
cryptanalytic security and leakage resilience to be stronger than that of Bivium. We

Tool-Assisted Verification
We begin our investigation by discussing how masked implementations operating over
prime fields can be verified using existing automated tools. We separate between formal
verification of security properties at gadget and composition level as well as simulation-based
verification of circuits.
2.1
Preliminaries
In this work, we consider the security of masked implementations in the robust probing
model [FGP+18], where an adversary can place t ϵ-extended probes in the masked circuit.
These ϵ-extended probes are convenient abstractions to capture physical defaults such as
glitches, transitions and couplings. In this work we focus on glitch-extended probes only, as
the resulting security properties at the gadget level are sufficient for our purposes to later
(under some additional conditions) obtain glitch+transition-robustness at the composition
level. Each glitch-extended probe placed on a wire in the circuit produces as leakage the
collective values carried by all wires in the combinatorial circuit that computes the probed
wire. Given a set of glitch-extended probes P in a masked gadget over a field Fq, the input
sharings x and the input randomness r, we denote by VP (x, r) the values observed by the
probes, as a map from P to Fq. For such probes, we can define the notion of simulatability,
which intuitively means that the probes only depend on some input shares, and not on
others.

2.2
Formal Verification
Formal verification tools for gadget- or circuit-level analysis of local and/or compositional
security properties include REBECCA [BGI+18], maskVerif [BBC+19], SILVER [KSM20],
Coco [GHP+21], CocoAlma [HB21], IronMask [BMRT22], VERICA [RFSG22], Prover [ZCF25]
and INDIANA [BFG+25]. Automated tools for the formal verification of full circuits com-
posed from gadgets satisfying certain composability notions include maskComp [BBD+16],
tightPROVE [BGR18], fullVerif [CGLS21] and MATCHI [Cas25]. In this work we focus on
SILVER for gadget-level verification of glitch-robust trivial composability (PINI) properties
and MATCHI for the composition-level verification of the glitch+transition-robustness of
the final pipelined circuits.
2.2.1
Gadget-Level Formal Verification
In this section, we want to verify that our masked gadgets are indeed glitch-robust PINI.
For this purpose, we use the verification tool SILVER [KSM20]. Since the tool is taking as
input a netlist in Hardware Description Language (HDL), SILVER allows us to directly
verify the implementation of the gadgets, avoiding any possible mismatch between a verified
specification and the concrete implementation. However, natively, SILVER can only verify
glitch-robust PINI for circuits masked over F2.
We solve this issue by building a circuit compiler C such that for any gadget G over
Fp, C(G) is a masked circuit over F2, and C(G) is glitch-robust t-PINI if and only if
G is glitch-robust t-PINI. The gadget G must contain only registers and combinational
gates for addition, subtraction and multiplication (including squaring as a special case of
multiplication).

2.2.2
Composition-Level Formal Verification
In addition to the local and compositional properties of our gadgets, we ideally would like
to perform an automated verification of the final composite circuits. MATCHI [Cas25] is
a recent tool for this purpose, taking as input a netlist and its respective input signals, to
simulate the execution of the synchronous circuit, tracking the dataflow and dependencies of

masked sharings and random inputs in each wire. Non-linear gadgets have to be annotated
explicitly by their PINI/OPINI property, which MATCHI assumes to be correct. Based
on this process the tool then evaluates the glitch+transition-robust probing security of the
circuit. Interestingly, MATCHI is not inherently limited to F2 verification and can handle
synthesized circuits of implementations defined over Fp. Yet, the correct distribution of
the inputs (data and randomness) according to the underlying prime field is not explicitly
verified and has to be guaranteed by the user. The user must only generate a netlist of their
masked implementation, correctly annotate the included PINI gadgets and their inputs and
outputs within the netlist, provide a testbench following the correct input distributions and
the verification can be performed. The full small-pSquare implementations that successfully
pass this check are described in Section 4.
2.3
Simulation-Based Circuit Verification
While formal verification tools can prove the conformity of circuits to specific security
notions, e.g., glitch-robust PINI, by exhaustively analyzing all possible circuit inputs,
outputs and internal states, the computational complexity of these checks usually scales
poorly to larger implementations, like fully masked cipher cores, or higher protection
orders. Therefore, incomplete evaluation approaches based on statistical analysis of a
restricted set of circuit states have emerged. These simulation-based tools can provide, at
least to some extent, evidence of the overall security of larger masked designs. Tools of
this family include PROLEAD [MM22] and PROLEAD_SW [ZMM23]. Specifically, the PROLEAD
tool assesses the security of a hardware circuit, represented as a gate-level netlist, under
the robust probing model by leveraging gate-level simulations and statistical independence
testing [MM22]. Since PROLEAD does not simulate all possible circuit states, its reliability
primarily depends on the number of simulations performed, that is, the error probability
decreases as more simulations are conducted. While PROLEAD was originally designed
to evaluate Boolean masked circuits, i.e., by sampling Boolean masked test vectors for
simulation, as successfully demonstrated in various works, its application to other masking
schemes is relatively new. Since the release of version three, PROLEAD has supported the
evaluation of masking in finite fields beyond F2. Specifically, PROLEAD has been extended
to sample test vectors masked in other finite fields, including odd prime fields, making
it well-suited for evaluating the designs considered in this work. To use this feature, the
user only needs to specify the finite field of the input and output shares. PROLEAD then
automatically interprets its simulation groups as vectors of elements in the chosen finite
field. For example, when using prime-field masking over F22−1, a simulation group such as
4′b$$$$ no longer represents four independent random bits, but instead two concatenated
elements of F22−1 which can subsequently be assigned bit-wise to the individual signals of
the implementation under evaluation. Yet, regardless of the finite field under consideration,
PROLEAD models formal adversaries with the ability to probe a single wire, i.e., an element
in F2, rather than a single element from the underlying finite field, as specified by the
robust probing model. Consequently, when the masking is not performed in F2, PROLEAD
evaluates a slightly weaker definition of probing security, known as bit-level security.

The common Mersenne prime reduction trick for p = 2n −1 consists in adding the
lower n bits of a variable x which is to be reduced, to its upper 1-to-n bits (depending
on how large x is), resulting in x′ = x mod p. If the initial value x turns out to be a
multiple of p, a complete modulo reduction should produce the output 0. However, the
addition-based reduction trick only results in a 0 output if the original value x was already
0 before reduction, i.e., x′ = x = 0. In all other cases where x′ should evaluate to 0, the
result will be p which fits into n bits and is thus not further reduced (even when repeating
the procedure). To implement a complete modulo reduction, it would be necessary to
check if the obtained n bits after reduction are equal to p and then replace it by 0. Of
course, this additional step incurs an overhead in form of extra instructions in software and
in form of additional area, delay and power consumption in hardware. We assume that
this final check has been dropped from prior implementations for such efficiency reasons.
Functionally, this is completely unproblematic as x′ = p is small enough to fit the required
data width and further computations can normally continue without risking an overflow.
However, for masked implementations, we need to be more careful.
Concretely, in the Eurocrypt’24 artifact of [GMM+24], the hardware modules used
for addition and subtraction modulo a Mersenne prime are implemented as depicted
in the column titled "version α" of Table 1. As explained above, the output c of the
addition module can only become 0 when both inputs a and b are 0. Obviously, if used for
randomness addition in a masked gadget, this can lead to trivial insecurity if inputs are

Even if output c passes through a register stage, as is common after randomness additions,
a single probe on the output then reveals whether the value to be blinded was already 0
before the randomness addition. In such cases, the probing security of the gadget would be
violated. Yet, if in all inputs, the 0-element is actually represented by p (11 . . . 12 in binary
representation) instead of 0 (00 . . . 02 in binary representation), leading to a, b ∈{1, . . . , p},
this problem disappears and distributions remain uniform. We assume this to be the
intended usage of these concrete squaring gadget implementations, as the physical security
analysis of prior works should have revealed this leakage otherwise. However, this simple
fix becomes insufficient in circuits where the modular addition is paired with a modular
subtraction behaving similarly, see Table 1.
Subtraction module version α outputs c = 0 whenever a = b. However, it only outputs
c = p when a = p and b = 0. While the latter case cannot natively occur when using
input distributions a, b ∈{1, . . . , p}, it may occur when composing multiple addition and
subtraction modules to create a larger circuit. For example, if both inputs to the addition
module described above are outputs of the subtraction module described above, the final
addition result can become 0 and therefore fall outside the intended distribution, leaking
about multiple intermediate values, even through register stages.
We have devised two straightforward methods to prevent this issue from occurring
and analyze their efficiency in the following. We first denote the variant of the public
squaring gadgets using the previously described modular addition and subtraction functions
as version α. Version β simply performs complete modulo reductions after arithmetic
operations, see Table 1. With all modular arithmetic using complete reductions, no security
issues are expected to arise, but this change increases the cost of the circuit. Hence, as
a cheaper alternative we can keep the addition module version α as long as we do not
pair it with the corresponding subtraction module version α, or in fact any module that
can result in a 0 output despite its inputs being distributed uniformly over {1, . . . , p}. To
devise an alternative subtraction module, we can leverage the observation that inverting
all bits of an element from a Mersenne prime field in binary representation, yields the
additive inverse of that field element. It is easy to see that two values with inverted bits at
each location result in the 1-vector (11 . . . 12) when summed together, which represents p
and therefore is supposed to be reduced to 0. Hence, if instead of using a module based on
actual arithmetic subtraction, we simply obtain the additive inverse of the second operand
and use addition instead, the result can never become 0 if the input distributions {1, . . . , p}
are observed. The corresponding subtraction arithmetic, internally based on inversion and
addition, is denoted as version γ in Table 1.

Like Bivium/Trivium/Kreyvium, Privium can be unrolled to produce an arbitrary number
of field elements as output stream per clock cycle. Unrolling can be performed in increments
of up to 18 F27−1 elements (18×7 = 126 bits) per cycle without increasing the critical path
of the circuit (for Bivium/Trivium/Kreyvium the equivalent is 64 bits, see [CMM+24]).
The initialization phase iterates 4× over the entire shift register, i.e., 4×72 = 288 rounds of
updating a single F27−1 element. The concrete unrolling factors for our masked serialized
pipelined implementations of small-pSquare (Figure 4) with 2, 3 and 4 shares are 12, 30 and
72 F27−1 elements, respectively. For these unrolling factors, the initialization of Privium
takes 24, 10 and 4 cycles, respectively, to complete. We have verified that the output
stream of Privium successfully passes all tests of NIST’s 800-22 test suite for random and
Pseudo-Random Number Generators (PRNGs) for cryptographic applications. Table 4
shows that the use of Privium is always preferable over the binary primitives with rejection
sampling for our selected probability threshold and the gap increases at higher orders.
This might change if solutions to stall the masked implementation are implemented or if
out-of-range random values are tolerated more often at some security risk.
We stress that we do not provide any in-depth black-box security analysis of this
primitive in this work, and explicitly do not suggest the use of Privium beyond the
generation of masking randomness, especially not as a cryptographically strong PRNG.
As discussed in [CMM+24], in the setting of masking randomness generation, adversaries
cannot observe the generated output stream but at most the leakage of it. This makes
cryptanalytic state recovery based on generated outputs significantly more difficult and
puts lighter requirements on the cryptographic strength. For this reason, Bivium is also
currently assumed to be sufficiently secure for this purpose (e.g., used in OpenTitan8). We
conjecture that the cryptanalytic security and leakage-resilience of Privium is stronger than
that of Bivium. This is due both to its larger state size (504 vs. 177 bits) and due to the
fact that for frequently observed (e.g., Hamming weight or similar) leakage functions, state
recovery attacks against prime-based LFSRs are expected to be considerably harder than
against their binary counterparts. This is supported by arguments similar to those on hard
physical learning problems like Learning With Physical Rounding [DMMS21, HMM+23].
We leave the further analysis of this conjecture as an interesting scope for further research.

ASIC Implementation Figures
Now that we have provably glitch+transition-robust cipher implementations as well as
a random number generator producing the required output distribution at our disposal,
it is time to merge them into fully integrated circuits and realize them on actual silicon.
Functionally, the merge requires only an additional state machine that makes sure that
the entire Privium initialization is performed before the masked cipher starts to execute.
Beyond that, the Privium outputs can be directly connected to the randomness inputs of
the masked ciphers. Table 5 reports the resulting cost and performance numbers after
synthesis (i.e., pre-layout) and after place&route (i.e., post-layout) of our ASIC prototype.
Area figures are reported separately for small-pSquare, Privium and their combination. The
chip is designed for ≥50 MHz operation under worst-case conditions (high temperature,
low voltage). The delay numbers are reported for typical conditions, indicating that
frequencies up to 100 MHz are feasible for the post-layout circuits. The reported power
consumption is estimated at 10 MHz for consistency.
The full ASIC prototype has been designed and manufactured in 65 nm Complementary
Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor (CMOS) technology. Its layout is depicted in Figure 6a. The
die is 2 × 2 millimeters in size and contains 9 metal layers of which the topmost is only
used for logo/orientation-purposes and not for functional routing. On each of its 4 sides it
has 7 logical IO cells and 4 additional power delivery cells to supply the standard-cell core
and the IO ring with VDD and GND respectively, resulting in 11 bond pads on each side
and 44 bond pads in total. With these parameters it fits nicely into the common JLCC44
package, as shown in Figure 6b. The custom circuit board shown in Figure 6c has been
optimized for low-noise power measurements and electrically decouples the control Field
Programmable Gate Array (FPGA) from the target ASIC. Logically, the two parts of the
board are connected through ADuM260N 6-channel digital isolators.

Conclusion
In this work we have presented our findings from formally verifying, securely integrating
and physically evaluating higher-order masked implementations of small-pSquare on real
silicon. Some parts of our results are generalizable to any prime-field masked circuit (e.g.,
the masked gadget/circuit verification), while others are specific to the Mersenne-prime
based squaring gadgets that have been used for protected cipher implementations in this
and contemporary works (e.g., the 0-issue and the associated fixes). We have devised a
technique to certify the composability properties of gadgets defined over Fp using existing
F2-focused formal verification tools and proved its soundness. Using this technique, together
with existing composition-level and simulation-based verification tools, we have identified
security issues resulting from incomplete modulo reductions in public source codes and
