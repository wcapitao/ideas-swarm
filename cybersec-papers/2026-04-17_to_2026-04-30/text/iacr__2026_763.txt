LEAH: Lightweight and Efficient Hardware
Accelerator for Code-based PQC Scheme HQC
Yazheng Tu and Jiafeng Xie
Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering. Villanova University, Villanova, USA
Email: {ytu1,jiafeng.xie}@villanova.edu
Abstract—The advent of quantum computing poses a signifi-
cant threat to modern cryptography. To address this challenge,
the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) has
initiated the Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) standardization
process, with several algorithms being selected for standardiza-
tion, including the recent code-based scheme HQC (Hamming
Quasi-Cyclic). Meanwhile, a good number of research works in
the field have switched to efficient hardware acceleration for
PQC schemes. Following this trend, in this paper, we present
a novel PQC hardware acceleration work, i.e., a Lightweight
and Efficient hardware Accelerator for HQC (LEAH). Our
design consists of three innovative hardware architectures for
Key Generation, Encapsulation, and Decapsulation of HQC,
respectively, while supporting all security levels. In total, we have
proposed three layers of contributions, including: (i) dedicated
design processes to obtain highly optimized major components
for HQC, i.e., sparse polynomial multiplier, sampler, encoder,
and decoder; (ii) novel data flow arrangement to design three
operational phases of HQC that supports all parameter sets; (iii)
a detailed comparison based on Field-Programmable Gate Array
(FPGA) implementation to showcase the significant efficiency of
the proposed design over the competing ones, e.g., Decapsulation
architecture has at least 13.66% (at most 49.87%) less Equivalent
Area-Delay Product (EADP) than the existing ones. We hope
this outcome can facilitate the deployment of HQC in various
applications and impact the ongoing NIST PQC standardization.
Index Terms—FPGA, HQC, hardware accelerator, LEAH,
PQC, decoder, sampler, sparse polynomial multiplier.
I. INTRODUCTION
The advent of large-scale quantum computers poses a signif-
icant threat to the security of currently deployed cryptographic
primitives, such as RSA and Elliptic Curve Cryptography
[1], [2], and has created an urgent need for Post-Quantum
Cryptography (PQC) [2]. The National Institute of Standards
and Technology (NIST) has initiated the PQC standardization
process in 2016 [3], [4]. After extensive evaluation, algo-
rithms like KYBER were selected [3], [4]. However, NIST
continues to evaluate alternative candidates, in order to protect
against the possible weaknesses introduced by leveraging a
single mathematical foundation [4]. Among these candidates,
Hamming Quasi-Cyclic (HQC), a code-based KEM (Key
Encapsulation Mechanism), has recently been selected for
standardization as a backup scheme to KYBER [4].
HQC is a code-based IND-CCA (Indistinguishable under
Chosen-Plaintext Attack) secure KEM [5]. Its security is based
on the computational hardness of the syndrome decoding
This paper was accepted in the 2026 IEEE International Symposium on
Hardware Oriented Security and Trust (HOST).
problem for quasi-cyclic (QC) codes [5]. Due to the increas-
ing attention on HQC from the research community, several
hardware implementation works of HQC have been reported.
Prior Works. A lightweight dense-sparse polynomial mul-
tiplication hardware accelerator was reported in [6], and a
high-throughput design was proposed in [7]. A constant-
time fixed weight sampler was proposed in [8]. Besides the
components, several designs related to three operational phases
of HQC, namely Key Generation (KeyGen), Encapsulation
(Encap), and Decapsulation (Decap), were also reported. [9]
proposed an architecture of HQC-1 based on Additive FFT
(Fast Fourier Transform) in decoding. A design deploying the
enhanced Parallel Inversionless Berlekamp-Massey (ePIBM)
algorithm was proposed in [10]. A high-level synthesis-based
architecture for HQC-1 was reported in [11]. Generic designs
for three operational phases of HQC were reported in [12].
Challenges and Motivation. While several HQC hardware
implementations have been reported, a more efficient and
generic solution is still lacking. First, most existing works are
incomplete, as they only address small components or a single
security level (e.g., HQC-1 [11]). Besides, although imple-
mented on the Artix-7 device, these designs require significant
resources like DSPs and BRAMs, making them unsuitable
for resource-constrained applications. Thus, there is an urgent
need for new hardware acceleration that utilizes lightweight
resources and achieves efficient performance, while supporting
all HQC security levels (HQC-1, HQC-3, and HQC-5).
Contribution. Based on these considerations, this paper
presents a Lightweight and Efficient hardware Accelerator for
HQC (LEAH), addressing the limitations mentioned above.
The proposed design comprises three innovative architectures
for Key Generation (KeyGen), Encapsulation (Encap), and
Decapsulation (Decap), respectively, and is fully compatible
with the latest HQC specification [5]. We have proposed novel
design strategies that result in substantial reductions in area-
time complexity for all core HQC operations. Overall, we have
proposed three layers of contributions as:
• We have optimized key components, including the fixed-
weight sampler, polynomial multiplier, encoder, and de-
coder, which strictly adhere to the HQC specification and
exhibit better performance in area-time complexities.
• We have proposed novel data flow to design generic
hardware architectures (LEAH) for KeyGen, Encap, and
Decap, respectively, while supporting all parameter sets.

• We have conducted a comprehensive performance eval-
uation for LEAH on the AMD Artix-7 FPGA, demon-
strating superior implementation results compared to the
recently released HQC hardware design works.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section
II provides a detailed background for the HQC algorithm.
Section III provides a detailed process for obtaining optimized
components. Section IV presents our proposed hardware ac-
celerator (LEAH), detailing respective architectures. Section V
presents the results and comparison with the existing works.
Finally, Section VI concludes the paper.
II. PRELIMINARIES
This section provides an introduction to both HQC.PKE and
HQC.KEM, including the mathematical foundation as well as
the algorithms for KeyGen, Encap, and Decap, respectively.
HQC. HQC is an IND-CCA secure KEM built on the
hardness of a decision version of the Syndrome Decoding
on structured codes [5]. It utilizes two types of linear codes:
a random quasi-cyclic code for security and a fixed public
code for error correction. The quasi-cyclic code is a [2p, p, d]
code with a public parity-check matrix H = [Ip | rot(h)].
The public fixed code is a concatenated code composed of
an external shortened Reed-Solomon code and an internal
duplicated Reed-Muller code. The dual-code structure allows
for a precise upper bound on the decryption failure probability.
Interested readers may refer to the document of [5] for details.

Hamming weights, ω(e) = we and ω(ra) = ω(rb) = wr.
Then, the message m is encoded with the generator matrix
G, and the resulting codeword is added to the first n1n2 bits
of s · rb + e to form the polynomial v. Finally, the ciphertext
c = (u, v) is returned.

The HQC decryption process (HQC.PKE.Decrypt) is used
to recover the original message m′ using the private key sk.
The algorithm begins by re-sampling the secret polynomials
x and y based on the secret key seed2. It then computes the
polynomial v −u · y and truncates the result. This resulting
polynomial is then subjected to a decoding procedure that
involves both Reed-Muller and Reed-Solomon decoding to
correct errors and recover the original message m′. The final
decrypted message m′ is then returned.
HQC.KEM. The HQC KEM scheme (HQC.KEM) is
constructed by wrapping the HQC.PKE scheme with the
Hofheinz-H¨ovelmanns-Kiltz (HHK) transformation [12], [13].

It derives a shared session key and includes mechanisms to
detect and handle decryption errors securely.
The KeyGen for HQC.KEM (HQC.KEM.KeyGen) is re-
sponsible for generating the public key pk and the private key
sk. This process includes an additional secret seed, σ, for the
Decap’s implicit rejection mechanism. The procedure begins
by generating this secret seed σ of k bits using SHAKE256.
It then proceeds with the HQC.PKE.KeyGen steps, where two
random 320-bit seeds, seed1 and seed2, are sampled. The
seed seed1 is expanded via SHAKE256 to sample a public
dense polynomial h ∈R, which forms the parity-check matrix
H = [Ip | rot(h)]. Then, seed2 is expanded to sample
two secret fixed-weight sparse polynomials, x, y ∈R, both
with a Hamming weight w. The syndrome polynomial is then
computed as s ←x + h · y. Finally, the HQC.KEM.KeyGen
procedure returns the public key pk = (seed1, s) and the
private key sk = (seed2, σ).

The HQC.KEM Encap (HQC.KEM.Encap) procedure (Al-
gorithm 5) takes the public key pk as input and produces
a ciphertext c and a shared secret key K. The procedure
begins by generating a digest θ from the concatenation of
the message m, the head bytes of the secret key (specifically
seed1), and the salt using a hash function G. This digest,
along with the public key pk and message m, is used as
input to the HQC.PKE.Encrypt routine, which produces the
ciphertext components (u, v). The shared secret key K is then
derived using a key derivation function K on the concatenation
of m and the ciphertext components. The final output is the
ciphertext c = (u, v, salt) and the shared secret key K.

Unlike HQC.PKE.Decrypt, the algorithm of HQC.KEM
Decap (HQC.KEM.Decap) (Algorithm 6) aims to recover
the shared secret key K instead of the message m′, with
possibly triggered implicit rejection mechanism. It includes
a re-encryption step for security against active attackers. First,
it calls HQC.PKE.Decrypt with seed2 and the ciphertext
components (u, v) to compute the decrypted message m′.
A new random value θ′ is then re-formed by hashing the
concatenation of m′, seed1, and the salt using G. A new
ciphertext c′ = (u′, v′) is generated by re-encrypting m′
using HQC.PKE.Encrypt with (seed1, s) and θ′. The received
ciphertext c is then compared with the re-computed ciphertext
c′. If they match (c = c′), the shared secret key K′ is derived
using K on the concatenation of m′ and the ciphertext c. If
they do not match (c ̸= c′), a new shared secret key K is
derived using K with the concatenation of a secret value σ and
the ciphertext components to prevent any information leakage.
Final output is the shared secret key K.

III. OPTIMIZED ARCHITECTURAL DETAILS OF MAJOR
LOW-LEVEL COMPONENTS
This section presents a detailed description of the proposed
hardware design processes for key components of HQC.
Following the stated algorithmic details of HQC in Section
II, we briefly list the involved key components as: (i) Keccak
core; (ii) sampler, (iii) polynomial arithmetic; (iv) encoder;
and (v) decoder. Accordingly, we provide a detailed design
process for these components, from algorithm to architecture.
A. Keccak
The Keccak core, more specifically, SHAKE256, is re-
sponsible for generating the raw data of non-zero indices
for the fixed-weight polynomials, dense polynomials, and the
shared secret. In this work, we adopt the Keccak core used
in [14] and modified the wrapper so that the input/output
length matches the memory width of the proposed accelerator
(LEAH). Interested readers may refer to [14] for more details.
B. Sampler
A few efficient sparse polynomial samplers have been
reported. However, existing works still involve extra resource

Fig. 1: The proposed sampler (idx: index).
usages such as DSP [9] and RAM tiles [12]. To further
reduce the resource usage, following Algorithm 1, we propose
a constant-time fixed-weight sparse polynomial sampler. As
shown in Fig. 1, this sampler consists of a Barrett reduction
unit, two single-port RAMs (one for raw data and the other for
reduced indices, both with a 128-bit data width and a 128-to-
64-bit shift register), and a control unit. The sampling process
involves three phases: the first phase receives and stores raw
data, while the remaining two phases correspond to the two
outer loops in Algorithm 1. During operation, the raw data
RAM initially stores incoming 128-bit random numbers from
the Keccak core. Once all data is stored, they are transferred to
the shift register and then segmented into four 32-bit chunks to
be fed into the Barrett reduction unit. The 32-bit width ensures
sufficient randomness for the indices, given that the reduced
indices require at most 16 bits. The Barrett reduction unit
utilizes pipelined Karatsuba multipliers, optimized for both 16-
bit and 8-bit variants, to reduce the critical path and increase
operating frequency. After reduction, the indices are stored in
the reduced indices RAM. The entire second phase is fully
pipelined, ensuring consecutive data processing with a latency
of O(ω + 18) cycles, where 18 refers to the pipelined delay.
The third phase verifies duplicate index entries in the
reduced indices’ RAM through the control unit. This phase
executes ω sequential rounds, where each round retrieves one
index from RAM, stores it in an internal 16-bit register, and
compares it against all other stored indices. When duplicates
are detected, the control unit writes the current round number
to the duplicate locations. The verification process requires
ω(ω + 1)/2 cycles due to exhaustive pairwise comparisons,
resulting in O(ω2) time complexity. This approach guarantees
uniform randomness in the sampled indices while eliminating
collision artifacts. The total cycle count can be expressed as
(ω(ω + 3)/2), maintaining the constant-time execution.
C. Polynomial Arithmetic Components
The proposed polynomial arithmetic components are re-
sponsible for performing calculations related to polynomials
involved in HQC. We have designed the components by opti-
mizing the existing designs or designing them from scratch.
Unit-1: Polynomial Multiplier. The polynomial multiplier
computes the product of a dense polynomial and a fixed-
weight polynomial during KeyGen, Encap, and Decap. The
process can be expressed as a matrix-vector product, where
the matrix is the rotation matrix of the dense polynomial
and the vector represents the sparse polynomial, and can be
optimized from an O(n2) operation to an O(ω·n) process due
to the sparsity of the polynomials involved, which is realized
by only accumulating the columns of the rotation matrix that
correspond to non-zero entries in the sparse polynomial. While
many existing works have been reported, there is still room
for optimization. For example, the existing designs introduce
redundant RAM usage by relying on dual-port RAMs [6] or
increasing the number of reading ports [12]. This often leads
to high logical complexity and inefficient resource utilization,
especially when forming column segments.
Following the strategy mentioned above and drawbacks of
the existing works, we have extended the work proposed in
[6] with the following optimizations (the proposed polynomial
multiplier is shown in Fig. 2): 1) we use single port RAM for
storing and fetching the coefficients of the dense polynomial
while [6] used dual port RAM; 2) we optimize the segment
former so that the register used for storing unused coefficients
decreases from 2N mem to N mem; 3) we introduce a cache
to store the last N −n1n2 bits to reduce the complexity
of the control logic for determining the reading address. In
this manner, the whole design leads to fewer resource usage
(especially in RAM) and lower computation latency because
it avoids the extra cycle used to fetch coefficients from three
consecutive addresses, typically the last address (where the
final N −n1n2 bits reside) and the adjacent one.
Overall, it takes a 16-bit index every N/Nmem cycles and
an Nmem-bit data read from the accumulation RAM every
2 cycles as inputs. It then calculates the corresponding 128-
bit coefficient segment using the dense polynomial RAM
data and index input, XORs it with the accumulation input,
and feeds the resulting Nmem-bit output back to the accu-
mulation RAM. The entire multiplication process completes
in O(ω(N/Nmem + 1)) time. Note this design is a generic
design where Nmem can be multiple values (64, 128, 256,
etc.). However, to better align with other components in the
proposed upper-level architectures, we use Nmem = 128.
Unit-2: Polynomial Adders. The index-based polynomial
adder is responsible for calculating the sum of a dense
polynomial and a sparse polynomial. We fully take advantage
of the sparse structure of (one of) the involved polynomials.
Each time it takes a non-zero index of the sparse polynomial,
calculating the address (0 to ⌈N/Nmem⌉−1) and the position
(0 to Nmem−1) among the bits stored in the address, negating
the bit, and finally produces the result by writing the result
back to the dense polynomial RAM. This process is depicted
by a control unit (that calculates the address and position), a
barrel shift register (that shifts ’1’ by correct bits according to
the calculated position), and an Nmem-bit long XOR gate to
calculate the final output by XORing the shifted ’1’ and the
data read from the dense polynomial RAM. The whole process
takes O(ω) time to finish the addition of two polynomials.
The other polynomial adder, namely the XOR-based poly-
nomial adder, is responsible for calculating the sum of two
dense polynomials. This component is simply implemented
by a control unit that calculates the reading address for two
RAMs storing the two dense polynomials and Nmem XOR
gates to calculate the addition under the ambient space Fn
2.

D. Encoder
The encoder is responsible for encoding the message m
during Encap or Decap by using Reed-Solomon and Reed-
Muller encoding. We found that abundant logic is introduced
when all Galois Multipliers are individually implemented in
[15] and thus can be improved to achieve a better resource
usage. The design in [15] uses p individual hard-coded Galois
field multipliers to calculate the product of the gate value and
coefficients of the generator polynomial, where p is the degree
of the generator polynomial. In this paper, we optimized the
design by merging all similar hard-coded Galois multipliers to
reduce area usage without compromising timing performance.
The proposed encoder consists of Reed-Solomon encoder,
Reed-Muller encoder, and a control unit, processing k bytes
of message m to produce an n1n2-bit codeword C (see Fig.
3). The encoding begins with the Reed-Solomon encoder,
which generates an n1-byte codeword over k cycles using
a systematic linear feedback shift register (LFSR) structured
around the Reed-Solomon generator polynomial. The LFSR,
initialized to zero, processes one byte of the message per
cycle, XORing it with the prior codeword and multiplying
the result with polynomial coefficients using parallel Galois
field multipliers. The resulting codeword is stored in a shift
register and passed byte-by-byte to the Reed-Muller encoder,
which performs Reed-Muller encoding to convert each byte
into a 128-bit Reed-Muller(1,7) codeword. The Reed-Muller
Fig. 4: Internal structure of the proposed Decoder.
encoding involves filling 32-bit chunks with message bits and
computing the codeword through XOR gates, AND gates, and
bit-shifters. The Reed-Muller encoder completes encoding in
O(n1) cycles. The final HQC codeword for HQC-1, HQC-
3, and HQC-5 consists of 3, 5, or 5 identical copies of the
concatenated Reed-Solomon-Reed-Muller codeword, respec-
tively, though only one copy is stored in memory to optimize
resources, with repeated reads generating the full codeword.
E. Decoder
Unlike existing design which requires huge resource us-
age [12] or high computation time [9]. A key feature of
the proposed decoder is that it combines a parallel byte-
level Reed-Muller decoder with a consistent resource usage
Reed-Solomon decoder in order to strike the optimal balance
between resource usage and speed. The proposed decoder
consists of a Reed-Muller decoder cascaded with a Reed-
Solomon decoder to decode the received codeword and recover
the original message. It processes an n1n2-bit codeword as
input through a two-stage decoding pipeline to generate the
final k-byte recovered message.
Reed-Muller Decoder. The Reed-Muller decoder consists
of three cascaded units: ExpandSum, Hadamard transform, and
FindPeak. The decoding process operates in three sequential
phases. First, the ExpandSum module receives 128-bit data
from the input RAM and performs accumulation. This data
then feeds into the Hadamard transform component, which
executes a Fast Walsh-Hadamard Transform using 8 hierar-
chical butterfly operations on 128 parallel data paths with
Ping-Pong architecture. Each butterfly stage performs simul-
taneous additions and subtractions, completing the transform
in a fixed 8-cycle latency. The transformed coefficients are
then delivered to the FindPeak component. This component
implements a 7-stage binary tree search to identify the index
of the maximum value. The time complexity of the decoding
is O(n1n2/128 × (Multiplicity + 8 + 7)) cycles, where the
Multiplicity is 3, 5, or 5 depending on the security level.
Reed-Solomon Decoder. The Reed-Solomon decoder con-
sists of five cascaded processing modules—syndrome compu-
tation, error locator polynomial (ELP) calculation, polynomial
z(x) computation, root finding, and error value calculation,
along with internal memory buffers and a centralized control

unit. The decoding process operates in five sequential phases
corresponding to the classical Berlekamp-Massey algorithm
[16] implementation. During the first phase, the syndrome
computation module receives 8-bit input data and calculates
2 · δ syndrome values through Galois field multiplications and
accumulations, requiring O(n1 · δ) cycles with polynomial
evaluation complexity. The second phase employs the ELP
module to execute the Berlekamp-Massey algorithm over 2 · δ
iterations, where each iteration performs discrepancy calcu-
lations, polynomial updates, and degree comparisons using
parallel Galois field multipliers, completing in O(δ2) time
complexity. The third phase feeds the computed syndromes
and ELP coefficients into the z-polynomial module, which
calculates the error evaluator polynomial through nested loops
with δ outer iterations and variable inner iterations based on
the ELP degree, resulting in O(δ2) complexity. The fourth
phase adopts additive FFT for root finding, transforming
the ELP coefficients to identify error locations through field
element evaluation. The final phase employs the error value
computation module that uses Forney’s algorithm [17] through
four sequential states, i.e., initialization, beta calculation, error
evaluation, and final error computation, using RAMs for
coefficient storage and parallel Galois field arithmetic units.
Overall, our design strategy offers a distinct advantage
because its resource usage remains consistent, in contrast to
the linear increase seen in [12] as the security level, and thus
the codeword length, changes. This leads to superior decoder
performance at a higher security level. Also, the smaller, more
consistent design area prevents a frequency drop that can occur
with larger designs, which improves the performance of the
higher-level architecture (Decap).
IV. INNOVATIVE ARCHITECTURAL DESIGN FOR
UPPER-LEVEL OPERATIONS
This section outlines a detailed design process for LEAH,
comprising three innovative architectures for KeyGen, Encap,
and Decap, respectively. The obtained hardware architectures
deploy the components in Section III and follow the novel
data flow introduced here for efficient computation.
A. KeyGen
Data Flow Overview. The KeyGen design optimizes perfor-
mance through concurrent operations (connecting Algorithm
4). This process begins with Keccak generating random data
for polynomial h and subsequent sampling. The data flow in-
volves the parallel execution of polynomial multiplication and
the sampling of another polynomial, which masks sampling
latency and ensures no duplicated hardware. The architecture
immediately proceeds to polynomial addition upon completion
of multiplication, minimizing idle time to form the final keys.
Architectural Details. The KeyGen includes the following
components: a sampler, a polynomial multiplier, an index-
based polynomial adder, a control unit, and RAMs for storing
both the public key (pk) and secret key (sk), along with
intermediate data including non-zero indices of polynomials
x and y and raw sampling data from Keccak. The architecture
Fig. 5: The proposed architecture for KeyGen.
takes Keccak outputs as its input and generates the final pk
and sk upon completion of the entire key generation process.
When the architecture begins to work, it feeds the initial
seed seed1 into the Keccak function. The resulting random
output is stored in the h-RAM, where it serves as the co-
efficients for polynomial h. Subsequently, the module pro-
cesses seed2 through Keccak and stores the generated random
numbers in the raw data RAM. The first half of this stored
data is specifically utilized to sample the non-zero indices
of polynomial y. Following sampling of y, two operations
are executed in parallel: polynomial multiplication between
h and y, and sampling of non-zero indices for polynomial
x. This parallel execution strategy effectively conceals the
sampling latency within the longer polynomial multiplication
operation, thereby optimizing timing performance without re-
quiring duplicate hardware components. Given that polynomial
multiplication counts as the dominant time factor compared
to sampling, the module proceeds to the polynomial addition
phase immediately upon multiplication completion. This stage
computes polynomial s and forms the final outputs pk and
sk. Note the HQC specification’s use of seeds for polynomial
representation requires seed re-expansion [5], which adds
processing time during Encap and Decap. Our design offers an
alternative with a configurable output format. Instead of just
seeds, our architecture can also output the actual polynomials,
controlled by a selection signal, allowing for faster processing
by eliminating the need for re-expansion.
B. Encap
Data Flow Overview. The proposed Encap’s data flow
is designed for maximum concurrency. This process begins
with forming a seed and using it with Keccak to generate
random numbers, while simultaneously encoding the message.
This is followed by the parallel execution of two polynomial
multiplications, which cuts processing time in half. Then three
additions compute the ciphertext, and the final stage uses
Keccak to calculate the shared secret key. This simultaneous
and parallel approach reduces overall latency. Note the con-

figurable polynomial format also contributes to this efficiency
by potentially saving the time required for seed re-expansion.
Architectural Details. Overall, the Encap architecture con-
sists of a sampler, two polynomial multipliers, an index-based
polynomial adder, a XOR-based polynomial adder, an encoder,
a control unit, and RAMs for storing the ciphertext c and the
shared secret key K, along with intermediate data such as raw
data for sampling r1 and r2. The architecture takes the public
key pk as input and outputs the ciphertext c as well as the
shared secret key K upon completion of the Encap process.
The architecture begins operation by forming the seed θ
using the message m, public key pk (seed1), and salt. The
seed θ is then sent to Keccak to await the derivation of
random numbers for use as raw data in sampling r1 and r2.
Concurrently, the encoder begins converting the message m to
codeword C, with the encoding time being masked by the Kec-
cak operation. Once all random numbers have been stored, the
architecture retrieves h based on the format selection, either
through re-generation using seed1 or by direct reading from
RAM. Subsequently, r2 is sampled and utilized in the next
stage, where two polynomial multiplications, h · r2 and s · r2,
are executed in parallel. Two distinct polynomial multipliers
are employed to perform these multiplications concurrently,
as polynomial multiplication dominates the processing time
during Encap, and eliminating one multiplication cycle sig-
nificantly reduces processing time, thereby enhancing overall
performance. Meanwhile, the sampler concurrently samples
e to save sampling time for subsequent operations. Following
this, three polynomial additions are executed serially using the
index-based and XOR-based polynomial adders: two dense-
sparse polynomial additions and one dense-dense addition,
which collectively compute the ciphertext c. The final stage
involves sending the message m and the ciphertext c to Keccak
to calculate the shared secret key K. Note that the calculated
K is stored in the same RAM where the raw data for r2
resides, thereby reducing the number of memory tiles required

in the implementation.
C. Decap
Data Flow Overview. The proposed Decap design achieves
high efficiency through its inherited parallel execution and
shared memory strategies. This process starts with initial poly-
nomial operations to recover the message, while the encoder’s
operation is masked by Keccak’s random number generation.
Re-encryption then begins, where the inherited parallel ex-
ecution significantly reduces processing time. Following re-
encryption, verification is performed using XORs. The final
stage involves generating the shared secret key with Keccak
and storing it in shared memory to optimize resource usage.
Architectural Details. The proposed Decap architecture
consists of a sampler, two polynomial multipliers, an index-
based polynomial adder, a XOR-based polynomial adder,
an encoder, a decoder, a control unit, and RAMs storing
intermediate data such as raw data for sampling r1, r2, and e,
along with the recovered message and validation results. The
architecture takes the public key pk = (h, s) and ciphertext
c = (u, v) as input, and outputs the shared secret key K upon
completion of the Decap process.
The architecture begins operation by computing u · y using
one of the polynomial multipliers, where y is part of the private
key. Subsequently, it calculates v −u·y using the XOR-based
polynomial adder to obtain the syndrome for error correction.
The result is then fed to the decoder, which performs error
correction to recover the original message m′ from the noisy
codeword. Once the message m′ is recovered, the architecture
proceeds to form the seed θ using the recovered message
m′, public key components, and salt. This seed θ is sent to
Keccak to generate random numbers for sampling r1, r2, and
e. Concurrently, the encoder begins converting the recovered
message m′ back to codeword C for verification purposes, with
the encoding time being masked by the Keccak operation and
sampling processes. After all random numbers are generated
and stored, the sampler sequentially samples r2, r1, and e from
the raw data. The architecture then performs two polynomial
multiplications in parallel: h · r2 and s · r2, using two distinct
polynomial multipliers to maximize performance. This parallel
execution significantly reduces processing time since poly-
nomial multiplication is the most computationally intensive
operation during Decap. Following the multiplications, three
polynomial additions are executed serially using the index-

VI. CONCLUSION
This paper has presented LEAH, which includes optimized
components such as the fixed-weight sampler, polynomial
multiplier, encoder, and decoder, as well as three generic
architectures for KeyGen, Encap, and Decap that strictly
adhere to the latest HQC specification. The proposed design
achieves significant improvements in latency and EADP while
maintaining compatibility with resource-constrained FPGA
platforms. Experimental results on Artix-7 device demonstrate
superior performance compared to existing state-of-the-art
implementations, making our accelerator a practical solution
for post-quantum secure applications. This work advances the

field of code-based cryptography and provides a foundation for
further optimizations and deployments of HQC in real-world
scenarios. We hope this research will contribute to the ongoing
PQC standardization efforts and inspire future innovations in
efficient hardware implementations of PQC schemes.
