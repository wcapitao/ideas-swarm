Integral Attack on Reduced-Round Kalyna
Nitish Kumar, Ranit Dutta, Bimal Mandal
Abstract
We study integral cryptanalysis of the Ukrainian block cipher Kalyna and focus on construct-
ing reduced-round distinguishers and key-recovery attacks with low data, time, and memory
complexities. Although Kalyna has an SPN-type round structure, its pre-whitening and post-
whitening layers use column-wise addition modulo 264, which makes the propagation of integral
properties more delicate than in XOR-only designs. By combining carefully chosen input multi-
sets with backward extension through inverse round transformations, we obtain integral distin-
guishers for Kalyna-128, Kalyna-256, and Kalyna-512 in the standard setting, under weak-key
assumptions, and in variants without pre-whitening. These distinguishers require as few as 28
or 216 chosen texts, substantially improving the data complexity of previously reported public
integral results on Kalyna. We further extend them to key-recovery attacks on reduced-round
Kalyna by partial decryption and balancedness tests on suitable intermediate states. For ex-
ample, we obtain a 5-round key-recovery attack on Kalyna-128/128 with data complexity 29
chosen plaintexts, time complexity 274 encryptions, and negligible memory.
To the best of
our knowledge, this is the first work to provide integral cryptanalysis of Kalyna-256/256 and
Kalyna-512/512. Overall, our results give a unified integral analysis of Kalyna across its stan-
dard block sizes and clarify the effect of modular whitening on reduced-round distinguishers
and key-recovery attacks.
Keywords:
Block cipher, Kalyna, Integral distinguisher, Key-recovery attack.
1. Introduction
The origin of integral cryptanalysis can be traced back to the study of higher-order deriva-
tives of Boolean and vectorial Boolean functions. In 1994, Lai introduced the notion of higher-
order derivatives as a natural generalization of classical differential cryptanalysis and discussed
their cryptographic relevance [1]. Soon after, Knudsen showed how higher-order and truncated
differentials can be used to attack block ciphers that are resistant to ordinary differential at-
tacks [2]. These ideas motivated the analysis of chosen sets of plaintexts rather than individual
plaintext pairs.
A major step in this direction was the square attack. The design and analysis of the block
cipher square demonstrated that, for carefully chosen plaintext structures, the sum of certain
intermediate or output bytes becomes balanced after a number of rounds [3]. This viewpoint
was later formalized by Knudsen and Wagner under the name integral cryptanalysis, where the
attacker studies how properties such as All, Constant, and Balanced propagate through the
cipher. They also described integral cryptanalysis as a dual view of differential cryptanalysis
and showed that it is particularly suitable for ciphers built from bijective components [4].
Related variants such as the saturation attack further extended the usefulness of this approach
to additional cipher structures [5].
Although the original integral method was highly insightful, its manual application becomes
difficult for ciphers with large states, complicated linear layers, or mixed arithmetic operations.

A major breakthrough came from Todo’s division property, introduced as a generalized integral
property that enables a more systematic description of the propagation of integral characteristics
through nonlinear and linear components [6]. This approach led to powerful results, including
the first full-round integral attack on MISTY1 [8]. To improve precision further, Todo and
Morii [7] introduced the bit-based division property, and Xiang et al. [9] showed how the search
for integral distinguishers can be automated through mixed-integer linear programming (MILP),
making the technique practical for a wide range of modern block ciphers.
In this work, we focus on the block cipher Kalyna [14], the Ukrainian national encryption
standard. Kalyna was selected in the Ukrainian public cryptographic competition and later
standardized as DSTU 7624:2014. It follows a Rijndael-like substitution–permutation network
design, but incorporates several distinctive features, including a larger MDS matrix, four dif-
ferent 8-bit S-boxes, and pre- and post-whitening by addition modulo 264. Kalyna supports
block sizes of 128, 256, and 512 bits, while the key size can be either equal to or twice the block
size.These design choices make Kalyna an attractive and nontrivial target for integral analysis:
the strong diffusion helps spread activity rapidly, while the modular additions and large linear
layer make the propagation of integral properties more subtle than in standard XOR-only SPN
designs.
The existing literature on Kalyna cryptanalysis has so far focused mainly on reduced-round
attacks of other types, especially meet-in-the-middle style techniques. For example, AlTawy et
al. [11] presented reduced-round meet-in-the-middle attacks on Kalyna-b/2b, and subsequent
work extended key-recovery results on reduced-round variants such as Kalyna-128/256 and
Kalyna-256/512 [12]. In contrast, integral cryptanalysis of Kalyna appears to be much less
explored. In particular, ElSheikh and Youssef [13] considered Kalyna in their MILP-based study
of block ciphers with large linear layers and reported, to the best of current public knowledge, a
4-round integral distinguisher for Kalyna-128 with data complexity 296, together with a 4-round
integral distinguisher without pre-whitening with data complexity 264. Hence, the currently
available integral results for Kalyna seem to originate primarily from a general automated MILP
framework. In the present work, we apply the traditional integral distinguisher methodology
by tracking the propagation of carefully chosen multisets directly through the round structure
of Kalyna, while also using backward propagation to extend the obtained round distinguishers.
For example, in the case of Kalyna-128, this approach leads to a family of integral distinguishers
based on a multiset of size 28. In particular, we obtain a 2-round integral distinguisher in the
presence of pre-whitening, a 3-round weak-key integral distinguisher, and a 4-round integral
distinguisher when the pre-whitening operation is omitted.
This approach provides a more
direct cipher-oriented understanding of how the substitution layer, byte permutation, linear
diffusion, and modular whitening influence the existence and extension of integral distinguishers.
Our goal is to analyze the propagation of integral properties through the round function of
Kalyna, derive meaningful distinguishers for reduced-round variants, and examine how these
properties may be exploited in key recovery. Beyond the specific results for Kalyna, such a
study also helps clarify how modern integral techniques interact with SPN ciphers that combine
AES-like diffusion with modular arithmetic components. In this paper, we study each variant
of Kalyna by starting from a carefully chosen input set. For each version, we represent the
internal state as a byte matrix of the corresponding size and select a structured set of plaintexts
in which certain byte positions take all possible values, while the remaining bytes are fixed to
constants. We then examine how this input structure propagates through the round function
and determine, for each byte of the intermediate state, whether it satisfies the integral property
All, Balanced, Constant, or Unknown. Such small and well-structured input sets are particularly
useful for understanding the diffusion behavior of the different Kalyna variants, for deriving
integral distinguishers on reduced-round versions, and for developing key-recovery attacks by
exploiting these distinguishers.

This paper presents a systematic integral analysis of all standard variants of Kalyna, namely
Kalyna-128, Kalyna-256, and Kalyna-512. Representing the internal state as a byte matrix
of the appropriate dimension, we study how carefully chosen plaintext multisets propagate
through the substitution layer, the ShiftRows operation, the MDS-based diffusion layer, and
the modular pre-whitening transformation. In addition, we use backward propagation to extend
the obtained round distinguishers and thereby derive longer integral properties. Based on these
distinguishers, we further describe corresponding key-recovery attacks, whose attacked rounds
and main complexities are summarized in Table 1. Our main contributions are as follows.
• For Kalyna-128, we derive a family of integral distinguishers based on a multiset of size 28.
In particular, we obtain a 2-round integral distinguisher in the presence of pre-whitening,
a 3-round weak-key integral distinguisher, and a 4-round integral distinguisher when the
pre-whitening operation is omitted.
• For Kalyna-256, we derive a 2-round integral distinguisher with data complexity 28, to-
gether with 3-round and 4-round weak-key integral distinguishers with data complexity
216.
• For Kalyna-512, we obtain a 3-round integral distinguisher with data complexity 28 in
the presence of pre-whitening, a 4-round weak-key integral distinguisher, and a 5-round
integral distinguisher when the pre-whitening operation is not taken into account.
• We further show that these distinguishers naturally extend to key-recovery attacks on
reduced-round Kalyna. In particular, the 3-round weak-key integral distinguishers for
Kalyna-128 and Kalyna-256 are extended to 4-round key-recovery attacks by partial de-
cryption of the last round, and also admit direct 5-round extensions for Kalyna-128/256
and Kalyna-256/512, respectively. In addition, the distinguisher without pre-whitening
for Kalyna-512 can be extended to a 6-round key-recovery attack.
• Altogether, our results provide a unified picture of how integral properties propagate in
Kalyna under the standard setting, under weak-key assumptions, and in reduced variants
without pre-whitening. They also show that, because Kalyna performs round-key addition
column-wise modulo 264, the natural recovery unit in the key-recovery phase is a full 64-bit
column subkey rather than an individual key byte.

We use the standard terminology of integral cryptanalysis. For a given byte position in a
chosen multiset, we write:
• All (A), if every possible byte value occurs equally often;
• Balanced (B), if the XOR sum of all values taken by that byte over the multiset is zero;
• Constant (C), if the byte takes a fixed value throughout the multiset;
• Unknown (U), if none of the above properties is guaranteed.
We use the standard integral notation A/C/B/U, where A, C, B and U mean All, Constant,
Balanced, and Unknown, respectively.

Before the first round, Kalyna applies a pre-whitening operation, and after the last round it
applies a post-whitening operation. In both cases, the key addition is performed column-wise
by addition modulo 264 [14]. A full encryption structure of Kalyna is shown in Figure 2, which
highlights the whitening layers and the sequence of round transformations. Each internal round
consists of the following four basic operations:
SB →SR →MC →ARK.
In the notation of the original specification, these correspond to the nonlinear substitution
layer, the row permutation layer, the linear diffusion layer, and the round-key addition, respec-
tively [14].
• SubBytes (SB): This is the nonlinear substitution layer. Kalyna uses four different 8-bit
S-boxes, denoted by S0, S1, S2, and S3. A byte in row j is substituted using the S-box
Sj mod 4 [14].
• ShiftRows (SR): This operation cyclically shifts the bytes within each row. The amount
of shift depends on the row index and the block size. More precisely, if the block size is

• MixColumn (MC): This is the linear diffusion layer. Each column of the state is multiplied
by an 8×8 MDS matrix over F28, where the field is defined by the irreducible polynomial
x8 + x4 + x3 + x2 + 1. This layer provides strong diffusion across the state bytes [14].
• AddRoundKey (ARK): In this step, the state is XORed with the round subkey. The
round subkeys have the same size as the internal state [14].
Thus, Kalyna combines byte-wise nonlinearity through the substitution layer and strong
diffusion through the permutation and MDS-based linear layer, together with modular key
addition in the whitening stage. These design features make Kalyna structurally similar to
AES in some respects, while the larger MDS layer, multiple S-boxes, and modulo 264 whitening

Before the first round, Kalyna applies a pre-whitening operation, and after the last round it
applies a post-whitening operation. In both cases, the key addition is performed independently
on each 64-bit column by addition modulo 264. The internal rounds, however, use XOR with
the corresponding round subkeys.For a 64-bit column written as (x0, x1, . . . , x7), Kalyna uses
the little-endian convention in the modular addition/subtraction layers. Therefore, x0 is the

following observation will be useful in our later analysis.
Lemma 1. In Kalyna, if a 64-bit column has one active byte at its most significant byte position
and all remaining seven bytes are constant, then the pre-whitening addition modulo 264 preserves
the corresponding A/C structure.
Proof. The pre-whitening operation is performed independently on each 64-bit column by addi-
tion modulo 264 using little-endian byte order. Hence, the most significant byte is affected only
by a fixed carry coming from the lower seven bytes. If these lower seven bytes are constant over
the whole multiset, then the carry into the most significant byte is also fixed. Consequently,
the most significant byte is transformed by addition with a fixed constant modulo 28, which is
a bijection, and therefore its All property is preserved. The other seven bytes remain constant
as well.
