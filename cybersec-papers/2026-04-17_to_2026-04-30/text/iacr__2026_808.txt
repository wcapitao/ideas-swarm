Abstract
Secure comparison is a fundamental building block frequently employed in various applications of
secure multiparty computation, such as secure machine learning. Such protocols based on secret sharing
(SS) typically excel in throughput compared to garbled circuits (GC), but they historically suffer from
higher (online) round complexity: while GC-based comparison ends in two rounds, the state-of-the-art
SS-based (plaintext) comparison protocol requires three rounds (Lu et al., USENIX Security 2025). To
break the barrier, in this paper we propose the first SS-based comparison protocol, built upon “round
absorption” via multi-fan-in gates, to match the two-round complexity of GC with online bit complexity
O(n log n) significantly lower than GC-based O(λn). We also propose the second two-round protocol,
built upon a new optimization technique for multiplication, that addresses the drawback of O(n3) offline
bit complexity in our first protocol and reduces it to O(n2) at the cost of increasing the online bit
complexity to also O(n2).
Furthermore, for the purpose of optimization in bandwidth, we propose the third (not constant-round)
protocol with asymptotically 6n online bit complexity, which is significantly lower than asymptotically
8n bits of the state-of-the-art protocol (Couteau, ACNS 2018).
Our protocol adopts the framework
based on ternary trees and quaternary integers of CrypTFlow2 (ACM CCS 2020) and its followers, but
departs from their oblivious-transfer-based computation at each input digit. Instead, we use a Boolean-
circuit-based approach driven by a new custom-tailored formula for processing quaternary integers and a
specialized multiplication protocol. The latter technique of “multiplication involving an auxiliary input
held by a single party” may be of independent interest.

Introduction
Secure multiparty computation (MPC) is a cryptographic framework that allows multiple mutually dis-
trustful parties to jointly compute a function without revealing their private inputs. While MPC spans a
broad spectrum of research directions, we focus on a fundamental primitive: secure comparison protocols,
traditionally known as the Millionaires’ Problem [25]. Secure comparison protocols are frequently used in
practical applications, and their high overhead is well-recognized. For example, a study on secure neural
networks, CryptGPU [23], reported that operations involving comparison (specifically ReLU and SoftMax)
accounted for 42–73% of the total execution time (Table 5 in [23]). While numerous studies have focused on
improving the efficiency of secure comparison protocols (cf. Table 1 below), it continues to be a dominant
performance bottleneck in practical privacy-preserving applications. In particular, while protocols based on
secret sharing (SS) are typically recognized for their superior efficiency in terms of both computation and
communication volume (i.e., the total number of bits transferred), they suffer from higher round complexity
than approaches based on other cryptographic primitives such as garbled circuits (GC). Specifically, al-
though the state-of-the-art SS-based plaintext comparison protocol of Lu et al. [14] has achieved a constant
1

three-round online complexity (as demonstrated in [14], such comparison over plaintexts can be extended to
secret-shared inputs by sacrificing a single bit of the plaintext space), it is still higher than the GC-based
two-round complexity. It has been a long-standing open problem to realize SS-based comparison achieving
the same two-round complexity as GC.
1.1
Our Contribution
We propose the first SS-based comparison protocol with a constant two-round online complexity, solving
the aforementioned open problem of achieving the same round complexity as GC-based protocols.
Our
protocol also maintains the O(n log n) online bit complexity of Lu et al.’s three-round protocol [14], which is
significantly lower than the GC-based O(λn). A drawback of our protocol is the O(n3) offline bit complexity
(in comparison to e.g., GC-based O(λn)): to address the issue, we propose the second two-round protocol
with offline bit complexity O(n2), while it increases the online bit complexity to also O(n2).
Furthermore, we also propose the third (not constant, approximately (log3 n)-round) protocol with
asymptotically only 6n-bit online communication. Except for protocols based on function secret sharing
(FSS) [11, 2], which incur exponential offline costs, the state-of-the-art protocol from the viewpoint of online
bit complexity (for which both offline and online phases are fully specified1) is the asymptotically 8n-bit
protocol by Couteau [5]. Our protocol reduces the asymptotic online bit complexity to 75% of Couteau’s
protocol.
A summary of these protocols alongside existing works is provided in Table 1. We also compare the
concrete efficiency of our proposed protocols with the existing protocols by Lu et al. [14] and by Couteau
[5] for the case of 64-bit inputs, where the parameters are set to λ = 128 and µ = 512 (see Table 1 for the
meaning of these parameters). See Table 2 for the result.

1.3
Related Work
Constant-Round Protocols.
Generally, garbled circuits (GC) [24, 26] provide the baseline for constant
(two)-round comparison but incur high bandwidth costs O(λn). Constant-round comparison protocols based
on (fully) homomorphic encryption (HE) are also proposed [9, 19, 4], but they either have higher round com-
plexity or suffer from large computational and communication overhead. While various SS-based approaches
have attempted to reduce rounds, they often rely on relaxed models (e.g., the five-round protocol in [16] based
on the client-aided model [15]) or expensive pre-computation (e.g., the use of multi-fan-in gates [20, 21, 7]
or function secret sharing (FSS) [11, 2]). Recently, Lu et al. [14] established the state-of-the-art for SS-
based comparison, achieving three rounds. However, matching the two-round efficiency of GC within the SS
framework has remained an elusive goal. Our Protocols 1 and 2 directly address this gap.
Bandwidth-Optimized Protocols.
Couteau [5] proposed an asymptotically 8n-bit protocol, though with
logarithmic rounds. More recent works like CrypTFlow2 [22] and Panther [8] leverage tree structures and
higher-radix representations (e.g., quaternary); however, these methods typically rely on OT for per-digit
computation, which dictates a lower bound on online communication. In contrast, our Protocol 3 adopts a
circuit-based approach for the quaternary integers, pushing the online complexity down to asymptotically
6n bits.

2.1
Model for MPC
We deal with secure two-party computation (2PC) protocols between parties P0 and P1 in the preprocessing
model, consisting of an input-independent offline phase and an input-dependent online phase. The protocols
in this paper are purely 2PC protocols, unlike the client-aided model [15] where the offline phase is supposed
to be executed by a trusted client other than the two parties. Our main aim is to reduce the communication
complexity (the numbers of communication rounds and communication bits) for the online phase, while
we also require the offline phase to still have polynomial computational/communication complexity (unlike
FSS-based approaches [11] using exponential offline computation).
We assume that the two parties are semi-honest, and follow the standard security notion in [10]. In this
setting, owing to Composition Theorem [10, Theorem 7.3.3], for any protocol Π involving some subprotocol
Π′, if Π is secure assuming oracle access to the ideal functionality F′ of Π′ (F′-hybrid model), then Π is
secure provided Π′ is securely implemented. Most of the protocols proposed in this paper can be easily
proven secure in such a hybrid model.

Conclusion
In this work, we presented three novel secure comparison protocols over plaintexts, achieving either a constant
two-round or online bandwidth of asymptotically 6n bits. While our contributions significantly advance
the state-of-the-art, two challenging directions remain for future research: (1) further reducing the round
complexity, and (2) further reducing the online communication complexity. Both tasks appear non-trivial.
Regarding the first challenge, our two-round constructions (Protocols 1 and 2) fundamentally rely on a
two-round equality testing framework. Achieving a one-round comparison protocol would likely necessitate
a one-round equality testing primitive—a feat that surpasses the inherent two-round barrier of GC—or a
complete departure from the “leftmost differing bit” strategy employed in this work. Regarding the second
challenge, while exploring different radix representations (e.g., N > 4) or alternative tree structures remains
