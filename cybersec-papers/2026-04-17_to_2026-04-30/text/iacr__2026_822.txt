Abstract
Exact fixed-point multiplication over Z2k is a fundamental primitive for secure
fixed-point arithmetic. However, in the honest-majority, maliciously secure 3PC
setting, no prior work simultaneously provides cross-ring compatibility, exact
semantics, and malicious security within this efficient framework. In this paper,
we address this gap by showing that the core cross-ring bottlenecks, namely
exact signed truncation and signed extension, share a unified algebraic structure.
Based on this insight, we propose a general quotient-correction framework
that reduces complex non-linear cross-ring operations to a highly efficient 2-
bit bounded-quotient extraction problem. We instantiate this framework to
construct maliciously secure protocols for exact truncation and extension. By
sequentially composing these primitives with standard in-ring multiplication, we
realize the first end-to-end exact fixed-point multiplication protocol that satisfies
all aforementioned requirements in the replicated 3PC setting. We also present
optimized variants under relaxed guarantees (e.g., 1-ULP error) that offer supe-
rior performance trade-offs. We formalize our constructions within the Universal
Composability (UC) framework and provide rigorous security proofs. Theoret-
ical analysis and experimental results demonstrate that our approach achieves
practical online efficiency while maintaining exact semantics and malicious secu-
rity, overcoming the limitations of prior baselines regarding security assumptions,
input domains, or output precision.

1 Introduction
Secure multi-party computation (MPC) provides a cryptographic method for perform-
ing joint computations without revealing the private inputs of the participating parties,
and has become one of the key technical approaches in fields such as privacy-preserving
machine learning[1–3]. In such applications, the underlying computations typically
involve decimal or real-valued quantities[4, 5]. Therefore, fixed-point arithmetic is com-
monly used in practical systems to represent and process these values[6]. Within this
computational layer, fixed-point multiplication is particularly fundamental, because
the core computation of many machine-learning tasks reduces to dot products, matrix
multiplications, and the induced linear-layer and convolution operations[7–9]. There-
fore, the secure and efficient implementation of fixed-point multiplication constitutes
a critical foundational requirement, rather than a mere localized arithmetic challenge,
for the practical deployment of higher-level secure multi-party computation-based
machine learning systems.
Among the various settings for secure computation, the honest-majority mali-
ciously secure replicated 3PC setting is an important and widely used target. In
practical scenarios, it is difficult to guarantee in advance that all participating par-
ties will strictly follow the prescribed protocol execution. Therefore, compared with
security against semi-honest adversaries only, security against malicious adversaries is
often the more realistic choice[10]. At the same time, honest-majority protocols typi-
cally achieve significantly lower communication cost than dishonest-majority ones[11].
As a result, honest-majority malicious security MPC has remained an important line
of research that continues to receive sustained attention[11]. Under this setting, repli-
cated secret sharing has become one of the mainstream tools in efficient MPC design,
and the literature explicitly notes that it underlies many recent efficient protocols and
has been used mostly in the context of three parties[12, 13]. Thus, it is meaningful
to study exact fixed-point multiplication in the honest-majority maliciously secure
replicated 3PC setting.
Moreover, when fixed-point multiplication crosses different precisions or rings, the
computational route is also naturally determined by representation alignment and
post-multiplication rescaling[14, 15]. In other words, to perform a complete fixed-point
multiplication, the input is first lifted to a compatible larger ring, the multiplica-
tion is then performed in that ring, and finally, the target precision and output type
are restored through truncation. In the setting considered here, this corresponds pre-
cisely to signed extension, multiplication in the product ring, and signed truncation.
Accordingly, depending on the semantics induced by the truncation step, fixed-point
multiplication protocols can be broadly divided into probabilistic, approximate, and
exact types.

In existing works, probabilistic truncation and approximate truncation are more
commonly used because they are considered sufficient for many PPML scenarios[16],
and due to their non-interactive or less-interactive nature, their communication over-
head is usually significantly lower than that of exact truncation[17]. However, these
relaxed semantics are not always satisfactory. Once fixed-point multiplication is repeat-
edly called within multiplication chains, or used as an intermediate step in larger
composite protocols, the semantics of truncation are directly propagated to the overall
computation[18]. CrypTFlow2[19] further points out that fixed-point multiplication
is necessarily followed by a truncation step, and that once correctness is sacrificed,
the resulting errors can accumulate and cause the final result to deviate substantially
from the expected cleartext execution. Therefore, the relevant gap is not merely the
lack of fixed-point multiplication protocols, but the lack of a complete cross-ring mul-
tiplication path that simultaneously preserves exact semantics and provides malicious
security.
In fact, several closely related studies have explored this target from different
perspectives, but to the best of our knowledge, no work has yet fully bridged the
aforementioned gap. Ditto[15] and Mosformer[20] provides efficient cross-ring fixed-
point multiplication, but relies on approximate semantics and a restricted input range.
SIRNN[14] supports exact cross-ring fixed-point multiplication, but only in the two-
party semi-honest setting. Escudero et al.[21] provides powerful maliciously secure
truncation primitives, but it does not by itself give a matching signed-extension com-
ponent or a complete end-to-end fixed-point multiplication protocol. These limitations
motivate a unified treatment of the cross-ring primitives required for exact fixed-point
multiplication.
In the exact cross-ring setting considered in this paper, the in-ring multiplication
itself already admits a standard realization in the replicated 3PC setting. The central
difficulty therefore lies in the two cross-ring nonlinear primitives required to connect
the input, product, and output rings, namely signed extension and exact signed trun-
cation. Although these two functionalities serve distinct purposes, we observe that
in both cases the target output can be written in the same quotient-correction form

baselines differ in security model, semantic guarantee, or input-domain restriction,
the results are interpreted under these qualifications and show that the proposed con-

security guarantees.
1.1 Contribution
The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:
1. We formulate a unified quotient-correction perspective for exact cross-ring arith-
metic. Based on the insight that the non-linear bottlenecks of exact signed
truncation and signed extension can both be rigorously reduced to a highly efficient
2-bit bounded-quotient extraction, we propose a generic quotient-correction
framework that captures these functionalities under a common protocol structure.
2. We instantiate this generic framework to construct maliciously secure protocols for
exact signed truncation and general signed extension in the honest-majority repli-
cated 3PC setting. By sequentially composing these two cross-ring primitives with
standard in-ring multiplication, we further obtain an exact fixed-point multiplica-
tion protocol over power-of-two rings. In addition, we provide several more efficient
variants under relaxed guarantees to offer practical trade-offs between efficiency
and guarantees.
3. We formalize the security of the proposed framework and its instantiated protocols
within the UC framework. The unified quotient-correction abstraction provides
a common interface of ideal functionality, which significantly simplifies security
analysis. This modularity allows the exact signed truncation, signed extension, and
fixed-point multiplication protocols to be rigorously established through concise
compositional proofs.
4. We provide both theoretical and experimental evaluations of the proposed proto-
cols. The theoretical analysis quantifies the online communication overhead, while
the experimental evaluation measures the online runtime at both the primitive level
and the end-to-end multiplication level. The results show that the protocols pro-
posed here achieve feasible online performance under stronger semantic and security
requirements than the closest baselines.
2 Related work
The target of this work is exact fixed-point multiplication over power-of-two rings, for
which previous works have been explored under different party settings, security mod-
els, and sharing domains. For this task, a natural route is to first lift the inputs to the
product ring through signed extension, then perform the multiplication in the product
4

ring, and finally apply signed truncation to recover the desired precision and output
ring. Therefore, previous works directly relevant to this target include not only end-
to-end fixed-point multiplication protocols, but also the signed extension and signed
truncation protocols that support this computation route. From this perspective, we
organize the related works along three closely connected lines, namely fixed-point
multiplication, signed extension, and signed truncation.
2.1 Fixed-point multiplication
Fixed-point multiplication has been widely studied in secure multi-party computation,
with relevant results covering various party configurations, security models, sharing
domains, and fixed-point semantics. Existing work includes implementations based
on fields, prime rings, and power-of-two rings; it encompasses schemes designed for
semi-honest scenarios as well as those aimed at malicious security; and in terms of
semantic guarantees, there are approaches with varying levels of semantic guarantees,
including exact, approximate, and probabilistic methods. Meanwhile, a more common
setup in existing work is that the inputs are already shared over a sufficiently large
field or ring, such that the exact product can be directly contained within that field
or ring, and the post-multiplication processing primarily involves semantic truncation
performed within the same field or ring[2, 5, 12, 13, 16, 19, 22–33]. To illustrate the
overall landscape of related work, we present a comprehensive survey in Appendix A.1,
with some representative results shown in Table 1.

At the level of end-to-end fixed-point multiplication, there is relatively little previ-

34–36]. Most protocols proposed in previous work either remain within a single field or
ring, or involve cross-ring processing only under relaxed functionality guarantees. To be
more specific, in the three-party setting, the only cross-ring result achieving malicious
5

security is Mosformer[20], published in 2025. However, it provides only approximate
semantics and, more importantly, is valid only under a restricted input range, where
the absolute value of each input must not exceed one quarter of the underlying ring
size. On the other hand, the cross-ring works that achieve exact semantics are all
confined to the two-party semi-honest setting[9, 14, 36].
In short, there remains a significant gap in the implementation of end-to-end fixed-
point multiplication in the widely used setting considered here. To the best of our
knowledge, no existing work has yet proposed a protocol that simultaneously satisfies
all four key requirements: cross-ring compatibility, exact output semantics, malicious-
security guarantees, and support for a three-party setting. However, the absence of the
complete functionality cannot be determined solely at the level of a complete multipli-
cation protocol. If we can obtain, separately, a general signed extension protocol, an
in-ring multiplication protocol, and an exact signed truncation protocol that satisfy
the aforementioned requirements, then a fixed-point multiplication protocol meeting
these goals can be constructed quite naturally. Among these components, maliciously
secure in-ring multiplication already admits a standard realization in the replicated
3PC setting. For the other two primitives, further study is necessary to determine
whether existing work is sufficient to directly assemble a fixed-point multiplication
protocol that meets the aforementioned requirements.
2.2 Signed extension
The existing works related to this functionality mainly include signed extension[9, 14,
35, 36], upcasting[15, 20], and the more general notion of cross-modulus lifting[34].
For the semantics considered here, signed extension and upcasting are not essentially
different. Both refer to the operation of lifting the input from Z2m to Z2n, where
n > m, while preserving the two’s-complement signed semantics. For this reason, in
this paper, both of them are collectively referred to as signed extension. In contrast,
although cross-modulus lifting is also related to modulus transformation, its target
semantics differ significantly from those required in this paper. Therefore, it can only
be regarded as related work rather than a direct counterpart to the target considered
here. A complete comparison of these works is given in Table 2.

the semantic effect of truncation, which means that the output value is rescaled, while
the secret sharing remains over the original field or ring[5, 6, 12, 19, 21, 22, 38]. At
the same time, some of the protocols proposed in existing works do perform an actual
reduction of the underlying sharing field or ring after truncation and are therefore func-
tionally closer to the functionality required here[14, 17, 36]. However, these protocols
only achieve semi-honest security. Overall, the existing works provide an important
research foundation for the exact truncation required in this paper, but they cannot
yet serve directly as the component needed here.
However, a further examination of the existing exact truncation protocols reveals
a more encouraging picture. The exact truncation protocol given by Escudero et al. in
CRYPTO 2020[21] was originally designed for semantic truncation that does not actu-
ally reduce the underlying sharing ring. Nevertheless, after several relatively simple
modifications, this protocol can also realize the smaller-ring output semantics required
here. In other words, from the viewpoint of feasibility alone, the modified protocol can
indeed serve as a component for exact fixed-point multiplication. Unfortunately, we did
not find a signed extension method that matches this modified protocol. This means
that if we adopt this modified truncation protocol as a component, the two cross-ring
primitives required for exact fixed-point multiplication would rely on different under-
lying logic and implementation routes. Although this is not impossible, implementing
these two functions in a unified manner would be more favorable for the modular
design of the overall protocol and would also be more conducive to establishing the
security proof of the overall construction.
3 Technical overview
This work focuses on maliciously secure exact fixed-point multiplication within
the honest-majority replicated 3PC framework. In MPC, fixed-point values are repre-
sented through ring encoding derived from two’s-complement integers, so the protocol
layer operates uniformly on the underlying ring elements. For this task, a natural
procedure involves first sign-extending the inputs from the current ring to the tar-
get ring for the product, then performing the multiplication in the larger ring, and
finally applying exact signed truncation to match the target precision and output ring.
The bottleneck lies in the two cross-ring nonlinear primitives surrounding it, namely
signed extension and exact signed truncation. Therefore, the efficiency and secu-
rity of exact fixed-point multiplication hinge on low-cost and composable realizations
of these two primitives.
Our key insight is that, although exact signed truncation and signed extension
serve distinct purposes, the nonlinear information required for recovery in each case
is significantly smaller than the full output value. In both scenarios, local linear oper-
ations initially yield a base value, while the remaining uncertainty is limited to a
small correction term with only a few possible values. Thus, the challenge is not to
reconstruct the entire output maliciously, but rather to recover the small nonlinear
8

correction term. Specifically, for exact truncation, the nonlinear core corresponds to
the wrap value; for signed extension, it involves both the wrap resulting from sum-
ming the shares and the sign bit of the input. Notably, both cases can be unified as
the recovery of a two-bit bounded quotient.
For the exact signed truncation, local truncation of each share already yields a
linear main term, while the remaining ambiguity arises only from the carry bits intro-
duced by the discarded local remainders. To be more specific, the sum of the three
local remainders can cross the threshold 2k zero, once, or twice. Thus, the exact result
differs from the locally truncated value only by a wrap term taking values in {0, 1, 2}.

and uses it to construct a unified, composable, and analytically transparent framework
for maliciously secure exact fixed-point multiplication.
4 Preliminaries
4.1 Notation and algebraic preliminaries
Algebraic structures. Protocols in this work primarily operate over the power-of-
two rings of the form Z2k (for various bit-widths k), which naturally support two’s-
complement fixed-point arithmetic. For any integer k > 1, let Z2k := Z/2kZ denote
the ring of integers modulo 2k, represented by the canonical set {0, 1, . . . , 2k −1}.
Additionally, we write ZK, where K = 2k, for the same ring.
However, since ring Z2k contains zero divisors, some verification steps used in our
malicious checks are performed after mapping elements from ring Z2k into a large
prime field Zp, where p is sufficiently large for the relevant bit-width. The ring-to-
field embedding is solely used to avoid pitfalls caused by zero divisors, hence such
an embedding cannot preserve the underlying two’s-complement semantics in general.
Nevertheless, this is sufficient for our purposes since we invoke the embedding only
when checking some specific algebraic identities.

embedding between two rings in some verification steps. For any m ≤n, since 2m | 2n,
there is a natural ring homomorphism:

Under the honest-majority assumption that at most one party is corrupted, repli-
cated arithmetic sharing and Boolean sharing provide information-theoretic privacy
against any single-party view. However, replicated sharing itself does not imply mali-
cious security for arbitrary protocol execution. In our protocols, malicious behavior
is detected through explicit consistency checks on the overlapping local views of the
replicated representation, such as through audited openings.
Opening and audited opening. In a standard opening, a party reconstructs the
secret value x and reveals it to all other parties. In contrast, during an audited opening,
the parties reconstruct and reveal x while simultaneously checking the replication
consistency (i.e., overlapping shares held by different parties must match). If the check
fails, the execution of the current protocol aborts. Typically, audited openings are not
used to reveal secret values, but rather to bind intermediate values to a well-defined
replicated sharing in maliciously secure verification procedures.

4.5 Threat model and security goal
Threat model. We consider the standard honest-majority three-party setting with
parties P1, P2 and P3, where at most one party is statically corrupted. Depending
on the protocol variant, the corrupted party can be either semi-honest or malicious.
All parties are assumed to communicate through secure authenticated point-to-point
channels.
Security goal. Unless stated otherwise, the main constructions aim for standard
simulation-based malicious security with abort over 2-out-of-3 replicated sharing. Cor-
rectness is defined with respect to the semantics in Section 4.3, under which signed
extension preserves the two’s-complement integer semantics and exact signed trunca-
tion implements arithmetic right shift exactly. Although the corresponding proofs are
carried out at the level of the integer semantics, the fixed-point semantics is induced
directly by the same ring encodings and is therefore preserved as well. Some weaker
variants may satisfy only relaxed guarantees, such as semi-honest security or explicit
bounded-error correctness.
Assumptions and scope. The focus of this work is the design and analysis of
exact cross-ring arithmetic protocols and their composition into maliciously secure
fixed-point multiplication. Standard preprocessing required by the invoked black-box
protocols is assumed to be available as described in Section 4.4. Denial-of-service
attacks, fairness, and guaranteed output delivery are out of scope.

9 Conclusion
This work investigates maliciously secure, exact fixed-point multiplication over power-
of-two rings within the honest-majority replicated 3PC framework. The central
challenge of this functionality arises from two cross-ring nonlinear primitives required
for multiplication: exact signed truncation and signed extension. The efficiency, secu-
rity, and composability of these primitives fundamentally govern the practical viability
of exact fixed-point arithmetic in this setting. A key insight is that, although these
functionalities serve distinct algorithmic roles, the nonlinear information that must
be securely reconstructed in each case is substantially smaller than the full output.
43

Specifically, local linear operations already determine a base value, and the resid-
ual ambiguity reduces to a small, bounded quotient. Leveraging this observation, we
identify a unified computational primitive, namely bounded-quotient extraction and
correction, and demonstrate that both exact signed truncation and signed extension
arise as direct, efficient instantiations thereof. Composing these instantiations sequen-
tially yields a complete exact fixed-point multiplication protocol. Beyond the concrete
constructions, this unification delivers conceptual and analytical benefits: the result-
ing protocols admit a common UC-style abstraction; their security proofs follow a
uniform compositional argument; and their dominant online communication and com-
putation costs are governed by a shared analytical model. The theoretical analysis and
experimental results further show that the proposed approach achieves meaningful effi-
ciency while providing exact semantics and malicious security in a setting where prior
baselines generally rely on weaker guarantees, restricted domains, or non-identical
semantic targets. Thus, the principal contribution of this work is not merely a set of
isolated protocols, but a unified, composable, and analytically transparent framework
for cross-ring exact arithmetic. We posit that this framework establishes a principled
foundation for building more sophisticated exact numeric protocols in replicated 3PC.
