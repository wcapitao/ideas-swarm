A Primer on Dependency in Polynomial Product:
Identify, Exploit, and Trim

1 State Key Laboratory of Cyberspace Security Defense, Institute of Information

Abstract. Many lattice-based encryption schemes allow a negligible but
nonzero decryption failure rate (DFR), which is closely tied to both
correctness and security through failure-based attacks. Several module-
lattice constructions (e.g., LAC (NIST PQC Round-2), DAWN (ASI-
ACRYPT 2025), average-case noise analysis in FHE) estimate DFR from
one-coordinate marginals together with an independence approximation
across the coefficients of polynomial products, namely, that treats the
noise as uniformly distributed on a sphere. In the rare-event regime rel-
evant to concrete security, this approximation can be optimistic because
polynomial convolution introduces structured dependencies with no ana-
logue in unstructured lattice settings. Geometrically, the noise spreads
towards a cube rather than a sphere due to the inherent dependencies.
To make this effect explicit, we study polynomial products in the
power-of-two cyclotomic ring through a norm-wise decomposition. The
decomposition separates an outer term (corresponding to the radius
of the sphere), which is effectively captured by coefficient-wise models,
and an inner term (representing the uneven parts of the spherical sur-
face), which is shown as a diagonal energy term and accounts for the
convolution-induced dependencies. This gives an exact explanation for
the heavier tails observed in polynomial products and for the resulting
gap between independence-based estimates and actual failure behavior.
This perspective has consequences for both attacks and design. On
the attack side, it gives a principled proxy criterion for constructing
high-DFR candidate ciphertexts in failure-based attacks. In particular,
it explains how the attack of Guo et al. (ASIACRYPT 2019) can target
LAC even when the Hamming weights are fixed, and it improves failure-
finding efficiency by identifying the underlying class of bad randomness
pairs beyond pattern-based subsets. On the design side, it motivates
trimming high-dependency samples during key generation and encryp-
tion. We first give a certified trimmed DFR bound based on conditional
spectral control, then isolate a separate labeled three-vector heuristic
for calibrated interpretation, and finally validate both layers on exact-
support and moderate-dimension experiments. We formalize the result-
ing approach as the generic frameworks TrimPKE and TrimKEM, prove
security in the QROM while accounting for rejection, and instantiate the
framework for LAC and DAWN as case studies.
Keywords: module lattice · decryption failures · PKE · KEM

Introduction
As post-quantum cryptography moves toward standardization, lattice-based con-
structions have gained increasing prominence due to their efficiency, relatively
direct reductions to well-studied hardness assumptions, and applicability across
a wide range of cryptographic primitives. In particular, the lattice-based encryp-
tion Kyber [6] has been selected in the NIST PQC standardization process. A
distinctive feature of many lattice-based encryption schemes, however, is that
decryption may fail with small probability. This phenomenon is common to con-
structions based on Learning with Errors (LWE) and its ring/module variants
that decode by rounding and tolerate bounded noise. While one can eliminate de-
cryption failures through sufficiently conservative parameter choices as in NTRU
Prime [4] and NTRU HRSS [34], this typically incurs noticeable efficiency and
bandwidth costs (e.g., larger public keys and ciphertexts). Consequently, modern
KEM designs tune parameters so that the decryption failure rate (DFR) is negli-
gible while retaining performance, as exemplified by Kyber [6] and DAWN [42].
In fully homomorphic encryption (FHE), DFR also plays an analogous role:
schemes such as BGV [9], BFV [8,22], and CKKS [12,7] estimate and control
failure probabilities to ensure functional correctness of the supported evalua-
tions.
DFR also has direct security implications in the chosen-ciphertext (CCA)
model. An adversary with access to a decryption oracle can exploit failures to
obtain information about the secret key, making careful parameterization essen-
tial for balancing efficiency and security. For example, the NIST PQC setting
allows up to 264 adversarially chosen ciphertexts, and security against quantum
adversaries typically calls for DFR well below 2−128. Failure-based CCA attacks
on code/lattice-based schemes have been extensively studied in [21,28,29,33].
Moreover, the advent of failure boosting [19] and subsequent refinements [20]
reduces the dominant attack cost to finding the first failing ciphertext, further
increasing the importance of accurate tail estimates. And since the IND-CPA-D
security has been proposed in [39], DFR plays a more important role in maintain-
ing the security of FHE schemes; several failure-based attacks on FHE schemes
have been studied in [10,11,44].
On the other hand, error correcting code (ECC) is a powerful tool to compress
the size in the module-lattice-based encryptions, one typical example is LAC [45]
(NIST PQC Round-2). However, underestimating DFR in such schemes leaves
them vulnerable to failure-based CCA attacks [29]. Also, average-case noise anal-
ysis is common for estimating DFR in FHE schemes, as in [5]. Both the DFR
estimation used in LAC [45], DAWN [42], and average-case noise estimation
methods used in FHE [5,14] share a common assumption: treating the relevant
post-convolution coefficients as independent. Several works provide empirical
evidence consistent with this assumption in typical regimes [14,41], but these
evaluations largely focus on matching the coarse shape of the probability mass
function (PMF), rather than quantifying the statistical distance to the true
distribution in the extreme tail. For CKKS, for example, the analysis of [7] re-
ports that the mean and standard deviation of the noise deviate from theoretical
2

predictions by at most 0.01 bits; such moment-level agreement, however, does
not rule out substantial discrepancies in rare-event probabilities. Indeed, [25]
observed that independence-based models can induce heavy-tailedness in cer-
tain settings and analyzed higher moments (e.g., kurtosis), revealing a non-
negligible bias relative to independence-based estimates. Further, [15] demon-
strated that convolution-induced dependencies can lead to underestimation of
DFR in schemes that combine module-lattice noise with error-correcting codes,
such as LAC [45]. That work quantified a gap between independence-based esti-
mates and dependence-aware estimates for LAC. However, its analysis still relied
on additional assumptions and did not capture dependency effects under fixed
Hamming-weight distributions.
1.1
Our Contributions
Several DFR analyses for module-lattice encryption rely on a coefficient-wise
independence assumption for polynomial products, which can lead to systematic
underestimation in the DFR. We study the dependencies induced by polynomial
product and develop a norm-wise framework that both exploits and mitigates
their impact. Our goal is to identify where these dependencies matter, exploit
them to shape a better failure-based attack, and quantify how much trimming
is required to suppress the resulting tail amplification in ECC-relevant settings.
Our contributions are as follows.
Identify: a norm-wise dependency model. We introduce a norm-wise per-
spective that makes the dependency contribution explicit and distinguishes mod-
ule lattices from the unstructured lattice setting in the tail regime. Using a new
decomposition-based view, we separate the behavior explained by coefficient-wise
models from the additional effect induced by polynomial convolution. This clar-
ifies when and why independence-based estimators become inaccurate for DFR
in schemes where failures are driven by many moderately large coefficients.
Exploit: a dependency-guided failure-finding heuristic. On the attack
side, our model explains why failure-based CCA attacks can still find useful can-
didates even when the relevant Hamming weights are fixed, and it recovers prior
pattern-based selection rules as a particularly structured example. It suggests
a dependency-guided proxy selector for high-risk candidates. In our small-scale
proxy experiment, this selector exhibits a stronger tail signal than the prior
pattern proxy under the same search budget.
Trim: a generic protection framework. On the design side, we propose
a lightweight mitigation based on rejection sampling that filters highly depen-
dent samples during key generation and encryption. We present a trimming-
conditioned worst-case DFR bound with spectral control, while the sharper
three-vector estimate is kept as a separate heuristic supplement. We formalize
3

this approach as two generic frameworks, TrimPKE and TrimKEM, prove their
security in the QROM while accounting for rejection, and explicitly analyze the
resulting concrete-security loss. We use the LAC and DAWN frameworks as case
studies to illustrate the trade-offs between DFRs and efficiency.

Our Techniques
Lattice-based encryption schemes may rely on different assumptions for key gen-
eration (e.g., NTRU/LWE-type assumptions), but their encryption and decryp-
tion procedures are uniformly governed by LWE or a structured variant (Ring-
/Module-LWE, and likewise LWR variants). This common structure admits a
generic decryption condition. Concretely, decryption recovers each message bit

To obtain compact keys and ciphertexts and to enable fast arithmetic, many
modern designs (e.g., Kyber [6], Saber [16], BAT [24], NEV [51], and DAWN [42])
adopt Ring-/Module-LWE (or Ring-/Module-LWR). In these structured set-
tings, vector entries are coefficient embeddings of ring elements. For a fixed
component index k, the entries (ai)[k] and (aj)[k] are not independent draws:
they are rotations of the same underlying polynomial in a quotient ring Z[x]/φ,
satisfying (ai)[k] ≡xj−i · (aj)[k]. As a consequence, the family of inner products
appearing across coordinates in decryption corresponds to different coefficients
of the same polynomial products. This is the algebraic source of the dependen-
cies that are absent in unstructured lattices, yet are often ignored by analyses
that model coefficients as independent.

2.3
Hard-Decision Error-Correcting Code
Let z ∈Rn denote the centered decryption-error polynomial before the final
decision rule is applied, and let ∆> 0 be the per-coordinate decoding threshold.
A coordinate error occurs at index i when |z[i]| > ∆. For schemes without an
outer ECC layer, decryption therefore fails exactly when
∥z∥∞> ∆.
For schemes with a hard-decision code that corrects up to t coefficient errors, we
define
N∆:= #{i ∈{0, . . . , n −1} : |z[i]| > ∆},
so the ECC failure event is {N∆> t}.
In the Module-LWE framework of Figure 1, the centered decryption error is
z := reT −e1sT + e2,
and the standard rounding decoder corresponds to ∆= ⌊q/4⌉after centering.
The same notation will be used throughout the paper: z is always the centered
pre-decoding error polynomial, ∆is always the per-coordinate threshold, and
N∆is always the number of threshold exceedances.

Exploit Dependencies in CCA Attacks of

This norm-wise perspective also yields an attack-side heuristic for cryptanalysis.
A key step in CCA attacks leveraging high DFR is to craft “bad” ciphertexts
whose failure probability is substantially above average. In the LAC setting, the
attacker searches over candidate fixed-weight randomness polynomials drawn
from the same sampler as the scheme and looks for instances whose depen-
dency score predicts elevated tail behavior. Prior work describes this search us-
ing message-derived patterns because those patterns induce particular instances
of randomness [29]; our point here is more generic: the relevant predictor is the
resulting polynomial dependency profile, rather than a specific syntactic pat-
tern family. The effectiveness of patterns in [29] is therefore explained by the
large inner distortion they induce. Our norm-wise model makes this dependency
12

explicit and suggests a dependency-guided proxy selector rather than a fixed-
pattern heuristic.
In LAC encryption, the distributions Xr and Xe1 are fixed Hamming weight
distributions, which effectively fix the outer distortion. From the norm-wise per-
spective, the relevant selector is therefore the inner-distortion score ∥(a¯a)[1:n/2)∥∞
from Section 3.2. The adversarial strategy of [29] focuses on polynomials with
an interval that contains only 1 or −1 and the rest are 0s, which was called the
TYPE 2 pattern, and the TYPE 1 pattern is a special case with no 0 in the
interval. While such patterns do indeed yield large inner distortion, our model
shows that they are merely a special case within a larger class of exploitable
ciphertexts; any polynomial whose coefficients exhibit the large inner distortion
tends to produce a heavier upper tail in the resulting product distribution.

distortion empirically, we conducted a simple experiment in R512, using the

Trim on Module Lattice Encryptions and
Encapsulations
Given the weaknesses introduced by dependencies, in this section we present a
generic, lightweight safeguard procedure for module-lattice-based public key en-
cryption (PKE) and key encapsulation mechanisms (KEM). The central idea is
to reject polynomials whose dependence statistics would otherwise lead to more
severe tail behavior. Outer-distortion screening is already familiar in lattice cryp-
tography, for example, in Falcon key generation [23] and BAT-style rejection [24],
where public predicates filter large-norm outliers. We separate that familiar outer
14

screening from the dependencies statistics identified. We bridge the dependency
to DFR via the canonical embedding, and then introduce a rigorous upper bound
of DFR based on the bounded spectrum and a heuristic but tight upper bound
of DFR based on the bounded inner distortion. Furthermore, given a module-
lattice-based PKE scheme with algorithms KeyGen, Encrypt, and Decrypt, we first
introduce the generic protection framework TrimPKE, then analyze its impact on
concrete security, and finally describe techniques for efficient implementation. By
applying the Fujisaki-Okamoto transform to TrimPKE, we obtain the correspond-
ing TrimKEM. The full security proofs of TrimPKE and TrimKEM are provided
in Section 6. The framework keeps the arithmetic parameters unchanged, but
it introduces explicit rejection rates, runtime overhead, and support-loss terms
that must be accounted for in the concrete security analysis. We instantiate it
in LAC [45] and DAWN [42] as case studies.
