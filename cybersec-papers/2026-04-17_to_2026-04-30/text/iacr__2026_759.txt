A Scalable Fault Countermeasure for SLH-DSA
Trade-offs Between Memory, Performance, and Fault Resilience
Melissa Azouaoui1, Tobias Schneider2 and Denise Verbakel3
1 NXP Semiconductors, Hamburg, Germany
2 NXP Semiconductors, Gratkorn, Austria
3 NXP Semiconductors, Eindhoven, The Netherlands

Abstract. We introduce compressed caching, a scalable and parameterizable counter-
measure against grafting tree fault attacks on SLH-DSA. Unlike standard caching,
which entails fully caching the WOTS+ signatures and public keys, compressed
caching achieves significant memory savings while maintaining strong fault detection
capabilities. It can be tuned to achieve a trade-off between caching memory size, fault
resilience, and performance, making it well-suited for deployment across devices with
varying resource and security constraints. We provide a security and performance
analysis of compressed caching and show that it can be configured to achieve high fault
detection probability and outperform standard caching, mainly in terms of memory
but also in terms of performance. Additionally, we explore granular variants of both
standard and compressed caching and study on a finer scale the memory-performance
trade-off of both standard and compressed caching. Our results demonstrate that
compressed caching is especially advantageous for constrained devices, outperforming
standard caching when less than approximately 256 kB of caching memory is available.

Introduction
In response to the growing threat posed by quantum computing to classical digital
signatures, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) has selected several
post-quantum digital signature schemes for standardization [NIS]. One of the selected
algorithms is SLH-DSA, which is a stateless hash-based digital signature algorithm derived
from the SPHINCS+ framework [BHK+19]. Being a hash-based signature scheme, the
security of the scheme is fully based on the cryptographic properties of the hash functions
used. This implies that it is resilient against many traditional cryptanalytic attacks;
however, it has been shown to be critically vulnerable to fault injection attacks [CMP18,
GKPM18, Gen23].
Fault attacks exploit unintended behavior in cryptographic implementations caused by
physical disturbances such as voltage glitches or laser injections [CML+11].These attacks
can compromise the integrity of digital signatures by manipulating internal computations.
In SLH-DSA, fault attacks are especially dangerous because the scheme depends on one-
time signatures (which should only be used to sign a single and fixed message) and hash
trees (which should stay the same during the signing process).
The grafting tree attack [CMP18] on the SPHINCS framework enables existential forgery
(the creation of a valid signature for any chosen message M) once both a valid and a faulty
signature for the same message are obtained. The attack works by introducing faults during

the computation of hash trees, which can lead to the reuse of one-time signatures. This
reuse violates the core security assumption of one-time signatures, allowing an attacker to
construct a counterfeit hash tree that appears validly signed. As a result, the attacker can
forge signatures for arbitrary messages, compromising the integrity of the entire scheme.
Subsequent studies have confirmed the broad applicability and severity of this attack.
Amiet et al. [ALCZ20] replicated the attack on custom hardware implementations, while
Genêt’s work [Gen23] extended the analysis to SPHINCS+ with randomized signing. These
studies revealed that even a single random bit flip during signing can significantly degrade
the scheme’s security, and that faulty signatures often pass verification undetected.
Despite the critical nature of these findings, the development of effective and efficient
countermeasures remains a significant challenge. Castelnovi et al. [CMP18] proposed
classical redundancy as a generic defense, while Kermani et al. [KAA17] suggested error-
detection mechanisms for hash functions that do not fully cover the SLH-DSA signing
procedure. Furthermore, Gavrilan et al. [GOW+24] proposed fault-resilient implementa-
tions using threshold techniques in hash functions, and Genêt [Gen23] proposed extensive
caching of the one-time signatures to mitigate fault attacks. However, these approaches
often incur significant performance or memory overheads, and for some their practicality
in constrained environments remains unclear.
Contributions.
In this paper, we present compressed caching, a flexible and scalable
countermeasure designed to protect SLH-DSA implementations against grafting tree
fault attacks. Compared to the WOTS+ caching countermeasure analyzed in [Gen23],
compressed caching significantly reduces memory requirements. It offers a tunable trade-off
between fault resilience, memory usage, and performance making it adaptable to a wide
range of devices and deployment scenarios. This flexibility makes compressed caching
particularly suitable for real-world applications where resources are constrained and fault
resilience is critical.
Outline.
This paper is structured as follows. Section 2 provides a high-level overview of
the SLH-DSA scheme, the grafting tree attack, and existing countermeasures. Section 3
introduces the proposed compressed caching countermeasure. Section 4 analyzes its fault
resilience against single- and multiple-fault attacks and provides guidance on parametrizing
the compressed caching countermeasure to achieve a desired memory to fault resistance
trade-off.
Section 5 provides the memory footprint, and performance improvements
(measured in hash calls) compared to the standard caching countermeasure [Gen23].
Section 6 presents a practical validation of the theoretical performance gains, includes fault
injection simulations to confirm the predicted fault detection probabilities and assesses the
impact of additional mitigations against multiple-fault adversaries. Finally, Section 7 briefly
explores granular variants of both the standard and compressed caching countermeasures,
with the main goal of clarifying when compressed caching is most beneficial.

Background
In the following sections and throughout the remainder of this paper, we adopt the same
or similar notational conventions as those presented in [Gen23] in order to maintain
consistency with existing literature. Nevertheless, as prior works predominantly focus on
SPHINCS+, we adapt the notation to conform to the SLH-DSA standard [NIS24].
2.1
SLH-DSA
SLH-DSA is a digital signature scheme that ensures data integrity, authenticates the identity
of the signer, and supports non-repudiation. It is derived from SPHINCS+, which was

selected for standardization under the NIST Post-Quantum Cryptography Standardization
initiative. SLH-DSA is a stateless hash-based signature scheme, distinguishing itself from
related schemes such as LMS [MCF19] and XMSS [HBG+18] by eliminating the need for
state management. This stateless design is achieved through the integration of a broad
few-time signature (FTS) scheme, layered with a hypertree structure composed of one-time
signature (OTS) schemes. Specifically, the FTS component is implemented using FORS,
while the hypertree is constructed using XMSS trees utilizing WOTS+ signatures. Below,
we provide a brief overview of the three main components of SLH-DSA. For comprehensive
technical details or more information on these building blocks, we refer the reader to the
SLH-DSA standard [NIS24].
Definitions and notations.
SLH-DSA uses a variety of hash functions and pseudo-random
functions across its APIs to support several cryptographic operations. These include
generating the randomizer R for randomized message hashing, generating a digest of the
message to be signed, generating secret values for WOTS+ and FORS, and compressing
input data of ℓn, 2n, or n bytes into n-byte outputs.

Forest of Random Subsets (FORS).
FORS is the few-time signature scheme used to
sign message digests in SLH-DSA. Unlike WOTS+, where key reuse can lead to feasible
forgeries, the security of a FORS key degrades gradually with repeated use. FORS operates
with two parameters:
• k: the number of FORS trees;
• t = 2a: the number of leaves in a FORS tree of height a.
The private key consists of k sets of t n-byte strings, all pseudo-randomly derived from
the secret seed SK.seed. Each set is organized into a Merkle tree, and the roots of these
trees are hashed together to form the FORS public key. A signature on a k · a-bit message
digest includes k elements from the private keys - one from each set, determined by a bits
of the digest - along with their corresponding authentication paths. The authentication
path consists of the sibling nodes along the path from a selected leaf to the root of the

Merkle tree. These nodes enable recomputation of the root, allowing verification of the
signature without iterating through the entire tree.
Winternitz One-Time Signature Plus (WOTS+).
WOTS+ is the one-time signature
scheme used within SLH-DSA to authenticate subtrees by signing their respective root
nodes. It utilizes the chain function, which repeatedly applies a hash function to an input,
using each output as the input for the next iteration, until a predefined number of hashing
operations has been completed. The WOTS+ scheme requires three parameters:
• n: the security parameter, length in bytes of the WOTS+ elements/messages;
• lgw = log2 (w): the number of bits that are encoded by each hash chain of length w;
• ℓ= ℓ1 + ℓ2: number of secret and public values in WOTS+.
A WOTS+ private key comprises ℓsecret values of length n, derived from the secret seed
SK.seed using a pseudo-random function. Each secret value is used to generate a chain
of length w hashes via a chaining function, with the final values of these chains forming
the public values. The WOTS+ public key is then computed as the hash of these public
values. To sign a message, it is first converted into an array of ℓ1 base-w integers, followed
by a checksum of ℓ2 base-w integers. The signature consists of selected intermediate values
from the chains, corresponding to the message and checksum integers.
eXtended Merkle Signature Scheme (XMSS).
XMSS extends the WOTS+ signature
scheme to enable the signing of multiple messages. It introduces an additional parameter,
h′, which specifies the height of a Merkle tree. This tree structure allows to authenticate
2h′ WOTS+ public keys using a single n-byte XMSS public key, which corresponds to
the root of the Merkle tree. An XMSS signature comprises a WOTS+ signature and an
authentication path, with a total size of (ℓ+ h′) · n bytes.
SLH-DSA hypertree structure.
Because signing a large number of FORS public keys
needs a large number of WOTS+ keys, using just one XMSS key pair is not practical
within the limits set by the standard. To address this, SLH-DSA employs a hypertree
structure, which consists of multiple layers of Merkle trees. In this configuration, XMSS
keys at the lowest layer are designated for signing FORS public keys, while XMSS keys at
higher layers are used to authenticate the XMSS public keys of the layer directly below
them. The hypertree structure needs two additional parameters:
• d: number of layers of XMSS trees;
• h = d · h′: the total height of the hypertree (d layers of XMSS trees of height h′).

Grafting tree attack on SLH-DSA
The grafting tree attack on SPHINCS+ was first proposed by Castelnovi et al. in [CMP18].
Its basic idea is to force one of the one-time signature schemes inside the tree to sign
two (or multiple) different messages via fault injection. Given these signatures, it is then
possible to extract information about the WOTS+ secret key, which is used to authenticate

an attacker-generated sub-tree and forge signatures. The high-level idea of such a grafting
tree attack is illustrated in Figure 1.
One of the main strengths of the attack is its very relaxed fault model. In the most
basic case, it requires a single, random value fault injected during signature generation on
a wide attack surface. In particular, Castelnovi et al. note that faulting the authentication
path will lead to a signature useful for an attacker, which is still valid and as such could
not easily be detected via signature verification. While the initial paper [CMP18] focused
on the deterministic variant of SPHINCS+ (relying on signing the same message multiple
times to ensure the same path through the tree) Genêt generalizes the grafting tree attack
in [Gen23] to randomized signing.
Overall, the grafting tree attack represents one of the most powerful fault attacks on
SPHINCS+ and SLH-DSA due to its relaxed attacker model and ability to avoid detection
via signature verification. Therefore, any practical implementation of SLH-DSA for which
fault attacks are in scope is required to integrate dedicated countermeasures to thwart it.
2.3
Grafting tree attack countermeasures for SLH-DSA
The grafting tree attack on SLH-DSA is a powerful attack with a large attack surface.
An ad-hoc countermeasure against it would imply recomputing the SLH-DSA signature
generation and comparing the output signatures. A second countermeasure proposed in
the literature involves caching the WOTS+ signatures. Below, we provide a brief overview
of recomputation and caching, accompanied by an analysis of their respective hash function
call counts and memory usage for caching.
Recomputation.
Recomputation is an ad-hoc fault attack countermeasure. It would
involve computing an SLH-DSA signature twice for the same inputs (message and random-
ness) and verifying that the output signatures are identical. This protects against a single
fault injected during a signature generation and can be generalized to protect against x
faults by computing the signature x + 1 times and ensuring that all output signatures are
equal. Such a recomputation can also be performed more granularly by recomputing each
layer or after each WOTS+ or XMSS signature. The average total hash function calls for
slh_sign protected with double computation is simply:

The proposed so-called compressed caching is conceptually simple. Instead of caching
the full WOTS+ signatures and corresponding public keys, we propose to cache the
WOTS+ signatures in a compressed form: a truncated hash of the WOTS+ signature. We
denote this truncated hash as Truncg(H(X)). Truncg(X) : B∗→Bg is defined as a function
that outputs the leftmost g bytes of the input byte string X [NIS24]. H denotes the hash
function selected to implement the compressed caching, e.g., the one used throughout
SLH-DSA and defined by the parameter set. Note that the input X should include at
a minimum the full WOTS+ signature, since the goal of the attacker is to inject a fault
that leads to the WOTS+ secret key signing an input it was not meant to sign and hence
produce a different WOTS+ signature. In the remainder of this paper, unless otherwise
stated, X includes only the WOTS+ signature. Compressed caching allows to perform an
integrity check on the generated WOTS+ signatures by comparing their truncated hashes
during runtime to the corresponding values in the cache.

reduction in memory requirements, we are able to cache more layers and depending on
the SLH-DSA parameter sets and parameters of the countermeasure, compressed caching
can be beneficial. Before discussing the memory improvements and performance impact of

the analysis to consider multiple faults per execution and explore attack vectors that the

4.2
Resistance to single-fault attacks
The fault resilience of the compressed caching depends on the value of the parameter
g, which dictates the output size in bytes of the truncated hash. Namely, if g is too
small, the collision probability over the truncated hash is relatively high, which in turn
makes bypassing the countermeasure easier. In the following, we analyze more concretely
the security of the compressed caching countermeasure against the grafting tree attack
assuming a single fault per signing and under different fault models.
The grafting tree attack as described in [Gen23] involves many steps. Some of which are
essentially brute-force searches. Such steps are not impacted by the fault injection difficulty
or caching. Instead what caching or other fault countermeasures mainly alter or lower is
the probability for an attacker to receive as output an exploitable faulty WOTS+ signature
after a fault injection. For example, this probability is 0 for the WOTS+ signatures cached
using standard caching as described in [Gen23], since the cached WOTS+ signatures are no
longer being computed and simply fetched from the cache and appended to the signature.
The attacker only requires a single faulty signature to achieve forgery with a non-
negligible probability. This forgery probability can be improved with more faulty WOTS+
signatures for the same WOTS+ key pair. For the following analysis, to get a lower bound
on the effectiveness of the compressed caching countermeasure, we assume the worst case,
i.e., that one single faulty WOTS+ signature compromises the whole scheme. In addition
to assuming perfect fault injection probability, i.e., that without any countermeasure
an attacker will always receive an exploitable faulty WOTS+ signature, we first analyze
random and skipping faults (which are more realistic) and then analyze controlled faults
(which assume a very strong attacker model).

A Scalable Fault Countermeasure for SLH-DSA
Given a caching memory of more than ≈218 bytes (i.e., 256 kB), the dashed and
continuous curves intersect signaling that when enough memory is available, standard
caching offers a better trade-off than compressed caching. This means that for most
embedded or constrained devices, compressed caching offers a better use of caching memory.
These are the types of devices for which fault attacks and optimized fault-hardened signing
are the most relevant. For devices or applications with the possibility to cache a large
amount of data, standard caching is the better option. The intersection point naturally
changes based on the parameter set and the value of g, however it seems that g has a
limited impact on this. For practical applications, given the parameter set and g the
exact intersection point can be computed from the equations provided in this paper or by
adapting the equations provided to the specific implementation.
Regarding granular vs. non-granular caching, while granular caching offers a way
to use a maximum memory budget to achieve an additional performance improvement,
non-granular caching is naturally easier to implement and provides a uniform execution
and fault resistance behaviour for all WOTS+ keys belonging to the same layer in the
hypertree.
8
Conclusion
In this paper, we propose and analyze a novel and alternative approach to protecting
SLH-DSA implementations against grafting tree attacks. We show that our so-called
compressed caching countermeasure can be parameterized to provide very strong resilience
against faults and use a fraction of the memory required by standard caching of the
WOTS+ signatures and public keys [Gen23]. In addition, it offers better performance-
memory trade-offs when low to moderate caching memory is available, i.e., memory sizes
that are most relevant for embedded devices. Only when over ≈256 kB of caching memory
are available, is it outperformed by standard caching.
The introduced compressed caching is a new approach to protecting SLH-DSA imple-
mentations against faults and hence offers multiple avenues for further research. First,
as mentioned in the paper, since compressed caching is relevant for embedded and more
constrained devices, one interesting aspect to explore is the impact of limited runtime
memory on compressed caching, or generally caching countermeasures for SLH-DSA, and
potentially strategies to deal with very limited runtime memory. Second, we’ve analyzed
compressed caching with software implementations in mind. One open question remains
for both standard and compressed caching as to whether their performance improvements
over recomputation are also translated to hardware optimized implementations, such as
proposed in [Saa24].
