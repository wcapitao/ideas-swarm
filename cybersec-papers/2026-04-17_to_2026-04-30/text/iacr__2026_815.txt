Non-Adaptive Programmable PRFs
and Applications to Stacked Garbling
Vipul Goyal∗
David Heath†
Abhishek Jain‡
Yibin Yang§
April 25, 2026
Abstract
Garbled circuits are a fundamental primitive in cryptography. While the size of garbled circuits
in Yao’s original scheme grows linearly with the circuit size, a recent line of work on stacked garbling
(SGC) [Heath-Kolesnikov, CRYPTO’20] has achieved near-sublinear size for branching computations,
based only on one-way functions. Specifically, these schemes achieve garbled size growing only with
the size of a single branch and the total input length to all the branches. Due to the latter dependence,
these results are best suited to “small” input settings.
We present a stacked garbling scheme for “large” input settings based on one-way functions.
The garbled size in our scheme grows only with the size of a single branch and its input length (up to
logarithmic factors), plus an additive term in the number of branches (as in prior SGC).
To obtain our result, we uncover a connection between stacked garbling and the notion of
(adaptive) programmable pseudorandom functions (apPRFs) [Boneh-Lewi-Wu, PKC’17]. While existing
apPRF constructions either rely on stronger assumptions (e.g., learning with errors or indistinguishability
obfuscation) or incur noticeable security losses under weaker assumptions, we identify a relaxed notion
of non-adaptive programmable PRFs (napPRFs) that suffices for our result, and establish its feasibility
based on one-way functions. Interestingly, we build on techniques from the SGC literature to construct
napPRFs with our desired efficiency, and then apply napPRFs back to SGC to obtain our main result.
Along the way, as an additional result of independent interest, we provide the first construction
of (adaptive) programmable PRFs for polynomial size domains based on one-way functions.

Introduction
Garbled circuits (GCs) [Yao86] are a fundamental primitive in cryptography. In Yao’s classical construction
based on one-way functions (OWFs), given a Boolean circuit C : {0, 1}I →{0, 1}, a garbler can generate a
garbled circuit bC together with short input encodings that associate garbled labels with each input wire.
Given bC and the labels corresponding to an input x ∈{0, 1}I, an evaluator can compute an output label that
decodes to C(x), while learning nothing beyond C(x). Standard instantiations of GCs have size O(|C| · λ)
and evaluation uses I λ-bit input labels, where λ is the security parameter.
While GCs provide a generic mechanism for securely evaluating circuits, many real-world computations
are more naturally expressed as programs with data-dependent control flow. Among these, a particularly
notable class is branching computation, where an intermediate step computes an n-bit selector α ∈{0, 1}n
and then executes one out of B = 2n candidate subcircuits (or branches). Concretely, let C0, . . . , CB−1 be
subcircuits, each of size C and with a common interface Cj : {0, 1}I →{0, 1}, and consider computing
y ←Cα(x). A naïve approach evaluates all B candidate circuits, then uses α to multiplex (i.e., select) the
output of circuit Cα. When garbled, this computation has size O(BCλ) bits.
Stacked garbling (SGC) [HK20a, HK21] provides smaller garbled encodings for this problem. At a
high level, SGC observes that the garbler can stack — by XOR-combining corresponding components —
the B garblings of C0, . . . , CB−1 into a single garbled artifact. The evaluator can then unstack locally once
it learns (obliviously) the randomness used for all but the active (i.e., the α-th) branch. Building on this
observation, [HK20a] achieves garbled size O(BIλ+Cλ) at the cost of O(B2) computation. The improved
scheme LogStack [HK21] retains the same size/communication profile, while reducing party computation
to O(B log B).
SGC eliminates the branch-duplication blowup — stacking reduces the garbled size for B branches
from O(BCλ) to O(Cλ) — but incurs an additive O(BIλ) term, intuitively the cost of privately routing
an I-bit input to the selected branch. Consequently, SGC is most beneficial when each branch performs
substantial computation relative to its input length. In contrast, when I is comparable to (or larger than1)
C, the improvement over the naïve approach becomes marginal and may even disappear.
This regime is indeed common in practice: many systems implement a large dispatch in which an n-bit
“mode” selector α chooses one of 2n simple behaviors, and the selected branch then performs only light
processing on a large input. Notable examples include network packet filtering, where α selects one of
many filtering rules and the system outputs accept/drop based on a few header fields while the payload may
be large. Similarly, in ML inference pipelines, a model index α selects one of many lightweight prediction
modules (e.g., a small linear classifier) applied to a large embedding.
This leaves open a natural question:
Can we retain SGC’s O(Cλ) benefit while decoupling the dependence on B from the input length I?
1.1
Our Results
We answer this affirmatively. Within the standard OWF-based (Yao) garbling framework, we give a new
SGC scheme with garbled size O(I log B · λ + Bλ + Cλ) and party computation O(B log B). This
asymptotically improves the prior state of the art LogStack [HK21], which has size O(BIλ + Cλ) with
the same computation.

Theorem 1 (Improved SGC, informal). Let C0, . . . , CB−1 be Boolean circuits of size C with a common
interface {0, 1}I →{0, 1}. There exists an SGC scheme for computing y ←Cα(x) from (black-box) OWFs
whose garbled size is O(I log B · λ + Bλ + Cλ), and computation of each party is O(B log B).
Our improvement stems from a new perspective on the O(BIλ) bottleneck in SGC. We show that the
relevant demultiplexing gadget (see next) can be cast as a one-point “programming” task closely related
to programmable PRFs [BLW17, BKM17, CC17, PS18], and that the SGC setting only needs a weaker non-
adaptive variant. This motivates a new primitive — non-adaptive programmable PRFs (napPRFs) — which
we formalize, construct from OWFs, and use to optimize SGC. We sketch this connection next, and defer a
more detailed overview to Section 2.
Demultiplexing as one-point programming.
The O(BIλ) overhead in prior SGC schemes comes
from the demultiplexer: a garbled gadget that, on a hidden selector α ∈{0, 1}n, must provide the evaluator
with the I input-wire labels for branch α, while producing dummy labels for every t ̸= α. These dummy
labels must (i) be independent of the real input x (so G can precompute the inactive-branch behavior) and
(ii) be indistinguishable from real GC labels (so the active branch remains hidden). Existing constructions
satisfy these constraints by essentially hard-coding B branch outcomes, incurring O(BIλ) size.
Non-adaptive programmable PRFs (napPRFs).
We observe that demultiplexing is naturally a one-point
programming task, reminiscent of (adaptive) programmable PRFs (apPRFs) [BLW17, BKM17, CC17, PS18].
An apPRF consists of a PRF family Fmsk(·) together with a programming procedure that, given (msk, α, β),
outputs a programmed key skα7→β defining a related function Fskα7→β(·). This function satisfies Fskα7→β(α) =
β, while for every t ̸= α we have Fskα7→β(t) ≡Fmsk(t), i.e., it behaves like the underlying PRF off the
programmed point. Moreover, when β is uniform, the programmed key should hide the programmed index
α (index-hiding). In the SGC setting, we take the payload to be the active-branch input-label vector, and the
off-point requirement corresponds to outputs that are independent of β and indistinguishable from real GC
labels.
Unfortunately, known index-hiding apPRFs rely on comparatively strong assumptions (e.g., LWE [CC17,
BTVW17, PS18] or iO [BLW17]), and their programming procedure is not obviously GC-friendly. For SGC,
however, full adaptive programmability is unnecessary: it suffices that off-point behavior is independent of
β (though it may depend on α), and that α is hidden when β is uniform. This motivates napPRFs, which
admit efficient OWF-based realizations and GC-friendly offline-online forms, and which we use to construct
SGC demultiplexers (Theorem 1).

Our technical contribution.
Motivated by the SGC application, we systematically study the napPRF
primitive. Our main technical contribution includes:
• A new primitive: napPRF. We formalize non-adaptive programmable PRFs (napPRFs), capturing
single-point programming with β-independent off-point behavior and index hiding under uniform
programming.
We also define an offline-online variant that decomposes the programmed key into an (α, β)-independent
offline key (which can be used as garbled material) and a short online key produced by the (garbled)
programming procedure.
We introduce suffix invariance, a correctness property needed for SGC.
• Black-box equivalence with DPFs. We establish a black-box equivalence between napPRFs and two-
party distributed point functions (DPFs), yielding OWF-based napPRFs for superpolynomial domains via
known DPF constructions [GI14, BGI15]. However, these generic instantiations do not suffice for our SGC
application: they lack suffix invariance or GC-friendly programming.
Theorem 2 (napPRF ⇔DPF, informal). A correct, index-hiding napPRF black-box implies a correct,
secure two-party DPF, and vice versa.
Corollary 1 (OWF-based DPF ⇒napPRF, informal). Assuming one-way functions (via black-box
use), there exists a correct and index-hiding napPRF over {0, 1}n →{0, 1}m for n ≤poly(λ). Moreover, its
programmed key has length nmλ + 2nm + mλ bits, and it is not suffix-invariant.
• Short online keys with suffix invariance from black-box OWFs. For polynomial-size domains,
we construct black-box OWF-based offline-online napPRFs with short online keys and built-in suffix
invariance.
Interestingly, our constructions are inspired by stacked garbling: the offline key is stackable, and the
online key consists solely of standard Yao garbled labels encoding (α, β), making the programming
procedure inherently GC-friendly.
Theorem 3 (Short-key offline-online napPRFs, informal). Assuming one-way functions (via black-box
use), there exists a correct and index-hiding offline-online napPRF over {0, 1}n →{0, 1}m for 2n ≤poly(λ).
Moreover, its offline programmed key has length 8nmλ+4n2λ−2nλ+m bits, whereas its online programmed
key has length (n + m)λ bits, and it is suffix-invariant.
• Improved stacked garbling. We instantiate LogStack’s demultiplexer using our offline-online napPRFs,
obtaining garbled size O(I log B · λ + Bλ + Cλ) while preserving LogStack’s O(B log B) computation
(cf. Theorem 1).
• apPRFs from black-box OWFs. As an additional result of independent interest, we show that pro-
grammable DPFs [BGIK22b] imply adaptive programmable PRFs; this yields the first OWF-based apPRF
construction (for poly-size domains). In contrast, prior apPRF constructions either target superpolynomial
domains under LWE [CC17, BTVW17, PS18] or iO [BLW17], or are OWF-based for polynomial domains
but incur a 1/poly(λ) security loss [GZSP25].
Theorem 4 (Programmable DPF ⇒apPRF, informal). Assuming one-way functions (via black-box
use), there exists a correct and index-hiding apPRF over {0, 1}n →{0, 1}m for 2n ≤poly(λ).

Related Work
Stacked garbling.
The technique of stacking for oblivious branching was introduced in [HK20b] in the GC-
ZK [JKO13] setting (i.e., privacy-free garbling), and later extended to standard Yao garbling in [HK20a, HK21].
It has since been applied and extended in several settings: [HKP20, GHAHJ22] extend it to MPC branch-
ing; [HHK+22, NK25] use it to build garbled PIR; [HKN24] uses it for garbled lookup tables; and [YPHK23]
applies it to build a constant-round 2PC system for RAM programs. We advance stacked garbling by
reducing the dominant garbled-size overhead that scales with the product of the number of branches and
the per-branch input length.
Adaptive programmable PRFs.
Programmable PRFs augment a standard PRF with a one-point program-
ming capability: from a key, one can derive a programmed key whose evaluation at a designated index α is
forced to a chosen value β, while evaluations on all other inputs remain unchanged [BW13, BGI14]. A cen-
tral refinement is index hiding (also called programming privacy, or privately programmable PRFs, PPPRFs),
which requires that the programmed key hide the programmed point α as long as the programmed value β
is uniform [BLW17, BKM17, CC17, PS18]. Without index hiding, programmable PRFs follow easily from
one-way functions via the classical puncturable PRF, essentially by embedding (α, β) into the punctured
key [BW13, BGI14, GGM86], yielding simple and concretely efficient schemes. In contrast, with program-
ming privacy, known constructions rely on substantially stronger assumptions — notably lattice-based
assumptions such as LWE [CC17, BTVW17, PS18] or indistinguishability obfuscation [BLW17] — and are
considerably more involved, typically layering homomorphic-encryption machinery. In this work, we use
“(adaptive) programmable PRF” to mean an index-hiding construction.
A recent attempt to obtain an OWF-based index-hiding construction for polynomial-size domains
appears in [GZSP25], but incurs a 1/poly(λ) security loss. While our focus is on non-adaptive programmable
PRFs, we also obtain the first adaptive OWF-based construction for polynomial-size domains without this
inverse-polynomial loss (cf. Section 5.4).
Distributed point functions.
The first two-party DPF construction from one-way functions was given
by Gilboa and Ishai [GI14], with subsequent works improving efficiency and concrete performance [BGI15,
BGI16, BGH+25]. In this work, we use the classical OWF-based two-party DPF constructions [GI14, BGI15]
as black boxes.
Beyond the two-party setting, recent work [GWW25] gives the first OWF-based multiparty DPF con-
struction. Earlier works from public-key assumptions (e.g., DCR and LPN) achieve succinct communication
for a fixed number of parties [CK24], later extended to arbitrarily many parties [ARS24, CKY25]. Separately,
[BGIK22a] studies information-theoretic DPFs for three and four parties tolerating one corruption.

Technical Overview
We present our techniques in sufficient detail to understand our contribution. Let X := {0, 1}n denote a
domain and let Y := {0, 1}m denote a range. We often refer to elements α ∈X as indexes or points, and to
elements β ∈Y as values.
2.1
Non-adaptive Programmable PRFs
A non-adaptive programmable PRF (napPRF) consists of three PPT procedures:
4

• KeyGen samples a master key msk;
• Prog takes as input a master key msk, an index α ∈X, and a value β ∈Y. It outputs a programmed key
sk on input (msk, α, β), where intuitively sk’s index α is programmed to output β.
• Eval takes as input a programmed key sk, a point x ∈X and outputs a value.
It satisfies the following informal correctness and security guarantees:
• On-point correctness: Eval(sk, α) = β, i.e., the evaluation at α outputs β.
• Off-point value independence (correctness): For every unprogrammed point x ̸= α, all β1, β2,
Eval(Prog(msk, α, β1), x) = Eval(Prog(msk, α, β2), x), i.e., at x ̸= α, the evaluation output is fixed by
α (and msk), but not by β.
• Index-hiding security: For uniform β, the programmed key sk hides α.
We will later prove that these guarantees already imply that for any fixed α and uniform β, the induced
function x 7→Eval(Prog(msk, α, β), x) is a PRF.
Offline-online napPRF.
We also consider an offline-online variant of napPRFs, where the programmed
key sk splits as (skoff, skon). Here the offline key skoff is sampled by KeyGen and is independent of (α, β),
while the online key skon is the (α, β)-dependent part produced by Prog. That is, KeyGen(1λ) outputs
(msk, skoff), Prog(msk, α, β) outputs skon, and Eval takes skoff, skon, x as inputs.
This variant’s correctness and security notions carry over verbatim. This split is w.l.o.g.: any napPRF is
offline-online by setting skoff = ⊥, and any offline-online napPRF is a standard napPRF by folding skoff
into msk.
The main motivation for considering this variant is online efficiency: skoff, which may be large, can be
transmitted before (α, β) is known. Then in the online phase, once (α, β) is fixed, only the short key skon
needs to be transmitted.
Key metrics.
We measure efficiency mainly by (i) the total programmed-key size |sk| and (ii) the online
part |skon|. We require |sk| ≤poly(n, m, λ), and call an offline-online napPRF short-key if |skon| is linear in
n+m (up to poly(λ) factors).
Difference from adaptive programmable PRFs.
An apPRF (see, e.g., [PS18, GZSP25]) has a single
master function Fmsk(x) (fixed by msk) that programming must preserve: for any programmed point
(α, β), evaluation agrees with F on all x ̸= α. In contrast, a napPRF only guarantees β-invariance off the
programmed point: for fixed α, changing β does not affect outputs on x ̸= α, but those off-point outputs
may still depend arbitrarily on α. Thus, apPRF correctness enforces cross-α consistency, while a generic
napPRF need not.
Suffix invariance.
We also introduce a structural correctness property, suffix invariance, needed for
stacked garbling applications. For any off-point query x ̸= α, let ℓbe the first position where bitstrings
x and α differ. Then Eval(sk, x) may depend on α only in terms of the first ℓbits of α. In other words,
evaluation Eval(sk, x) is invariant under changes to the suffix αℓ+1..n. Hence, for any fixed x, as α ranges
over {0, 1}n \ {x}, there are only n possible off-point outputs (one per ℓ), rather than 2n.

2.2
Stacked Garbling: A Concise Overview
Stacked garbling (SGC) [HK20a, HK21] optimizes oblivious branching: a computation selects one of B = 2n
subcircuits C0, . . . , CB−1 (with B ≤poly(λ)), where each Cj : {0, 1}I →{0, 1} has C gates. We consider
subcircuits with one-bit output for simplicity. The evaluator E holds labels encoding a selector α ∈{0, 1}n
and input labels encoding x ∈{0, 1}I (possibly arising as intermediate values), and must get output label
for Cα(x). Naïvely garbling all branches costs O(BCλ).
Heath and Kolesnikov [HK20a] show that the garbler G can stack the B garblings into a single artifact
by simply XORing them; later, E can unstack locally once it obliviously learns seed randomness for all but
the active branch Cα. This yields garbled size O(BIλ+Cλ), with the computation improved to O(B log B)
in [HK21].
The O(BIλ) term comes from a garbling gadget called the demultiplexer. Unstacking produces
B candidate branch GCs, only one of which (the α-th) is well-formed, and E cannot tell which. To
evaluate candidate branch t, E must also obtain the corresponding I input-wire labels for that branch. The
demultiplexer provides these labels: on the active branch it outputs the real labels encoding x, and on
inactive branches it outputs dummy labels.
These dummy labels must satisfy two constraints: (i) they must be independent of x (since G does not
know x but must precompute/merge the inactive-branch behavior), and (ii) they must be indistinguishable
from fresh GC labels, so E learns nothing about which branch is active. Prior schemes implement this by
naïvely hard-coding B branch outcomes, each consisting of O(I) λ-bit labels.
2.3
Demultiplexers from napPRFs for Stacked Garbling
We outline how an (offline-online) napPRF yields the demultiplexer gadget at the heart of SGC. Conceptually,
the demultiplexer enables E to derive a candidate input-wire label vector for every branch. On the active
branch, this candidate must equal the true input-wire labels consistent with the garbled input; on every
inactive branch, it should look like an unrelated dummy label vector, independent of the garbled input and
indistinguishable from the active one.
• To start with, a direct approach — used by [HK20a, HK21] — would communicate input-wire labels
separately for each branch (e.g., via a per-branch garbled selection table). With B = 2n branches and
input length I, this incurs Θ(BI) overhead — precisely the cost we seek to remove.
• A tempting but insufficient compression attempt. One might hope to generate all dummy label
vectors from a short PRF key, so E can derive them locally. However, this runs into an immediate obstacle:
on the active branch, the correct labels necessarily depend on the garbled input, so they cannot be fixed
independently of the input. Still, this attempt is instructive: on every inactive branch, the PRF output
looks pseudorandom (hence indistinguishable from the active branch) and is independent of the garbled
input.
• Programmability resolves the obstacle. We instead use a programmable PRF: we generate a key that is
“patched” at exactly one point so that its output equals the correct input-wire labels on the active branch,
6

while its outputs on all other branches remain pseudorandom. Crucially, the programmed key hides the
patched point and hence does not reveal the active branch identity.
• Non-adaptive programmability is sufficient. At this point, a full programmable PRF would impose
more structure than SGC needs. In SGC, inactive-branch label vectors can depend on which branch is
active; we only require that they be independent of the garbled input. That is, once the active branch is
fixed, changing the programmed output should not affect the PRF output on any other branch — this is
exactly a napPRF.
• Garbled programmer gadget. Programming requires (a) the identity of the active branch and (b) the
input-wire labels for that branch. In our setting, neither party can simply write these values down in the
clear; they are available only as garbled labels. Our solution is to realize the programming procedure
itself as a small GC (a “programmer gadget”) that takes the selector labels together with the input-wire
labels and outputs a programmed key.
• Offline-online napPRF. In particular, we use an offline-online napPRF so that the programmed key
decomposes into an offline part that is independent of both the active branch and the input, and a short
online part that depends on them. The garbled programmer gadget outputs only the online part, while
the offline part can be sent in the clear. This is strictly beneficial: outputting a long offline key from a
garbled circuit would bloat the gadget (one output wire per bit), yet hiding it buys no security.
• Two remaining challenges. We have two further major challenges.
1. Garbling the napPRF programming procedure would potentially lead to non-black use of the underlying
cryptographic primitives (including OWFs): something we are trying to avoid.
2. At first glance, the programmer gadget seems to need access to the input-wire labels of GC for every
branch, since it is not known in advance which branch will be programmed. Storing these labels
explicitly would make the gadget (and hence the garbling) too large, essentially reintroducing a Θ(BI)
cost. A natural workaround is to generate all per-branch label vectors from a short PRG seed, and
have the gadget store only this seed. However, this pushes the burden into the gadget: it must now
expand the PRG (and thus rely on its internal structure) inside the garbled circuit, which is exactly the
kind of non-black-box use we seek to avoid.
We next preview how to address the remaining challenges; but even before doing so, if Prog has small
Boolean circuit complexity (polynomial in n, m, λ, where n = log B and m = Iλ here), this blueprint
yields an SGC demultiplexer of garbled size poly(log B, I, λ), already improving over prior O(BIλ)-size
demultiplexers.
Resolving the challenges via our offline-online napPRFs.
In this work, we construct a short-key (and
suffix-invariant) offline-online napPRF over a polynomial-size domain from OWFs in a black-box way. In our
construction, Prog essentially selects among n + m standard Yao label pairs, so Challenge 1 can be handled
using standard garbled truth tables (black-box in OWFs). Plugging this napPRF into the blueprint yields an
SGC demultiplexer of garbled size O(nI · poly(λ) + n2λ), and total SGC size O(nI · poly(λ) + Bλ + Cλ),
where the O(Bλ) term is inherited from SGC’s oblivious seed-delivery gadget. We overview this napPRF
construction in Section 2.5.
We further tailor this offline-online napPRF to SGC by coupling its internal GGM/PRG expansions with
SGC’s label generation, so the programmed value need not be materialized as an explicit Iλ-bit string inside
7

a garbled circuit. That is, the tailored napPRF directly outputs GC labels (e.g., when I = 1, programming
(α, 0) makes Eval(sk, α) output the actual SGC λ-bit wire label encoding 0 on branch α, and similarly for
1). This makes Challenge 2 fully black-box with respect to OWFs as well, yielding an SGC demultiplexer of
garbled size O(nIλ + Bλ) and total SGC size O(nIλ + Bλ + Cλ).
Toward preserving state-of-the-art SGC computation complexity.
Beyond reducing garbled size, we
also preserve LogStack’s O(B log B) computation for both parties [HK21]. This relies on our offline-online
napPRFs as follows.
• Garbler. The garbler runs KeyGen, incurring O(B) computation.
• Evaluator. The evaluator requires outputs for all t ∈{0, 1}n; rather than B = 2n separate calls to Eval,
our napPRFs support a FullEval procedure that evaluates the napPRF on the entire domain in O(B log B)
time.
• Suffix invariance. We remark that suffix invariance is crucial for preserving LogStack’s O(B log B)
computation. At a high level, during a subsequent garbage collection step, the garbler must predict, for
each branch t ∈{0, 1}n, the dummy output that E obtains on that (inactive) branch in order to combine
B candidate outputs into a single garbled label. Without suffix invariance, the dummy output on a fixed t
could vary with the full identity of α, yielding up to B possibilities per branch and total work O(B2) —
the bottleneck in the original SGC scheme [HK20a]. Suffix invariance restricts this dependence to only
O(n) possible dummy outcomes, so the total work is O(B log B).
See Section 7 for full details. The remainder of this section focuses on constructing various (offline-online)
napPRFs.
2.4
Black-box Equivalence between napPRFs and Two-party DPFs
Recall that a two-party DPF encodes a point function Pα,γ : X →Y — which outputs γ on input α and
0m elsewhere — into two short keys (k1, k2). Given kb and x, party b ∈{1, 2} locally outputs yb such that
y1 ⊕y2 = Pα,γ(x), while each individual key hides (α, γ).
It is easy to see that a napPRF implies a two-party DPF in a black-box way.
Here, we focus on the converse direction. Consider Y = {0, 1} and a DPF key pair (k1, k2) ←
Gen(1λ, α, 1). By DPF correctness, the two local DPF evaluations coincide at every x ̸= α, while at x = α
they differ by 1 (i.e., one evaluates to 0 and the other to 1). To obtain a correct napPRF (including off-point
correctness), it suffices to output as the programmed key whichever of k1 and k2 evaluates to β at α. This
DPF-based trick echoes the one-point equivocation underlying somewhere equivocal encryption [HJO+16],
and we draw inspiration from it.
This approach ensures index-hiding security for the induced napPRF. At a high level, when β
$←{0, 1}
is uniform, Prog outputs k1 or k2 with probability exactly 1/2 each. By DPF security, each share distribution
is simulatable without α, and hence so is their half-half mixture.
To support range {0, 1}m for any m, we simply repeat this procedure independently for each output
bit and concatenate the resulting keys.
See Section 5 for formal details of this equivalence. Instantiating it with the well-known OWF-based DPF
constructions [GI14, BGI15] yields a napPRF from black-box OWFs with total key size nmλ+2nm+mλ
bits. However, this particular OWF-based instantiation has two drawbacks:
8

1. No meaningful offline-online split. The programmed key is generated entirely as a function of
(α, β), i.e., there is no nontrivial (α, β)-independent offline component (and the total key size scales
with nm).
2. Missing suffix invariance. The induced napPRF does not satisfy suffix invariance: off-point behavior
depends on the full identity of α.
These limitations persist for the constructions of [GI14, BGI15] absent substantial modifications. Intuitively,
the so-called correction words underlying these constructions depend on α, so the resulting key structure
varies with α.
Finally, we highlight an additional result of independent interest. In Section 5.4, we demonstrate that
programmable DPFs [BGIK22b] imply adaptive programmable PRFs via the same black-box reduction,
giving the first OWF-based construction (for polynomial-size domains) of apPRFs.2
2.5
Constructing Short-key Offline-online napPRFs
We discuss how to construct short-key offline-online napPRFs. If we allow non-black-box techniques, there
is a straightforward compiler: given any napPRF Π = (KeyGen, Prog, Eval) whose Prog has small Boolean
circuit complexity, we garble Prog [Yao86] and place the garbling in skoff, so that skon consists only of the
input labels for (α, β) and has size |skon| = (n + m)λ.
However, this generic compilation is not ideal. First, it is inherently non-black-box: when instantiated
from OWF-based (DPF-induced) napPRFs, it requires garbling large subcircuits (notably the O(nm) PRG
expansions), leading to substantial overhead in skoff. Second, this compilation does not endow suffix
invariance: if the underlying napPRF lacks it, so does the compiled one.
To this end, we give a nontrivial construction of short-key and suffix-invariant offline-online napPRFs
over polynomial-size domains from black-box OWFs. Interestingly, this construction is inspired by the
stacked garbling technique.
A warm-up: extending the domain by one bit.
Before presenting the full construction, it is helpful to
isolate an instructive subproblem. Suppose we have an offline-online napPRF Π(k) : {0, 1}k →{0, 1}m
whose online key consists of only k+m Yao labels (e.g., as obtained by the generic garbling-based compiler).
Can we construct an offline-online napPRF Π(k+1) : {0, 1}k+1 →{0, 1}m — i.e., extend the domain by one
bit — treating Π(k) as a black-box building block?
Here is a natural first (but flawed) attempt. The offline key simply contains two independent copies of
the k-bit napPRF’s offline key, one meant for inputs whose first bit is 0 and the other for inputs whose first
bit is 1.
To program a point α = α1∥α′ with value β, we proceed as follows. If α1 = 0, we run the k-bit
programming procedure on the “0-copy” at index α′ with value β and take the resulting k+m labels as the
online key. If α1 = 1, we do the same but on the “1-copy.” In either case, the online key consists of exactly
k+m garbled labels; the only difference is which copy they belong to.
To evaluate at an input x = x1∥x′, we route to the corresponding copy: we run the k-bit evaluation
procedure using the offline key of the x1-copy, together with the online key and the suffix x′.
It is easy to see that this attempt satisfies on-point correctness: when x = α, the output of the (routed)
k-bit evaluation procedure is exactly β.

This attempt fails as it only partially satisfies off-point value independence. To see this, w.l.o.g. assume
α = 0k+1, so programming is performed on the 0-copy at point α′ = 0k. When x = 0∥x′, we do achieve

invokes the 1-copy but is given an ill-formed online key for that copy. Indeed, the online key consists
of labels produced for the 0-copy and varies with β, so we cannot appeal to the k-bit napPRF off-point
correctness of the 1-copy.
This suggests a simple patch. During the (k+1)-bit programming procedure, we additionally include a
uniformly sampled dummy online key for the α1-copy (i.e., k+m random garbled labels). The online key
now contains two parts, one for each copy, and evaluation routes to the appropriate part. Crucially, when
evaluation goes into the α1-copy, its online key is independent of β.
We briefly examine the index-hiding security of this (k+1)-bit construction. Both components hide
α′: the real one by the index-hiding of Π(k), and the dummy one by uniformity. Thus, the only remaining
potential leakage is α1, via distinguishing which online key is real. Ensuring this indistinguishability
depends on how we instantiate Π(k), so we defer it to the end.
Garble-then-recurse.
Given a (k+1)-bit offline-online napPRF, the natural next step is to extend the
domain further to (k+2) bits by applying the above idea once more. However, an immediate issue arises:
the (k+1)-bit online key already contains two components (one per copy), and thus has size proportional
to 2(k+m). Repeating the same transformation would double the number of components again, causing
the online key to grow exponentially with the recursion depth. (A similar issue arises for the offline key;
we address it shortly.)
The fix is simple: we further apply the garbling-based compiler idea to the (k+1)-bit construction,
thereby compressing its online key. Concretely, we represent the (k+1)-bit programming procedure itself
as a small garbled circuit stored in the offline key, so that the online key consists only of the input labels for
(α, β) and has size ((k+1) + m)λ. More explicitly, we proceed as follows:
• In the master key, sample (k+1) + m Yao label pairs corresponding to the bits of α ∈{0, 1}k+1 and
β ∈{0, 1}m. The online key is simply the selection of one label per bit, determined by (α, β).
• In the offline key, include a garbling of the “programmer gadget” implementing the (k+1)-bit program-
ming procedure described above. Given the input labels for (α, β), this garbled circuit outputs the two
internal online keys (one real and one dummy) for the two k-bit copies.

4.1
Non-adaptive vs. Adaptive Programmable PRFs
We clarify the distinction between napPRFs and apPRFs (cf. Section 3.4).
Indeed, any apPRF ΠapPRF = (KeyGen, MEval, Prog, Eval) that is correct and index-hiding immediately
yields a (standard) napPRF in a black-box way by simply dropping the master evaluation algorithm MEval.
Correctness and index hiding of ΠapPRF immediately carry over.
Note that Theorem 5 also applies to the napPRF induced from an apPRF as above. In particular, under
this embedding, we have ΠnapPRF.Eval ≡ΠapPRF.Eval. This implies that for an apPRF, correctness together
with index hiding already suffices to derive PRF security under uniform programming (cf. Section 3.4).
We now consider the converse direction and explain why, in general, a correct and index-hiding
napPRF does not yield a correct and index-hiding apPRF via a black-box transformation. Interestingly, the
obstruction is not security but correctness: an apPRF requires the existence of a single “master” evaluation
procedure (i.e., MEval) that is preserved under programming.
To see this, recall that in an apPRF we have a master function F(x) := ΠapPRF.MEval(msk, x) such
that programming at (α, β) produces a key sk whose induced oracle agrees with F everywhere except at
α: ∀x ̸= α, ΠapPRF.Eval(sk, x) = F(x). That is, the off-point value is independent of both α and β.
16

In contrast, napPRF correctness guarantees only off-point invariance with respect to the programmed
value: For every fixed α and all β, β′, the evaluations on x ̸= α are identical, but they may still depend
arbitrarily on α. That is, while a napPRF can ensure that changing β does not affect off-point outputs,
it does not enforce that programming different indices α’s is consistent with a single underlying master
function. This missing cross-α consistency is exactly what prevents a generic black-box construction of an
apPRF from a napPRF.
