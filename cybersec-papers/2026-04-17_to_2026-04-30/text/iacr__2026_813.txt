Abstract
We present a new framework for constructing practically ef-
ficient publicly verifiable secret sharing (PVSS) with non-
interactive dealers, in that the dealer may go offline after
sending a single message, and is not involved in the share ver-
ification process. We use identity-based encryption (IBE) and
commitments as the main ingredients and avoid expensive
zero-knowledge proofs. Instantiating them with post-quantum
secure schemes, a lattice-based IBE and a hash-based commit-
ment, we obtain our first construction – a post-quantum secure
PVSS with non-interactive dealers that outperform the prior
lattice-based practical construction, Gentry et al. [Eurocrypt
2022] by two orders of magnitude.
However, to enable the aggregation of PVSS transcripts
(which facilitates many additional applications such as se-
cure voting), we propose our second construction by replac-
ing hash-based commitments with Pedersen’s homomorphic
commitments. While this does not achieve full-fledged post-
quantum security (as Pedersen’s scheme is not quantum safe),
it still provides privacy against a post-quantum adversary. We
prove the security of this construction in a new model, which
we call long-lasting security. This model guarantees that the
protocol is secure in the present (pre-quantum era) while
maintaining privacy in the long term (post-quantum era). This
new model is of independent interest for constructing efficient
schemes that are resilient to harvest-now-decrypt-later line of
attacks. In this model, we propose a blockchain-compatible
secure voting scheme using our PVSS scheme.
Our PVSS schemes demonstrate practical efficiency: our
post-quantum PVSS shares a secret among 1024 receivers in
692 ms and verifies the dealing in 128 ms, and communicates
4MB, overall yielding a two orders of magnitude improve-
ment over the state of the art [Gentry et al., Eurocrypt 2022].

Introduction
Publicly verifiable secret sharing (PVSS) schemes [33] en-
able a dealer to share a secret s among n receivers (typically
using Shamir’s secret sharing [56]) in a publicly verifiable
manner, such that anyone, even outside the system, can verify
that the sharing has been done correctly. Any t + 1 (t < n)
receivers can reconstruct the secret, while it stays hidden from
any (possibly colluding) subset of t parties. PVSS has found
numerous applications in the security space, including dis-
tributed key generation [11], threshold cryptosystems [30],
secure voting [55], private auctions [59], and secure random-
ness on blockchain [46]. In particular, the public verifiability
feature has emerged as one of the most crucial requirements
for blockchain-based systems as it allows any potential user
to ascertain the system’s integrity.
In PVSS applications such as secure voting or secure auc-
tions on blockchain, the protocol transcript is stored forever
on the blockchain. In such cases, post-quantum security is
crucial due to the ease of executing harvest-now-decrypt-later
attack [15,16,31]1 on the transcript. For example, votes cast
now privately, if they get leaked in a decade with a plausible
quantum attack, that could have significant repercussions for
voters – such a possibility poses a pragmatic resistance for
large-scale adaptability of any such applications today.
Moreover, for many client-server applications, including
those that can be built over blockchains, such as ZK-as-a-
service [35], MPC-as-a-service [14, 58, 60], private statis-
tics [8] etc., it is important to keep the dealer/client non-
interactive – the dealer posts/sends its message, and then can
go offline. In particular, in order to produce a (publicly verifi-
able) correctness proof, the dealer should not be involved at
all. We say that such PVSS has a non-interactive dealer.

With growing applications in the blockchain space, a few
PVSS schemes has been proposed recently [24,27,40,43,45].
However, [4, 49] 2 are two proposals that are both post-
quantum secure and have a non-interactive dealer follow
a standard paradigm 3: (i) given individual public keys for
every receiver, the dealer encrypts their corresponding share
with them plus also commits to the actual secret s; (ii) along
with that it sends a non-interactive zero knowledge (NIZK)
proof of correctness attesting to the fact that the encryptions
indeed contains a t out of n Shamir shairng of the commit-
ted s. Clearly, as long as all the ingredients, namely, encryp-
tion, commitments, and NIZK are post-quantum secure, the
scheme satisfies post-quantum security. Furthermore, the pres-
ence of NIZK ensures that dealer’s message is already pub-
licly verifiable without any additional step, thereby achieving
the dealer’s non-interactivity feature. Unfortunately, the final
scheme incurs substantial overhead due to the use of post-
quantum secure (lattice-based) NIZK proofs. This motivates
us to ask the following question: p
Can we construct a practically-efficient post-quantum secure
PVSS with a non-interactive dealer without expensive NIZK?
We answer the question in the affirmative by proposing a new
approach to obtaining PVSS with non-interactive dealers.
1.1
Our Contributions
A new approach to PVSS with non-interactive dealer: We
use a post-quantum secure identity-based encryption scheme
(IBE) as the core tool. Each receiver-i publishes an individual
master IBE public key mpki for which they store a master
secret key mski. The dealer posts a commitment to each re-
ceiver’s share JsKi as Com(si), and encrypts each receiver’s
share JsKi (and opening randomness) under index i and the
dealer’s identity idd using mpki and then goes offline. The
receivers check that the posted commitments commit to a
degree t sharing of a polynomial. Then the i-th receiver, once
decrypts the share and opening randomness, checks with the
commitment Com(JsKi) whether the share it obtained is cor-
rect – if not, then the receiver uses mski to extracts dealer’s
decryption key ski,idd – using this, anyone (even someone
who is neither the dealer nor a receiver) can decrypt the ci-
phertext and can verify whether the receiver’s claim is correct
by matching with the commitment. Firstly, this clearly does
not involve a NIZK proof; and secondly, the IBE enables the
dealer to remain non-interactive, as the receiver can extract
the dealer’s decryption key locally using her master secret key
mski.
Practically efficient PQ secure PVSS with non-interactive
dealer. In our first construction, we use a lattice-based

1.2
Related Works
We compare our PQ-secure and long-lasting secure PVSS
protocols with existing PVSS protocols from the literature. A
qualitative comparison can be found in Table. 1.
Discrete Log-based protocols: In traditional discrete log-
based protocols [26, 33, 36, 50, 55], the dealer shares a se-
cret using Shamir’s scheme, publishing commitments and
encrypted shares. Some protocols [36, 50] allow receivers
to complain about inconsistent shares, requiring dealer inter-
action for resolution. These schemes are not publicly verifi-
able unless receivers add an extra multisignature round. The
works of [26,33,55] make the dealer non-interactive by pub-
lishing NIZK proofs that each encrypted share matches the
secret polynomial. While verifiable, these protocols are not
post-quantum secure due to reliance on DL, and replacing
DL-based components with lattice-based ones is impractical
due to the high cost of lattice-based NIZK proofs, preventing
efficient post-quantum or long-lasting security.
Class-group-based protocols: The works of [24, 43] con-
struct non-interactive PVSS using class group encryptions,
with NIZK proofs ensuring correct encryption. Receivers ver-
ify the proofs and decrypt their shares. However, class group
encryptions are not known to be post-quantum secure, and
the NIZKs are customized for class group encryptions; hence,
long-lasting secure protocols are hard to construct.
Lattice-based VSS protocols: The recent work of [4] con-
structed a post-quantum non-interactive PVSS from vector
commitments that satisfy linear opening, functional hiding,
and NIZK proof of smallness properties, and an encryption
scheme that supports efficient proofs of correct decryption.
Concurrently, the work of [49] constructed a non-interactive
PVSS from an encryption and a NIZK proof system for gap
language. However, the practicality of these works is unclear
as they lack a concrete implementation and both of them rely
on expensive NIZK proofs over lattices. The work of Gentry
et al. [37] designed a PVSS scheme that uses an LWE-based
encryption scheme and uses DL-based Bulletproofs [20] to
prove that the ciphertext is well-formed. Using Bulletproofs
improved efficiency over the pure lattice-based solutions, but
it failed to provide practical efficiency, as it takes around 34
minutes for the dealer to deal a single secret among 1024
receivers, whereas our work performs the dealing and veri-
fication in less than a second, yielding over three orders of
magnitude improvement. Moreover, due to reliance on Bullet-
proofs, the PVSS of [37] achieves long-lasting security and
not post-quantum security.
Hash-based protocols: The work of [10] introduced an effi-
cient hash-based VSS where the dealer non-interactively dis-
tributes shares using hash-based distributed ZK proofs. How-
ever, during share verification, receivers can file complaints,
requiring the dealer to reveal corresponding shares—making
the protocol interactive and its transcript not publicly ver-
ifiable. A later improvement by [13] proposed a general
framework for computational VSS in the honest-majority
setting using Shamir secret sharing, random oracles, and non-
homomorphic commitments. Yet, both [10] and the post-
quantum secure variants of [13] require dealer interaction
in the verification phase. In contrast, our post-quantum secure
PVSS removes this interaction by leveraging IBE and thereby
enables many new applications.
Bivariate-Polynomial Based: The works of [5, 7, 47] con-
struct asynchronous VSS using bivariate polynomials, where
the dealer commits to a random low-degree polynomial and
distributes partial evaluations with proofs to nodes, who then
collaborate to reconstruct shares. These protocols avoid PKI
but require O(n2) exponentiations and communication, and
rely on q-SDH commitments, making them insecure against
quantum adversaries. Long-lasting security and public verifi-
ability are possible from them using perfectly hiding commit-
ments, lattice-based encryption for channels, and multisigna-
ture rounds, though at the cost of higher dealer computation.
Signature-based VSS protocols: The recent work by [27]
constructs an asynchronous dual threshold VSS protocol us-
ing Polynomial commitments [44] and signatures where the
dealer shares the secret among n receivers and waits for signed
confirmations from the receivers. Once the dealer receives
confirmations from t +1 receivers it broadcasts the shares of
the rest of the n−t −1 receivers and the t +1 signatures. This
enables efficient reconstruction later on. However, their dealer
is interactive in nature. We observe that this VSS protocol
can be made long-lasting secure (albeit with an interactive
dealer) if the underlying Polynomial commitment is instan-
tiated using the Pedersen variant of KZG commitment, as it
provides computational binding and perfect hiding, and the
point-to-point channels are replaced by a PQ-secure public
key encryption.
Related works of Long-lasting Security and Voting:
The
notion of long-lasting security is closely related to everlast-
ing security [61]. Everlasting security requires secure quan-
tum channels whereas long-lasting secure protocols can be
deployed using existing post-quantum infrastructure, hence,
long-lasting is more realistic security notion. Our long-lasting
secure voting protocol provides vote privacy against future
quantum adversaries. We provide a detailed comparison with
other relevant voting protocols in Appendix. A.

Technical Overview
A popular line of PVSS protocols with non-interactive deal-
ers follow the commit–encrypt–prove paradigm: the dealer
commits to a polynomial f(x) with secret s := f(0), encrypts
each share JsKi := f(i) under the receivers’ public keys, and
provides a NIZK proof that each encrypted f(i) is an evalua-
tion of the committed polynomial f(x). Classical DL-based
approaches [26,33,36,50,55] yield highly efficient schemes
using group homomorphisms but are vulnerable to quan-
tum attacks via Shor’s algorithm [57]. Lattice-based PVSS
schemes [4,49] in this paradigm offer post-quantum security
but rely on costly lattice-based NIZKs, making them compu-
tationally expensive.
Another line of work [5, 7, 47] explores dealing the secret
using a bivariate polynomial so that the receivers error-correct
the shares without dealer interaction. However, this approach
incurs quadratic communication overhead for the dealer.
We explore a third paradigm [54] where the dealer neither
incurs the computation overhead of an NIZK nor does it incur
the quadratic communication overhead of sharing a bivariate
polynomial. We observe that in the commit-encrypt-prove
paradigm the NIZK proof can be broken down into two parts:
(i) The commitment to secret s and the commitment to the
shares JsKi lie on a committed t-degree polynomial, and
(ii) The committed shares are indeed the same as the en-
crypted values under each receiver’s public key.
The first statement can be performed quite efficiently, e.g.
using a non-interactive threshold ZK proofs [10] if the com-
mitment is non-homomorphic, or using a SCRAPE test [23]
if the commitment is additively homomorphic. The second
statement is expensive to prove because the NIZK uses the en-
cryption and commitment in a non-black-box fashion. There-
fore, one key idea in our approach is to defer this verification
to a later phase, which does not involve the dealer, and yet
achieves public verifiability.
One-Time Public Key Encryption Approach: The high-level
intuition is that the dealer encrypts the share and the decom-
mitment and posts these encryptions and commitments on a
public bulletin board or a blockchain, denoted by FBC. The
receivers read the commitments on FBC and check that the
committed shares indeed lie on a t-degree polynomial. Then,
each i-th receiver decrypts its share (and opening randomness)
from the i-th encryption. If the decrypted values is not a cor-
rect opening to the i-th committed share, then the i-th receiver
simply reveals their secret key on FBC. Anyone can publicly
decrypt the dealer-posted i-th encryption using the this de-
cryption key, and verify the validity of the i-th receiver’s claim.
This avoids using NIZK for step-(ii): encrypted share and the
committed share is the same.
Non-Interactive Key Exchange Approach: Leaking the re-
ceiver’s long-term secret key is non-scalable, requiring fresh
public-key setup for each incident and preventing key reuse
across applications. Prior work like Eth-DKG [54] uses Diffie-
Hellman key exchange: the dealer and each receiver main-
tain DH public keys, enabling pairwise secret key establish-
ment for encrypting shares and opening randomness. For com-
plaints, receivers reveal the pairwise secret and provide effi-
cient Chaum-Pedersen [25] NIZK proofs of correctness. Pub-
lic complaint verification involves checking the NIZK proof,
decrypting the share/randomness, and validating against the
commitment. This approach is limited to discrete-log settings
due to cheap NIZK proofs. Extension to lattice-based key ex-
change [34] is prohibitively expensive: efficient NIZK proofs
for the required relations are unknown, and millisecond-level
lattice-based decryption proofs remain unavailable.
Our Protocol: We circumvent the need for a lattice-based
NIZK by replacing the public key encryption with an IBE [6,
32] – we use a lattice-based construction to obtain post-
quantum security. In a one-time setup phase, each i-th receiver
4

publishes an individual master IBE public key mpki for which
they store a master secret key mski. The dealer commits to
the shares and the secret, and encrypts the openings to the
i-th shares under each mpki and the dealer’s identity idd. The
dealer posts these commitments and encryptions, and then
goes offline. The receivers check that the posted commitments
commit to a degree t sharing of a polynomial. Then, each i-
th receiver checks that the i-th encrypted values is indeed a
valid decommitment to the i-th commitment – if not, then the
receiver uses mski to extract dealer’s decryption key ski,idd
and post it on FBC. It takes < 5ms to generate identity secret
key. Anyone (even someone who is neither the dealer nor a
receiver) can decrypt the i-th ciphertext using ski,idd and ver-
ify whether the receiver’s claim is correct or not by matching
the decrypted values with the commitment – this ensures pub-
lic verifiability. Decrypting the IBE ciphertext takes < 1ms.
This approach yields two orders of magnitude improvement
in overall runtime, while maintaining crucial properties such
as public verifiability and non-interactive dealers. Concretely,
our dealer deals a secret among 1000 receivers in < 1 sec.

encryption. We note that a malicious receiver cannot frame an

Concrete Instantiations: To complete our protocol, we need a
PVSS scheme with a non-interactive dealer that allows ver-
ifying that the committed shares correspond to a t-degree
sharing of a polynomial. Our post-quantum secure instantia-
tion consists of the hash-based VSS protocol of [10]. In their
PVSS, once the dealing is done, the dealer uses a distributed
ZK proof (DZK) to prove that the committed shares lie on a
t-degree polynomial, and in case, there is a complaint by a re-
ceiver the dealer broadcasts the opening to the receiver’s share.
We note that it suffices for us that the committed shares are
partially verified as the IBE allows a receiver to prove later on
that the dealer dealt bad shares, while removing the dealer in-
teraction during the share verification phase. The DZK proofs
are instantiated using hashes, modeled as a random oracle.
For our long-lasting secure instantiation, we use a variant
of Pedersen PVSS [50] where the dealer commits to the se-
cret and the shares using (homomorphic) Pedersen commit-
ments. Due to homomorphism, the degree-check (part-(i) of
the proof) is possible using a so-called SCRAPE test (de-
scribed in Appendix. B.1). The rest of the protocol stays unal-
tered: the receiver outputs the identity secret key in case the
encrypted share does not match the committed share. Finally,
Pedersen’s unconditional hiding along with the post-quantum
CPA-security of the IBE scheme yield long-lasting security
by ensuring the post-quantum privacy of the secret.

the following probabilistic polynomial-time algorithms. We
also define an additional algorithm VerKey to verify a secret
key skID corresponding to an identity ID.
• KeyGen(1λ) →(mpk,msk): Outputs a master public key
mpk and a master secret key msk.
• Extract(msk,ID) →skID: Outputs the private key skID
corresponding to identity ID.

2.2
Shamir’s Secret Sharing Scheme
We recall the definition of an (n,t) Shamir secret sharing
scheme [56] as follows:
Definition 2.1 (Shamir’s Secret Sharing Scheme). Let F be a
finite field. It consists of two algorithms:

Security Model and Security Definitions
3.1
Definition: Publicly Verifiable Secret Shar-
ing with Non-interactive Dealer
A (n,t) Verifiable Secret Sharing (VSS) [10, 27] consists of
the following PPT algorithms:
1. Setup(1λ) →(pp,{pki,ski}i∈[n]): Given the security pa-
rameter λ as input, the public parameters are generated,
and each party generates its own public and secret key
pair. The public parameters and the public keys are
shared among all the parties and they are implicit in-
puts to all the following algorithms.
2. Share(s,n,t) →({JsKi,ei}i∈[n],π): Given the number of
parties n, corruption threshold t, and the secret s, the
dealer computes shares JsKi for the i-th party and the
corresponding encryption under pki as ei. The dealer
also computes a proof π of correct sharing. The dealer
outputs ({JsKi,ei}i∈[n],π).
3. Decrypt(ei,ski) →(JsKi,sti): Given the encrypted share
ei and secret key ski compute the decrypted share as JsKi
and a publicly verifiable decryption state sti (∈{0,1}∗).
4. PubVer(sti,ei,π,n,t) →{0,1}: Given the public decryp-
tion state sti any party can verify that the encrypted share
ei is invalid or not, corresponding to the proof π. If the
verification succeeds, then PubVer outputs 1, else 0.
5. Recon({ij,JsKij}j∈[t+1],π,n,t) →s/L: Given any t + 1
shares, the algorithm either reconstructs the secret s or
outputs a list L of malformed shares.
Public Verifiability:
A VSS is publicly verifiable if the
transcript of the VSS protocol can be publicly verified by
any external party to check that the dealing was valid/invalid.
6

Given the decryption state sti any external party can publicly
verify, via PubVer algorithm, whether the encrypted share ei
is valid or not, given the proof π.
Definition 3.1 (Computationally Secure PVSS). A publicly
verifiable VSS scheme (PVSS) is computationally secure if
the dealer and receiver and all adversaries are computation-
ally bounded algorithms (PPT) and the scheme satisfies the
following properties:
• Correctness: Any set of t +1 or more valid shares cor-
rectly reconstructs the secret s.
• Termination: All honest nodes will eventually terminate
the Share, Decrypt and PubVer phase.
• Reconstructability: If a dealing verifies successfully,
then, given any set of ≥t + 1 shares, the Recon algo-
rithm either reconstructs a unique s or identifies a non-
empty list L ̸= /0 of malformed shares even if the dealer
is adversarial.
• Computational Secrecy: Assuming an honest dealer deal-
ing secret s, then any set of ≤t shares, ≤t secret keys, n
states {sti}i∈[n], n encrypted shares and π hides s against
an adversary, i.e. for any two secrets s,s′ following holds:
{s,{i j,JsKij,ski}j∈[t],{ei,pki}i∈[n],π}
c≈
{s′,{ij,JsKij,ski}j∈[t],{ei,pki}i∈[n],π},
where the {ij}j∈[t] indices are chosen by the adversary.
Post-Quantum Secure PVSS: We say that a PVSS scheme
achieves post-quantum security if all the properties hold even
against quantum PPT adversaries.
Long-lasting Secure PVSS: We say that a PVSS scheme
achieves long-lasting security if all properties except compu-
tational secrecy holds against classical PPT adversaries, but
the secrecy holds against quantum PPT adversaries.
Non-interactive Dealers: A PVSS is said to have non-
interactive dealers if the dealer non-interactively performs
the sharing by locally producing its message and sending it
over and then each receiver can verify that their share JsKi,
obtained via Decrypt, is valid without further interaction with
the dealer.
3.2
A New Model: Long-lasting Security
A protocol is said to be long-lasting secure if it is secure
against a PPT classical adversary during the protocol execu-
tion. And after the protocol terminates, the protocol transcript
would remain secure (does not leak any information about
honest parties’ inputs) against a future PPT quantum adver-
sary. We formally define the notion of long-lasting security
following the simulation-based real-ideal world paradigm
of [21] in Appendix. C, Definition. C.1. We note that our
PVSS protocol satisfies game-based definition and so we do
not need the simulation-based definition for this paper. How-
ever, we believe this model can be interesting independently
for building schemes that are to be deployed today, but require
long-term privacy.

Our Post-Quantum Secure PVSS Scheme
We build our PVSS scheme on top of the distributed ZK proof
and hash-based PVSS of [10]. In the PVSS protocol of [10],
the dealer shares its secret s and commits to it and its shares
using (hash-based) non-homomorphic commitments. Then,
the dealer computes a distributed ZK proof (DZK) using a
Schnorr-like proof on committed polynomials to prove that
the sharing lies on a t-degree polynomial. The dealer sends
the shares and the proofs to each receiver. Upon receiving its
share and part of the ZK proof, each receiver verifies its local
share, and raises a complaint if its ZK proof does not verify. In
case of a complaint, then the dealer leaks the receiver’s share,
and anyone can verify the legitimacy of the claim. Their PVSS
protocol requires the dealer to interact to resolve the receivers’
complaints. The overview can be found in Section 1.3.
In our PVSS, the dealer encrypts the receiver’s state under
the receiver’s master public key and the dealer’s identity. To
raise a complaint, the receiver produces the identity secret
key, and anyone can decrypt the receiver’s state(encrypted
by the dealer) and verify the legitimacy of the claim. This
removes the dealer interaction from the PVSS protocol.

6.1
PVSS Experimental Results
Along with the two PVSS schemes from this paper, we also
experiment with the lattice-based non-interactive VSS scheme
by Gentry et al [37]. We compare all three schemes on the
following dimensions: dealer running time, receiver running
time, and communication cost. The dealer time includes the
time for a dealing client to generate encrypted shares and
the proof for all servers. The communication cost is the size
of the payload that the dealer must publish/broadcast and
the pairwise communication. The receiver verification time
includes the time to derive the IBE decryption key, decrypt
the IBE-encrypted share, and verify the proof.
The empirical measurements in Table 2 clearly demonstrate
that interaction between the receivers/verifiers can avoid ex-
pensive NIZKs and achieve three orders of magnitude im-
provement in running time (and memory consumption).
Runtime Comparison. In our post-quantum secure PVSS,
the identity-specific secret key derivation accounts for 90-95%
of the running time, while the other computations – deserial-
ization, IBE encryptions/decryptions polynomial evaluation –
are relatively inexpensive. In contrast, our long-lasting PVSS
computes a SCRAPE test during verification, whose running
time grows with the server count. The SCRAPE degree-test

requires Multi-Scalar Multiplication and that forms a domi-

picted in Sec. 5.2. The voters use a command-line interface
(CLI) app to submit their vote on-chain7, which includes the
IBE-encrypted Shamir shares, commitments, and NIZK proof.
Next, the servers decrypt and validate the votes, and perform
any complaints as mandated by the PVSS protocol. Finally,
the servers aggregate the votes and compute the outcome, as
described in section 5.2.

Discussion. Our evaluation shows scalable, secure voting with
post-quantum vote privacy, low client overhead, a lightweight
smart contract, and efficient server-side computation - making
it practical for large scale deployment.

Conclusion
As PVSS gains attention for its use in decentralized ecosys-
tems like blockchains, it is important to study PVSS com-
patible with such settings – we began by examining PVSS
with non-interactive dealers. Since the lack of post-quantum
security hinders adoption of applications like private polling,
we focus on post-quantum secure (or at least post-quantum
private) PVSS. Our construction improves upon the state-of-
the-art post-quantum PVSS results by two orders of magni-
tude. Along the way, we propose a new model, long-lasting
security, which guarantees post-quantum privacy. Exploring
schemes in this new model is an exciting direction, especially
for blockchain-based applications.
