Abstract
The plausible threat of a Cryptographically Relevant Quantum
Computer (CRQC) has rightly stimulated a move away from tradi-
tional methods of asymmetric cryptography to new post-quantum se-
cure equivalents. Digital signature is the cryptographic primitive that
authenticates an Internet server‚Äôs identity by signing each certificate in
an X.509 certificate chain. A suggested response to the CRQC threat is
to deploy a hybrid classical/post-quantum digital signature, combining
a traditional tried-and-tested scheme with a post-quantum alternative,
where certificates are signed using both methods. Here we propose a
fused signature scheme that adopts the same approach, but introduces
minimal friction into existing TLS architectures.
1
Introduction
A Cryptographically Relevant Quantum Computer (CRQC) represents a
plausible threat to classic methods of asymmetric cryptography. That is
plausible, not imminent, not even inevitable. Nevertheless, it is a threat
which deserves a response. The cryptographic community has responded
quickly with new methods for key exchange and digital signature which
are immune to a CRQC, and have already advanced to a stage where such
methods are standardised.
TLS is the most important cryptographic protocol in use today. It is
activated everytime a website is accessed. It protects and hence makes pos-
sible e-commerce on the Internet. You will have activated it in the process of
viewing this preprint. Ideally our response to the threat of a CRQC would
merely require the replacement of older primitives with newer and improved
alternatives, something which indeed has happened in the past as TLS has
evolved. However, there are 3 additional problems that can arise in a move
to a post-quantum world. (1) The new primitives will have different com-
putational requirements. (2) The new primitives will impact the bandwidth
requirements of the protocol, that is the amount of data that needs to be
exchanged between server and client during a protocol handshake. (3) The
1

new primitives will not have yet survived the all-important ‚Äútest of time‚ÄĚ,
that is their true security in the face of a CRQC, or indeed classical methods
of cryptanalysis, is still uncertain.
In the interests of making a TLS implementation as ‚Äúagile‚ÄĚ as possible,
adapting to a post-quantum world should simply require the replacement of
certain cryptographic primitives, without further deeper structural changes.
This would be desirable, but is it possible?

Preliminaries
2.1
TLS
TLS version 1.3 is a three-pass protocol that uses a Key Exchange Mecha-
nism (KEM) to establish a mutual secret key, from which are derived keys
to encrypt the client-server communication. Until now the X25519 Diffie-
Hellman key exchange has been popular, and requires the exchange of only
32 bytes of data. However, already the threat of harvest-now-decrypt-later
attacks has motivated a move to a hybrid method. Post-quantum KEMs
have been proposed and standardised, the most popular being the lattice-
based scheme known as ML-KEM, and the X25519MLKEM768 hybrid is
already on its way to becoming a de-facto standard in TLS.
Let us briefly recap how the TLS protocol works in its most common
configuration.
We consider only the most recent version of TLS, that is
version 1.3 [38].
A client sends a bunch of stuff to the server, including an ephemeral KEM
public share. The server sends a bunch of stuff back, including its ephemeral
KEM public share. This exchange of public shares is used to derive a mutual
(but unauthenticated) secret key. The client sends some final stuff before
dropping down to an encrypted and one-way authenticated communication
between client and server. As early as possible the protocol data flow is
encrypted using the secret key, which is possible partway through the server‚Äôs
response (or even earlier if Encrypted Client Hello [39] is enabled).
Typically the client-to-server protocol messages tend to be small. How-
ever the server-to-client communication can be quite large, as it may include
a PKI (Public Key Infrastructure) certificate chain. This certificate chain
performs a vital function ‚Äď it authenticates the server to the client by se-
curely providing a public key to the client, which allows it to verify the
server‚Äôs signature on the protocol transcript.
It is important to note here that once the server has authenticated itself,
it may send the client a resumption ticket, to be stored typically as a browser
cookie. This allows the client to resume at a later time (maybe as long as
a week later) without the requirement for the certificate chain to be sent
again. This greatly reduces the amount of communication required in the
handshake (the KEM share values typically remain, but resumption removes
2

digital signature/verification completely from the handshake). However we
will consider here only the case where a certificate chain will be transmitted.
2.2
PKI
All X.509 digital certificates contain details of the entity that owns it and an
associated Public Key. Appended is the digital signature of another entity
which attests to the certificate‚Äôs validity. Each certificate entity owns and
protects the private key associated with that embedded Public Key.
A TLS certificate chain typically consists of three digital certificates.
At the root lies a self-signed Certificate Authority (CA) certificate. The
recognised certificate authorities in the world number in the low hundreds.
Next is an intermediate authority certificate, signed by the root owner‚Äôs pri-
vate key. Finally comes the leaf (or server) certificate, which represents the
‚Äúbusiness end‚ÄĚ of the chain. Its owning entity is typically the identity/URL
of the website being visited by the client. The leaf certificate is signed by
the intermediate‚Äôs private key. The public key of the leaf certificate and its
associated private key are used as described above by the TLS protocol.
Root certificates, since there are a finite number of them, are commonly
stored by the client, and hence do not need to be transmitted during the
protocol handshake.
Each certificate has a lifetime. Typically a root certificate might have a
lifetime of 5 years, an intermediate certificate of 2 years, and a leaf certificate
of 1 year. However there are moves afoot to reduce leaf certificate lifetimes
down to as low as 47 days, or even shorter. This has benefits, as it means that
(a) the leaf certificate may be justified in supporting a lower security level
as an attacker would need to break it within its lifetime, and (b) certificate
revocation, a major bugbear of PKI, becomes less of an issue.

Isogeny-based cryptography
The idea of using isogenies on elliptic curves as a basis for post-quantum
cryptography has been around for a long time. But unfortunately it got off
to a very bad start. The key exchange algorithm based on isogenies known
as SIDH [22] had a surprisingly long history before it was comprehensively
broken [10]. Sadly by the time it was broken considerable investments in
time and effort had been expended in order to make it practical, which made
its fall particularly devastating.
However the isogeny community has picked itself up, and is again quite
bullish about the prospects for isogeny-based cryptography [14], [4].
We
suspect that it is only a matter of time before a scheme is developed which
is competitive with those already standardised. But even now the proposal
SQIsign [14] is on the verge of practicality.
One negative characteristic of SQIsign is that signature generation is
slow, and hard to make constant time. However, in the scenario considered
above SQIsign signing is never required during the execution of TLS, by
either client or server.
It is only required for the offline construction of
the certificate chain.
On the other hand SQIsign verification, which is
required by the client, is relatively fast (compared with signing) and is easy
to implement in constant time.
Many currently proposed schemes like SQIsign use finite field arithmetic
based on the primes 5.2248‚ąí1, 65.2376‚ąí1 and 27.2500‚ąí1. Fields of these sizes
would be familiar to implementors of standard elliptic curve cryptography,
and schemes based on such primes would be expected to hit levels 1,3, and
5 of security in both classic and isogeny-based contexts. These primes p are
chosen such that p + 1 is divisible by a large power of 2, which is important
for isogeny-based proposals. However such primes are also Montgomery-
friendly [3], and in the past primes of this form were suggested for use in
standard classical Elliptic-Curve Cryptography [9].

Hybrid Signatures
Just as with KEMs it makes a lot of sense in the near-term to use hybrid
signatures. That is to sign everything twice, once with a tried and tested
classical scheme, and again with a CRQC-immune post-quantum scheme.
Both signatures would need to verify for the overall signature to be accepted.
The problem is to decide which methods to pair, and a concern is that given
the number of possible combinations TLS could end up having to support
multiple proposals. Working out the exact security parity between possible
pairings could also be an issue. See [7] for consideration of some possible
9

combinations. In this recent draft, no less than 18 combinations involving
ML-DSA as the post-quantum component are suggested [36].
5.1
Hybrid Signature Requirements in TLS Depend on the
Role
One important aspect to take into account in post-quantum authentication
in TLS is that there are several TLS signature problems. Typically, a first
connection in the Web PKI involves several signatures with different perfor-
mance and deployment constraints. In the case of a unique or single visit
to a website, the client-server interaction may involve not only the certifi-
cate chain and the TLS handshake signature, but also additional so-called
certificate transparency signatures [45].
We note that there exist two scenarios that are especially relevant. First,
some signatures are accompanied by their public keys during the handshake,
whereas others are not. On the other hand, for the signature of the root on
the intermediate certificate, and for signed certificate timestamps (SCTs),
the public key is typically not transmitted during the handshake. In these
cases, signature size is the dominant metric. By contrast, the leaf certificate
and the TLS transcript signature do involve transmitted public-key material,
so for those roles the main design factor to be taken into account is the
combined size of the public key and signature.
Second, some signatures are produced offline while others are produced
online. Certificate signatures are generated ahead of time and then verified
many times by clients. However, the TLS transcript signature must be com-
puted during the handshake protocol and is therefore critical for the overall
latency of the protocol. For that reason, the online handshake signature
requires a better balance between signing and verification time, whereas the
offline certificate signatures can tolerate a slower signing, and even non-
constant time signing, if in exchange they offer compact sizes and efficient
verification.
Hence, in this paper we suggest that it is better to view TLS authen-
tication as a combination of signatures, where different signatures in the
handshake have different constraints on bandwidth, latency and verifica-
tion cost.
Today, the certificate material is a substantial fraction of the
data transferred on non-resumed connections, and a naive blunt migration
to large post-quantum signatures risks producing certificate chains that are
prone to compatibility and performance degradation issues.

5.3
Security semantics of the fused construction
In this work, we intend the fused approach to achieve a same-message, all-
components-must-verify composition of two signature schemes. We make
this semantics explicit, while deliberately keeping the discussion high-level.
We focus only on the signature-composition semantics of the proposed hy-
brid design, and we do not attempt to model revocation, CA compromise,
side-channel leakage, or the broader standardisation questions related to
X.509 encoding. These issues are clearly important, but they are orthogo-
nal to our central claim: under a conjunctive same-message verification rule,
the proposed fused construction has the expected hybrid-authentication se-
mantics.
The notion of existential unforgeability under chosen-message attack
(EUF-CMA) goes back to the seminal work of Goldwasser, Micali, and
Rivest [20], and later it was formalized by Bellare and Rogaway [6].
Intuitively, EUF-CMA security means that given the public key and ora-
cle access to signatures on adaptively chosen messages, an efficient adversary
still cannot output a valid signature on a fresh message outside its signing
queries.

Proposition 1 captures the basic security intuition behind the fused con-
struction: because verification requires both components to verify on the
same message, an attacker who breaks only one component does not au-
tomatically obtain a forgery against the fused scheme. In this sense, the
fused construction inherits the security of each of its components, provided
verifiers enforce the conjunctive verification rule of Definition 1.
Remark 1 (What happens if one component fails?). Suppose one compo-
nent scheme becomes insecure, while the other remains EUF-CMA secure.
Under the all-components-must-verify rule, the fused construction still can-
not be forged unless the remaining secure component is also forged. Thus
the fused construction provides the usual hybrid ‚Äúsecurity hedge‚ÄĚ sought in
certificate applications.
Role-specific interpretation in our TLS design.
The proposed TLS
instantiation uses different signature mechanisms for different authentication
roles.
‚ÄĘ For root and intermediate certificate signatures, we use the fused con-
struction based on SQIsign-3 and Ed376. A certificate is accepted
only if both component signatures verify on the same TBSCertificate
encoding.
‚ÄĘ For the leaf certificate and the online TLS transcript signature, we use
a level-1 hybrid ML-DSA/Ed25519 public key and the corresponding
conjunctive verification rule.
‚ÄĘ Therefore, every mandatory authentication step in the non resumed
handshake is interpreted under an explicit all-components-must-verify
semantics.
Proposition 2. Consider a non resumed TLS 1.3 handshake in our setting,
and assume:
1. the client performs standard certificate-path validation;
2. each mandatory signature check in the chain and transcript uses the
conjunctive verification rule described above;
3. the relevant private signing keys have not been compromised; and
4. the underlying signature schemes used in each role are EUF-CMA se-
cure.
Then any successful server-impersonation attack on a fresh handshake im-
plies at least one of the following events:

1. a successful forgery of a mandatory certificate signature in the accepted
chain; or
2. a successful forgery of the mandatory transcript signature under the
accepted leaf public key.
Proof sketch. Under standard TLS 1.3 authentication semantics, acceptance
of the server identity requires both an accepted certificate chain and a valid
transcript signature under the authenticated leaf key. Therefore an imper-
sonating adversary must either cause the client to accept a certificate chain
that was not legitimately issued, or else produce a valid transcript signature
for the authenticated leaf key without access to the corresponding private
key. These are exactly the two events stated above.

A proposal
A fairly obvious idea would be to implement a fused hybrid scheme based
on a level 3 SQIsign implementation combined with an Edwards signa-
ture based on Ed376. Note that this implementation could share highly
optimised finite field arithmetic code for the prime p = 65.2376 ‚ąí1. The
combination would hit the 192-bit security level suitable for intermediate
and root certificates. The combined public key size would be 97+48=145
bytes, and the combined signature size would be 224+96=320 bytes.
6.1
TiigerTLS
TiigerTLS [41] is a small footprint implementation of TLS1.3 [38] which
provides a TLS client written in C++ and Rust, and a TLS server written
in Rust.
It does not support versions of TLS earlier than 1.3.
Session
resumption is fully supported. It is primarily aimed at Internet of Things
applications, and so does not allocate any heap memory. It was designed
from the ground up with agility in mind, and hence easily adapts to novel
cryptographic primitives via its Security Abstraction Layer (SAL), a feature
that proved invaluable for this exercise. The distribution includes some tools
for certificate chain construction.

7
Conclusion
TLS supports multiple configurations, and many of these would affect our
findings. For example, optional client side PKI authentication would greatly
increase the client to server traffic. And other choices of PQ signature algo-
rithm for the leaf certificate (like FN-DSA) would further reduce server-to-
client traffic. Another contributing factor could be the adoption of certificate
compression [19].
Nevertheless, we conclude that an open mind should be kept regarding
the future of TLS in the context of the possible emergence of a CRQC. TLS
will need to change, but premature and ultimately unnecessary changes,
which impact agility, could do more harm than good.
