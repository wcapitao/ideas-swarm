Abstract. A verifiable shuffle proves that a list of output ciphertexts
is a rerandomized permutation of a list of input ciphertexts, without
revealing either the permutation or the rerandomization factors. Verifi-
able shuffles are a core primitive in mix-nets and are deployed in national
electronic voting systems and blockchain-based anonymization protocols.
Existing deployed verifiable shuffles typically have proof size O(N) or
O(
√
N) in the number of ciphertexts N, making shuffle proofs a primary
bandwidth cost. The only prior construction with O(log N) proof size
(Hoffmann et al., CCS 2019) requires roughly 30N prover and 10N ver-
ifier group exponentiations, with a proof consisting of 6 log N + 8 group
elements and 4 field elements.
In this paper, we present a new verifiable shuffle for ElGamal ciphertexts
whose proof consists of 2 log N + 8 group elements and 8 field elements,
reducing the prover and verifier costs of Hoffmann et al. to 15N and 6N
group exponentiations, respectively. Our protocol is public-coin, non-
interactive via the Fiat-Shamir transform, and relies on an updatable
structured reference string generated once in a powers-of-tau ceremony
and reusable across applications.
We implement the protocol and, to the best of our knowledge, provide
the first benchmarks for a verifiable shuffle with logarithmic proof size.
At N = 220 (about one million ciphertexts), the proof is only 2.5 KB,

Introduction
Mix-nets, introduced by Chaum [10], are a foundational tool for anonymous
communication. In a mix-net, a sequence of servers each receives a batch of en-
crypted messages, permutes the order, applies fresh randomness to re-encrypt
them, and forwards the result to the next server. As long as at least one server
is honest, the correspondence between inputs and outputs remains hidden. This
simple paradigm has found applications across a wide range of privacy-sensitive
settings. In e-voting [17,4,3], mix-nets are used to break the link between voters
and their ballots: encrypted votes are shuffled through a sequence of mix-servers,

2

after which they can be decrypted and published without revealing which voter
cast which ballot. Mix-net-based systems have been deployed in national elec-
tions in countries including Norway and Switzerland [38,27], routinely handling
shuffles of several hundred thousand encrypted ballots. More recently, mix-net
shuffles have been adopted in blockchain-based coin mixing protocols such as
MixEth [35] for anonymization of cryptocurrency transactions on Ethereum.
A central challenge in these applications is verifiability: the party responsible
for the shuffle may modify, drop, or substitute ciphertexts, thereby compromising
correctness. In the standard mix-net setting, this is addressed by requiring each
server to produce a zero-knowledge proof that its outputs are a rerandomized
permutation of its inputs. A protocol that provides such a proof is called a
verifiable shuffle, and constructing efficient verifiable shuffles has been one of the
central topics in cryptographic protocol design.
The efficiency of a verifiable shuffle is typically measured by proof size, prover
time, and verifier time. Among these, proof size plays a distinguished role in prac-
tice. In e-voting, each mix-server publishes its proof on a public bulletin board,
and any auditor must obtain all such proofs. With k servers, the total proof
volume scales linearly in k. In blockchain-based protocols such as MixEth [35],
the situation is more extreme: shuffle proofs are posted on-chain, where on-chain
storage is particularly expensive. A reduction in proof size therefore, translates
directly into lower gas costs and higher throughput.
A natural way to build verifiable shuffle is to rely on generic zkSNARKs, such
as PLONK [19] and Groth16 [23], to write the shuffle relation as an arithmetic
circuit and prove in zero knowledge that the circuit is satisfied. For shuffling,
however, this approach typically incurs substantial overhead. First, routing N
ciphertexts through a secret permutation requires a permutation network of
size Ω(N log N). Second, the shuffle relation is defined over an elliptic curve
group. When the zkSNARK’s native field does not match the curve’s field, these
operations must be implemented via non-native arithmetic inside the circuit,
leading to substantial constraint overhead. Dedicated shuffle arguments avoid
these costs by working natively with group elements and exploiting the algebraic
structure of the shuffle relation.

where N denotes the number of ciphertexts. Groth’s first sublinear construc-

1.1
Technical Overview
A common strategy in verifiable shuffle design [32,22,6,28] is to decompose the
proof into two relations: first, the prover knows a valid permutation of the input
ciphertexts, and second, applying this permutation together with suitable reran-
domization factors transforms the inputs into the output ciphertexts. We adopt
the same high-level approach for ElGamal ciphertexts, the standard ciphertext
type in mix-net-based e-voting and coin mixing.
At a more concrete level, our construction and that of Hoffmann et al. [28]
differ in how these two relations are proven. For the permutation relation, Hoff-
mann et al. reduce it to a product relation and prove it using inner-product
arguments (IPAs). We instead adopt the permutation argument of [19], based
on the KZG polynomial commitment scheme, which yields a constant-size proof
via a single batched pairing equation. For the consistency argument, Hoffmann
et al. formulate the relation as a linear map and again prove it with an IPA. We
instead express consistency directly as three ElGamal relations, aggregate them
into a single inner-product instance via a random challenge, and prove the result
with a single invocation of an IPA. As a result, our proof is more compact overall
since the permutation component is constant-size, and consistency requires only
one logarithmic-size IPA proof.

Related Work
The study of verifiable shuffles originates from two paradigms. We survey each
in turn, progressing from linear-size to sub-linear and logarithmic-size schemes.

Permutation-matrix paradigm (linear size). The first paradigm, introduced by
Furukawa and Sako [18], has the prover commit to a permutation matrix and
use Schwartz–Zippel-style checks to show that it is consistent with the shuffle.
Furukawa et al. [17] gave an implementation for large-scale elections, and Fu-
rukawa [15,16] later extended the idea to combine shuffle-and-decryption proofs
and aggregate arguments for multi-server mix-nets. Wikström [37] decomposed
the argument into offline and online phases, which Terelius and Wikström [36]
simplified into a compact argument based on polynomial evaluation over re-
stricted challenge spaces. This has been deployed in real-world elections through
the Verificatum library [38] and further optimised by Krips and Lipmaa [30]. The
permutation-matrix approach also underlies a line of pairing-based NIZK shuf-
fle arguments in the common reference string model [26,31,11,13,12,2], which
progressively improved efficiency and soundness guarantees. Recently, Abdol-
maleki et al. [1] proposed shuffle arguments based on subset-checking techniques
with improved verifier efficiency. All schemes in this line have O(N) proof size.
Polynomial-roots paradigm and sub-linear schemes. The second paradigm, due to
Neff [32], exploits the invariance of polynomials under permutation of their roots.
Groth [20,22] refined this with Pedersen commitments and multi-exponentiation
techniques. Building on ideas from both paradigms, Groth and Ishai [24] achieved
the first sub-linear shuffle argument by arranging the N ciphertexts in an m × n
matrix, achieving O(m2 + n) communication at the cost of an O(Nm) prover.
Bayer and Groth [6] significantly improved this by bringing the communication
down to O(
√
N) with an O(N log N) prover, combining Groth’s sub-linear ar-
guments for linear algebra [21] with techniques for computing diagonal sums in
the exponent.
Logarithmic-size schemes. Hoffmann, Klooß, and Rupp [28] obtained the first
verifiable shuffle with logarithmic proof size by combining the Bayer–Groth
framework with inner product arguments [7,9]. Their scheme achieves O(log N)
proof size, O(N) prover complexity, and O(N) verifier complexity, in the plain
discrete-log setting with a transparent setup.

Discussion. The measurements in Table 2 are consistent with the theoretical
cost analysis. Prover time grows essentially linearly with N, with an empirical
doubling ratio close to 2. Verifier time also approaches linear scaling for larger
N, while for smaller instances the constant cost of the pairing checks remains
visible. The proof size grows only logarithmically, from 1.28 KB at N = 27 to
2.5 KB at N = 220.
Prover/verifier gap. In practice, the prover is much slower than the verifier,
although the coarse operation counts differ by only a constant factor. The main
reason is that the verifier’s dominant work is concentrated in a single large
MSM, which is highly optimized in existing libraries. By contrast, the prover’s
work is distributed across multiple protocol stages and, in particular, across the
recursive rounds of the IPA, where it is spread over many smaller computations.
As a result, the measured prover/verifier gap is substantially larger than what
the raw 15N versus 6N accounting alone would suggest.
