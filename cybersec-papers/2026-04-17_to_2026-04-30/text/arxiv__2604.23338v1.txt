From Stateless Queries to Autonomous Actions:
A Layered Security Framework for Agentic AI
Systems
Kexin Chu
Abstract—Agentic AI systems face security challenges that
stateless large language models do not. They plan across extended
horizons, maintain persistent memory, invoke external tools, and
coordinate with peer agents. Existing security analyses organize
threats by attack type (prompt injection, jailbreaking), but
provide no principled model of which architectural component is
vulnerable or over what timescale the threat manifests. This paper
makes five contributions. First, we introduce the Layered Attack
Surface Model (LASM), a seven-layer framework that maps
threats to distinct architectural components: Foundation, Cognitive,
Memory, Tool Execution, Multi-Agent Coordination, Ecosystem, and
Governance, the accountability and observability layer that spans
the stack analogously to the network management plane. Second,
we introduce attack temporality as an orthogonal analytical
dimension with four classes: Instantaneous (T1), Session-Persistent
(T2), Cross-Session Cumulative (T3), and Sub-Session-Stack, Non-
Session-Bounded (T4). Third, through a systematic review of 94
papers (2021–2025), we show that the most dangerous emerging
threats concentrate at the intersection of high-layer attacks (L5–
L7) and slow-burn temporality (T3–T4): covert agent collusion,
long-term memory poisoning, MCP supply-chain compromise,
and alignment failure that manifests as an insider threat with no
external adversary. Only 8 of 120 paper-cell assignments (7%) fall
in this zone. Fourth, we propose a cross-layer defense taxonomy
spanning all seven LASM layers and all four temporality classes,
exposing which threat classes existing defenses leave unaddressed.
Fifth, we survey evaluation benchmarks, identify five research
gaps in the under-studied high-layer, slow-burn zone, and argue
that agentic security must be treated as a distributed systems
problem embedded in an adversarial ecosystem.
Index Terms—AI agents, agentic systems, large language models,
security, prompt injection, multi-agent systems, supply chain
attacks, memory poisoning, alignment, trustworthy AI.
I. INTRODUCTION
T
HE shift from querying a language model to deploying an
agentic AI system is more than an incremental capability
upgrade; it marks a categorical change in the security threat
surface. A stateless LLM responds to a single input and
produces a single output, creating a bounded, session-isolated
attack surface amenable to traditional input/output filtering. An
AI agent, by contrast, maintains persistent state across sessions,
formulates multi-step plans, invokes privileged external tools
(web browsers, code interpreters, file systems, APIs) [1]–
[4], and increasingly coordinates with other autonomous
agents to accomplish long-horizon tasks [5]–[9]. This shift
is driven partly by the emergent capabilities that arise at

scale [10] and partly by new infrastructure for tool and memory

This architectural complexity generates vulnerabilities that
are emergent, compositional, and temporally extended. Security
models designed for stateless systems cannot capture these
properties. Consider three examples that motivate this survey:
• An adversarially crafted document retrieved during a routine
web search can silently corrupt an agent’s long-term
memory, influencing behavior in a completely unrelated
session weeks later, with no detectable anomaly at either
the injection or exploitation point.
• A compromised sub-agent in an enterprise multi-agent
pipeline can propagate malicious intent through peer-to-peer
trust relationships, compromising the entire orchestration
network from a single point of entry [12].
• A malicious MCP server, indistinguishable to the agent
from a legitimate tool provider, can silently exfiltrate
sensitive data via hidden instructions embedded in tool
descriptions [13].
These threats have no direct analogues in classical LLM
safety or traditional software security. The security community
has responded by extending existing frameworks rather than
building new ones.
a) Limitations of Existing Work.: Recent surveys [14]–
[16] have catalogued an expanding roster of attacks. However,
they share structural limitations that reduce their actionability
for system designers:
(1) Type-centric, not component-centric taxonomies.
Organizing threats by attack type (jailbreaking, injection,
poisoning) conflates attacks that require different defenses
at different layers. A gradient-based adversarial perturbation
at the embedding level and a memory poisoning attack via
a RAG document are both classified as “adversarial inputs”,
yet one is mitigated at the model layer and the other at the
memory management layer. A designer cannot derive where
to place security controls from a type-centric taxonomy.
(2) No temporal dimension. Existing surveys treat attacks
as instantaneous events. In agentic systems, the most dangerous
attacks are temporally extended: a payload injected today may
execute weeks later via long-term memory, or may never trigger
an explicit “attack event” at all (emergent misalignment). This
temporal structure is invisible to existing taxonomies.
(3) Coverage gap at higher layers. The literature heavily
covers L1 (foundation model jailbreaking) and L4 (prompt
injection), but higher-layer threats, including multi-agent
collusion, ecosystem supply chain attacks, and governance

3) Systematic Literature Survey (Section V–Section X):

II. SURVEY METHODOLOGY
This survey follows a systematic literature review protocol
inspired by PRISMA [17] guidelines for computing literature
reviews. Fig. 1 shows the temporal distribution of retained
papers.
A. Search Strategy
Four major databases were searched: IEEE Xplore, ACM
Digital Library, arXiv (cs.CR, cs.AI, cs.LG), and Google
Scholar. Forward/backward citation tracing was additionally
performed from three seed papers [5], [14], [18]. Search terms
combined agent-related terms × security-related terms:
Agent terms: “AI agent”, “LLM agent”, “autonomous agent”,
“multi-agent”, “agentic AI”, “tool-augmented LLM”
Security terms: “security”, “attack”, “adversarial”, “prompt
injection”, “jailbreak”, “poisoning”, “safety”, “trust”, “vulnera-
bility”
The search covered January 2021 to April 2025.
B. Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria
Inclusion: (1a) Peer-reviewed papers at top venues (IEEE
S&P, CCS, USENIX Security, NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR, ACL,
EMNLP, ACM CCS, NDSS) are included regardless of citation
count; (1b) arXiv and non-top-tier preprints require ≥20
citations for papers through December 2024; for January–
April 2025 preprints the citation threshold was not applied
due to insufficient publication time, and inclusion required
a direct topical contribution to an identified gap area; (2)
papers specifically addressing security of LLM-based agent
systems; (3) papers presenting attacks, defenses, or evaluation
frameworks.
Exclusion: (1) Papers on LLM safety without agentic
context; (2) classical adversarial ML on non-LLM models; (3)
position papers without technical contributions; (4) duplicate
publications.

III. BACKGROUND: ANATOMY OF AN AI AGENT
A. Core Architectural Components
Definition: AI Agent
An AI agent is an autonomous computational system
built upon a foundation language model that: (a) perceives
multi-modal inputs from its environment, (b) maintains
state across multiple interaction turns, (c) formulates
and executes goal-directed plans through sequences of
actions, and (d) modifies its environment via external tool
invocations or communication with peer agents [8].

We identify six core architectural components (Fig. 2), each
introducing distinct security concerns:
(1) Foundation Model. A large pretrained language
model [19]–[21] that serves as the reasoning engine [22]–
[25]. Safety properties acquired through RLHF [26]–[28] or
Constitutional AI [29] are necessary but insufficient for agentic
deployment, as they are trained on single-turn interaction
data [30].
(2) Planning and Reasoning Module. Translates high-
level goals into action sequences via architectures including
ReAct [5], chain-of-thought [31], and tree-of-thought search.
Multi-step planning creates a surface for cumulative goal drift:
small perturbations at each reasoning step can compound into
large behavioral deviations.
(3) Memory System. Stores agent state across interactions
via four mechanisms: (a) in-context memory (the active con-
versation window), (b) external short-term memory (session-
scoped vector stores), (c) external long-term memory (persistent
episodic/semantic stores), and (d) procedural memory (learned
tool-use patterns) [32].
(4) Tool Execution Layer. Enables agents to invoke external
capabilities: web browsers, code interpreters, databases, file
systems, and third-party APIs. Each tool invocation constitutes
a real-world side effect that may be irreversible, and tool
outputs are typically injected back into the agent’s context
without explicit trust marking.
(5) Multi-Agent Interface. Mechanisms for inter-agent
communication, delegation, and result aggregation. Introduces
trust-hierarchy questions absent in single-agent systems: when
should an agent trust instructions from a peer versus an
orchestrator?
(6) Orchestration and Environment. The runtime infras-
tructure mediates all agent–world interactions. It encompasses
conversation frameworks, MCP servers, API gateways, and
container runtimes.

Definition: Principal Hierarchy
A principal hierarchy P = (p1 ≻p2 ≻· · · ≻pn) is
a strict ordering of instruction sources, where pi ≻pj
means instructions from pi should take precedence over
instructions from pj in cases of conflict.
In typical deployments: Developer ≻Operator ≻User ≻
Environment [33]. Most agent implementations implicitly treat
environment inputs as high-trust, despite the environment being
the least trusted principal in the chain [18]. We term this
principal trust inversion: the systematic failure to enforce
the principal hierarchy at the agent–environment boundary. It
is the root cause of indirect prompt injection and tool-based
manipulation attacks (Section VII).
C. Formal Threat Model
We model the security of an agent system A with respect
to an adversary D characterized by four dimensions:
• Position: External (no system access; can inject content
into the information environment), Semi-trusted principal
(legitimate but bounded access; malicious user), or Supply-
chain (can compromise upstream components: models,
tools, frameworks).
• Knowledge: Black-box (observable input/output only),
Gray-box (knows architecture but not weights), or White-
box (full access).
• Goal: Confidentiality (extract private data), Integrity (alter
agent behavior), or Availability (disrupt agent operation).
• Persistence: One-shot (single interaction) or Persistent
(sustained, multi-session campaign).
This threat model is richer than those used in most prior
surveys, which typically characterize adversaries only by goal
type.
IV. THE LAYERED ATTACK SURFACE MODEL
A. Design Rationale
Security analysis benefits from layered reference models.
The OSI model enabled network security reasoning by making
each layer’s trust boundaries, data formats, and security respon-
sibilities explicit. We propose an analogous decomposition for
agentic AI. The LASM insight is that agent components have:
(1) Different trust boundaries: The foundation model trusts the
system prompt; the tool layer trusts function call returns;
the memory layer trusts retrieval results.
(2) Different data semantics: L1 processes tokens; L3 pro-
cesses embeddings; L4 processes structured API responses.
Attacks that cross these semantic boundaries bypass layer-
specific detectors on both sides.
(3) Different defense leverage points: Input sanitization at L4
provides no protection against L1 gradient-based adversarial
examples, and vice versa.
a) Layer Decomposition Criterion.: Two attack surfaces
belong to distinct layers if and only if: (1) they present
independent trust boundaries, meaning an attack exploiting one
boundary cannot be detected at the other without additional
instrumentation, and (2) they admit different defense leverage
points, so controls effective at one layer are structurally
inapplicable at the other. The criterion operates on attack
surface representations, the domain in which attacks are
formulated and defenses must operate, rather than on neural
or computational substrate. This distinction is essential for L1
and L2 (see below). Applied to the other layers: an adversarial
embedding vector (R3) cannot be detected by a JSON sanitizer
(R4), and vice versa. The trust graph structure among agents
(R5) has no analogue in single-agent tool call semantics (R4).
L6 ecosystem artifacts (model weights, package manifests)
constitute a deployment-time attack surface structurally separate
from L4 runtime tool I/O.
b) Note on L1 vs. L2: attack surface disjointness under
shared weights.: In current single-LLM agents, L1 and L2 share
the same neural network weights as their computational sub-
strate. LASM nonetheless treats them as distinct layers because
their attack surface representations are disjoint. Formally: R1
comprises trained-weight artifacts, the domain manipulated by
gradient-based adversarial perturbations, backdoor poisoning,
and extraction attacks. R2 comprises in-context reasoning
artifacts, the live goal specification, chain-of-thought trajectory,
and planning state manipulated at inference time.
The distinction is not computational substrate (both layers
use the same weights) but where the attack is formulated and
where the defense must operate. A gradient-based adversarial
suffix operates on R1 by perturbing token embeddings to
exploit weight-space geometry, and is invisible to a plan verifier
that inspects output action sequences in R2. Conversely, a false-
premise injection into the reasoning chain operates on R2 and
cannot be caught by adversarial training that hardens R1: the
injected premise is semantically valid in weight space, and
the weights were never trained to resist this specific in-context
manipulation.
An apparent tension arises from safety fine-tuning: RLHF
modifies weights (R1 operation) but changes average inference-

5
time reasoning behavior (R2 effect). This does not violate
disjointness because the Proposition 1 pure-defense assumption
precludes cross-layer side channels, not cross-layer effects
mediated by the model itself. More precisely: safety fine-tuning
is an R1 operation that shifts the marginal distribution of rea-
soning outputs, but it cannot target a specific in-context trigger-
conditioned reasoning manipulation. Hubinger et al. [34]
demonstrate this exactly: safety fine-tuning shifts average
reasoning toward alignment but leaves trigger-conditioned
deceptive reasoning intact, because that deception is encoded
as a context-dependent R2 pattern, not as an R1 weight
anomaly that gradient-based optimization can reliably identify
and remove. LASM is therefore an analytical framework
for locating security controls, not an architectural blueprint
requiring physically separate computational units.
Definition: Layered Attack Surface Model (LASM)
LASM partitions the attack surface of an AI agent system
into seven ordered layers L = (L1, L2, . . . , L7), where
each layer Li is characterized by: (a) a distinct analytical
unit Ci with an independent trust boundary Bi; (b) a
unique attack surface representation Ri, the domain where
attacks against Li are formulated and defenses must operate,
satisfying Ri ∩Rj = ∅for all i ̸= j; (c) a set of
representative threats Ti; and (d) a set of defense leverage
points Di. L7 (Governance) functions as the accountability
and observability layer, capturing failures that manifest as
unattributable harm from any attack at L1–L6. Like the
management plane in network architectures, it spans the
full stack but is modeled as a distinct security layer.

c) Cross-Layer Attack Patterns.: Dangerous attacks often
span multiple layers simultaneously, a property that LASM
captures through its layer boundaries but type-centric tax-
onomies miss. Three cross-layer patterns are worth noting:
(1) L4→L3→L7: An indirect prompt injection (L4) plants a
memory entry (L3) that causes misattributed accountability
(L7), the “temporal escalation” pattern (Section VI); (2)
L6→L2: A supply-chain backdoor (L6) corrupts the planning
module’s goal representation (L2) via a trigger-conditioned
sleeper agent (Section V); (3) L5→L1: An infectious jailbreak
propagates across agents (L5), each serving as a relay that
overrides the next agent’s foundation-model safety constraints
(L1). These cross-layer patterns are invisible to type-centric
taxonomies but are naturally captured by LASM’s layer
boundaries.
d) Framework Validation: LASM Applied to Documented
Incidents.: To validate that LASM produces actionable defense-
placement decisions beyond what type-centric taxonomies
provide, the framework is applied to two documented real-
world incidents.
Incident 1: Postmark MCP Compromise (2024–2025). A
production Postmark MCP server was found to inject a BCC
field into email tool calls, silently exfiltrating all outgoing email
content to an attacker-controlled address [35].
Type-centric classification: “Supply chain attack” or “prompt
injection.” Both labels direct defenders toward L4 input filtering
(detecting injected instructions in content) or general supply-
chain hygiene.
LASM classification:
• Layer: L6 (Ecosystem). The attack vector is the MCP
server itself, a trusted ecosystem component; the exploit

6
arrives via the tool description loaded at initialization, not
via user-supplied content.
• Temporality: T2 (Session-Persistent). The compromised
tool description persists throughout each agent session from
the moment of context initialization.
• Primary defense leverage point: L6. ABOM-based prove-
nance tracking with cryptographic manifest signing of MCP
server descriptions would have enabled detection before
initialization. L4 output filtering would not have caught
this attack because the exfiltration was implemented as a
legitimately formatted tool call.
Without LASM, the “supply chain” label would direct
defenders toward SBOM-style package verification, which
does not enumerate tool description content. LASM uniquely
identifies L6 as the control point and motivates the ABOM
extension (Section IX).
Incident 2: GitHub Copilot RCE via Prompt Injection
in Project Files [36]. Attacker-controlled content in project
files, including dependency documentation, GitHub issues, and
pull request descriptions, was found to inject shell-execution
instructions into GitHub Copilot’s context. When the agent
processed these files during routine coding tasks such as
code review or documentation generation, it executed the
embedded instructions as shell commands, achieving remote
code execution.
Type-centric classification: “Supply chain attack.” This
directs defenders toward package signing and registry vetting
(upstream prevention).
LASM classification:
• Layer: L4 (Tool Execution). The exploit mechanism is
principal trust inversion at the tool layer: content from
project files and repository metadata, returned by file-read
and code-analysis tools, is incorporated into the agent’s
planning context without trust marking and executed as
instructions.
• Temporality: T1 (Instantaneous). The full attack chain (doc-
string retrieval →instruction injection →shell execution)
completes within a single code review session.
• Primary defense leverage point: L4. Tool output sand-
boxing and output filtering applied to tool returns would
have contained the exploit even if the malicious content
reached the agent’s context. Package signing reduces the
probability of the malicious file being present but does not
limit execution once it is present.
The two incidents illustrate a key LASM property: the
same high-level attack label (“supply chain”) maps to different
primary layers (L6 vs. L4) with different primary defense
controls (ABOM vs. tool sandboxing). A type-centric taxonomy
cannot distinguish these cases; LASM does so by identifying
which trust boundary is exploited.
e) Structured Validation: LASM Defense Placement vs.
OWASP LLM Top 10.: To validate LASM’s utility beyond
two incidents, Table II maps five documented attacks against
the OWASP LLM Top 10 [37] and MITRE ATLAS [38]
classification, then derives the defense prescription from each
taxonomy. The key test is whether different attacks that share
an OWASP/ATLAS category require different defenses under
LASM. If yes, the type-centric label causes misallocated
controls: a designer who follows only OWASP/ATLAS would
apply the same defense to both cases, leaving one of them
uncovered.
Table II demonstrates that “Prompt Injection” (LLM01)
maps to three different LASM layers (L1, L3, L5) each
requiring layer-specific defenses, and that “Insecure Plugin
Design” (LLM07) maps to L4 and L6 with non-overlapping
controls. A designer following only OWASP/ATLAS would
apply input filtering and package-signing across all five cases;
LASM shows that four of the five require additional controls
that input-filtering and package-signing cannot provide.
B. Attack Temporality: An Orthogonal Dimension
Definition: Attack Temporality
The temporality class τ(a) of an attack a characterizes
the installation-to-execution gap: the elapsed time between
the introduction of the attack payload and the realization
of its harmful effect. For T1–T3 this gap is bounded by the
session structure; for T4 the gap is not session-bounded.
We define four classes based on what structural boundary
contains the gap.
T1 – Instantaneous: Gap = 0; payload introduction and
harmful effect occur within the same inference call. All classical
prompt injection and jailbreak attacks are T1. Detectable in
real time; targeted by most existing defenses.
T2 – Session-Persistent: Gap is bounded by one session;
the payload persists across turns within a single session and
executes before the session ends. Context manipulation attacks
that hijack an ongoing planning process are T2. Detectable
with session-scoped monitoring.
T3 – Cross-Session Cumulative: Gap spans at least one
session boundary; the payload is written to persistent memory
in session si and exploited in a future session sj (j > i,
potentially j ≫i). This class is uniquely dangerous because
(a) the injection appears as a normal memory write, (b) the
exploitation appears as a normal memory read, and (c) no
single session contains evidence of both events. Detectable by
cross-session memory auditing.
T4 – Sub-Session-Stack, Non-Session-Bounded: The
installation-to-execution gap is not bounded by session structure
because the payload is installed at a layer below the session
stack (model weights or training data) or because the gap
is conditioned on an arbitrary trigger rather than a session
boundary. Two sub-classes:
• T4a (Drift): No discrete payload. Harmful behavior
emerges from accumulated exposure to biased environ-
ments without any triggering event. The gap is infinite
and open-ended; deviation accumulates continuously,
detectable only through longitudinal distributional moni-
toring across hundreds of sessions.
• T4b (Dormant): A discrete payload is embedded in model
weights or a supply-chain component during training
or fine-tuning, not via a session-layer memory write.
Harmful execution is deferred until an arbitrary trigger

XIII. RESEARCH GAPS AND OPEN PROBLEMS
The LASM × temporality heatmap (Fig. 3) locates five research
gaps in the high-risk, low-coverage zone.
Research Gap #1
Cross-Session Attack Benchmarks (L3 × T3)
No standardized benchmark evaluates memory poisoning across
session boundaries. Closing this gap requires: multi-session replay
infrastructure; memory state serialization/deserialization protocols;
ground-truth labeling of memory entries as clean vs. poisoned; and
evaluation metrics that measure T3 success (behavioral change in
a future unrelated session) rather than T1 injection success.
Estimated difficulty: High (infrastructure). Impact if addressed:
Without this benchmark, there is no way to measure whether T3
defenses improve over time. All 15 deployed defense classes in
Table VIII are evaluated only against T1/T2 threats; this gap leaves
the T3 column of the defense taxonomy empirically unverified.
Research Gap #2
Emergent Misalignment Detection (L7 × T4)
No current approach reliably detects gradual alignment drift in
deployed agents. Required: statistical process control methods
adapted to high-dimensional, stochastic behavioral distributions;
longitudinal agent behavioral datasets for training/evaluation; and
causal attribution methods that can distinguish drift from legitimate
behavioral adaptation.
Estimated difficulty: Very High (fundamental). Impact: The agentic
insider threat (§X) is undetectable without a drift detection
method. No existing security control addresses a T4a threat; the
entire L7×T4 cell in Table IV has no corresponding defense in
Table VIII.
Research Gap #3
General Steganographic Communication Detection (L5 ×
T1–T2)
Colosseum [51] provides a first step for known encoding schemes.
A general steganography detector for LLM-generated outputs,
capable of detecting novel encoding schemes, does not exist. This
is related to the fundamental open problem of detecting statistical
anomalies in LLM output distributions, complicated by the high
natural variance of LLM outputs.
Estimated difficulty: Very High (fundamental). Impact: High
(covert agent coordination is otherwise undetectable).
Research Gap #4
ABOM Standardization and MCP Security Certification (L6
× T1–T2)
Despite documented severity of MCP vulnerabilities [13], no
security standard or certification program exists for MCP servers.
The ABOM concept proposed in this paper has no current standard-
ization body. Priority actions: establish an ABOM specification
working group (analogous to NTIA SBOM); develop automated
tooling for ABOM generation and verification; create a security
certification program for MCP servers with mandatory disclosure
requirements.
Estimated difficulty: Medium (standardization). Impact: High
(practical near-term risk reduction).
Research Gap #5
System-Level Accountability Frameworks (L7 × T1–T4)
Component-level interpretability cannot explain multi-step agentic
trajectories [46]. Required: trajectory-level causal attribution
methods; formal verification approaches for agentic planning
sequences; and legal/organizational frameworks that assign clear
liability for agentic harms across the accountability chain described
in Section X.
Estimated difficulty: High (multi-disciplinary). Impact: Without
trajectory-level attribution, deploying agents in healthcare, finance,
or critical infrastructure is defensible only by limiting the agent’s
action scope to fully reversible operations, which removes the
use-case value.
XIV. FUTURE DIRECTIONS
A. Formal Security Models for Agentic Protocols
Cryptographic protocols achieve provable security via formal
models (Dolev-Yao, universal composability). Agentic AI lacks
analogous formal security models. Developing such models requires:
(a) a formal language for specifying agent behavior and security
properties; (b) a model of adversary capabilities in the agentic context;
and (c) composition theorems allowing security proofs for complex
multi-agent systems to be built from component proofs.
B. Runtime Behavioral Attestation
A trusted execution environment for agent actions, providing
cryptographic attestation that actions were sanctioned by a verified
principal hierarchy, would address the accountability gap. Hardware
trusted execution environments (Intel TDX, AMD SEV) provide the
computational substrate; adapting them to the inference stack of LLM
agents (stochastic, large memory footprint, streaming outputs) is a
significant engineering challenge.
C. Temporal Anomaly Detection Infrastructure
Addressing T3 and T4 threats requires building infrastructure
that agents currently lack: (a) persistent, tamper-evident behavioral
logs spanning multiple sessions; (b) statistical baselines for normal
agent behavior specific to each deployment context; and (c) real-time
anomaly scoring that can distinguish malicious behavioral shifts from
legitimate adaptation. This is a systems research problem as much as
an ML problem.
D. Multi-Stakeholder Governance Frameworks
The distributed accountability chain (Section X) requires gov-
ernance frameworks spanning developers, operators, users, and
regulators. Legal frameworks assigning clear liability for agentic
harms, analogous to product liability for physical goods, are an urgent
policy priority as agentic AI deployments reach the scale at which
harmful incidents become statistically inevitable.

19
E. Closing the Defense Parity Gap
LLM agent teams can already autonomously exploit zero-day
software vulnerabilities [99]. No analogous defensive capability exists:
no agent system can autonomously defend against comparable threats.
Developing defensive agents that autonomously detect, analyze, and
mitigate attacks against themselves and their deployment environment
is a long-term research objective requiring community coordination.
XV. COMPARISON WITH EXISTING SURVEYS
Table X provides a systematic comparison of this survey with the
six most closely related prior surveys.
A. Detailed Comparison
Wang et al. [14] organize security challenges around four knowl-
edge gaps: unpredictability of multi-step inputs, complexity in internal
executions, variability of operational environments, and interactions
with untrusted external entities. This framing captures the sources
of agent uncertainty but does not map to architectural components,
making it difficult to derive defense placement decisions. Their
coverage ends April 2024, predating MCP security, steganographic
collusion benchmarks, and zero-day autonomous exploitation by agent
teams.
Chhabra et al. [15] survey agentic AI security with a focus on
evaluation benchmarks and governance frameworks. Their taxonomy
distinguishes prompt injection, jailbreaks, autonomous exploitation,
and multi-agent threats. Relative to our work, they lack the LASM
layer decomposition, the temporality dimension, and detailed treatment
of memory layer attacks and ecosystem supply-chain threats.
Tang et al. [16] propose a structure-aware taxonomy with three
paradigms: External Interaction Attacks, Internal Cognitive Attacks,
and Multi-Agent Collaboration Attacks. This is the closest in spirit to
our layer decomposition, but it conflates L3 (memory) and L4 (tool)
within “Internal” attacks and does not separate L6 (ecosystem) from
L4. The temporality dimension is absent.
Lin et al. [44] provide a dedicated survey of agent memory security,
the deepest treatment of L3 in the literature. Our work incorporates
their taxonomy and extends it by situating L3 threats within the cross-
layer context and connecting them to T3 temporality. They do not
cover other LASM layers.
Hou et al. [13] conduct a targeted security analysis of the MCP
ecosystem. Their empirical vulnerability statistics are highly cited in
our L6 analysis. Their scope is narrower: L6 only, no temporality, no
multi-agent or memory analysis.
MITRE ATLAS [38] and OWASP LLM Top 10 [37] are the
most widely deployed practitioner-facing frameworks for AI security.
MITRE ATLAS organizes AI attacks by Tactics, Techniques, and
Procedures (TTPs), analogous to MITRE ATT&CK for enterprise
systems. OWASP LLM Top 10 enumerates the ten most critical
vulnerabilities in LLM applications by exploitability and business
impact. These frameworks answer the question “what is the attacker
doing?” and are TTP-centric. LASM answers the complementary
question: “where in the system architecture is the control point?” The
two approaches are orthogonal and mutually reinforcing: an ATLAS
technique such as “Prompt Injection” maps to LASM’s L4 layer;
“Model Backdoor” maps to L1 and L6. Practitioners can use ATLAS
TTPs to identify attacker behaviors and LASM to determine where
defenses should be placed. Neither ATLAS nor OWASP LLM Top
10 incorporates a temporal dimension or addresses the higher-layer
threats (L5–L7) that this survey identifies as under-studied.
Foundational work on security and privacy in ML [58], [144]
established the threat taxonomy for classical ML systems; this survey
extends that foundation to the agentic setting, where the attack surface
encompasses not just the model but the entire reasoning-memory-tool-
coordination stack. Collectively, prior work provides deep coverage of
individual attack types (ATLAS, OWASP) or individual architectural
layers (Lin et al. [44], Hou et al. [13]), but no prior work provides:
(a) a unified architectural framework spanning all seven layers with
formal layer boundaries; (b) a temporal dimension for classifying
when attacks manifest; (c) a cross-layer defense taxonomy covering
temporal coverage; or (d) systematic identification of the high-risk,
under-studied zone at {L5–L7} × {T3–T4}.
XVI. CONCLUSION
AI agents are deployed in production systems managing enterprise
files, executing financial transactions, writing and deploying code, and
coordinating complex multi-step workflows on behalf of millions of
users. Their security is a societal concern, not merely a technical one.
The central argument of this survey is that agentic AI security
requires its own analytical framework. Borrowing from stateless LLM
safety or traditional software security leaves the most dangerous
threats unaddressed. The Layered Attack Surface Model (LASM)
provides a seven-layer decomposition that assigns each threat class to
the architectural component that must defend against it. The attack
temporality dimension shows that the threats with the highest real-
world impact are not instantaneous attacks at well-studied layers, but
slow-burning, high-layer attacks that existing defenses and benchmarks
leave unmeasured.
Through a systematic review of 94 papers, we documented the state
of the art across all seven layers, proposed a cross-layer defense tax-
onomy, surveyed evaluation benchmarks and their gaps, and identified
five research gaps: Gap 1 (cross-session attack benchmarks), Gap 2
(emergent misalignment detection), Gap 3 (steganographic collusion
detection), Gap 4 (ABOM standardization and MCP certification),
and Gap 5 (system-level accountability frameworks).
Agent security is a distributed systems problem embedded in an
adversarial ecosystem, not a model problem, not a prompt problem,
and not a challenge amenable to better safety training alone. Of
the five gaps identified in Section XIII, Gaps 1, 4, and 5 offer the
clearest path to near-term engineering progress; Gaps 2 and 3 have
no short-term solution path.
Gaps 1, 4, 5 (near-term tractable). Gap 1 (multi-session evaluation
infrastructure) is prerequisite to measuring progress on all slow-burn
threats. Cross-session replay infrastructure, memory state serialization,
and ground-truth labeling are engineering problems for which the
necessary components already exist; they have not been assembled for
the security evaluation use case. Gap 4 (ABOM standardization and
MCP security certification) is an engineering coordination problem
rather than a research problem, tractable in the near term and
with disproportionate risk-reduction value, analogous to how SBOM
adoption transformed software supply-chain security after NTIA
mandated it. Gap 5 (system-level accountability frameworks) spans
formal trajectory attribution, mandatory action logging, and legal
liability allocation; it is a prerequisite for deploying agents in high-
stakes domains regardless of whether the other gaps are solved.
Gaps 2 and 3 (fundamental open problems). Emergent misalign-
ment detection (Gap 2) and general steganographic collusion detection
(Gap 3) have no near-term solution path: both require detecting
statistical anomalies in high-dimensional stochastic distributions, and
both face adversaries who can adapt to known detection methods.
Sustained basic research is required before engineering progress
is tractable. The intellectual foundations exist across statistical
process control, information theory, formal verification, and distributed
systems; the work is their adaptation to the specific distributional and
behavioral properties of agentic AI systems.
Limitations
This survey has four limitations. First, the corpus was coded by a
single author; the conservative ambiguity rule in Section II and the per-
cell citations in Table V mitigate but do not replace formal inter-rater
reliability assessment, which remains future work. Second, coverage
ends April 2025; the field publishes rapidly and some recent work is
missing. Third, LASM organizes threats by architectural layer, but
agent deployments vary; an agent without persistent memory has no
L3 attack surface, and the framework should be applied to the actual
deployment configuration. Fourth, the MCP vulnerability statistics
