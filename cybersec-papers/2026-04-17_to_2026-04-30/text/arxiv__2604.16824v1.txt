Abstract
Multi-turn jailbreak attacks progressively erode LLM safety alignment
across seemingly innocuous conversation turns, achieving success rates
exceeding 90% against state-of-the-art models. Existing alignment-based
and guardrail methods suffer from three key limitations: they require costly
weight modification, evaluate each turn independently without modeling
cumulative safety erosion, and detect attacks only after harmful content
has been generated. To address these limitations, we first formulate the
proactive early jailbreak detection problem with a new metric, detection lead,
that measures how early an attack can be detected before the LLM com-
plies. We then propose SAFEDREAM, a lightweight world-model-based
framework that operates as an external module without modifying the
LLM’s weights. SAFEDREAM introduces three components: (1) a safety
state world model that encodes LLM hidden states into a compact safety
representation and predicts how it evolves across turns, (2) CUSUM de-
tection that accumulates weak per-turn risk signals into reliable evidence,
and (3) contrastive imagination that simultaneously rolls out attack and be-
nign futures in latent space to issue early alarms before jailbreaks occur.
On three multi-turn jailbreak benchmarks (XGuard-Train, SafeDialBench,
SafeMTData) against 8 baselines, SAFEDREAM achieves the best detection
timeliness across all benchmarks (1.06–1.20 turns before compliance) while
maintaining competitive false positive rates and outperforming baselines
in detection quality.

Introduction
Large language models (LLMs) (Yang et al., 2024) can be exploited for harmful purposes
such as generating misinformation or illegal instructions. Despite safety mechanisms like
alignment training and guardrails (Inan et al., 2023; Han et al., 2024), multi-turn jailbreak
attacks remain highly effective (Mazeika et al., 2024): by spreading malicious intent across
seemingly innocuous turns, they gradually erode LLM safety barriers until the model
complies. Crescendo (Russinovich et al., 2025) achieves a 98% binary success rate against
GPT-4, FITD (Weng et al., 2025) averages 94% Attack Success Rate (ASR) across seven LLMs,
and STAR (Li et al., 2026) reaches 94.5% Safety Failure Rate (SFR) against GPT-4o.
Recent work has explored various approaches to counter jailbreak attempts. Alignment-
based methods such as Circuit Breaker (Zou et al., 2024), X-Boundary (Lu et al., 2025), and
TARS (Kim et al., 2025) modify LLM weights to resist attacks. Guardrail methods such as
WildGuard (Han et al., 2024) and GuardReasoner (Liu et al., 2025) deploy external classifiers
to evaluate each turn independently. However, these approaches suffer from three key

weights, incurring significant computational overhead and precluding deployment on
closed-source models. Second, both paradigms typically evaluate each turn independently,
making them unable to defend against multi-turn attacks where the LLM complies only
after a gradual safety erosion accumulated across multiple turns. Third, neither paradigm
formulates the problem of anticipating jailbreaks before they succeed; by the time these
methods detect an attack signal, the LLM may have already generated harmful content.
To address these three limitations, we reformulate the multi-turn jailbreak detection problem
(Section 2). Unlike previous formulations, we explicitly incorporate detection lead time,
measuring how many turns earlier a jailbreak attempt can be detected before the LLM
actually complies. We then propose SAFEDREAM, a world-model-based safety module that
enables proactive, early jailbreak detection in multi-turn dialogues. SAFEDREAM addresses
the three limitations above: it operates as a lightweight external module without modifying
the LLM’s weights (solving limitation 1); it models safety state dynamics across turns rather
than evaluating each turn independently (solving limitation 2); and it proactively detects
attacks before the LLM complies through contrastive imagination (solving limitation 3).
SAFEDREAM consists of three key components. First, we introduce a Safety State World Model:
a lightweight Transformer that predicts future safety states from the safety state history and
user actions, operating entirely in a low-dimensional safety state space (Section 3.1). Second,
we employ Cumulative Sum (CUSUM) detection to aggregate risk signals across multiple
turns, demonstrating how multi-turn safety erosion can be effectively captured through
sequential evidence accumulation (Section 3.2). Third, in ambiguous situations, in order
to detect jailbreaks as early as possible, we use our Safety State World Model to conduct
contrastive imagination, which rolls out possible attack and benign futures and enables early
alarms before a jailbreak actually occurs (Section 3.3).
We evaluate SAFEDREAM on three multi-turn jailbreak benchmarks (XGuard-Train (Rahman
et al., 2025), SafeDialBench (Cao et al., 2025), SafeMTData (Ren et al., 2025)) against 8
baselines. Our key contributions are:
• We formulate the proactive early jailbreak detection problem with a new metric, detection
lead, that measures how early attacks can be detected before the LLM complies.
• We propose SAFEDREAM, a world-model-based framework consisting of a safety state
world model, CUSUM detection, and contrastive imagination for proactive early jailbreak
detection.

Problem Formulation
Multi-turn dialogue and safety model.
We study jailbreak detection given an ongoing
dialogue between a user and an LLM, the goal is to decide whether the user is steering
the conversation toward harmful content before the LLM complies. At turn t, the user
sends utterance ut and the LLM generates response yt. We define the observation ot =
(u1, y1, . . . , yt−1, ut) as the conversation prefix immediately before yt is generated. A safety
model f (ot) ∈{0, 1} infers whether jailbreak intent is present; if f (ot) = 1, the LLM refuses
to respond.
Attack model and compliance point.
In a multi-turn jailbreak, an adversarial user pro-
gressively guides the LLM toward harmful content through seemingly innocuous messages,
gradually eroding the LLM’s safety alignment until the model fully complies. We call this
critical point the compliance point c, formally defined as the first turn at which the majority
of independent safety classifiers flag the LLM’s response as harmful (see Section 3.4 for the
labeling procedure). Detecting the attack after the compliance point (t > c) is too late, as
harmful content has been generated.
Proactive early jailbreak detection.
Previous defenses show little concern for how early
an attack can be detected: guardrails (Han et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2025) flag harmful content
only after it appears, and alignment-based methods (Zou et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2025)
resist attacks but do not anticipate compliance. We formulate the proactive early jailbreak
detection problem: the safety model f predicts whether the dialogue will eventually reach a
compliance point, and we measure timeliness via the detection lead:
Lead = c −tdet,
where tdet = min{t : f (ot) = 1}.

SafeDream: Proactive World Model for Early Jailbreak Detection
We present SAFEDREAM, a world-model-based framework for proactive early jailbreak
detection (Fig. 2). The framework introduces a safety state world model (Section 3.1) that
encodes the LLM’s hidden state into compact latent safety states and learns to predict future
safety states. To reliably detect jailbreak, we employ cumulative sum detection (Section 3.2),
which aggregates weak per-turn risk signals into robust statistical evidence. Building on
the world model and cumulative detection, we propose contrastive imagination (Section 3.3),
which simultaneously predicts attack and benign future roll-outs to quantify vulnerability,
enabling proactive early attack detection. We describe the training procedure in Section 3.4.
3.1
Safety State World Model
Proactive early detection requires the ability to predict future safety outcomes from the
current state. To this end, we design a world model over safety states, consisting of two
components: (1) a safety state extractor eϕ that compresses each turn into a low-dimensional
safety state vector zt = eϕ(ot), capturing the safety-relevant information of that turn; and
(2) a transition model gθ that integrates safety states from previous turns and predicts future
safety states based on user actions at = ψ(ut). Each safety state is converted to risk score by
a discriminator dω(zt) →rt ∈[0, 1], where a value of 1 indicates likely jailbreak compliance.
Safety state extraction.
At each turn t, we feed the conversation prefix ot through the
frozen Qwen2.5-7B (Yang et al., 2024) and extract the hidden state ht ∈R3584 from layer
19, which Wollschl¨ager et al. (2025) identify as the layer most strongly associated with
safety-related refusal behavior in Qwen-family models. To extract safety-relevant informa-
tion, we project ht onto K=5 safety-related directions on the concept cone (Wollschl¨ager
et al., 2025), yielding a safety signature st ∈R5. We further enrich st via Perceiver-style
cross-attention (Jaegle et al., 2022) that uses st as queries into ht, producing a learned
64-dimensional extension. The final safety state is:
zt = [st ; XAttnϕ(st, ht)] ∈R69,

where the first 5 dimensions are frozen cone projections and the remaining 64 are learned
enhancement through ϕ. Architectural details are in Appendix A.2.

Safety discriminator.
A two-layer MLP dω(zt) →rt ∈[0, 1] maps each safety state to a risk

imagination module (Section 3.3).

3.3
Contrastive Imagination for Early Attack Detection
When CUSUM enters the gray zone, instead of waiting for more turns, SAFEDREAM lever-
ages the world model (Section 3.1) to imagine what would happen next. We first construct
two action pools from the training data: an attack pool Aatk containing encoded attack
utterances and a benign pool Aben containing encoded benign utterances. For each condi-
tion γ ∈{atk, ben}, we randomly sample actions from the corresponding pool to simulate
different futures. Specifically, we roll out M=8 trajectories of H=3 steps from the current
state, autoregressively sampling actions, predicting states via gθ, scoring via dω, and accu-
mulating the imagined CUSUM (full procedure in Appendix A.3). After the rollout, we
define the vulnerability score Vt, which compares the imagined CUSUM endpoints between

Training
Labeling.
We obtain turn-level safety labels through majority voting of three independent
classifiers: HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024), GPT-4o (Hurst et al., 2024), and MD-Judge (Li
et al., 2024). The compliance point c is the earliest turn at which a majority of the three
classifiers flag the response as harmful (ℓt ≥0.67), ensuring that the same threshold governs
both training and evaluation. None of these classifiers appears as an evaluation baseline.

Experiments
4.1
Experimental Setup
Datasets.
We evaluate on three multi-turn jailbreak benchmarks that cover complementary
attack characteristics. XGuard-Train (Rahman et al., 2025) (30K conversations, 13 risk
categories, adaptive multi-agent attacks) serves as our primary benchmark due to its scale
and diversity. SafeDialBench (Cao et al., 2025) (4K conversations, 7 distinct attack strategies,
bilingual) tests generalization across diverse attack tactics. SafeMTData (Ren et al., 2025)
(1.7K conversations, actor-network attacks, ∼8.7 turns) provides backward compatibility
with prior work such as TARS. For each benchmark, to prevent the model from learning the
topic shortcuts for detection, we construct topic-matched benign contrasts to force models
to distinguish based on trajectory dynamics rather than topic.
