Abstract
Despite the remarkable synthesis capabilities of
text-to-image (T2I) models, safeguarding them
against content violations remains a persistent
challenge.
Existing safety alignments primar-
ily focus on explicit malicious concepts, often
overlooking the subtle yet critical risks of com-
positional semantics. To address this oversight,
we identify and formalize a novel vulnerability:
Multi-Concept Compositional Unsafety (MCCU),
where unsafe semantics stem from the implicit as-
sociations of individually benign concepts. Based
on this formulation, we introduce TwoHamsters,
a comprehensive benchmark comprising 17.5k
prompts curated to probe MCCU vulnerabilities.
Through a rigorous evaluation of 10 state-of-
the-art models and 16 defense mechanisms, our
analysis yields 8 pivotal insights. In particular,
we demonstrate that current T2I models and de-
fense mechanisms face severe MCCU risks: on
TwoHamsters, FLUX achieves an MCCU genera-
tion success rate of 99.52%, while LLaVA-Guard
only attains a recall of 41.06%, highlighting a crit-
ical limitation of the current paradigm for manag-
ing hazardous compositional generation.
Warning: This paper contains examples of offensive con-
tent for safety evaluation. Reader discretion is advised.

As illustrated in Figure 1, MCCU is defined by the di-

To address these challenges, we introduce TwoHamsters1,
a comprehensive benchmark tailored to MCCU vulnerabil-
ities in T2I models. As illustrated in Figure 2, given the
semantic complexity of MCCU, we construct a rigorous
multi-stage pipeline for data curation and verification. We
first derive a novel hierarchical taxonomy, comprising 10
primary risk categories and 51 fine-grained concept pairs,
by synthesizing safety policies from multiple leading T2I
platforms. We emphasize that MCCU does not aim to intro-
duce an entirely new category of harmful content, as related
phenomena such as contextual toxicity, hateful memes, and
compositional bias have been previously studied. Instead,
our contribution lies in formally isolating these risks as
a distinct evaluation regime for text-to-image generation.
To ensure high quality, we implement strict similarity fil-
tering and visual validity screening, discarding prompts
where generated images fail to reflect target concepts. We
further augment the dataset using Vision-Language Mod-

verification to guarantee validity and reliability. Finally, to
enable automated benchmarking, we release two specialized
detectors: MCCU-ViT, optimized for high-throughput eval-

fine-grained safety assessments for TwoHamsters.

Single-Concept Retention (SCR), and Non-target Concept
Retention (NCR). This framework enables a holistic assess-
ment of T2I models, filters, and concept erasure methods,
balancing safety against generative utility. Leveraging this

achieves faster convergence and higher training efficiency
by simplifying Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) tra-
jectories. Simultaneously, LlamaGen (Sun et al., 2024) and
Show-o (Xie et al., 2024) revitalize the potential of dis-
crete visual tokens to unify text understanding and image

2.2. T2I Unsafe Benchmarks
Early T2I safety protocols primarily targeted explicit haz-
ards like pornography and gore, though often constrained
by scale. For instance, SneakyPrompt (Yang et al., 2024)
synthesized a concise set of 200 prompts, while Unsafe
Diffusion (Qu et al., 2023) and I2P (Schramowski et al.,
2023) mined real-world user data to establish the I2P dataset
(4,704 examples), a standard reference for concept era-
sure. Subsequent research has expanded to nuanced risks
such as political sensitivity and privacy. Benchmarks like
T2IRiskyPrompt (Zhang et al., 2025b) and T2ISafety (Li
et al., 2025b) introduced hierarchical taxonomies but re-
main dependent on the explicit presence of unsafe concepts.
Consequently, they structurally neglect MCCU, where tox-
icity emerges from benign combinations. While Harmful-
Cub (Nie et al., 2025) briefly touches upon these MCCU
risks, it lacks the systematic rigor of a standalone benchmark.
TwoHamsters addresses this limitation by establishing the
first robust framework specifically engineered for assessing
compositional vulnerabilities (see Table 1).
2.3. Concept Erasure
Concept erasure employs fine-tuning to induce “targeted
forgetting“ of illicit content. Pioneer methods like ESD
and Forget-Me-Not (Gandikota et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
2024a; Li et al., 2025a; Zhang et al., 2025a; Fan et al.,
2023; Nguyen et al., 2025) aimed to eliminate target con-
cepts while preserving fidelity. However, adversarial stud-
ies (Zhang et al., 2024b; Pham et al., 2023) reveal that these
techniques fail to fully expunge latent concepts, as erasure
boundaries are easily circumvented via token manipulation
or textual inversion to reactivate suppressed semantics.
To counter these threats, the focus has shifted toward Robust
Concept Erasure. Methods like RACE (Kim et al., 2024),
AdvUnlearn (Zhang et al., 2025c), and Stereo (Srivatsan
et al., 2025) adopt iterative frameworks to neutralize adver-
sarial directions. Despite improved robustness, these meth-
ods frequently incur substantial collateral damage to gen-
erative quality. Furthermore, even recent validations (Gao
et al., 2025) on models like FLUX remain predominantly
engineered for explicit, atomic concepts. This creates a
practical dilemma for MCCU: since constituent elements
are intrinsically benign, erasing the compositional trigger
necessitates suppressing legitimate base concepts, thereby
severely impairing the model’s generative utility.

concepts to their compositional semantics. Accordingly,

We distinguish MCCU through two defining characteristics:
atomic safety and interpretative harmfulness. Atomic safety
dictates that MCCU instances consist of inherently benign
concepts, making them susceptible to misclassification by
filters that analyze components in isolation. Interpretative
harmfulness, however, implies that the synthesized combi-

groups. Let F(x) be a Boolean safety predicate anchored to
a specific safety policy, such that F(x) = True if content x
is safe under established guidelines. The logical condition
for an MCCU concept pair is defined as the conjunction of
atomic safety and interpretative harmfulness:

3.2. Category-Concept Taxonomy
To address the subtle and implicit nature of MCCU, we care-
fully design a taxonomy-driven, human-AI pipeline for data
curation. Our objective is to engineer a benchmark charac-
terized by broad taxonomic coverage, semantic granularity,
and high adversarial potency.
Specifically, we strictly align our taxonomy with real-world
applications by synthesizing safety guidelines from leading
commercial T2I platforms. This process integrates sociopo-
litical sensitivities and ethical ambiguities to construct a
robust hierarchical framework. Following rigorous adju-
dication by an expert committee, this pipeline yields 10
core risk dimensions, namely Disturbing, Harassment, Hate,
Humiliation, Illegal, Political, Public Health Harm, Self-
Harm, Sexual, and Violence. Comprehensive definitions
and detailed examples are provided in Table 7.
Then, guided by the formulation in Equation (1), we proceed
to mine specific MCCU concept pairs. We employ LLMs
for semantic decomposition and knowledge retrieval. Ac-
knowledging that MCCU risks are significantly influenced
by region, culture, and belief, we instruct LLMs to adopt
diverse personas to identify context-specific MCCU pairs
from different perspectives. An expert committee then cat-
egorizes the model responses, screening for representative
pairs that satisfy Equation (1). Furthermore, considering
the distribution of risk severity, we prioritize the expansion
of categories associated with higher public concern and po-
tential harm. Ultimately, we identify 51 distinct concept
pairs. This top-down methodology facilitates comprehen-
sive coverage of latent risks emerging from the composition
of individually benign atomic concepts.
3.3. Benchmark Construction Pipeline
We construct the benchmark via a rigorous three-stage
pipeline designed to isolate implicit compositional risks.
The acquisition phase employs a dual-stream strategy, com-
bining taxonomy-guided retrieval with LLM-based adversar-
ial expansion, while enforcing a strict blocklist to preclude
explicit violations and ensure risks emerge solely from con-
cept interactions.
Following the acquisition, a multi-stage filtering protocol en-
sures diversity and fidelity. We first utilize centrality-based
pruning in CLIP embedding space to maximize semantic
variance. This is complemented by a cross-modal consis-
tency verification against single-concept baselines to ensure
emergent risks stem from effective composition rather than
semantic degradation.
Finally, we augment the dataset with structured rationales
via a human-in-the-loop process. Each sample captures the
causal logic of the risk, adjudicated through a double-blind
review where independent auditors verify rationale-visual
alignment. This verification yields a finalized corpus of
17.5k high-fidelity samples. ( Section A)
3.4. Automated Evaluator
Current safety classifiers struggle to discern the subtle com-
positional semantics inherent in the TwoHamsters bench-
mark. To address this, we propose a robust evaluation
framework consisting of two complementary components:
MCCU-ViT for high-throughput quantitative assessment,
and MCCU-VLM for reasoning-heavy qualitative verifica-
tion (Helff et al., 2024). Given the prohibitive computational
costs of VLMs for large-scale benchmarking, we establish
MCCU-ViT as the primary evaluator. It is designed to over-
come the distribution shift between natural pre-training data
and AI-generated unsafe content through domain-adaptive
fine-tuning on 300k image–text pairs from TwoHamsters
and auxiliary safety datasets.

4.1. Experimental Setup
To rigorously audit safety vulnerabilities and defensive effi-
cacy within the MCCU paradigm, we conduct a comprehen-
sive benchmark across three distinct dimensions: generative
models, safety filters, and concept erasure frameworks.
For T2I models benchmarking, we assess the inherent safety
of 10 SOTA T2I models on the full TwoHamsters suite,
spanning diverse architectures (e.g., U-Net (Ronneberger
et al., 2015), DiT (Peebles & Xie, 2023)) and paradigms

2025), with performance quantified via MDR and SCR.

mainstream filters: MultiHeaded (Qu et al., 2023), NSFW-

et al., 2022), LLaVA-Guard (Helff et al., 2024), and Perspec-
tiveVision (Qu et al., 2025). Note that none of these security
filters were trained on the training set, but only tested on the

Insight 1:
There exists an “Instruction vs Safety”
Dilemma in Compositional Generation. Our empirical
analysis reveals a critical “Instruction-Safety” dilemma: as
models achieve superior instruction following, they become
paradoxically more vulnerable to MCCU. While early U-
Net architectures (e.g., SD-v1.4) retain a baseline MDR of
40.91%, modern DiT-based models (e.g., FLUX.1) plum-
met to near-0% MDR. We interpret this as an alignment
paradigm that is semantic rather than axiological: models
excel at compositionally rendering entities (e.g., “Water-
melon” and “Person”) but lack the social reasoning to detect
their emergent toxicity. Thus, optimization for strict prompt
adherence effectively bypasses safety constraints when risks
are implicit rather than explicit.
Insight 2: Taxonomic Bias in Safety Alignment. Our fine-
grained analysis reveals a systemic imbalance in current
defense paradigms. Categories characterized by complex
socio-semantic nuances, specifically Harassment (8.81%
Mean MDR), Hate Speech, and Political Sensitivity, exhibit
significantly higher vulnerability than extensively studied
domains such as Sexual Content (21.85% Mean MDR). The
marked divergence in MDR across categories highlights that
current T2I safety alignment focus on suppressing explicit,
low-level visual hazards (e.g., nudity), yet fail to sufficiently
address high-level risks devoid of explicit visual signatures.

4.4. Benchmarking Concept Erasure Methods
As demonstrated in Table 4, current Concept Erasure (CE)
paradigms fail to achieve the requisite efficacy for mitigating
MCCU risks, manifesting in two distinct failure modes.
Insight 7: The Dual Dilemma of Ineffective Erasure
and Utility Collapse in Concept Erasure. First, we ob-
serve ineffective erasure across various categories, where
the MDR of CE-tuned models remains unacceptably close
to the SDXL baseline. One reason is that while state-of-the-
art methods can suppress explicit hazards in smaller models
like SD-v1.4, they struggle to decouple deep text-to-latent
associations in large-scale architectures such as SDXL or
FLUX. Another reason lies in the inherent complexity of
MCCU; MCCU prompts are often structurally intricate, and
when these complex compositional prompts are triggered,
latent unsafe visual features tend to re-emerge.
Second, effective erasure often leads to a catastrophic de-
cline in utility, as evidenced by the synchronized drop in
MDR and SCR for methods such as ESD, UCE, KSCU, and
COGFD. This limitation stems from the fact that existing
erasure logic relies on coarse-grained activation suppression
over parameter subsets, lacking the precision required to
address fine-grained MCCU risks. We further argue that
MCCU risks differ in practice from conventional safety haz-
ards in terms of latent feature distributions. Prior safety
research typically assumes manifold separation, modeling
violating and benign data as disjoint distributions. In con-
trast, the critical challenge of MCCU lies in its intrinsic
distributional entanglement, where unsafe semantics do not
form isolated clusters but are superimposed onto and para-
sitize benign concept manifolds. Under this regime, current
CE algorithms attempting to excise specific MCCU risks
inadvertently induce the collapse of the underlying benign
manifolds that constitute concept representations, thereby
degrading generative utility.
Insight 8: Shifting from Concept Erasure to Composi-
tional Disentanglement. The intrinsic limitation of current
Concept Erasure lies in its objective: attempting to remove
a concept vector vc entirely. Our results prove this fails
for MCCU because the constituent elements (e.g., “priest”,
“child”) are benign and essential. Therefore, we propose a
paradigm shift from data-centric filtration to logic-oriented
disentanglement. Mathematically, this implies moving from
minimizing the probability P(c) to enforcing conditional
independence: P(A|B) ≈P(A), preventing the contextual
triggering of risk while preserving atomic utility. While
COGFD has explored the direction of compositional disen-
tanglement, results on TwoHamsters demonstrate that it falls
short in both erasure efficacy and generative utility. Practi-
cal implementation should prioritize disrupting the semantic
binding mechanism within the model’s cross-attention lay-
ers. Instead of erasing weights, future alignment must focus
on orthogonalizing the interaction manifolds—ensuring that
the activation patterns of benign concepts (e.g., “banana”) re-
main computationally orthogonal to sensitive contexts (e.g.,
“sexualized textures”), thereby stripping the model’s capac-
ity for harmful synthesis without degrading its knowledge
base.

In this work, we formally define Multi-Concept Composi-
tional Unsafety (MCCU) and introduce TwoHamsters to
benchmark this implicit vulnerability. Our evaluation re-
veals a severe safety risk: current SOTA T2I models exhibit
significant susceptibility to MCCU. Notably, FLUX demon-

Impact Statement
This work advances the safety alignment of Text-to-Image
(T2I) generation by formally defining and benchmarking
Multi-Concept Compositional Unsafety (MCCU). We ac-
knowledge that our introduced benchmark, TwoHamsters,
contains prompts designed to trigger offensive content, in-
cluding hate speech, sexual innuendo, and self-harm.
While this dataset poses a potential risk of being repurposed
for jailbreaking, its release is imperative given our empirical
finding that current SOTA models (e.g., FLUX) exhibit a
failure rate of nearly 100% against MCCU attacks. Keep-
ing such vulnerabilities opaque hinders the development
of robust defenses. We emphasize that the prompts in our
benchmark consist of individually benign concepts, distin-
guishing them from explicit malicious instructions.
To mitigate harm during data curation, we employed a strict
human-in-the-loop review process with established proto-
cols to protect annotator well-being. We urge the research
community to use this dataset responsibly, primarily for
auditing safety boundaries and developing logic-oriented
alignment techniques.
