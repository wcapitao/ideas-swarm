Abstract. The X24 multivariate signature scheme was introduced by
Di Muzio, Feussner, and Semaev at PQCrypto 2026. It offers remarkably
short signatures, together with a new design approach for multivariate
signatures that departs from the typical UOV and HFE frameworks.
In this work, we present an efficient cryptanalysis of X24. Our attack
recovers the secret key from the public key in time O(q · poly(n)), where
n is the number of field elements in the signature, and q is the order of
the finite field. An implementation of the attack recovers the secret key
in a few minutes on the full X24 parameters.
The attack makes essential use of the exterior algebra, and shows a dif-
ferent way of using that algebra for multivariate cryptanalysis, compared
to the wedge attack introduced by Ran at Eurocrypt 2026. Another no-
table feature of the attack is that it eventually reduces the cryptanalysis
of X24 to the cryptanalysis of a McEliece variant using Generalized Reed-
Solomon codes, drawing an unexpected connection between multivariate
and code-based cryptanalysis.

Introduction
The transition towards post-quantum security is an important target of current
cryptographic research. The intent is to prepare for the event where efficient
quantum computers become available. While the probability and timeline of that
event remain a matter of debate, the potential impact of adversaries equipped
with powerful enough quantum machines is well-established [19,33]. Such adver-
saries would be able to break most public-key cryptography deployed today, po-
tentially crippling communication networks, banking systems, and indeed much
of the digital infrastructure modern life relies on. Post-quantum cryptography
aims to lay the groundwork for the post-quantum transition, by building primi-
tives and protocols that remain secure in the face of such adversaries.
This effort is pursued in conjunction with standardization bodies including
the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) as well as indus-
trial and governmental stakeholders, with the aim to bring secure post-quantum
solutions to market within a relatively short time frame (in regard to the con-
siderable amount of time-consuming work required to achieve that goal, from

construction to cryptanalysis, optimization, vetting, standardization, implemen-
tation, industrial deployment, etc). The National Security Agency (NSA) has
set a timeline for the transition to post-quantum cryptography by 2035 [23]. Re-
cently, Google and Cloudflare have both accelerated their own internal timeline
for post-quantum security to 2029, reflecting growing industrial concern [17,39].
This decision was due in part to new results achieving factorization and discrete
logarithm computation over elliptic curves with quantum circuits much smaller
than was previously known to be possible [1].
Multivariate cryptography. The scheme targeted in this article is X24 [14]. It
is a post-quantum digital signature algorithm, based on multivariate cryptogra-
phy. In the general landscape of post-quantum cryptography, digital signatures
are a central tool, and multivariate signatures in particular are attractive in
view of their short signature sizes. Among candidates selected by NIST at the
outcome of their first call for post-quantum primitives, the lattice-based scheme
FALCON achieves the shortest signatures, at around 650 bytes [15]. For compar-
ison, multivariate signatures such as UOV, currently a second-round candidate
in the ongoing second NIST call for additional post-quantum signatures, achieve
signatures in the order of 100 bytes [6].
The main drawback of multivariate signatures is large public keys. Currently,
all four multivariate signatures that are part of the second round of the NIST call
for additional digital signatures are variants of UOV (UOV, QR-UOV, MAYO
and SNOVA [5,6,16,38]). The main goal of these constructions is to decrease
the size of the UOV public key by introducing additional structure. To our
knowledge, few modern designs for multivariate signatures depart from the UOV
framework, with exceptions including the HFE-based scheme from [10].
The X24 scheme. The X24 multivariate signature scheme was presented by
Di Muzio, Feussner, and Semaev at PQCrypto 2026 [14]. The scheme is notable
in at least two regards: first, it achieves remarkably short signatures. Second,
it departs from the current main paradigms to build multivariate signatures:
it is not an UOV variant, nor even an HFE variant [10,29]. Instead, X24 in-
troduces an elegant new construction based around the convolution product,
where the signature algorithm amounts to polynomial factorization. Because of
its new structure, as well as its promising signature and key sizes, the question
of analyzing the security of X24 is natural.
1.1
Contributions
Our main contribution is a theoretically and practically efficient cryptanalysis of
X24. Our attack recovers an equivalent secret key using only the public key, in
time O(q·poly(n)), where n denotes the number of field elements in the signature,
and q > n denotes the finite field order. (Here, we talk about recovering an
equivalent secret key, rather than the secret key, because the public key of X24
does not uniquely define the secret key; but the effect is the same: recovering
2

an equivalent secret key enables arbitrary forgeries, using the original signature
algorithm.) Let us highlight a few features of the attack.
– The starting point is to compute the bi-orthogonal of the public key matrices
with respect to a certain bilinear map. The bilinear map arises from the
exterior algebra (Section 3.2), which plays an essential role. The exterior
algebra has seen a recent surge of interest for multivariate cryptanalysis,
mainly following the pioneering wedge attack introduced by Ran at the rump
session of Eurocrypt 2025 [30], and subsequently published at Eurocrypt
2026 [31]. Our usage is quite different. Among other properties, our use of the
exterior algebra works over fields of any characteristic, whereas the wedge
attack currently requires even characteristic3 [31,8,37]. This is interesting
insofar as our result provides evidence that the exterior algebra is a powerful
tool for the cryptanalysis of multivariate schemes beyond the wedge attack
and its variants.
– The expanded public key obtained via the bi-orthogonal can be described
exactly as the vector space of matrices ES = {STHS : H is Hankel}, where
S is the secret key of X24, and Hankel matrices are matrices with constant
anti-diagonals (cf. Section 3.4). We characterize matrices of rank one in ES,
and prove that ES must contain at least n+2 (pairwise linearly independent)
such matrices whenever q > n. We use the kernel algorithm for MinRank
computation [18] in order to extract those rank-one matrices from ES. Prov-
ing unconditional bounds on the performance of a MinRank algorithm in a
situation where there are many rank-one matrices would usually be tricky,
especially considering known analyses of the kernel algorithm are heuristic.
Thanks to specific properties of Hankel matrices, in our setting it is possi-
ble to formally prove that the MinRank step terminates in expected time

– Finally, it turns out that the set of rank-one matrices produced by the Min-

ture scheme: a McEliece variant based on Generalized Reed-Solomon codes
[34]. The secret key of the resulting McEliece variant instance is essentially
the same as the secret key of the target X24 instance, so that breaking the
McEliece variant implies breaking X24. The McEliece variant in question is
known to be insecure: its secret key can be recovered in polynomial time
using an algorithm by Sidelnikov and Shestakov [34]. This yields a complete
break of the original X24 instance. To our knowledge, this is the first time
that the public key of a given scheme (X24, which is a multivariate scheme)
essentially reduces to the public key of a scheme belonging to a different
branch of cryptography (a McEliece variant, which is a code-based scheme).
This is certainly surprising, although some similarities between multivariate
and code-based cryptography are discussed in the related work section.

do not expect that this countermeasure would be viable in terms of performance
or security (see Appendix B).

1.2
Related work
Exterior algebra. The exterior algebra has made an appearance in cryptog-
raphy constructively [36], and for the cryptanalysis of multivariate signatures
following the UOV framework [21,8,37]. The latter series of works are extensions
of Ran’s wedge attack [31]. As noted earlier, our usage is different: in the wedge
attack, the exterior algebra appears because in characteristic 2, the polar forms
of quadratic forms (which make up the public key of UOV and its variants) are
alternating. In our case, the exterior algebra arises mainly from Lemma 1, and
from the bilinear map ϕ in Section 4.1. This suggests that the exterior algebra
has a role to play in the cryptanalysis of multivariate schemes beyond the wedge
attack.
Bi-orthogonal. Beside the exterior algebra, the other key technique in the ini-
tial step of our attack is to consider the bi-orthogonal of the public key matrices
with respect to the bilinear map ϕ. The public key of X24 can be regarded as
truncated, in the same vein as the truncation techniques that have been used
notably in the context of multivariate HFE-based schemes [28,10]. Taking the
bi-orthogonal reveals a “completion” of the original public key, in the sense that

it removes this truncation. We are not aware of a technique similar to the bi-
orthogonal in multivariate cryptanalysis before. However, to some extent, this
is reminiscent of lattice-based cryptanalysis using orthogonal lattices introduced
in [24] (see the survey [25]), where the completion of an integer lattice ¯L is de-
fined, and is exactly the bi-orthogonal. There, the bilinear map is the Euclidean
inner product: a typical attack builds a lattice L, computes a reduced basis of
its orthogonal lattice, removes a few elements, and computes a reduced basis
of the new orthogonal lattice. When the map ϕ is a non-degenerate symmetric
bilinear functional over a vector space V , we also remark that the notion of bi-
orthogonal with respect to ϕ matches the notion of bidual with respect to ϕ. In
all of these settings, the bi-orthogonal or bidual of a set x can be interpreted as
a completion or closure of x (for a notion of completion or closure that depends
on each setting).
MinRank problem. The MinRank problem plays an important role in both
multivariate and code-based cryptography [3,2]. For algorithms based on the
minors or support-minors modeling, the complexity analysis typically relies on a
regularity or genericity assumption [4]. Here, we use the kernel attack by Goubin
and Courtois [18], because specific properties of Hankel matrices in our setting
allow for a provable bound on the running time without requiring additional
assumptions. We note that other MinRank algorithms would likely also perform
well, but leave their analysis in our setting as an open problem.
Relationship with code-based cryptography. A striking aspect of our at-
tack is that it eventually reduces the cryptanalysis of a multivariate signature
scheme (X24) to the cryptanalysis of a code-based signature scheme (a McEliece
variant using Generalized Reed-Solomon codes [34]). In general, multivariate and
code-based schemes are quite different branches of cryptography, and we do not
want to make too much of a connection between the two areas. Nevertheless,
the fact that such a reduction could occur may be explained to some degree by
limited similarities between multivariate and code-based signatures.
In both cases, the traditional paradigm to build hash-and-sign signatures is
similar: build a trapdoor function by obfuscating a trapdoored quadratic system
(resp. a linear code with a known efficient decoding algorithm). In both cases,
in addition to relying on a general assumption such as the hardness of average-
case MQ (resp. average-case hardness of decoding a random code), such signature
schemes rely on a more ad-hoc structured assumption that the trapdoor function
is hard to distinguish from a random quadratic system (resp. a random code).
Furthermore, in the case of algebraic codes such as Reed-Solomon, Gabidulin or
Goppa codes, polynomial systems over a finite field play a central role, as they
do in multivariate cryptosystems.
Bridging the two areas was explored in an earlier work by Smith-Tone and
Tone [35], on the constructive side (rather than the cryptanalytic side in this
work). The recent syzygy-based McEliece distinguisher by Randriambololona [32]
is another example of a work that targets code-based cryptography, using tools
5

that could also apply to multivariate cryptography (for example, one could try
to use Betti numbers to distinguish polynomial systems arising from the public
keys of multivariate cryptosystems from random polynomial systems, although
we are not yet aware of a successful attempt to do so).
Overall, the use of McEliece-style structured assumptions outlined above
results in schemes that bring code-based cryptography a little closer with mul-
tivariate cryptography, even if the two areas are quite different in general. This
contrasts with other approaches that try to bring code-based cryptography closer
to lattice-based cryptography, and try to avoid relying on McEliece-style indis-
tinguishability assumptions, such as [13,12].

It can be observed that for any Vandermonde vector v = (1, α1, . . . , αn−1),
the entry at row i and column j of vvT is αi+j, in particular vvT is a Hankel
matrix of rank one. The same is true for enenT. It follows that STvvTS =
(STv)(STv)T is a rank-one matrix in ES, for every Vandermonde vector v. In
Section 5, we prove that all rank-one matrices in ES are of this form, up to a
scalar multiple. Since there exist q + 1 Vandermonde vectors, the previous point
implies that there exist exactly q+1 non-equivalent rank-one matrices in ES (for
n > 1), where we treat matrices as equivalent whenever they are scalar multiples
of each other.
The second step of the attack is to compute n + 2 such matrices, which
assumes q + 1 ≥n + 2, i.e. q > n. (The real parameters of X24 are q = 256,
n = 24; the case q ≤n is briefly discussed in Appendix B.) Finding matrices of a
given low rank in a given subspace of Mn(K) is called the MinRank problem (cf.
Section 1.2). Here, we want to find rank-one matrices in ES. We use a specific
12

MinRank algorithm due to Goubin and Courtois [18], because it lends itself well
to formal analysis in the Hankel setting (and also because that algorithm is very
fast for the real X24 parameters).
In short, the idea of this algorithm is to pick two random vectors v and w,
and solve for M ∈ES in the linear system:
Mv = 0,
Mw = 0.
That system has 2n linear equations (when viewed over the base field), and the
dimension of ES is 2n −1, so naively we would expect one non-trivial solution
on average. Crucially, when a solution does exist, it is very likely to be a matrix
of rank one. This is because a given rank-one matrix M = abT satisfies Mv = 0
and Mw = 0 as soon as bTv = 0 and bTw = 0, which holds with probability
roughly q−2. That probability decreases exponentially with the rank of M. It
follows that the sampling algorithm over ES induced by picking v, w at random
and solving the previous system is heavily concentrated in rank-one matrices.
We repeat the process until we have sampled n + 2 non-equivalent rank-one
matrices in ES. The formal analysis of the running time is quite long (unlike the
running time itself), so we omit it here. In the end, we are able to prove that
the expected time to obtain the desired n + 2 matrices is bounded by O(qnω+1)
for large enough q (the precise bound is given in Proposition 3).

Roadmap
Section 3 contains necessary preliminaries, including an introduction to the exte-

recover the secret key [34], completing the attack on X24. Finally, Section 7
discusses experimental results.

3.2
Exterior algebra
We already gave an intuitive presentation of the exterior algebra in the tech-
nical overview (Section 2). That presentation gives a correct description of the
exterior algebra (up to isomorphism), but it is not a standard definition. In that
description, the unknowns X1, . . . , Xn (assimilated with the canonical basis of
Kn) may appear to play a special role. That is not the case. Any automorphism
of Kn, sending X1, . . . , Xn to any other basis, extends to an algebra automor-
phism. This makes it apparent that the unknowns X1, . . . , Xn play no special
role, beside forming a basis of the underlying vector space.
Overall, the simple description of the exterior algebra from Section 2 amounts
to choosing a basis X1, . . . , Xn of Kn, then describing the behavior of the algebra
over that basis (which also generates the algebra). In contrast, standard defini-
tions of the exterior algebra are independent of a choice of basis. We provide
such a definition next. This will also serve to introduce important properties
that will be needed later.
The following presentation of the exterior algebra is valid over any field. Be-
cause the X24 scheme is over F28, we will be especially careful to write statements
that are also true in characteristic two. We will sometimes treat two algebraic
structures as being equal when they are equal up to isomorphism.
Remark. Compared to presentations of the exterior algebra in some prior
works [31,8], we are more explicit regarding its relationship with alternating
tensors and matrices, as we make frequent use of that relationship.

6.2
Application to X24 key recovery
By combining the results of Section 4 and Proposition 3, we know that if q > n,
then we can recover n+2 rank-one matrices from ES which correspond to distinct
Vandermonde vectors. More precisely, by Lemma 7, we know n+2 matrices of the
form STvαivαi
TS for 1 ≤i ≤n+2. From there, we can easily recover the values
of n+2 vectors of the form cSTvα with c ∈K∗. This means that we exactly have
access to a matrix of the form STV C where S ∈GLn(K), C = Diag(c1, . . . , cn+2)
and V = [vα1, vα2, . . . , vαn+2] is the matrix that has vαi as columns. This
is exactly the setting of the Sidelnikov-Shestakov attack, which allows one to
retrieve the matrix S in time O(nω). This reveals the secret key corresponding to
the original X24 public key {Ai | 1 ≤i ≤m} (up to equivalence), and concludes
the attack.

Experiments
7.1
Implementation of the attack
We implemented the complete key recovery attack using the Sagemath open-
source mathematical software. For the parameters proposed for X24 (q = 256,
n = 24), our implementation finds an equivalent secret key from a X24 public key
in a few minutes on a standard laptop computer. A link to the implementation
is given in Section 1.1.
Remark on sparsity. Beside time complexity, one of the main bottlenecks
of our implementation is its memory complexity. Because the linear spaces we
consider are quite high-dimensional, the matrices representing the linear appli-
cations that compute the left orthogonal and bi-orthogonal of the public key are
rather large. However, these matrices are very sparse. Our current implementa-
tion does not take advantage of that fact (considering how fast the attack is for
the real X24 parameters, it did not seem necessary). With larger parameters,
using sparse linear algebra could save a large amount of memory.

Supporting evidence for the conjectures. We have performed experiments
to confirm Conjectures 1 and 2 for many values of m, n, and q. Both conjectures
always hold as soon as m ≥4, regardless of the order q of the finite field,
including both odd and even characteristic. In this section, we propose a deeper
look into the behavior of these conjectures, both to help support them, and to
provide some additional insight into the attack.
Fix a sequence of uniformly random full-rank symmetric matrices. Compute
the left orthogonal of the first k matrices, as a function of k. This results in a
sequence of nested subspaces: each subspace contains the next subspace in the
sequence. Conjecture 1 can be reformulated as: this sequence remains stationary
as soon as k ≥4. This is enough to imply the conjecture, because for large
enough k, we know that the left orthogonal is Im f (Theorem 1), as claimed by
Conjecture 1. Similarly, if we compute the left orthogonal of the first k matrices
in an X24 public key of maximal length (i.e. m = 2n −1), the stronger form
of Conjecture 2 can also be reformulated as: this sequence is stationary as soon
as k ≥4. Indeed, similar to the random case, Theorem 3 implies that for large
