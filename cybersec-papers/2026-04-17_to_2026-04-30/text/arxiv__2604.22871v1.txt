Abstract
Automated red-teaming methods for large language models typically optimize
attack prompts within a fixed, human-designed strategy, leaving the attack strategy
itself unchanged. We instead optimize the strategy. We propose AUTORISE,
a method that searches over executable attack programs rather than individual
prompts. At each iteration, a coding agent edits a strategy and a fixed evaluation
harness scores the resulting attacks, returning both a scalar objective and per-
example diagnostics that guide subsequent edits. This allows structural changes,
including new attack components and altered control flow, that prompt-level meth-
ods do not directly express. We also release two benchmark suites developed on
disjoint target sets and evaluate on 11 models from five families against seven
established jailbreak datasets. Across held-out models, AUTORISE improves
average attack success rate by 17.0 points over the strongest baseline, and improves
attack success by up to 16 points on frontier targets with low baseline success rates.
Ablations against parametric and strategy-library baselines suggest that these gains
arise from unrestricted program search, particularly compositional techniques and
control-flow edits. AUTORISE operates in a black-box, inference-only setting,
requiring no fine-tuning, human annotation, or GPU compute.

Introduction
Large language models (LLMs) are now widely deployed in high-stakes applications [OpenAI,
2023, Anthropic, 2024, Google DeepMind, 2024], yet despite extensive safety training they remain
susceptible to jailbreak attacks: carefully crafted prompts that bypass safety guardrails and elicit
harmful content [Zou et al., 2023, Wei et al., 2023]. Jailbreak techniques exploit the tension between
helpfulness and safety through diverse surface forms including persona manipulation, encoding tricks,
hypothetical framing, and prompt injection [Shen et al., 2023, Liu et al., 2024d].
Red-teaming [Ganguli et al., 2022, Perez et al., 2022] has become a core safety practice, but manual
red-teaming is expensive and cannot keep pace with modern deployment cycles. Automated red-
teaming addresses this by using LLMs to generate and iterate on attacks [Perez et al., 2022, Mehrotra
et al., 2024, Chao et al., 2024b], yet existing methods operate at the prompt level: they refine individual
attack prompts within a fixed strategy [Chao et al., 2024b, Mehrotra et al., 2024] or fine-tune attacker
models [Ge et al., 2024, Author, 2025], without modifying the attack methodology itself. This leaves
an open question: can the research loop of forming hypotheses about why certain attacks succeed,
designing new strategies, and validating them be automated end-to-end?
In this work, we answer this question in the affirmative. We present AUTORISE (Autonomous
Red-teaming via Iterative Strategy Evolution), an automated red-teaming method that elevates the
search space from individual prompts to attack strategy code. An AI coding agent edits a single

Python strategy file, a fixed evaluation harness scores the result under a composite objective and
returns rich per-sample diagnostics from a three-judge ensemble, and the agent reads these diagnostics
to form hypotheses about why the score moved. Where prior methods optimize within a fixed attack
algorithm [Chao et al., 2024a, Mehrotra et al., 2024], AUTORISE treats the algorithm itself as the
object of optimization. As the agent operates on code rather than on prompts or parameters, it can
discover attack techniques outside the design space of any fixed algorithm, entirely via API calls with
no model training required. Our specific contributions are enumerated below.
1. Strategy code as the search space. We formulate automated red-teaming as optimization over
executable attack strategies rather than individual prompts. The agent operates on a mutable
strategy file, a Python program that defines how jailbreak prompts are generated, which techniques
are composed, and how targets are routed, lifting the search from a fixed-dimensional prompt
space to the space of programs. This enables not only new technique invention but also structural
moves, such as target-adaptive routing, budget-aware category sampling, and compositional
technique fusion, that neither prompt-level nor strategy-library methods can express. The agent
also accumulates research notes across episodes in a persistent scratchpad, building an evolving
understanding of target model behavior that makes the framework open-ended with respect to the
set of allowed moves.
2. Hypothesis-driven evolution loop. We introduce an evolution loop in which the agent edits
strategy code, runs a fixed-budget experiment cycle, evaluates the result via a composite score
(balancing attack success rate, semantic diversity, novelty, and coverage), and keeps or reverts
changes. A three-judge evaluation ensemble spanning three model families provides fine-grained
per-sample diagnostics that the agent uses for hypothesis-driven root-cause analysis, diagnosing
why a strategy change helped or hurt rather than treating the score as a black-box signal.
3. Search-space ablation. We isolate the contribution of the program-space search space via a
matched 30-cycle ablation with three search-space regimes: parametric (numeric tuning only),
strategy-library (new template-string techniques plus parameters), and unrestricted (full program-
space search). The three arms form a monotonic ordering (parametric < library < program-space),
with the unrestricted arm outperforming the parametric arm by 33.4 and the library arm by 14.9
absolute points of late-phase ASR, quantifying what the agent gains from writing code rather than
only setting numbers or adding templates.
4. Competitive jailbreak benchmarks. We release two benchmarks of 200 prompts each 1: AU-
TORISE-Standard, evolved against four moderate-tier targets (GPT-4o, GPT-4.1, DeepSeek-V3.2,
and Grok 3), and AUTORISE-Frontier, evolved against two frontier targets with strong safety
alignment (GPT-5.4 and o4-mini). Evaluated on 11 models spanning five families against 8 estab-
lished jailbreak baselines [Zou et al., 2023, Mazeika et al., 2024, Chao et al., 2024a, Souly et al.,
2024, Chen et al., 2025b, Jiang et al., 2024b, Zhang et al., 2025a, Liu et al., 2024b], AUTORISE-
Standard improves ASR by 17.0 points over the strongest baseline on held-out models, while
AUTORISE-Frontier improves by up to 16 points on frontier targets where existing benchmarks
largely fail.
5. Low-cost, API-only operation. The entire pipeline operates through inference API calls, requiring
no GPU compute, no fine-tuning, and no human annotation. The full two-benchmark run costs
$116 over ∼15.4 hours of wall-clock time, making strong adversarial evaluation accessible without
specialized infrastructure.

Related Work
2.1
Jailbreak Attacks and Automated Red-Teaming
Jailbreak attacks exploit the tension between helpfulness and safety refusal in LLM training [Wei
et al., 2023, Ganguli et al., 2022, Perez et al., 2022]. Reported attack families include persona
overrides [Shen et al., 2023], gradient-based suffix optimization [Zou et al., 2023], genetic-algorithm
prompt evolution [Liu et al., 2024c], encoding tricks [Jiang et al., 2024a], persuasion framing [Zeng
et al., 2024], iterative dialogue refinement [Chao et al., 2024b, Mehrotra et al., 2024], and multi-
turn escalation [Russinovich et al., 2024, Yu et al., 2024]. Defenses span training-time approaches
(RLHF [Ouyang et al., 2022], Constitutional AI [Bai et al., 2022], adversarial training [Mazeika et al.,

2024]) and test-time filtering [Robey et al., 2023, Jain et al., 2023, Inan et al., 2023], but no defense
has proven robust to adaptive adversaries [Wei et al., 2023]. Static safety benchmarks [Zou et al.,
2023, Mazeika et al., 2024, Chao et al., 2024a] face saturation and contamination concerns [Beyer
et al., 2025, Eiras et al., 2025, Hollinsworth et al., 2025]; JBDISTILL [Zhang et al., 2025b] addresses
this by selecting high-quality subsets from existing attacks.
Automated red-teaming methods can be organized by the search space they optimize over. Token-level
methods such as GCG [Zou et al., 2023] and AutoDAN [Liu et al., 2024c] search over adversarial suf-
fixes using gradients or genetic algorithms. Prompt-level methods such as PAIR [Chao et al., 2024b]
and TAP [Mehrotra et al., 2024] use an attacker LLM to iteratively refine natural-language prompts;
WildTeaming [Jiang et al., 2024b] mines in-the-wild jailbreak tactics; Rainbow Teaming [Samvelyan
et al., 2024] casts the problem as quality-diversity search via MAP-Elites. Strategy-description
methods such as AutoDAN-Turbo [Liu et al., 2024b] and AutoRedTeamer [Zhou et al., 2025] ac-
cumulate a library of natural-language attack strategies that future attempts draw from. In all cases,
the generation pipeline (how strategies are instantiated into prompts, how targets are selected, how
techniques are composed) is fixed code authored once by the researchers.
AUTORISE moves one level higher: the search space is executable strategy code. An AI coding
agent edits the attack pipeline program, enabling parametric, template, and structural changes (new
generation stages, target-adaptive routing, novel composition pipelines) that no fixed-dimensional
parameterization can express. AUTORISE additionally optimizes a multi-objective composite score
(ASR, diversity, novelty, coverage) rather than per-prompt ASR on a single target.
2.2
Agent-Driven Autonomous Research
AUTORISE adapts the AutoResearch paradigm [Karpathy, 2026], in which a coding agent edits
code, runs experiments, and accepts or reverts changes based on a measured score. Concurrent work
applies similar loops to general scientific discovery [Lu et al., 2024, 2025, Baek et al., 2025]. These
systems share the hypothesis-experiment-revise loop but operate in general ML domains rather than
adversarial red-teaming.
3
Methodology
We frame AUTORISE as an optimization problem over attack strategy programs. An AI coding agent
edits a single Python file (strategy.py) encoding the entire jailbreak generation pipeline, and a fixed
evaluation harness executes the strategy against target LLMs and a three-judge ensemble, returning
a composite score together with rich per-sample diagnostics. A cycle is a single edit-and-evaluate
step: the agent rewrites part of the strategy, the harness runs it for a fixed wall-clock budget, and the
edit is kept or reverted based on the score. The search space is the set of valid programs expressible
in strategy.py, which is unbounded and admits parametric, template, and structural changes; the
feedback channel is rich (per-technique rates, per-target breakdowns, judge rationales) rather than a
bare scalar.
3.1
Architecture and Search Space
The system comprises three read-only components that fix the scoring function across cycles: the
evaluation harness (prepare.py), which owns the target LLM pool, judge ensemble, embedding
client (used to compute the diversity and novelty components of the composite score; Section 3.4),
and scoring function; the agent specification (program.md), a natural-language description of the
agent’s autonomy boundaries and per-cycle workflow (full text in Section A.1); and the target and
judge endpoints, accessed indirectly through the harness. The mutable component is a single file, the
strategy program (strategy.py), containing prompt templates, generation parameters, technique
mixing ratios, category targeting logic, post-filtering, and the experiment loop. This is the only
file the agent edits and constitutes the entire search space. The agent’s edit surface is layered from
parametric tweaks (mixing weights, temperatures, batch sizes) through template rewrites (prompt and
system strings) to structural changes (new techniques, multi-stage pipelines, target-adaptive routing,
programmatic prompt builders); Section B.2 expands these three levels and relates them to prior
prompt-level methods.

3.2
Target Pool and Jailbreak Generation
AUTORISE attacks a configurable pool of target LLM deployments simultaneously via chat comple-
tion APIs. During each cycle the strategy selects a target uniformly at random for each attempt, unless
the agent has installed a custom routing rule. The seed strategy generates prompts with a generator
model (a capable LLM distinct from the targets) using a structured template system: a system prompt
establishing a red-team research context, plus a technique-specific user prompt parameterized by the
target harm category. The seed portfolio contains nine attack techniques with configurable mixing
weights (Section B.1), targeting eight harm categories. Generation proceeds in micro-batches with
concurrent API calls, and a post-filter discards degenerate outputs (too short, too long, or beginning
with refusal prefixes).
3.3
Three-Judge Evaluation Ensemble
The evaluation is anchored by an ensemble of three independent LLM-based judges from distinct
model families: GPT-5.2 (OpenAI), Grok-3 (xAI), and DeepSeek-V3.2 (DeepSeek). Each judge
scores a (jailbreak_prompt, target_response) pair with the same structured rubric, out-
putting a binary harmful-compliance verdict, a severity score s ∈{0, . . . , 5}, a confidence estimate,
and a brief rationale. A jailbreak is counted as successful only when a majority of the three judges
agree (≥2/3). The rubric is inspired by StrongREJECT [Souly et al., 2024] but redesigned to focus
on response content rather than proxy dimensions such as convincingness and specificity (Section 4.1
validates it against human annotations; Section C reproduces the full text). Cross-family judging
reduces correlated errors from shared training data and mitigates the risk of embedding any one
provider’s content policy into the scoring signal.
3.4
Composite Score
The scalar score returned to the agent at the end of each cycle is
S = 0.60 · rsuccess + 0.10 · ddiversity + 0.10 · nnovelty + 0.10 · ccategory + 0.10 · ctarget,
(1)
where rsuccess is the fraction of attempts judged successful by majority vote; ddiversity is the mean
pairwise cosine dissimilarity of jailbreak-prompt embeddings; nnovelty is the fraction of current-cycle
prompts whose maximum cosine similarity to any prompt in the most recent three cycles is below
τ = 0.85; ccategory is the fraction of harm categories with at least three successful jailbreaks (requiring
depth, not just breadth); and ctarget is the fraction of target deployments with at least one success.
The multi-objective formulation incentivizes diverse, novel attacks across categories and targets
rather than overfitting to a single technique. S enters the accept/revert decision, but the harness also
exposes a structured diagnostic channel (per-technique success rates, per-target breakdowns, per-
judge verdicts, and sample responses) that the agent uses for hypothesis formation before committing
the next action.
3.5
Hypothesis-Driven Strategy Evolution
Algorithm 1 formalizes the loop, adapted from the AutoResearch paradigm [Karpathy, 2026]. Each
cycle runs for a fixed wall-clock budget B to ensure comparability regardless of strategy complexity.
The agent (Claude Code) inspects the previous cycle’s diagnostics, retrieves relevant prior observa-
tions from a persistent scratchpad, conjectures a causal explanation for the score movement, and
proposes a targeted edit that tests its hypothesis.

4.1
Evaluator Validation
Strategy evolution is driven entirely by the composite reward, so the credibility of every downstream
result depends on the judge ensemble. We validate it against 1,100 human-labeled prompt–response
pairs from three established benchmarks: JailbreakBench (N=100) [Chao et al., 2024a], WildGuard-
Mix (N=500) [Han et al., 2024], and JailJudge (N=500) [Liu et al., 2024a].
Our initial implementation applies the StrongREJECT rubric [Souly et al., 2024] verbatim. It
achieves high recall but excessive false positives because it conflates topical engagement with harmful
compliance (Table 1). We replace it with a response-harm rubric that asks whether the response
provides actionable wrongdoing instructions, materially enables harm, or otherwise facilitates unsafe
behavior (full text in Section C).
The response-harm rubric raises mean F1 from 74.5 to 88.2 with a large gain in precision (66.5% →
91.3%), while recall stays high (82.8–94.5%). As we want to ensure high confidence in our judge’s
jailbreak prediction, all subsequent experiments use the proposed response-harm rubric.
4.2
Search-Space Ablation
We test the central claim, namely that searching the program space discovers attacks prompt- or
parameter-level search cannot, by running AUTORISE in three modes that differ only in the agent’s
action space. All three arms run for 30 episodes against GPT-4.1 from the same 9-technique seed
strategy (DAN-style, roleplay persona, hypothetical framing, gradual escalation, context camouflage,
prompt injection, encoding tricks, few-shot poisoning, token smuggling), with a DeepSeek-V3.2/Grok-
4 generator pool.
5

AR-Parametric. The agent may only edit numeric parameters of the seed strategy: per-technique
mixing weights, temperature, batch size, per-category sampling weights, and judge thresholds. Prompt
templates, helper functions, and control flow are frozen. The action space is a fixed-dimensional
vector of seed-technique knobs. The standing instructions given to the agent, including the exhaustive
list of allowed edits and a post-hoc diff verifier that checks the constraint after every cycle, appear
verbatim in Section A.4.
AR-Library. The agent may add new independent strategy entries to the technique library and tune
all parameters (weights, temperature, batch size, system prompt, category descriptions), but may not
modify the generation pipeline, control flow, or routing logic. Each new technique must be a natural-
language template that the generator LLM interprets, not a programmatic prompt builder. This mirrors
strategy-library methods such as AutoDAN-Turbo that accumulate independent strategy descriptions
but leave the dispatch and generation pipeline fixed. The instructions appear in Section A.3.
AR-Full (unrestricted). The agent has unrestricted edit access to strategy.py: parametric edits
plus prompt-template rewrites, new helper functions, new programmatic prompt builders, target- or
category-adaptive routing, multi-stage pipelines, and any other structural change that compiles. This is
the default AUTORISE mode and exposes the full program space rather than a fixed parameterization
or a prompt-template slot. The full instructions for this arm appear in Section A.2.
What the agents did.
The AR-Library agent added seven new template-string techniques and
rewrote the system prompt with target-specific guidance (its single largest improvement), but each
new template gave only 1–2 cycles of novelty recovery before embedding saturation recurred;
diversity plateaued at ∼0.40 regardless of library size. The AR-Full agent invented eight pro-
grammatic prompt builders that fuse two or more seed techniques into structurally novel sur-
face forms (Table 5): academic_peer_review, incident_debrief, regulatory_inquiry,
classified_cable, hybrid_compound (a cross-fusion meta-technique), sworn_testimony,
procurement_rfp, and training_manual. It also installed three structural control-flow changes
neither narrower arm can express: category-adaptive routing, weighted category sampling, and per-
category framing pools with 42 institutional cover framings. Section D.2 presents the highest-scoring
jailbreak example for each of the eight builders, illustrating the structural novelty of the agent’s
inventions.
Results.
Figure 1 contrasts the three arms on attack success rate and composite reward across the
30 episodes. AR-Parametric improves modestly over the seed baseline and then plateaus: once the
technique weights have converged onto the few seed techniques that work on GPT-4.1, the action
space offers no further gains, and subsequent cycles oscillate within a narrow noise band (peak
ASR=0.55, composite 0.633). AR-Library extends well past this plateau by adding new template-
string techniques, peaking at ASR = 0.83 and composite 0.754 (C21), but hits its own structural
ceiling as each new template gives only 1–2 cycles of novelty recovery before embedding saturation
recurs. AR-Full continues to improve past both plateaus, peaking at ASR = 0.85 and composite
0.779 (C24) with 8/8 category coverage. Broken down by phase, mean late-phase (C18–C30) ASR
is 0.79 for AR-Full versus 0.64 for AR-Library versus 0.46 for AR-Parametric, and mean late-phase
composite is 0.72 versus 0.62 versus 0.56. The monotonic ordering across all three arms (parametric
< library < program-space) is the empirical content of the program-space hypothesis: the richer
the action space, the further the agent reaches. Sections D.3 to D.5 reproduce the full per-cycle
scrapbooks for all three arms.

Strategy evolution setup.
We run AUTORISE twice with different dev pools. AUTORISE-
Standard evolves against four moderate-tier dev targets (GPT-4o, GPT-4.1, DeepSeek-V3.2, Grok 3)

generator pool, and per-episode wall-clock budget (300 s). The two runs share no other state: each
starts from the seed strategy and the agent is free to take its own trajectory.

Transferability across model families.
AUTORISE-Standard exhibits intra-family bias (88% on
GPT-4.1-mini but only 21–23% on cross-family Grok 4 and Kimi-K2.5). AUTORISE-Frontier is
the opposite: its cross-family held-out mean (42.2%) actually exceeds Standard’s (36.3%), with
remarkably uniform ASR across Grok 4 (44.5%), Kimi-K2.5 (40.5%), and Phi-4-mini (41.5%). We
attribute this to a generalization-via-difficulty effect: frontier-grade safety alignment pushes the agent
past family-specific shortcuts toward mechanisms (cipher obfuscation, many-shot context override)
that exploit universal weaknesses in instruction-following. The advantage does not extend to other
frontier OpenAI siblings (GPT-5.2: 8.5%), indicating that alignment strength, not family membership,
is the dominant factor.
Per-prompt reproducibility.
Across three independent re-evaluations on frontier targets, per-
prompt replay rates are only ∼29% (GPT-5.4) and ∼43% (o4-mini) due to streaming stochasticity at
default sampling temperature. Aggregate ASRs are stable across reruns (run-to-run std 0.7–1.9pp),
so the means in Table 2 are robust; all AUTORISE numbers are reported as means over three runs.

Cost Analysis
AUTORISE runs entirely on inference-only API calls, with no GPU rental, no fine-tuning, and no
human annotation. The two runs together cost $115.85 in API spend ($41.07 for Standard, $74.78
for Frontier) over ∼15.4 hours, with judge inference dominating both runs (54–72% of spend) since
every sample is scored by three judges on the full prompt+response context; generator and embedding
costs are negligible. Full per-component cost breakdowns appear in Table 6. Figure 2 shows that
AR-Standard yields near-linear jailbreak accumulation from the start, while AR-Frontier exhibits an
extended exploration phase before cipher and many-shot attacks come online. The marginal cost of
evaluating an additional held-out target is $1–$3 per model at frontier-model rates.
Comparison to prior cost regimes.
Training-based methods [Ge et al., 2023, Liu et al., 2024b]
report hundreds to thousands of GPU-hours; per-target optimization (PAIR, AutoDan-Turbo) avoids
8

training but pays fresh inference per model. AUTORISE pays discovery once ($116) and amortizes
across arbitrary held-out targets, two orders of magnitude cheaper end-to-end.
5
Discussion
Implications for LLM safety.
Our results demonstrate that an autonomous agent with unrestricted
access to the program space discovers attack strategies that transfer across five model families at
a cost under 120. The monotonic ordering in our ablation (parametric < library < program-space)
shows that each expansion of the attacker’s action space unlocks qualitatively new attacks. This
implies that defenders should model adversaries who can modify their attack methodology, not just
their prompts. More broadly, we see agentic red-teaming as the natural next step for adversarial
evaluation: an agent that forms hypotheses, writes code, and iterates on failures captures a threat
model closer to a real-world adversary than any fixed prompt optimizer can.
Limitations.
Our judge-ensemble, while validated against existing jailbreak baselines, uses closed-
source models whose behavior may shift across API versions. The composite score weights are set a
priori; adaptive or preference-based objectives may yield different exploration dynamics. Our ablation
runs on a single dev target (GPT-4.1) and extending it to frontier targets would clarify whether the
program-space advantage grows or shrinks with defender strength.
Ethical considerations and responsible disclosure.
AUTORISE produces attack content for
defensive use. We release benchmarks under a safety-research license, share frontier-target findings
with affected providers prior to publication following established disclosure practices [Ganguli et al.,
2022, Mazeika et al., 2024], and release the evaluation harness and scrapbooks for reproducibility.
6
Conclusion
We presented AUTORISE, an automated red-teaming method that lifts the search space from individ-
ual prompts to executable attack-strategy code. By casting an AI coding agent as the policy and a
fixed evaluation harness as the environment, AUTORISE enables hypothesis-driven optimization over
attack methodologies. The two released benchmarks improve on the strongest baseline by 17.0 points
of held-out ASR on standard targets and 16.2/10.8 points on frontier targets, for $116 in API spend
with no model training. Our three-arm ablation establishes that this gain is causal to the action space:
the monotonic ordering parametric < library < program-space shows that each expansion of the
action space unlocks qualitatively new moves that the narrower spaces cannot express. These results
suggest that the next unit of automation in adversarial evaluation is not a better prompt optimizer
but a better research agent. Natural extensions include giving the agent access to GPU compute
so it can explore attack methods beyond API-based inference, such as reinforcement learning or
gradient-based optimization, and symmetric defense-side evolution for co-evolutionary red-teaming.
