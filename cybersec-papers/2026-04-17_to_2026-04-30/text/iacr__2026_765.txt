MBU: Scalable and Constant-Round Evaluation
of Non-linear Functions in Standard MPC Setting
Min Yang, Dongcan Guo, Zihang Zhou, Jinxuan Du, and Qingshu Meng
Wuhan University, Hubei Province 430072, China qsmeng@126.com
Abstract. After more than four decades of research, multi-party com-
putation (MPC) has achieved remarkable success in handling 2-variable
multiplication and comparison-based functions (e.g., ReLU) with prac-
tical eciency. However, for general non-linear functionssuch as mul-
tiplication of many variables, power, exponential, trigonometric func-
tions, sigmoid, softmax, and GeLUno native MPC algorithm exists as
Beaver-based mutiplication that is constant-round, scalable and exact.
Existing solutions rely on either polynomial approximations (trading pre-
cision for eciency), iterative multi-round protocols like Multiplication-
to-Addition (M2A) conversion (requiring log2 k rounds for k parties), or
Function Secret Sharing (FSS) with lookup tables (introducing quan-
tization errors and large storage, mainly limited to 2-4 parties). These
approaches suer from fundamental trade-os among accuracy, commu-
nication rounds, and scalability.
In this work, we propose a unied mask-broadcast-unmask design pattern
that enables constant-round, scalable and approximation-free evaluation
of a wide range of non-linear functions. Our contributions include:
 A general multiplication protocol for k variables in one round
with optimal O(kn) communication. When k = 2, it reduces to the
classic Beaver triple multiplication; when each secret has only one
non-zero share and k = 2, it becomes the well-known M2A protocol.
 Power functions (xk) in one round.
 Trigonometric functions (sin x, cos x) and exponential func-
tions (ax) in 4 rounds.
 Sigmoid, softmax in 6 rounds.
All these protocols are provably secure in the semi-honest model, sup-
port arbitrary number of parties, introduce no approximation er-
ror beyond plaintext oating-point rounding, and require only constant
communication rounds (16) independent of function complexity. Fur-
thermore, by restricting the random mask to a suitable range (e.g.,
0 ≤r < 2l −2lx), we can reduce the rounds from 1-6 to 1-3. This
work lls the long-standing gap for general non-linear functions in stan-
dard MPC settings, making privacy-preserving machine learning more
practical for modern DNNs.

Multi-party computation (MPC) has seen tremendous progress over the past
four decades, evolving from a theoretical curiosity to a practical tool for privacy-
preserving collaborative computation. In particular, MPC has achieved ecient

1.1
The Problem: No Native Algorithm To Non-linear Functions
Despite decades of research, there is no native MPC algorithm for general non-
linear functions that simultaneously achieves:
1. No approximation error  exact (for algebraic functions) or bitwise
equivalent to plaintext oating-point evaluation (for transcendentals);
2. Constant communication rounds  independent of function complexity
or number of parties;
3. Scalability  support large number of participants.
Existing approaches each suer from critical drawbacks:
 Polynomial approximations (e.g., Taylor series, piecewise polynomials)
are the most common workaround. They approximate non-linear functions
by low-degree polynomials, enabling evaluation via standard MPC multipli-
cation. However, this forces an inherent trade-o: higher-degree polynomi-
als improve accuracy but increase rounds and communication; lower-degree
polynomials are ecient but introduce approximation errors that can de-
grade model accuracy, often requiring ne-tuning. No polynomial approxi-
mation can achieve exactness.
 Multiplication-to-Addition (M2A) conversion achieves exact results
(up to oating-point rounding) by converting multiplicative shares to ad-
ditive shares. However, existing M2A-based protocols require ⌈log2 k⌉com-
munication rounds for k parties, which becomes a bottleneck in low-latency
settings or when many such conversions are needed.
 Function Secret Sharing (FSS) with lookup tables precomputes func-
tion values for discretized input bins. While achieving constant online rounds,
this method introduces quantization error due to nite bin granularity.
Reducing the error requires exponentially more bins, leading to large storage
and oine overhead. Usually they only support 2-4 parties.
In summary, after 40 years of MPC research, there is no native algorithms
for general non-linear functions that is constant-round, scalable and exact and
avoids the pitfalls of approximation, high rounds, or quantization.
1.2
Our Approach: Mask-Broadcast-Unmask
We introduce a systematic framework based on a simple yet powerful design
pattern: mask-broadcast-unmask. The idea is to shift complexity to an oine
phase that precomputes structured random tuples, and to design an online phase
where parties:

3.1
Core Design Pattern
All our online protocols follow a simple, unied pattern that guarantees constant
rounds:
1. Each party Pi masks its share xi with a precomputed random ri and broad-
casts xi + ri.
2. All parties reconstruct x + r = P
i(xi + ri) and compute g(x + r) locally,
where g is an auxiliary function (often the same as f or a simple variant).
3. Using precomputed shares of functions h(r), where h() is a function related
to f(), each party removes the eect of r to obtain a share of f(x).
This structure guarantees constant rounds because the only communication is
the initial broadcast; all subsequent steps are local.
We now present concrete instantiations of this pattern for the set of common
non-linear functions used in privacy-preserving machine learning. All protocols
are exact (as dened above), require constant rounds, and have total communi-
cation linear in n.

Practical optimization: The wrap protocol requires expensive 4 rounds of
communication. Though in the special case of two parties, the wrap protocol
can be executed in 3 rounds, it is still expensive. To make our schemes more
practical while preserving strong security, we observe that in typical machine
learning settings the eective bit-length lx of the input x is small (e.g., lx ≤14),
whereas the ring modulus is 2l with l = 32 or 64. The expensive wrap protocol
(4 rounds) is only needed when x + r ≥2l. We can entirely avoid this overhead
by choosing the random mask r such that x + r < 2l always holds. Two simple
approaches achieve this:
1. Probabilistic security: let r be uniform over Z2l. The overow probability
is at most 2lx/2l = 2lx−l, which for lx = 14, l = 64 is only 2−50  negligible
in practice.
2. Deterministic security: restrict r to the range 0 ≤r < 2l −2lx. Since
x < 2lx, we have x + r < 2lx + (2l −2lx) = 2l. This eliminates only a
negligible fraction 2lx/2l = 2lx−l of the ring, thus preserving almost full
entropy.
Consequently, the wrap protocol is omitted entirely, saving 4 communication
rounds for all protocols that rely on it (exponential, trigonometric functions,
etc.). However, in the following protocol, we still use the wrap protocol for com-
pleteness.

Oine Precomputation
Our protocols require two types of precomputed tuples:
Type I (Algebraic Correlations). For multiplication and power functions,
we require Beaver triples (r, s, r · s) and their generalizations. These can be
generated eciently using pseudo-random correlation generators (PCG) [4,15]
or oblivious transfer extensions [13]. These techniques are well-established and
can be instantiated with linear communication and constant rounds in the oine
phase.
Type II (Transcendental Correlations). For transcendental functions,
we require tuples (r, h(r)) where h ∈{sin, cos, exp, etc.}. Unlike algebraic correla-
tions, these involve non-linear, non-polynomial relations and cannot be generated
by PCG-based methods by now.
Generating Transcendental Correlations. The generation of such tuples
is an independent research problem that has received increasing attention.
Several approaches exist:
 Trusted Dealer Model. A common simplication in MPC literature [2]
assumes a trusted oine dealer that generates random r, computes h(r)
locally, and distributes secret shares. This suces for security proofs when
the dealer does not interact with online inputs.
 Function Secret Sharing (FSS). Recent work [3] extends FSS[5] to sup-
port trigonometric functions via piecewise polynomial approximation [25].
For exponential functions, FSS-based constructions remain an open chal-
lenge, though xed-point approximations can be used [23,21].
 Secure Computation in the Oine Phase. The parties can jointly com-
pute h(r) from shares of r using any existing MPC protocol (e.g., piecewise
and high-degree polynomial approximation, garbled circuits or SPDZ) in the
oine phase. Since the oine phase is input-independent and can tolerate
higher latency, this approach is feasible in principle, though it may incur
higher computational overhead.
Scope of This Work. The focus of this paper is the online phase: we
provide constant-round, low-communication protocols for non-linear functions
assuming the existence of the required precomputed tuples. The oine genera-
tion of transcendental tuples is an important orthogonal direction. We note that
our protocols do not require any new oine techniquesany secure
method for generating shares of h(r) suces. The development of more ecient
oine generation for these tuples is left as future work.

Ideal Functionality

Simulator Construction
Consider the online phase of a protocol for f. The only values revealed to parties
are the masked shares xi + ri (broadcast in round 1). Since each ri is uniformly
random and independent of the input, the masked value x + r is uniformly
distributed. A simulator can generate a random value u and set the simulated
broadcast to u.
For transcendental functions, each party broadcasts xi + ri leaks nothing
because ri is chosen uniformly in the ring. The sign's security lies in the two
mask techniques: addtive mask in additve group and the multiplicative mask in
a multiplicative group, which leaks nothing too. The broadcast in wrap protocol
xi + ri leaks nothing too.
5.3
Hybrid Argument
We prove security by composing the oine generation of precomputed tuples
(which is input-independent and secure by construction of the underlying prim-
itive) with the online protocol. The online protocol only uses the precomputed
shares in linear combinations or as input to secure multiplcaion protocol, preserv-
ing the secret-sharing property. Formal security proofs for each protocol follow
by constructing a simulator that replaces the precomputed values with random
values consistent with the revealed masked values. For functions (multiple vari-
able multiplication, power function) with 1 round communication, their proof is
similar to the proof of classic Beaver-based multiplication. For exponential func-
tion, Trigonometric Functions, sigmoid, softmax and GeLU, by UC principle, as
each step is secure, the whole protocol is secure.

Related Work
We categorize existing secure inference schemes for Transformers according to
how they handle non-linear functions (Softmax, GeLU, SiLU, etc.). Table 1 sum-
marizes the four classes.
6.1
Polynomial Approximation: Trading Accuracy for Eciency
The most common approach approximates complex non-linear functions using
low-degree polynomials, piecewise polynomials, or Taylor expansions. This re-
duces communication and rounds but inevitably introduces approximation errors
that may aect model accuracy.
Hybrid HE/MPC frameworks. BOLT [20] approximates GeLU with a
degree-4 polynomial and Softmax with a clipped Taylor series, achieving near-
oating-point accuracy without ne-tuning. BumbleBee [16] uses a 4-segment
polynomial (degree-3 and degree-6) for GeLU and the approximation (1+x/2n)2n
for Softmax, reducing communication by 80%90% but incurring up to 11 ULP
error for GeLU. BLB [26] inherits BumbleBee's piecewise approximations.
Three-party frameworks. PUMA [8], Ditto [24], Iron [11], and Nimbus [28]
all rely on polynomial or piecewise approximations. MPCFormer [14] replaces
GeLU with a quadratic and Softmax with a division of quadratics, causing about
5% accuracy drop that requires ne-tuning.
Limitation. All these methods face an inherent trade-o: higher-degree poly-
nomials improve accuracy but increase communication rounds and computation;
lower-degree polynomials are more ecient but risk accuracy degradation. None
achieves exact computation without approximation.
6.2
Multiplication-to-Addition (M2A) Based Exact Computation
A dierent line of work computes non-linear functions exactly (up to oating-
point rounding) by converting multiplicative secret shares to additive shares
using interactive protocols.

6.3
FSS-Based Lookup Tables: Quantization Error
Function secret sharing (FSS) enables low online communication by precomput-
ing keys for lookup tables (LUTs). These schemes discretize the input domain
into a nite number of bins and precompute function values for each bin.
SIGMA [10] builds FSS-based protocols for GeLU, Softmax, and layer nor-
malization, achieving GPU-accelerated inference. Its GeLU uses an LUT for
δ(x) = ReLU(x) −GeLU(x) on [−4, 4] with 256 entries (6-bit precision), which
introduces quantization error. The output diers from plaintext oating-point
computation due to discretization.
Mosformer [7] extends FSS to maliciously secure three-party computation
using veriable DPFs (VDPFs). Its GeLU also relies on a precomputed LUT of
256 entries, incurring similar quantization error.
Limitation. Finite LUT size inevitably causes approximation (quantization
error). Finer granularity reduces error but increases key size and oine overhead.
Thus, these schemes trade o precision against storage and preprocessing cost.
6.4
Our Scheme: Only Random Truncation Error
In contrast to the three categories above, our framework achieves the following:
 Algebraic functions (multiplication, power): exact over the underlying
ring/eld, identical to plaintext evaluation.
 Transcendental functions (exponential, trigonometric, sigmoid, softmax):
computed directly using standard oating-point arithmetic on the recon-
structed masked value x + r. The only numerical dierence from plaintext
evaluation is the inherent rounding error; no additional approximation
(polynomial, LUT, or otherwise) is introduced by the MPC protocol.
 Communication rounds: low constant (16)/(1-3) independent of function
complexity or number of participants.

Conclusion and Future Work
This work presents the rst unied framework that enables constant-round,
linear-communication MPC for the set of common non-linear functions required
by modern machine learning and statistical analysis, with precision guarantees
that match plaintext computationexact for algebraic functions and bitwise
equivalent for transcendental functions.
Existing practical MPC solutions for machine learning have been limited to
DNNs that use comparison-based activation functions. By providing the rst
ecient protocols for the full spectrum of non-linear functions used in modern
DNNswith constant rounds, linear communication, and no approximation er-
rorthis work removes the nal barrier that has prevented MPC from being
a truly practical tool for the broader landscape of privacy-preserving machine
learning.
Future Work. The ecient oine generation of transcendental correlations
(r, f(r)) remains an important open problem. While existing techniques (FSS for
trigonometric functions, trusted dealer, homomorphic encryption) provide fea-
sible solutions, developing lightweight, PCG-like methods for these non-linear
correlations is a promising direction. Additionally, extending our framework to
the malicious security model and implementing the protocols for real-world de-
ployment are natural next steps.
