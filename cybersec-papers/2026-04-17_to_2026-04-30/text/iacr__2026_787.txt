Abstract. BBS+ signatures are widely adopted in privacy-preserving
systems such as anonymous credentials and Direct Anonymous Attesta-
tion (DAA). To strengthen key security and eliminate single points of
failure, threshold variants of BBS+ signatures have become increasingly
important. However, existing constructions suffer from notable inefficien-
cies: some entail excessive communication overhead (e.g., DKL+23, S&P
2023), while others impose substantial computational costs and require
additional interaction rounds (e.g., WMC24, NDSS 2024).
In this work, we present a novel and efficient three-round threshold BBS+
signature scheme from the Castagnos–Laguillaumie (CL) cryptosystem.
Our construction achieves best communication–computation trade-offs
than previous works. Specifically, compared to the four-round WMC24
scheme, our protocol reduces communication by 77.4% and demonstrates
faster computation, with benchmarks indicating speedups of 10.6–16.6×
in single-threading and 3.3–5.4× in multi-threading. Against the three-
round protocol DKL+23, our scheme exhibits an asymptotic slowdown
factor of 4×, but enhances communication by two orders of magnitude.
We further extend our techniques to threshold BBS signatures, Dodis-
Yampolskiy verifiable random functions (DY VRFs), and multiplication
protocols (DNP25 and LLZ+25, CCS’25). This yields: (1) a three-round
threshold protocol for the original BBS scheme; (2) two-round threshold
protocols for both DY VRFs (focusing on its oblivious variant) and the
AGM-secure BBS variant; and (3) one fewer group element in broadcasts
for the multiplication protocol with reduced ZKP costs via simplified
relations.

group signature scheme [2] and later explicitly formulated as a standalone scheme

as a provably secure version. The pioneering works [7, 8] by Camenisch and
Lysyanskaya show that, BBS(+) signatures are amenable to several privacy-
preserving techniques such as anonymous credentials [18,23] and direct anony-
mous attestation (DAA) [5, 6]. Notably, BBS+ has been deployed in the En-
hanced Privacy ID (EPID) system of Intel’s SGX platform [5]. Recognizing its
broad applicability, the Internet Research Task Force (IRTF) has initiated stan-
dardization efforts for BBS(+) schemes [30], underscoring their growing impor-
tance in practical cryptographic employments.
To strengthen key protection, a threshold BBS+ construction can be em-
ployed to mitigate the risk of a single point of failure. For instance, in con-
ventional anonymous credential systems based upon BBS+, the signing key is
controlled solely by the issuer, who generates credential signatures to authenti-
cate users. If an adversary compromises the issuer, they gain access to the signing
key, enabling the generation of unlimited fraudulent credentials. Furthermore,
because these credentials are anonymous, such malicious activity remains unde-
tectable and untraceable. A (t, n) threshold scheme [16] distributes the signing
key among n parties, requiring the cooperation of at least t parties to produce a
valid signature. This approach mitigates attacks by ensuring that an adversary
must compromise at least t parties to forge a signature.

The key challenge in thresholdizing BBS+ lies in how to securely perform
operations (1) and (2) in a distributed manner, i.e., with the values x and/or
e, s being secret-shared among the parties.
To perform the above operation securely, DKL+23 protocol [18] employs
a commitment scheme to distributively generate e and s in an unbiased man-
ner, and utilizes the Multiplicative-to-Additive conversions (MtA conversions,
also known as oblivious linear evaluation) for the distributed computation of A.
Here, MtA is a two-party protocol that converts multiplicative shares (a, b) into
additive shares (α, β) such that α+β = ab. While DKL+23 achieves remarkable
speed—thanks to its OT-based MtA instantiations—it needs O(t) outgoing com-
munication complexity per party due to pairwise MtA interactions. This leads
to high concrete cost: for example, each party transmits roughly 670KiB in a
10-party setup.
Recently, WMC24 [31] introduced a threshold scheme based on threshold
additively homomorphic encryption (TAHE). They cast threshold BBS+ sign-
ing as a secure multiparty computation (MPC) task and realize it via the

Cramer–Damgård–Nielsen (CDN) paradigm [14] instantiated with TAHE. Con-

Motivation. Designing an efficient threshold BBS+ scheme for large-scale de-
ployment—particularly in cryptocurrency settings where trust is paramount—re-
mains a central challenge. As the number of signers (i.e., the parties participating
in the signing execution) increases to strengthen security, existing solutions of-
ten incur substantial trade-offs between communication and computation. This
raises the following fundamental research question:
Can we design a threshold BBS+ signature scheme that achieves a su-
perior trade-off between communication and computation costs by lever-
aging cryptographic primitives better suited for threshold settings?
1.1
Our Contributions
We address this question by proposing an efficient threshold BBS+ signature
scheme that achieves a more favorable trade-off between computation and com-
munication than both DKL+23 and WMC24.
– We present an efficient 3-round threshold BBS+ scheme combining a com-
mitment scheme with CL KEM (a conceptual key encapsulation mechanism
abstracted from threshold CL encryption to demonstrate our approach, but
is not a secure KEM), matching DKL+23’s round complexity while achieving
O(1) communication like WMC24. Our protocol reduces WMC24’s require-
ments from 9 to 2 broadcast class-group elements per signer and from ∼29t
to ∼2t class-group exponentiations. While computationally more expensive
than DKL+23’s OT-based approach, our CL-KEM-based solution achieves a
superior computation-communication trade-off (Table 1), with security proven
in the simulation-based model.
– We extend our threshold techniques to both BBS signatures [2,29] and Dodis-
Yampolskiy verifiable random functions [17] (denoted as DY VRFs), as shown
in Section 4. This is because their signing/evaluation processes require only
the nonce generation and/or computation of A. As a result, we obtain (1) a
3-round threshold protocol for the original BBS scheme [2], and (2) 2-round

The Framework of Threshold BBS+. We begin by outlining the framework
adopted by DKL+23 [18] and WMC24 [31]. It comprises four phases:
1. Key Generation: All parties jointly generate the verifying key and secret-
share the signing key x.
2. Nonce Generation: Signers jointly sample the nonces e and s.
3. Signature Components: The signers jointly compute R := γB and u :=
γ(x + e), where γ is a secret value.
4. Signature Derivation: Set A := R/u and output the signature (A, e, s).
Phase 1 is executed once to establish the verifying-signing key pair, whereas
Phases 2–4 are repeated for each message to sign a new signature. In the sub-
sequent content, we discuss the key differences between DKL+23 and WMC24,
which arise in Phases 2 and 3.

ing Phases 2 and 3 into three subtasks. The core innovation lies in subtask 2,
where we redesign the CL-based TAHE process. Specifically, our modified pro-
cess eliminates broadcasting half part of individual ciphertexts by embedding it
directly into partial decryptions, simultaneously reducing communication costs
and computation costs (by eliminating associated ZKPs). Additionally, we sim-
plify ZKP relations for the homomorphic scaling operation ⊙by leveraging the

for the threshold-decryption ZKP. Consequently, our protocol achieves better
communication efficiency (2 vs. 9) and nearly an order-of-magnitude reduction
in computational overhead (∼2t vs. ∼29t).

Related Works
Other threshold BBS+. Faust et al. [19] introduced a threshold BBS+
scheme leveraging pseudorandom correlation generators and functions, but their
construction operates in the preprocessing model—explicitly avoided in our work
for reasons aligned with DKL+23: preprocessing consumes finite correlated states
offline, risking reuse and requiring periodic replenishment.
Gennaro et al. [22] and Tang-Xue [28] designed threshold BBS+ protocols
that, like DKL+23, employ MtA conversions to compute u = γ(x + e) (subtask
2). Gennaro et al. instantiate their MtA using Paillier encryption, but require
expensive zero-knowledge range proofs, incurring significant overhead. Tang-Xue
(hereinafter referred to as TX25) implement MtA via CL encryption and achieve
robust and IA properties by attaching ZKPs to each MtA instance. They further
separate signing execution into an offline phase (message-independent) and an
online phase (message-dependent), shifting all costly operations offline to op-
timize online efficiency. Since this separation coincides with preprocessing, we
instead focus on overall performance. TX25 exhibits O(t2) complexity from veri-
fying O(t2) MtA instances, resulting in slower execution than WMC24 for t ≥20.
Its bandwidth also grows linearly with t, exceeding WMC24 when t ≥10.
Recent two-round threshold ECDSA. Recent threshold ECDSA protocols
by DNP25 and LLZ+25 introduce novel secure multiplication techniques that
save one round compared to previous approaches (e.g., computing u via MtA in
DKL+23). Both protocols operate within the CL framework. We observe that
our homomorphic scaling technique from Section 1.3 can be adapted to these
protocols: parties broadcast a commitment component structurally similar to the
first element ϕK′ of the scaled CL ciphertext. By replacing this with an element of
form ϕK∗, we achieve reductions in both communication and computation costs
during their multiplication protocol. Additional discussion appears in Section 5.
These multiplication protocols are applicable to threshold BBS+/BBS sign-
ing with asymptotic complexity equivalent to our technique. While they out-
perform our method for one-time key generation by avoiding CL distributed
key generation (DKG), our approach proves more efficient for signing execu-
tions. Section 5 provides a concrete comparison (measured in exponentiations
over class-group elements) between our method and the optimized multiplica-
tion protocols from DNP25 and LLZ+25.

Zero-Knowledge Proofs
Let R denote a relation with corresponding NP language L. A zero-knowledge

– ΠR.Prv(x, w) →π: this algorithm is executed by the prover and outputs a
proof π.
– ΠR.Vrf(x, π) →{0, 1}: this algorithm is executed by the verifier and outputs
1 iff π is a valid proof for x ∈L.

Comparison with DKL+23. Benchmarks show our protocol runs 4-16×
slower than DKL+23, with the slowdown factor asymptotically tightening to

∼100× better communication efficiency. Consequently, we contend our solution
provides a better computation-communication trade-off.
