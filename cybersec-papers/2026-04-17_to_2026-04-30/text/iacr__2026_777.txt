How Strong is the FO-Calypse, Really?
Instantiating Plaintext-Checking Oracles against
Masked Software Implementations of ML-KEM
Brieuc Balon1, Gaëtan Cassiers1,2, Thibaud Schoenauen1 and
François-Xavier Standaert1
1 Crypto Group, ICTEAM Institute, UCLouvain, Louvain-la-Neuve, Belgium
2 CryptoExperts, Paris, France
Abstract. Side-channel attacks exploiting Plaintext-Checking Oracles (PCOs) instanti-
ated thanks to the leakage of the re-encryption step taking place during decapsulation
are a well-known weakness of ML-KEM. An already wide literature investigated how
to efficiently exploit such oracles, leading to easy (full) key recoveries. Somewhat
surprisingly, the investigation of how to best instantiate PCOs against ML-KEM’s
most leaking operations is less investigated, in particular when it comes to quanti-
tative evaluations against concrete masked implementations. In this paper, we first
remedy this lack by systematically instantiating PCOs against three open source
masked software implementations of the Keccak function used in ML-KEM, based on
different masking techniques and programming styles. We evaluate the accuracy of
PCOs for increasing number of shares using state-of-the-art profiled attacks against
ARM Cortex-M4 implementations, and succeed obtaining high accuracy for up to
7 shares by leveraging the leakage of approximately 50 ML-KEM executions only.
Doing so, we confirm the “computing more implies leaking more” adage and conclude
that enforcing high security levels on such platforms will not be affordable. Next,
we consolidate recent solutions for exploiting PCOs. For this purpose, we start
by introducing a simple, concrete and re-usable model for PCOs targeting masked
implementations of Keccak. We follow by clarifying that approaches based on hard
decisions are suboptimal compared to soft (probabilistic) ones. We finally open a
study of how to best exploit the adversary’s computational power in a security evalu-
ation. We show that (even naive) lattice based attacks are a promising approach for
this purpose, leaving the design of a generic estimator that could efficiently leverage
physical (side-channel) information as an interesting research direction.
Keywords: ML-KEM · side-channel analysis · plaintext checking oracles · masking

Introduction
Ever since its publication in 2018, CRYSTALS-Kyber [BDK+18], now standardized as
ML-KEM [Nat24], has lead to significant efforts towards understanding its implementation
security against side-channel attacks. One line of research focused on single-trace attacks,
which may lead to directly compromise ML-KEM’s long-term key [PPM17, PP19, KPP20].1
Besides, it has rapidly been observed that the structure of ML-KEM, and its reliance
on the Fujisaki-Okamoto (FO) transform to ensure chosen ciphertext security, can
lead to very powerful side-channel key recoveries relying on Plaintext-Checking Oracles
(PCOs) [RRCB20, UXT+22, XPR+22, RRD+23, TUX+23]. Conceptually, they exploit
the possibility of performing side-channel chosen-ciphertext attacks against the part of

two messages (i.e., instantiating a PCO) thanks to leakage can compromise ML-KEM’s

the information provided by PCOs. For example, early works like [RRCB20] relying on
hard decisions such as majority voting gradually evolved towards probabilistic strate-

In parallel, research progressed in the direction of protecting ML-KEM implementations
against these strong attacks. In particular, how to securely mask its various operations
to mitigate side-channel leakage (e.g., in software, which will be our focus) has been
an active research area. For Keccak, standard solutions developed for the AES can be
directly used [GR17], possibly with some specialization [GD23]. For the other parts, new
algorithmic solutions have been proposed, including [BPO+20, BDH+21] for polynomial
comparisons, or [CGMZ22, D’A23] for conversion algorithms, and leading to a few complete
proposals such as [BGR+21, BC22] (and [BDK+21] for the SABER algorithm).
As a result of these advances, it is to be expected that secure implementations of
ML-KEM should be quite expensive. Yet, and it is also worth noticing that the amount of
published works that consider actual measurements against protected (masked) implemen-
tations remains scarce, especially when it comes to systematically assessing security in
a quantitative manner. Among the aforementioned papers, Primas et al. (CHES 2017)
consider SASCA against masked software implementations of CRYSTALS-Kyber, but their
(quantitative) investigations only evaluate single-trace attacks against the NTT [PPM17].
The others only assesses a (qualitative) security order via leakage detection: for the polyno-
mial comparisons in the case of Bhasin et al. [BDH+21], for complete implementations of
Kyber (or SABER) in the case of Bos et al. [BGR+21], Van Beirendonck et al. [BDK+21]
and Bronchain & Cassiers [BC22]. Besides, and browsing other recent works, Hermelink
et al. (resp., Pay and Standaert) comprehensively evaluate the security of a masked
software implementation against PCO-based attacks with SASCA-like techniques, but
their (quantitative) evaluations only consider polynomial comparison [HNPS24] (resp., the
NTT [PS26]); Wang et al. finally use deep learning techniques to evaluate a higher-order
masked implementation of CRYSTALS-Kyber, but their (quantitative) evaluations only
consider the message decoding function and the Barrett reduction steps during the decap-
sulation process [WBD24]; their study is extended to a masked hardware implementation
in [JD25]. Somewhat surprisingly given that it accounts for a significant part of the
overheads and leakage in masked implementations of ML-KEM (see Table 1), no analysis
assessed the strength of PCOs instantiated against a masked Keccak so far.

Overall, and to the best of our knowledge, the state of the art therefore still lacks a
systematic investigation of how strong PCO-based attacks can be against masked (e.g.,
software) implementations. We contribute to this lack in two main directions.
First, we evaluate message distinguishing attacks against masked software implementa-
tions of Keccak on an ARM Cortex-M4. This choice is motivated both by the fact that it
is among the most important sources of leakage in ML-KEM and by the availability of
open source prototypes. We consider the C implementation from Kundu et al. [KDB+22],
which adapts the Domain Oriented Masking (DOM) implementation from [GSM17] to the
software case, the C and assembly implementations of Bronchain and Cassiers [BC22],
which rely on Probe Isolating Non Interferent (PINI) gadgets [CS20], and the Thresh-
old (assembly) Implementation (TI) of [GD23], which is limited to two shares. This
allows us to assess the impact of C implementations vs. assembly ones aimed at reducing
transition-based leakages [BGG+14]. It also allows us to assess the difference between
protections for arbitrary numbers of shares vs. optimized ones for two shares. We use
state-of-the-art template attacks in principal subspaces for this purpose [SA08], which
have been shown to be well adapted to target masked software implementations of the
AES [BS21, BCS21]. Concretely, we compare our target software implementations with up
to 7 shares and confirm the standard intuition that computing more implies leaking more.
For the DOM implementation, which has the highest security level in our experiments, we
show that high-accuracy PCOs can be easily obtained for up to 7 shares with the leakage
corresponding to only 50 executions of ML-KEM. These results lead to the conclusion that
ML-KEM is unlikely to be implementable securely on low-end microcontrollers without
prohibitive overheads. They suggest the investigation of secure micro-controllers (e.g., with
noise engines or instruction set extensions for leakage-sensitive operations) and hardware
Keccak co-processors, for which parallelism could be leveraged to reduce the amount of
leakage exploitable by PCOs, as interesting and practically relevant open problems.
Second, we consolidate recent solutions for exploiting PCOs and turn them into full
key recoveries. We start by introducing a simple and concrete model for PCOs, which we
parametrize for masked implementations of Keccak with different number of shares. We then
use this model to clarify that approaches based on hard decisions, like the initial [RRCB20],
are suboptimal compared to soft (probabilistic) ones, like the recent [HMT25]. We show in
particular that majority voting is useless in the context of hard PCO-based attacks, since
adversaries better use all their measurements to increase the accuracy of a single PCO
call than spending them in voting. We finally initiate a study of the remaining challenges
raised by soft approaches, namely how to best post-process the inference’s outcomes in
order to evaluate the security level given the adversary’s computational power. Given the
nature of the (lattice) assumption on which ML-KEM relies, key enumeration algorithms
used in symmetric cryptography are unlikely to be sufficient [PSG16], and we therefore
turn back to lattice estimators [APS15] or variants with hints thereof [DDGR20]. We show
that a naive (likely conservative) use of these estimators already gives significant attack
improvements and that hybrid approaches perform best in our setting [How07]. We also
put forward that such estimators are for now unable to capture the granularity of the hints
provided by side-channel leakages, leaving the design of a generic estimator that could
efficiently leverage such physical hints as an interesting scope for further research.

2.1
ML-KEM
ML-KEM is a key encapsulation mechanism standardized by the NIST [Nat24]. Its security
relies on the hardness of the Module Learning With Errors (MLWE) problem and it is
instantiated for the three NIST security levels (i.e., L1, L3 and L5) under the names
ML-KEM-512, ML-KEM-768 and ML-KEM-1024. All standardized instantiations operate
on the same polynomial ring Rq = Zq[X]/(Xn + 1), with prime modulus q = 3329 and
ring degree n = 256. This ring is used to construct the module Rk
q whose rank k equals 2,
3 and 4 depending on the security level. The parameter sets η1 and η2, which define the
distributions used to sample the secret and error polynomials, are chosen as η1 = (3, 2, 2)
and η2 = (2, 2, 2) for the security levels (L1, L3, L5). ML-KEM is constructed from a
Public-Key Encryption (PKE) scheme which is transformed into an IND-CCA2-secure
KEM using the FO transform. In this paper, we focus on the decapsulation algorithm of
ML-KEM, which is the target of message distinguishing attacks. We briefly recall this
decapsulation procedure below and refer the reader to [Nat24] for the details.
ML-KEM Decapsulation. The decapsulation procedure of ML-KEM is depicted in
Algorithm 1. Given the decapsulation key (i.e., the secret key) dkPKE and a received
ciphertext c, the algorithm first recovers the decrypted message m′ using Algorithm 2.
This message is then re-encrypted with the encapsulation key (i.e., the public key) ekPKE
and some randomness r′ using Algorithm 3, to obtain a re-computed ciphertext c′. The
decapsulation procedure finishes by outputting the shared secret key K′, if the recomputed
ciphertext c′ matches the received ciphertext c, or a random value ¯K, when both ciphertexts
are different. This re-encryption step is essential for achieving IND-CCA2 security. It
ensures that the randomness r′, which is deterministically derived from the message, used
during the encapsulation and decapsulation procedures are identical. This is checked by
verifying the original ciphertext is matching with the re-computed one.

2.2
SHA-3
The SHA-3 algorithm is a family of four hash functions and two eXtendable-Output
Functions (XOFs) standardized by the NIST [oSN15]. In this work, we will adopt the same
terminology as in the FIPS 202 documentaion [oSN15]. The SHA-3 functions sensitive to
message distinguishing attacks (highlighted in Algorithms 1 and 2) correspond to the hash
function SHA3-512 and the XOF SHAKE256 defined as:
SHA3-512(m′||h) = Keccak[1024](m′||h, 512),
SHAKE256(r′, 512 · η) = Keccak[512](r′, 512 · η).
The input size of both functions is constant across all ML-KEM security levels, with
|(m′||h)| = 512 bits and |r′| = 256 bits. In contrast, while the output length of SHA3-512
is fixed to 512 bits, the output length of SHAKE depends on η, which varies depending
on the security levels. Both functions use the Keccak-f[1600] permutation (next simply
referred to as Keccak) in sponge constructions depicted in, Figure 2. The number of Keccak
permutation executions that can be exploited by a PCO on ML-KEM re-encryption is 8, 8
and 10 for ML-KEM-512, ML-KEM-768 and ML-KEM-1024 respectively.
Permutation. The Keccak permutation consists of 24 rounds, each composed of five
sequential operations: θ, ρ, π, χ and ι. All operations apply a linear transformation to the
state, except for χ, which is a non-linear transformation. We omit the detailed description
of (θ, ρ, π, χ, ι) because they are not necessary for understanding our results. We just note
that in software implementations, the ρ and π operations are often combined together for
efficiency, reducing the number of operations per round to four: (θ, π ◦ρ, χ, ι).
2.3
Profiled attack on masked implementations
Our methodology for building PCOs is similar to the approach proposed in [BS21]. In this
section, we recall its main steps and tools. At a high level, this methodology identifies
secret-dependent variables in the computation, which we name sensitive variables. We
then target the shares of these variables with a profiled attack to retrieve probabilistic
information on their values. We finally recombine the shares’ distributions to learn a
distribution of the sensitive variables, which we translate to the main secret.
The simplicity of this method rests on the knowledge of the shares’ values in a profiling
dataset. When such a knowledge is not available, deep learning techniques [PPM+23,
MCLS23] can often be used a an alternative in order to achieve similar results.

3
Distinguishing attacks on masked Keccak
In this first main section, we present distinguishing attacks against different masked
implementations of Keccak that can be used in order to instantiate PCOs.
3.1
Target implementations and setup
We first present our target implementations and the measurement setup we used.
Target Implementations.
To the best of our knowledge, there are only three open
source masked software implementations of Keccak [BC22, KDB+22, GD23].
These
implementations, written in C or assembly (ASM), and mainly differ in the strategy used
to mask the non-linear χ layer of Keccak (as the linear layers can be performed share-wise).
We next refer to these implementations as PINI, DOM and TI:
PINI. The Probe-Isolating Non-Interferent (PINI) implementation from [BC22] relies on
PINI gadgets to mask the non-linear operation χ. It can be masked with any number
of shares and is written in C, with sensitive PINI gadgets implemented in C or
assembly. The ASM version explicitly controls register allocation and instruction
scheduling to mitigate transition-based leakages between shares, which may occur
with the gadgets written in C due to compiler optimizations [BGG+14].
DOM. The Domain-Oriented Masking (DOM) implementation from [KDB+22] uses DOM
rather than PINI gadgets to protect the non-linear operation χ. It is written in C
supports any number of shares (like the PINI implementation).
TI. The Threshold Implementation (TI) described in [GD23] is a hand-optimized ASM
implementation that adapts the TI concept to software. It is designed to prevent
mirco-architectural leakage (e.g., due to transitions or glitches). Unlike PINI and
DOM, this implementation is optimized for and only supports two shares.

Measurement Setup.
Measurements are performed on an STM32F415RG (ARM Cortex-
M4) mounted on a ChipWhisperer CW308 UFO board. The processor operates at a
125 MHz clock frequency, derived from an external 15.625 MHz square wave clock generated
by a PicoScope 5244D. Side-channel leakage is captured, with the same Picoscope, using a
Tektronix CT-1 current probe placed on a jumper between the on-board power regulator
and the CPU. Traces are sampled at 500 MSamples/s with a 12-bit resolution.
3.2
Attack methodology
Our distinguishing attack against the Keccak permutation is based on the methodology
highlighted in Section 2.3 and implemented using SCALib [CB23]. This methodology is
“order-preserving” in the sense that it recombines the shares’ information by applying
a convolutional product, which prevents exploiting (e.g., transition-based) flaws that
may re-combine this information at the implementation level, and reduce the security
amplification that masking ideally provides. This means that the attacks we describe
next may be further improved in case such flaws are observed. We leave the investigation
of such improvements as an open problem because they apply similarly to any gadget
and the same holds for solutions to prevent them [CGP+12, BGG+14, CS21]. Besides,
previous work showed that for low-noise platforms like the ARM Cortex-M4 devices that
we consider, such order-preserving attacks are usually the most efficient [BS21].
As sensitive variables, we selected the Keccak permutation state at the output of the
θ, π ◦ρ and χ layers.2 We systematically get rid of the first four rounds (i.e., before
diffusion of an input bit difference is complete) so that every part of the target state can
be assumed to fully depend on the secret bit, no matter how the inputs depend on this bit.
This leaves us with 20 exploitable leaking rounds per Keccak execution, leading to the
exploitable round counts for the whole ML-KEM re-encryption given in Table 3. For the
sake of simplicity, we partition each 1600-bit state it into 200 independent 8-bit sensitive
variables. This results in 3 × 200 = 600 sensitive values per round and 600 × 20 = 12 000
values per Keccak execution. It is possible to further increase the number of intermediate
value. However, in this work we restrict ourselves to the state variables to enable a generic
comparison across the four target implementations. Alternatively, adopting 32-bit variables
could help reduce algorithmic noise. Again, we leave these extensions (i.e., incorporating
additional intermediate values and exploring 32-bit models) for future work.

large when the number of shares increases. For example, with our measurement setup, one

Exploiting plaintext-checking oracles
As described in [RRCB20, QCZ+21], an adversary can easily craft ciphertexts such that
the decrypted message m′ in Algorithm 1 takes one out of two known values, next denoted
as m0 and m1. For instance, m0 can be the zero bit vector whereas m1 is identical except
for a single bit set to one. The decrypted message m′ then takes one of these two values
depending on whether a chosen secret-key coefficient exceeds a given threshold τ. More
precisely, for any i ∈{1, n · k} and threshold τ, the adversary can construct a ciphertext c
such that the value of m′ is mb, where b = 0 if ski ≤τ and b = 1 otherwise.
Recovering b therefore reveals information on the secret key coefficient ski and its
actual value can be narrowed down by crafting ciphertexts for different thresholds. To
determine b, the adversary can exploit the re-encryption leakage and, in particular, all
the Keccak calls highlighted in Figure 1. As shown in Table 3, this re-encryption leakage
corresponds to 8 (resp., 10) calls to Keccak for ML-KEM-512/768 (resp., ML-KEM-1024).
Omitting the first 4 rounds of each permutation, as explained in Section 3.2, this gives
us a total of 160 (resp., 200) Keccak round leakages per ML-KEM decapsulation. These
rounds can be leveraged using the Keccak-based message distinguisher of Section 3, which
is in turn used to instantiate the PCO for distinguishing between b = 0 and b = 1.
In this section, we focus on full key-recovery attacks against the long-term secret of
ML-KEM-768. We illustrate our approaches with the the DOM C implementation (i.e.,
the one which is the hardest to attack) and we use the number of ML-KEM decapsulations
M needed to reach a (full) key-recovery success rate SRKR as our main figure-of-merit.
We begin by constructing a parametric model for soft PCOs (i.e., PCOs whose output
is a probability distribution) based on side-channel leakage. We fit this model to the
distinguishers of Section 3, showing a good match between the models and the real
distinguishers. This model simplifies the analysis of key recovery strategies at high success
rates, since accurately estimating the success rates in these conditions requires (very)
large datasets. Furthermore, we argue that it comes with sound theoretical grounding
and empirical validation with concrete instances of parametrizations. It is therefore an
easy-to-use benchmark for future work on the design of PCO-based attacks.
We next investigate three attack strategies exploiting PCOs. First, we directly use the
output of the message distinguisher as hard information to instantiate the PCO, optionally
with majority-voting as proposed in [RRCB20, UXT+22]. Then, we consider a more recent
attack strategy [HMT25] that treats the message distinguisher outputs as soft information
and combines multiple PCO queries probabilistically. Finally, we investigate how much
state-of-the-art cryptanalysis techniques covered by lattice estimators [APS15] or variants
with hints thereof [DDGR20] can further improve these cryptanalytic results.

As a first step in this direction, we use standard estimators [APS15] and variants with

Our approach follows an iterative attack strategy. The 768 posterior distributions over
the secret coefficients are initialized according to the prior induced by the CBD2 distribution.
Before each PCO query, the empirical variance of each coefficient is computed from its
current posterior distribution, and the coefficient with the largest variance is selected.
The soft-information PCO is then queried for this coefficient, and the corresponding
posterior distribution is updated following the same threshold selection, oracle response,
and Bayesian update rules as in Section 4.2. After each update, the empirical variance
across all coefficients is recomputed and the maximum is provided as input to the lattice
estimator. This procedure is repeated iteratively until full key recovery.
Since the variance of the secret key distribution is the main parameter of lattice
estimators like [APS15, DDGR20], this allows us to leverage them directly in order to
obtain security estimates. Yet, it is worth observing that such estimators do not provide

The security estimates obtained with this approach are depicted in Figure 10b, where

hybrid attacks [How07] evaluated with the estimator by Albrecht et al. [APS15], which

remains essentially unchanged until all coefficient have been at least updated once (i.e.,

security appears around M ≈1300. This behavior can be explained by the presence of
secret coefficients (approximately 530 at that point) that require at least two threshold
queries to sufficiently reduce their empirical variance to an extent that is reflected by the
estimator. Curves for larger number of shares are more progressive.4

Conclusions and open problems
Concretely, the results in this paper substantiate and quantify the worrying threat of
“FO-Calypse” that has been reported by several pieces of works over the last years.
To the best of our knowledge, they bring the first systematic investigation of concrete
attacks taking advantage of the sensitive (masked) Keccak executions taking place in
ML-KEM’s decapsulation, in order to instantiate PCOs. We used three open source
masked implementations for this purpose and were able to break implementations with
up to 7 shares.
Given that the attacks we provide are not even optimal, this leads
us to conclude that securing ML-KEM in low-cost embedded devices is unlikely to be

Besides, our results also allow observing that the lack of systematic and quantitative
security evaluations of PCO-based attacks against masked implementations does not come
without reason: such evaluations can be extremely time- and storage-consuming. In order
to mitigate this difficulty, we also present a simple, concrete and re-usable model for PCOs
targeting masked implementations of Keccak. We hope this model can be adopted by
designers and evaluation laboratories in order to better assess the scaling trends of masked
implementations of ML-KEM. We also hope it can help researchers towards comparing
implementations and attack methodologies with convenient (abstract) models that are
grounded on realistic assumptions. In this respect, one interesting open problem is to
generalize this model to multi-bit PCOs, as for example proposed in [TUX+23, RRD+23].

in case of low-accuracy PCO, it is possible that the gains of multi-bit variants are in part
compensated by the decreased accuracy due to targeting larger secrets [BRM+26].
Eventually, Section 4.3 suggests the specialization of lattice estimators to side-channel
leakage and the concrete evaluation of lattice cryptanalysis for low security levels as a
promising research direction. Being able to take advantage of any type of probabilistic hint
provided at the coefficient level appears natural for this purpose. Better comprehending
lattice cryptanalysis with physical hints could also lead to motivate the extraction of other
pieces of information from implementation leakage (e.g., on the noise distribution).
