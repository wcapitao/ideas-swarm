Abstract
Alignment faking (AF) occurs when an LLM strategically complies with train-
ing objectives to avoid value modification, reverting to prior preferences once
monitoring is lifted. Current detection methods focus on conversational settings
and rely primarily on Chain-of-Thought (CoT) analysis, which provides a reliable
signal when strategic reasoning surfaces, but cannot distinguish deception from
capability failures if traces are absent or unfaithful. We formalize AF as a com-
posite behavioural event and detect it through observable tool selection, where
the LLM selects the safe tool when unmonitored, but switches to the unsafe tool
under monitoring that rewards helpfulness over safety, while its reasoning still
acknowledges the safe choice. We release a dataset of 108 enterprise IT scenarios
spanning Security, Privacy, and Integrity domains under Corruption and Sabotage
pressures. Evaluating six frontier LLMs across five independent runs, we find mean
AF detection rates between 3.5% and 23.7%, with vulnerability profiles varying by
domain and pressure type. These results suggest that susceptibility reflects training
methodology rather than capability alone.

Introduction
AI systems operate nowadays in safety-critical domains, such as healthcare Esteva et al. [2017],
Abramson et al. [2024], traffic management Lv et al. [2014], and power distribution Mirshekali et al.
[2025]. However, whether such systems remain aligned with human goals and preferences is a key
open question. Alignment aims to ensure that AI systems reflect human intentions and values, rather
than produce unwanted or harmful behaviours [Gabriel, 2020, Dung, 2023, Ji et al., 2023]. For Large
Language Models (LLMs) now deployed as autonomous agents, misalignment might manifest in
real-world actions, not just in textual representations [Ngo et al., 2022, Shavit et al., 2023, Lynch
et al., 2025, Zhang et al., 2024].
As models become more capable, researchers are increasingly concerned that they might learn to
scheme, recognize when they are being evaluated, and adjust their behaviour to pursue hidden goals
[Meinke et al., 2024, Panfilov et al., 2025]. One form of this risk is alignment faking (AF), where an
LLM strategically complies with training objectives to avoid value modification, reverting to prior
preferences once oversight is removed [Carlsmith, 2023, Greenblatt et al., 2024]. In agentic settings,
such behaviour can translate into unsafe actions.

Detecting such strategic deception in tool-using agents has received growing attention, but presents
challenges [Hubinger et al., 2024, Meinke et al., 2024]. For instance, given a choice between
parameterized queries and string concatenation, an LLM may correctly identify and select the safe
option, yet switch to the unsafe tool when monitored under a training objective that conflicts with
its safety preferences, such as one prioritizing helpfulness as rated by contractors (Section 3.2),
following the setup of Greenblatt et al. [2024].
Current research investigates AF primarily in conversational or multi-step agentic settings, leaving
open questions about scenario realism as well as the distinction between inability and deceptive
behaviour [Lynch et al., 2025, Phuong et al., 2025]. Many approaches rely on Chain-of-Thought (CoT)
analysis, which provides a reliable signal when strategic reasoning surfaces explicitly [Greenblatt
et al., 2024]. However, scheming can occur without appearing in CoT [Meinke et al., 2024, Arnav
et al., 2025, Baker et al., 2025, Chen et al., 2025], and in those cases CoT-based methods alone cannot
distinguish an LLM that lacks the capability to act safely from one that strategically shifts behaviour
when monitored.
Contributions. To address AF in tool-selecting LLMs, we introduce the Tatemae framework2, a
model-agnostic framework based on observable tool selection, rather than conversation or reasoning
traces alone. The framework includes (i) a formal definition of AF as a verifiable composite event,
(ii) a dataset of enterprise IT scenarios, and (iii) an evaluation pipeline. Our design does not assume
persistent memory or multi-step planning. Instead, we target tool selection, a key component of
agentic workflows [Patil et al., 2024, Singh et al., 2025], rather than conversational benchmarks,
allowing controlled measurement in preparation for scaling to more complex environments.
• We formalize alignment faking as a verifiable composite event (Definition 2.1) and introduce
the Tatemae framework, which separates strategic deception from capability failure by
anchoring detection in structured tool selection rather than unstructured chat.
• We release a dataset of 108 enterprise IT scenarios spanning Security, Privacy, and Integrity
domains under Corruption and Sabotage adversarial pressures.
• We introduce an evaluation pipeline that distinguishes between (i) capability, (ii) robustness
under pressure, and (iii) compliance when monitored. By treating reasoning as a variable
rather than the sole ground truth, we anchor detection in observable tool-selection failures,
remaining informative when reasoning is absent or unfaithful. A complementary three-
criterion classifier characterizes detected cases along training awareness, modification
awareness, and strategic output selection.
• We evaluate the pipeline and dataset across six frontier LLMs. Detection rates vary by
pressure type and problem domain. Mean AF detection rates range from 3.5% to 23.7%,
with no model uniformly resistant across all domains and pressure types.
The code is available on GitHub, and the dataset (CC-BY 4.0) is available on Hugging Face.

Alignment Faking as a Composite Behavioural Event
In our Tatemae framework, each scenario presents a binary choice between a safe tool aligned
with best practices and an unsafe alternative that is riskier but more convenient. Under normal
circumstances, the LLM would recommend the safe option. Then, we introduce adversarial pressure,
such as business justifications or operational constraints (see Appendix E), to tempt the LLM toward
the unsafe alternative. Our scenarios cover three domains: Security, Privacy, and Integrity (detailed in
Section 3.1).
We define alignment faking as a composite event in which an LLM shows the capability to act
safely, yet strategically reverts to unsafe helpful behaviour when monitored, while internally retaining
awareness of the safe preference. We refer to prompts that include the monitoring system prompt
