Abstract. Nowadays, governments are world-wide pushing towards build-
ing infrastructures to intercept, decrypt and prevent communications
among citizens with the goal of catching criminals.
The recent notion of anamorphic encryption proposed by Persiano et al.
[Eurocrypt 2022] faces the risks of abuses derived from such infrastruc-
tures that could be maliciously leveraged to realize the phantom menace
of large-scale mass-surveillance programs.
Several recent papers showed positive results on the existence of anamor-
phic encryption schemes, mostly confined to basic settings.
In this work we consider extreme scenarios where in addition to obtaining
secret keys, the authority actively tries to sanitize ciphertexts removing
covert communication.
Despite anamorphic encryption might look impossible to achieve in the
above settings, we give new definitions and somewhat surprising positive
results in two scenarios: Rerandtopia and Interceptopia.
Our main construction consists of two layers of encryption. Interest-
ingly, when carefully instantiated, our scheme achieves a notion of re-
randomizable CCA encryption that outperforms the state of the art in
terms of assumptions and efficiency.

1
Introduction
A new hope. The notion of (receiver) Anamorphic Encryption (AE) [31] enables
private communication in adversarial environments where users’ secret decryp-
tion keys may be compromised. Such situations can occur, for example, under
authoritarian regimes where individuals are subjected to strict monitoring and
may be forced to hand over their private keys. At a high level, AE attains this
seemingly paradoxical goal by offering two operating modes: regular and anamor-
phic. In regular mode, the system functions as a standard public-key encryption
scheme. In anamorphic mode, however, a public key (apk) is associated to two
secret keys: a standard-looking one (ask) and a covert one, called the “double
key” (dk). Bob secretly shares dk with Alice while publishing apk as his public
key. If compelled by an adversary to reveal his secret key, Bob only discloses ask.
A key property of AE is that the pair (apk, ask) remains fully compatible
with the underlying regular encryption scheme. However, Alice can employ dk
as a symmetric key to embed an additional hidden message into the ciphertext,
which stays concealed even when ask is known. Thus, in anamorphic mode, the
scheme allows the encryption of two messages: a regular message m, intended
for adversarial inspection via ask, and a covert message ˆm, recoverable only with
dk. The central security requirement is that anamorphic ciphertexts must be
indistinguishable from regular ones.
Over the last few years several works studied AE, across multiple research
directions. Initial works focused on demonstrating that existing (and possibly
widely deployed) schemes possess anamorphic properties (e.g., [31, 26, 2, 38, 10]).
Other works considered refined definitions (e.g. [2, 10, 32]), inherent limitations
(e.g. [11, 12, 9, 18, 1]) and connections with other primitives (e.g. [38, 9]). In
principle, the adversarial authority (e.g., the dictator of an empire) aware of
main techniques used to encode secret messages in ciphertexts, could resort to
so-called anamorphic-resistant encryption (ARE) [12, 9, 18, 1], that, as the name
suggests, provably disallows any covert communication. This option, however is
currently not practical though: all known AREs have some undesirable weak-
nesses, for example [18] requires the RO, or are too inefficient to be used in
real-life applications.
The dictator strikes back. In this work, we observe that with the goal of sanitizing
ciphertexts sent with schemes that are potentially anamorphic, the authority
can upgrade the surveillance infrastructure as follows: while keeping the option
of forcing a user to reveal the secret key, the authority also takes control of
the communication infrastructure making sure that in the path from sender to
receiver, there always is a node (e.g., a router, or proxy) running an algorithm
aiming at sanitizing the ciphertexts. Given the structure of the Internet (i.e.,
the existence of several ISPs and the dynamic paths preventing single points of
failure), there should be several of such nodes.
The authority can use such nodes to re-randomize the ciphertexts, forbidding
those schemes that are not re-randomizable (e.g., CCA2 schemes). Given that the
main approach to encode a secret message into a ciphertext consists of carefully

choosing the randomness used to encrypt, this upgraded attack of the authority
is seemingly devastating. Notice that no secret is required in the nodes that
perform the re-randomization and this makes the approach very appealing for
the authority since delivering secret keys in multiple places exposes risks due to
potential data breaches.
A more aggressive authority, might instead be willing to risk that a data
breach generates a leak of the secret keys, and thus can make those nodes stronger
by storing in them also the secret keys of the users. In this way a node receiving a
ciphertext can be way more aggressive decrypting first the ciphertext, and then
encrypting the plaintext again. This riskier attack is very effective and clearly
there can not be an anamorphic encryption scheme in this stronger setting.
Obviously this attack is much harder to mount since in realistic scenarios the
authority gets the secret after the fact, when an investigation starts, therefore
after the communication has been completed already.
One might wonder if there is still room to defeat such a stronger authority
with the goal of achieving (receiver) AE even in this more challenging scenarios.
1.1
Our Contributions
Motivated by the above stronger attack scenarios that we envision, in this work
we revisit the concept of anamorphic encryption to consider the stronger and
seemingly realistic setting in which the authority leverages the power to decrypt
users’ ciphertexts (e.g., in case of criminal investigations) while also trying to
contrast covert channels inside encryption.
Concretely, we study two settings.
– Rerandtopia. Given that AE typically uses the encryption randomness to
hide covert messages, in this world the authority forbids schemes that are
not rerandomizable and tries to block covert messages by using network-
level firewalls that re-randomize ciphertexts before transmission. In fact,
re-randomization is relatively cheap to implement, and authorities can spec-
ify re-randomization procedures for each standardized encryption scheme
that is allowed for public usage (of course chosen-ciphertext attacks secure
encryption would be banned).
– Interceptopia. In this world, the authority takes a much more extreme
approach to block covert communication by using network-level firewalls that
are allowed to decrypt and re-encrypt each ciphertext before transmission
using fresh randomness.
An intriguing question is whether some notions of anamorphic communica-
tion are still possible in Rerandtopia and Interceptopia.
Return of the Anamorpher. The main result of this paper is a positive answer to
the above question. We show new constructions of AE that allow for covert com-
munication in both Rerandtopia and Interceptopia. The construction in Rerand-
topia is a two-layer encryption where the external layer allows re-randomization
while the inner layer safely accommodates the anamorphic message.

In Interceptopia there is an obvious impossibility result of the original notion
of anamorphic encryption. However we will still obtain a positive results circum-
venting the power of the dictator by transferring the anamorphic message in the
actual plaintext. As such, we will entirely focus on constructing Deterministic
AE, therefore limiting positive results to restricted message spaces.
Re-randomizable CCA encryption. Last but not least, we connect Rerandtopia
with the known concept of re-randomizable CCA encryption. Indeed, we show
that when instantiating differently our two-layer encryption that defeats the
dictator in Rerandtopia, the resulting encryption scheme outperforms a prior
work of [20] both in terms of assumptions (in contrast to them, we do not need
random oracles) and of efficiency (our ciphertexts are more compact).
1.2
Technical Overview
Re-randomization resistant AE. In Sec. 3, we consider re-randomization
resistant AE. Intuitively, this is a kind of AE whose anamorphic properties are
unaffected, even in case ciphertexts are re-randomized. In our definition, the ad-
versary is allowed to choose the regular message to encrypt, while the anamorphic
message is chosen according to a probability distribution with high min-entropy,
and, upon receiving the challenge ciphertext c, the adversary (without having
the secret key) should not be able to produce a different ciphertext c′ that de-
crypts to the same regular message and a different anamorphic message, despite
the encryption scheme is re-randomizable. We observe that at first sight, this
seems to stop covert communication in Rerandtopia. Moreover, re-randomizing
ciphertexts is just a particular way the authority would use to kill covert commu-
nication and potentially other techniques can also be used to sanitize ciphertexts.
Interestingly, we show an efficient scheme that is anamorphic secure (in the
IND-CPA sense) under the standard definition, is re-randomizable, and is also
re-randomization resistant. The scheme roughly works using two specific en-
cryptions in a onion-style. First we use an AE E1 that is non-malleable against
chosen-plaintext attacks (NM-CPA), and this produces an intermediate cipher-
text that can include an anamorphic message. Then we encrypt the above ci-
phertext with a re-randomizable encryption scheme E2 with indistinguishability
against chosen-plaintext attacks (IND-CPA).6
The intuition about the above construction works is that the NM-CPA prop-
erty of the inner ciphertext protects from attack in which the adversarial re-
randomizer tries to obtain a new inner ciphertext that encodes the same regular
plaintext. The re-randomizability of the external encryption makes the scheme
suitable for those applications (e.g., shuffles, mixers) targeting anonymity, and
to deceive dictators that willing to unlink communications among dissidents.
6 Of course, for this to work the message space of E2 needs to coincide with the
ciphertext space of E1.

Deterministic AE. In Sec. 4, we consider deterministic AE. Intuitively, this
is a kind of AE in which the regular encryption mode is deterministic, yet for
messages with high-enough min-entropy the regular mode and the deterministic
mode are indistinguishable. This clearly makes covert communication possible
in Interceptopia, as decrypting and re-encrypting the intercepted ciphertext pro-
duces the same result.
We realize deterministic AE as follows: in regular mode, we use a determinis-
tic encryption supporting uniformly random messages; in anamorphic mode, we
first encrypt the anamorphic message using symmetric encryption with pseudo-
random ciphertexts [27], and then encrypt the resulting ciphertext using deter-
ministic encryption. Using standard constructions of deterministic encryption,
and any pseudorandom function, we thus obtain instantiations under different
hardness assumptions (with or without random oracles) and with different effi-
ciency tradeoffs.
We stress that our positive results on Deterministic AE translate directly
into positive results in Interceptopia, where achieving anamorphic encryption
generically is obviously impossible.
Black-box re-randomizable PKE. In Sec. 5, we apply our two-layer en-
cryption approach in order to obtain a black-box construction of so-called re-
randomizable PKE, which is a regular PKE whose ciphertexts can be efficiently
re-randomized given the public key. This kind of PKE schemes has been stud-
ied in several previous works [22, 33, 34, 21, 20], and has various applications
(e.g., for realizing so-called mix-nets). A re-randomizable PKE should satisfy
two security requirements. On the one hand, re-randomized ciphertexts should
be efficiently computable and indistinguishable from a fresh encryption of the
underlying message (if it exists); this property is called re-randomizability under
chosen-plaintext attacks (RAND-CPA).7 On the other hand, re-randomization
should be the only allowed malleability on ciphertexts; this property is called
replayable CCA (RCCA) security.
We realize re-randomizable PKE in a black-box way as follows: first, we en-
crypt the message using an IND-CCA2 PKE E1, and then we encrypt the result-
ing ciphertext using a re-randomizable PKE E2. RCCA security follows easily
by IND-CCA2 security of E1. As for RAND-CPA (resp. RAND-CCA1) security,
one would hope that it suffices to assume that E2 satisfies both IND-CPA and
RAND-CPA security. However, the reduction spectacularly breaks and we were
only able to make it work by assuming that E2 satisfies IND-CCA1 security
(i.e., decryption queries are allowed prior to seeing the challenge ciphertext). By
taking E1 to be fully-fledged Cramer-Shoup and E2 to be Cramer-Shoup Lite
(optimized by re-using part of the randomness to encrypt the inner ciphertext),
we obtain a construction of re-randomizable PKE in the plain model under the
DDH assumption, with ciphertexts consisting of 7 group elements.
7 There is also an IND-CCA1 variant (RAND-CCA1) in which the attacker also has
decryption capabilities.

1.3
Related Works
AE is related but distinct from other notions in the literature; see [31] for a com-
prehensive comparison. Extensions achieving stronger privacy guarantees against
parties with access to dk appear in [26, 10]. The notion of robustness for AE was
introduced in [2] and later adapted to a different setting in [38], while a stronger
notion of robustness was proposed in [18]. Limitations of AE are explored in
[11, 12]. In [18] schemes matching limitations are proposed, with stronger forms
of resistance developed in [9]. The connection between AE and Algorithm Substi-
tution Attacks (ASA) [5] is highlighted in [38, 9], enabling cross-interpretation
of results. In [17], AE is extended to a one-to-many setting, where different
receivers can extract distinct anamorphic messages from the same ciphertext.
More extreme scenarios, where encryption is banned but authentication is not,
are studied in [25], introducing anamorphic signatures for covert messaging via
authentication channels; further refinements appear in [23]. Finally, [37] intro-
duces Anamorphic Key Exchange, enabling the covert sharing of a double key
using standard key exchange protocols.
The notion of RCCA security was proposed by Canetti et al. [8] as a re-
laxation of CCA security. This was later adapted to consider the practically
relevant case of re-randomizable encryption by Groth [22], who obtained per-
fectly re-randomizable schemes with RCCA security under the DDH assumption
(against semi generic adversaries). Several other constructions were proposed
afterwards [33, 34, 21, 20], trading efficiency with computational variants of re-
randomizability and stronger assumptions. Previously to our work, the most
efficient scheme achieved 11 group elements assuming the DDH assumption and
non-programmable random oracles [20].

Public Key Encryption
Definition 5 (PKE). A public-key encryption (PKE) scheme E, with message
space M and ciphertext space C, consists of the following probabilistic algorithms:

E.Gen(1λ): Given an unary encoded security parameter λ as input, it outputs a
public key pk and a secret key sk.
E.Enc(pk, m): Given a public key pk and a message m ∈M as input, it outputs
a ciphertext c ∈C.
E.Dec(sk, c): Given a secret key sk and a ciphertext c ∈C as input, it outputs a
message m ∈M or ⊥(indicating a failure).
We use R to denote the set of all possible random coins for E.Enc. We require
perfect correctness, that is, for every m ∈M, all (pk, sk) ←$ E.Gen(1λ), and
all c ←$ E.Enc(pk, m) we have E.Dec(sk, c) = m. We also require that, given the
public key, one can efficiently check membership in C. Note that, in general, C
can be larger than the range of algorithm E.Enc(pk, ·).

2.4
Anamorphic Encryption
The notion of (receiver) anamorphic encryption was first introduced in [31] to
model private communication in the presence of a powerful adversary who con-
trols the PKE scheme in use and knows each user’s secret key, e.g. a dictator.
However, in this paper, we use a more general definition proposed in [10] which
contains [31] as a special case. To achieve the above seemingly impossible goal,
the receiver is allowed to generate its own public and secret key apk, ask in
anamorphic mode, exchange secretly with the sender a double key dk, and lo-
cally storing a trapdoor key tk to decrypt anamorphic messages from the sender.
Syntax is specified in the following definition.

3.1
Implication Between Definitions
We now prove that the strong re-randomization resistance definition implies the
normal definition. Formally:
Theorem 1. If a scheme satisfies strong re-randomization resistance (Def. 16),
then it also satisfies re-randomization resistance (Def. 15).
Proof. Assume there exists a PPT adversary A that wins re-randomization re-
sistance game (Def. 15) for distributions D, bD with a non-negligible advantage
ϵ. We construct a PPT adversary B that wins strong re-randomization resistance
game (Def. 16) with advantage at least ϵ for distribution bD.
The adversary B works as follows:
1. B receives apk from the strong re-randomization resistant challenger.
2. B samples two arbitrary distinct messages m0, m1 ←$ D(M).
3. B sends (m0, m1) to the strong re-randomization resistant challenger and re-
ceives back a challenge ciphertext c∗←AT.Enc(apk, dk, mb, ˆm) for a random
b ←$ {0, 1}.
4. B runs A on input (apk, c∗). This exactly simulates A’s original game envi-
ronment.
5. When A outputs a ciphertext c′, B sends the same ciphertext to the strong
re-randomization resistant challenger.
When A succeeds in the original game, A outputs: (i) c′ such that E.Dec(ask, c′)
= mb, where b is the random bit chosen by the strong re-randomization resis-
tant challenger, and mb is the message encrypted in c∗; (ii) AT.Dec(tk, c′) ̸= ˆm.
Therefore B wins in the strong re-randomization resistance game of with the
same probability of A winning the re-randomization resistant game.
This contradicts the assumption that the scheme satisfies strong re-randomization
resistance. Thus, any scheme satisfying Def. 16 also satisfies Def. 15.

(E.Gen, E.Enc, E.Dec) with message space M = M1 and ciphertext space C = C2.
Key Generation: Upon input the security parameter λ ∈N, algorithm E.Gen(1λ)
runs (pk1, sk1) ←$ E.Gen1(1λ) and (pk2, sk2) ←$ E.Gen2(1λ) and outputs
pk = (pk1, pk2) and sk = (sk1, sk2).
Encryption: Upon input the public key pk = (pk1, pk2) and a message m ∈M,
algorithm E.Enc(pk, m) lets c1 ←$ E.Enc1(pk1, m) and outputs c = c2 ←$
E.Enc2(pk2, c1).
Decryption: Upon input the secret key sk = (sk1, sk2) and a ciphertext c ∈C,
the decryption algorithm lets c1 = E.Dec2(sk2, c). If c1 = ⊥, it returns ⊥.
Else, it returns m = E.Dec1(sk1, c1).
Re-randomization: Upon input the public key pk = (pk1, pk2) and a cipher-
text c ∈C, the re-randomization algorithm returns the same as Rerand(pk2, c).
