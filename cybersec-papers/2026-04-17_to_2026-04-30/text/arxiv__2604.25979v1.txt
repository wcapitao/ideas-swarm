A Quantitative Confirmation of the Currier Language Distinction
Christophe Parisel
ch.parisel@gmail.com
April 30, 2026
Abstract
We present a quantitative analysis of character-pair substitution ratios in the Voynich
manuscript[10], testing whether Currier’s A/B language distinction (1976) reflects a genuine
structural property of the text. A Beta-Binomial mixture model applied to raw character
counts without access to labels recovers the Currier split with ARI = 0.383, assigning 113 of
185 folios with > 90% posterior confidence. A supervised Beta-Binomial classifier trained on a
subset of folios predicts the A/B identity of held-out folios at 89.2% accuracy. The character
pairs separate into three functional regimes that constrain any theory of the Voynich writing
system.
Keywords: Voynich Manuscript; Currier languages; Beta-Binomial mixture model; substitu-
tion cipher
1
Introduction
In 1976, NSA cryptographer Prescott Currier[1] proposed that the Voynich manuscript contains
at least two distinct “languages,” designated A and B, based on statistical differences in character
frequencies and word patterns. This claim has been widely cited but rarely tested[2, 3, 4] with
modern quantitative methods against explicit null hypotheses.
Three challenges confront any attempt to validate the Currier distinction.
The first is
whether the observed differences could arise from natural statistical fluctuation within a single
homogeneous text. The second is whether the differences reflect a genuine property of the
text or a confound of physical structure, since Currier’s original assignment correlates with
quire boundaries. The third and most demanding is whether the A/B distinction is an intrinsic

property of the character statistics that can be discovered without access to Currier’s labels and
used to predict the behavior of unseen text.
We address all three challenges through a specific mechanism: if the Voynich script employs a
substitution system, and if A and B represent different substitution regimes, then pairs of visually
similar[6, 7] characters should exhibit systematically different frequency ratios in A versus B
sections. These ratio shifts should be irreproducible by a single-language source, detectable at
within-quire language boundaries, recoverable by unsupervised generative models, and predictive
of character statistics on held-out folios.

Method
2.1
Data
We use the Takahashi EVA transcription of the Voynich manuscript in IVTFF format[5], com-
prising 32,693 word tokens across 185 folios with confident Currier A/B assignments (108 A, 77
B).
2.2

We define eleven pairs of EVA characters that are visually similar or occupy analogous structural
positions (Table 1).

For each pair (a, b), we compute the ratio r = count(a)/(count(a) + count(b)) at the folio

2.3
Boundary analysis
All consecutive folio pairs in manuscript order are classified by transition type: same (same
language, same quire), quire only (quire boundary, same language), lang only (language
change within a quire), and lang+quire (language change at a quire boundary). For each
transition, the mean absolute ratio change across all qualifying pairs gives the transition’s jump
magnitude.
2.4
Simulation baselines
Model 1 (Label Shuffle): Real text with A/B labels randomly permuted (1,000 iterations).
Model 2 (Single Markov): Synthetic text from one order-2 character-level Markov model
with real labels applied (200 iterations).
Model 3 (Split Markov): Separate A and B Markov models generating text for their
respective folios (200 iterations).
2.5
Unsupervised recovery
Four clustering algorithms (K-means, GMM, hierarchical, spectral) applied to the folio × pair
ratio matrix at k = 2 without access to labels, evaluated by Adjusted Rand Index. Permutation
test (1,000 iterations) for significance.
Optimal k search via BIC, AIC, and silhouette for
k = 2 through 8. Leave-one-pair-out and single-pair analyses for feature diagnostics. PCA for
dimensionality analysis.
2.6
Generative latent model
A Beta-Binomial mixture model[9] fitted to raw folio-level counts (counta, countb) for each pair.
This is the natural generative model for the data: each folio’s pair counts are drawn from a
binomial distribution whose success probability follows a Beta distribution specific to the folio’s
latent regime. Missing pairs (below the minimum token threshold) are handled by omitting their
likelihood contribution. The model is fitted via EM with method-of-moments M-step updates,
using 10 random restarts, for k = 1 through 6 components. Model selection uses BIC.
2.7
Predictive validation
Cross-validated label prediction. A supervised Beta-Binomial classifier learns regime-specific
(α, β) parameters for each pair from training folios and predicts A/B labels on held-out folios.

Evaluated via 20-repeat 5-fold stratified cross-validation.
Spatial split prediction. Training on the first half of the manuscript (in folio order)
and predicting the second half, and vice versa. Also an even/odd folio split for balanced class
representation.
Ratio prediction. For each held-out folio, the A/B label is used to predict the expected
character-pair ratio (from training-set class means). The reduction in mean squared error relative
to a global mean baseline gives R2.
Permutation baseline. Labels are shuffled and cross-validation is repeated 500 times to
obtain a null distribution for prediction accuracy.

3.4
Unsupervised clustering recovers the A/B split
K-means on the standardized ratio matrix yields ARI[8] = 0.208 (p < 0.001 vs. 1,000 permuta-
tions). PC1 alone (22.7% of variance) gives ARI = 0.408 by median split. Removing the two
most disruptive pairs (e/ch, e/ee) raises K-means ARI to 0.456.
3.5
The Beta-Binomial mixture selects k = 2
The generative mixture model fitted to raw character counts without access to labels selects k = 2
as optimal by BIC (Table 4). The k = 2 model assigns 113 of 185 folios (61%) with posterior
confidence > 90%. The two recovered regimes align with Currier’s A and B, with the model
independently discovering the same partition that Currier identified from manual inspection.

3.6
Predictive validation confirms the distinction is real
The model predicts held-out folio labels at 89.2% accuracy with ARI = 0.612. Zero of 500 label
permutations achieved comparable accuracy (p < 0.002). The backward split (41.3%) fails due
to class imbalance: the late folios are predominantly B, so the backward-trained model has
insufficient A examples. This is a training-set limitation, not a signal failure. Knowing the A/B
label explains 29.3% of variance in character-pair ratios on unseen folios (R2 = 0.293).
3.7
Three functional regimes
The character pairs separate into three regimes confirmed across all analyses:
Categorical (d/l, or/ar, s/r, e/ee): Large Cram´er’s V (> 0.20), heavy PC1 loadings, large
regime differences in the mixture model, essential for clustering. Near-binary markers of A/B
identity.
Intermediate (ol/al, y/dy, k/t): Moderate Cram´er’s V (0.04–0.15), moderate PC1 loadings,
carry the boundary-jump signal.
Free variation (o/a, ch/sh, f/p): Near-zero Cram´er’s V , near-zero PC1 loadings, minimal
regime differences. Independent of the A/B distinction.
The e/ch anomaly. This pair has near-zero aggregate Cram´er’s V (0.007) but the strongest
boundary effect (p = 2.45 × 10−6) and actively suppresses clustering when included (removing it
doubles ARI from 0.208 to 0.456). Its variation is conditioned by factors beyond the binary A/B
regime—possibly word-level or positional context—making it simultaneously the most informative
pair for local transitions and the most disruptive for global partitioning.

4.2
The distinction is intrinsic, not imposed
The Beta-Binomial mixture, fitted to raw character counts with no knowledge of Currier’s
labels, selects k = 2 by BIC and recovers the Currier partition with ARI = 0.383. The data’s
own statistical structure implies two groups, and those groups happen to be the ones Currier
identified. The predictive validation goes further: a model trained on some folios can predict
the character-pair ratios of unseen folios given only their A/B label (R2 = 0.293). This is not a
description of the training data; it is a prediction about data the model has never seen.
4.3
Structure beyond the binary split
Three findings converge on the conclusion that the manuscript contains structure beyond the
A/B distinction. The split-Markov model underestimates folio-level variance by 1.5–6× for every
pair, indicating sub-regimes or gradients within A and B. The R2 of 0.293 quantifies this: the
A/B label explains 29% of inter-folio variance. The remaining 71% is real structure that the
binary distinction does not capture.
4.4
The spatial asymmetry
The forward spatial split (79.6%) substantially outperforms the backward split (41.3%). This
asymmetry arises from class imbalance: the early manuscript is predominantly A, the late
manuscript predominantly B. The cross-validated result (89.2%), which balances classes across
folds, gives the best estimate of true predictive power.
7

4.5
Implications for the writing system
The three-regime taxonomy constrains theories of the Voynich writing system. Categorical pairs
behave like cipher substitutions: one variant is used in regime A, the other in regime B, with
near-complete switching. Intermediate pairs behave like graded preferences. Free-variation pairs
are unconditioned by the regime and may represent genuine allographic variation. Any proposed
decipherment must explain why d/l, or/ar, s/r, and e/ee switch near-categorically between
regimes while o/a and ch/sh do not.
4.6
Limitations
The analysis depends on the accuracy of the EVA transcription and the Currier A/B assignments.
The minimum token threshold of 20 per folio excludes some pairs from some folios. The Beta-
Binomial model assumes independence across pairs within a folio, which may not hold if pairs
share underlying conditioning factors. The backward spatial split’s poor performance highlights
sensitivity to class balance in the training set.

5
Conclusion
The Currier A/B language distinction is a genuine, statistically robust, intrinsic, and predictive
property of the Voynich manuscript text. It is the dominant partition discovered by a generative
model fitted to raw character counts without labels (BIC-optimal k = 2, ARI = 0.383). It
predicts the character-pair statistics of unseen folios at 89.2% accuracy and explains 29% of
inter-folio variance. It persists at within-quire language boundaries and cannot be produced by a
single-language source. The manuscript contains additional structure beyond the binary split, but
the A/B distinction is the primary axis of variation in the character-pair statistics. The character
pairs separate into three functional regimes—categorical, intermediate, and free-variation—that
any theory of the Voynich writing system must accommodate.
