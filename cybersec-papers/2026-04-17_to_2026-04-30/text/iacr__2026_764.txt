AbstractвҖ”The rapid development of quantum computing has
driven a new wave of cryptographic innovation: Post-Quantum
Cryptography (PQC), a class of algorithms that resist quan-
tum attacks. In particular, the National Institute of Standards
and Technology (NIST) has initiated the PQC standardization
process, selecting five algorithms. Notably, HQC (the newest
selection) is a code-based PQC scheme that has not been widely
studied in the literature, especially with respect to its hardware
acceleration. This paper follows the current trend to design
CEDAR, a Compact and Efficient Decoder Architecture for Reed-
Solomon Reed-Muller (RS-RM) code in HQC. We have proposed
three layers of contributions in total: (i) an optimized RM
decoder is designed; (ii) an efficient low-complexity RS decoder
is also presented; (iii) a complete HQC decoder is implemented,
along with a comprehensive evaluation (it is shown that CEDAR
outperforms the existing approach). We hope this outcome will
facilitate a more efficient hardware acceleration of HQC and
impact the ongoing NIST PQC standardization process.
Index TermsвҖ”Compact design, hardware accelerator, PQC,
HQC decoder, Reed-Solomon, Reed-Muller.
I. INTRODUCTION
The development of quantum computing brings threats
to conventional cryptographic systems, particularly RSA and
Elliptic Curve Cryptography, which are vulnerable to quantum
attacks using ShorвҖҷs algorithms [1]. In response, the National
Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) initiated the
Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) standardization process in
2016 [2]. Several algorithms have been selected, including the
most recent code-based Hamming Quasi-Cyclic (HQC) [3].
Brief background. HQC is a code-based Key Encapsula-
tion Mechanism (KEM) method, whose hardness relies on
the syndrome decoding problem for quasi-cyclic codes [4].
HQC performs polynomial reduction of computationally hard
problems to ensure its security guarantees without requiring
indistinguishability assumptions [5]. HQC contains two linear
codes: a random quasi-cyclic code to ensure security, and
an efficient and error-correction-capable fixed public concate-
nated code, which consists of Reed-Solomon (RS, external
code) combined with Reed-Muller (RM, internal code). This
dual-code structure reduces the ciphertext size while meeting
the security requirements for NIST security levels 1, 3, and 5.
Existing works. Recent research in PQC has gradually
switched to the hardware implementation side. For HQC,
This work was accepted in the 2026 IEEE International Symposium on
Circuits and Systems (ISCASвҖҷ26).
however, there are still not many related hardware designs:
a sparse polynomial multiplier design is presented in [6]; a
constant-timing fixed-weight sampler is reported in [7]; a high-
level Synthesis designed HQC is released in [8]; hardware-
implemented HQC is presented in [9]; a low-latency polyno-
mial multiplication with RS and RM decoders is given in [10].
Challenges. Compared to operations like sparse polynomial
multiplication and encoding, decoding in HQC involves more
complex arithmetic procedures. Despite the existing hardware
design works achieving various advances, there still lacks suf-
ficient attention to a resource-optimized decoder for HQC: ex-
isting designs prioritize the optimization of sparse polynomial
multiplication while the decoder part remains under-explored.
Finally, recent reports like [9] has indicated HQC decoder is a
critical component and its efficient implementation determines
the overall hardware acceleration efficiency for HQC.
Contributions. Therefore, this paper presents CEDAR, a
Compact and Efficient Decoder Architecture for RS-RM con-
catenated codes in HQC. The primary contributions include:
вҖў A compact and pipelined RM decoder is proposed, with
optimized Fast Hadamard Transform and a compare unit.
вҖў An efficient RS decoder with low resource consumption
is presented, which includes a novel low-complexity
error location/evaluation polynomial calculator deploying
Enhanced Parallel Inversionless Berlekamp-Massey Al-
gorithm (ePIMBA) and a novel low-complexity iterative
Chien Search and Error Evaluation (CSEE) component.
вҖў A comprehensive evaluation is given to demonstrate supe-
rior performance compared to an existing implementation
across all NIST security levels.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section
II presents the preliminaries; Section III details the proposed
decoder architecture; Section IV provides implementation re-
sults and comparision; and Section V draws a conclusion.

A. Top Level Architectural Overview
As shown in Fig. 1(a), the HQC decoder implements a
concatenated decoding that recovers the original message from
the received codeword corrupted by errors, with the ability to
recover at most Оҙ-byte errors. The decoder operates on the
2-tage hierarchical structure where the duplicated RM code
serves as the inner code and the shortened RS code serves as
the outer code, forming an [n1n2, k, Оҙ] concatenated code.
The decoder accepts a 128-bit wide input stream rep-
resenting the received codeword and processes it through

two sequential stages. In the first stage, the RM decoder
performs soft-decision decoding on each of the duplicated RM
codewords, producing codeword maximum likelihood indices
(cdw) that indicate the most likely transmitted codeword for
each segment. These decoded symbols are then fed to the RS
decoder in the second stage, where algebraic decoding over the
finite field GF(28) corrects residual errors and recovers the k-
byte message by utilizing the ePIMBA algorithm as shown in
Algorithm 1. The decoderвҖҷs parameterization supports all three
security levels (128, 192, and 256 bits), with corresponding
adjustments to the code parameters: Оҙ вҲҲ{15, 16, 29}, n1 вҲҲ
{46, 56, 90}, and K вҲҲ{16, 24, 32}. The decoder also takes in
start and outputs done and reading address for the interaction
with outer components e.g. BRAM, with the decoded message
output on msg having width 8В·k bits. The modular architecture
facilitates pipeline operation where RM decoding can keep
working while the RS decoding of previous blocks proceeds.
B. RM Decoder
The RM decoder implements a Fast Hadamard Transform
based decoding algorithm to identify the maximum-likelihood
codeword and its corresponding index from the received
codeword. The decoder consists of three main components:
Expansion-and-Sum, Hadamard and Compare, and Control.
The Expansion-and-Sum component first processes the 128-
bit input stream with multiplicity factor MULTIPLICITY вҲҲ
{3, 5} to accumulate replicated codewords. For HQC-1, three
replicated codewords are summed element-wise; for HQC-2
and HQC-3, five replicas are summed. The sums are then sent
to the Hadamard and Compare component. This component
contains 128 3/5-bit full adders executing the additions and
a 128 Г— 3/128 Г— 5 register storing the accumulated results,
depending on the selected security level.
With optimization to a similar design as [9], the Hadamard
and Compare component implements a 7-layer butterfly
pipeline computing the 128-point Fast Hadamard Transform
through iterative pairwise additions and subtractions, along
with the comparison of the calculated values to select the
peak values and corresponding indices among all transformed
coefficients. Each layer contains two adders to calculate the
addition and subtraction between two selected coefficients and
four simplified FIFOs to store intermediate results for the first
and second halves of even and odd indexed coefficients. The
FIFOs are optimized specially for this by saving abundant con-
trol and calculation read/write addresses and logic complexity.
In the last layer, the newly calculated sum/difference will be
compared with the existing peak value to determine the value
with the maximum absolute value and its corresponding index
(two counters are also involved to calculate the final index).
C. RS Decoder

field GF(28) using the primitive polynomial, implementing a
shortened RS code with parameters [n1, k, Оҙ] where the error
correction capability Оҙ ranges from 15 to 30 depending on
the security level. The decoder receives codeword symbols
decoded concatenated RS-codeword from the RM decoder
in 8 bits and processes the codeword through four primary
components: the Syndrome Calculator and CSR (circular shift-
register), the ePIMBA component, the CSEE component, and
a Message CSR. The Syndrome Calculator first computes 2t
syndrome values Si = Pn1вҲ’1

IV. EVALUATION AND COMPARISON
Experimental Setup. The experimental setup is as follows:
(i) the proposed CEDAR is coded in Verilog and implemented
on the Artix-7 xc7a200t-3 FPGA through Vivado 2022.2
(after place & route, with functionality verified); (ii) we have
obtained its implementation performance under three security
level parameter sets of HQC; (iii) the obtained implementation
results, including resource usage (LUTs, FFs, and BRAMs),
maximum frequency (Fmax), delay, and equivalent Slice (eS-
lice) are listed in Table I along with those of [9].
Comparison of Implementation Results. Table I presents
a comprehensive comparison of our decoder implementation
against the state-of-the-art design [9]. Overall, our implemen-
tation demonstrates significant improvements in several critical
metrics while maintaining competitive performance in others.
In terms of resource usage, our design achieves a notable
reduction in eSlice count. For HQC-1, CEDAR achieves
slice reduction by 6.6%, while for HQC-3 and HQC-5, the
reductions are 7.9% and 9.7%, respectively. This improvement
stems from our optimized control logic and efficient resource
sharing in the ePIMBA processor. Although we use 0.5 more
ROM to store the multiplicative inverses in CSEE, our eSlice
still reaches a reduction of 6.2%, 1.6% and 6.6%, respectively.
Meanwhile, the maximum operating frequencies of the
proposed design reach over 227MHz across all security levels,
compared to 205-212MHz in [9], resulting in at least 9.4%
frequency improvement. The enhanced frequency is attributed
to our pipelined architecture and the streamlined datapath in
the syndrome calculator, ePIMBA, and CSEE component.
The equivalent area-delay product (eADP) and its reduction
rate (eADPR) further demonstrate the efficiency of the pro-
posed design. Our HQC-1 design achieves an eADP of 39,409,
achieving an eADPR of 25.11% compared [9]. For HQC-3, the
eADPR is 16.71%, while HQC-5 shows a modest 2.78% im-
provement. The diminishing eADPR at higher security levels is
primarily due to the increased error correction capability of the
RS decoder. This increase causes latency to grow quadratically
as (2Оҙ + 1)2, leading to in a drop on the eADPR at higher
security parameters. However, the reduced area consumption
ensures that the proposed design maintains lower eADP, which
demonstrates an overall superior efficiency.

This paper presents CEDAR for the efficient implementation
of the decoder within the newly selected NIST PQC algo-
rithm HQC. We have presented the optimized pipelined RM
decoder with Hadamard Transform and Compare architecture
for efficient decoding. Then, the details of the efficient low-
complexity RS decoder with iterative syndrome calculation,
iterative ePIMBA calculation, and a streamlined CSEE unit
with serial Chien search are provided. Finally, the imple-
mentation results are presented, and a comparison with the
state-of-the-art design is provided, demonstrating eADPRs of
25.11%, 16.71%, and 2.78% for HQC-1, HQC-3, and HQC-5,
respectively, confirming the superior efficiency of CEDAR.
