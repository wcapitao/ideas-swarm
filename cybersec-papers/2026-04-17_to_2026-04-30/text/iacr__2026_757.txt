Integral Distinguishers and a 4-Round Key-Recovery Attack on
Kuznyechik Without Initial Key Whitening
Nitish Kumar, Ranit Dutta, and Bimal Mandal
Department of Mathematics, Indian Institute of Technology Jodhpur,
Karwar–342030, India
{p23ma0010, p22ma207, bimalmandal}@iitj.ac.in
Abstract. Kuznyechik is a 128-bit block cipher standardized in GOST R 34.12–2015. In this paper We
study Kuznyechik from the viewpoint of integral cryptanalysis, i.e., we track how structured multisets of
chosen plaintexts propagate through the round functions. Starting from a first-order structure of 28 plain-
texts (one byte takes all 256 values while the remaining bytes are fixed), we obtain a 2-round distinguisher:
after two rounds, every byte position is balanced, meaning that the XOR-sum over the 256 texts equals
zero. Next, in the setting without initial key-whitening, we extend this distinguisher to three rounds by
applying one inverse round to the original structure to construct a new input set. Finally, we turn the
3-round balanced property into a 4-round key-recovery attack by partially inverting the last round and
filtering last-round key-byte guesses using the balanced test; multiple independent structures remove false
candidates.
Keywords: Kuznyechik · Integral cryptanalysis · Key recovery

Introduction
The widespread deployment of digital services has made efficient and well-studied encryption a basic require-
ment in modern systems. Symmetric-key primitives, and block ciphers in particular, remain the main tool for
high-throughput confidentiality in practice. Kuznyechik is a 128-bit SP-network block cipher standardized in
GOST R 34.12–2015 [1] and in use since 2016. Given its standardization and deployment, evaluating reduced-
round security under modern cryptanalytic techniques remains of practical and scientific interest. Cryptanalysis
aims to assess the strength of cryptographic algorithms by identifying potential weaknesses that could be ex-
ploited by an adversary. Over the years, numerous cryptanalytic techniques have been developed for analyzing
symmetric block ciphers, including differential cryptanalysis, linear cryptanalysis, algebraic attacks, and inte-
gral cryptanalysis. Algebraic attacks attempt to exploit systems of low-degree polynomial equations describing
a cipher. In Kuznyechik, the high algebraic degree of the S-box reduces the feasibility of such representations,
thereby enhancing resistance to algebraic cryptanalysis [2]. Differential cryptanalysis studies how pairs of plain-
texts with a chosen input difference propagate through a block cipher, aiming to predict output differences with
non-negligible probability. This paradigm was established by Biham and Shamir [3] and became a foundational
technique for analyzing iterated block ciphers. A key conceptual bridge toward integral attacks is to move from
reasoning about pairwise differences to reasoning about higher-order derivatives (and, equivalently, cancellation
phenomena over structured sets of many texts). Knudsen [4] formalized truncated and higher-order differential
viewpoints, showing that when a cipher’s round function behaves like a low-degree Boolean polynomial (over
F2), summing (XORing) evaluations over carefully chosen input subspaces can cancel terms and yield determin-
istic relations after a small number of rounds. This set-based viewpoint appears concretely in the Square family:
one chooses a set of 28 plaintexts where one byte takes all 256 values while other bytes are held constant. After
a few rounds, certain state bytes become balanced (their XOR over the set equals zero), yielding a distinguisher
and, with additional steps, a key-recovery strategy. This is the classic integral [5] phenomenon.
Knudsen and Wagner [6] later unified and generalized these ideas under the name integral cryptanalysis: an
attack methodology that tracks how multisets propagate, predicting when components become constant, active,
or balanced, and giving a systematic framework that subsumes square-like arguments as special cases. Each
of these techniques explores different structural and statistical properties of encryption algorithms. In contrast
to classical cryptanalysis methods that focus on individual plaintext–ciphertext pairs, integral cryptanalysis
examines the behavior of the cipher over structured sets of plaintexts. This approach analyzes how specific
properties of multiple plaintexts propagate through the encryption rounds and affect the resulting ciphertexts.
Various type of cryptanalysis of Kuznyechik mainly targets reduced-round variants or implementation settings.
AlTawy and Youssef [7] presented meet-in-the-middle attacks on reduced-round Kuznyechik (reported up to 5
rounds). On the implementation side, AlTawy, Duman, and Youssef [8] studied fault attacks: in a random-byte
fault model targeting rounds 7 and 8, they recover the master key with only a few faults on average, and they
also analyze a setting with a secret S-box using ineffective fault analysis. Biryukov, Khovratovich, and Perrin
[9] developed multiset-algebraic techniques leading to attacks on reduced Kuznyechik (up to 7 rounds out of

Contributions. In this paper we studied 3 round integral distinguisher of Kuznyechik cipher without key
prewhitening. In this paper we aim to focus on the areas as follows:
– We revisit the known first-order integral property of Kuznyechik and present a concise 2-round distinguisher
based on a 28-plaintext structure (one active byte, 15 constant bytes), where after two rounds all 16 state
bytes are balanced. [12]
– In the no-prewhitening setting, we extend this distinguisher by one round to obtain a 3-round distinguisher.
The extension is achieved by constructing a preimage structure via one-round inversion using L−1 and S−1.
– We convert the resulting 3-round balanced property into a 4-round key-recovery attack (still without initial
key-whitening). By partially inverting the last round and applying byte-wise balanced tests, we recover the
last-round key byte-by-byte; multiple independent structures are used to eliminate false candidates.
Organization. The rest of the paper has been organised as follows: Section 3 recalls the notation and the
components of Kuznyechik (round function, S/S−1, L/L−1, and key schedule). Section 4 develops the integral
distinguishers, including the one-round inversion technique to reach three rounds and presents the 4-round key-
recovery attack and its complexity analysis. Section 5 describes limitations of our work. Section 6 concludes the
paper.

2.2
Kuznyechik cipher structure
Kuznyechik is a symmetric block cipher standardized under GOST R 34.12–2015. It encrypts 128-bit data blocks
using a 256-bit secret key and is based on a substitution–permutation network (SPN) architecture comprising
nine rounds and a final key addition. The cipher operates over the finite field F28 defined by the irreducible
polynomial
p(x) = x8 + x7 + x6 + x + 1.
Each 128-bit block is represented as a vector of 16 bytes, with each byte interpreted as an element of F28.
The S-box.
The Kuznyechik substitution box (S-box) is a bijective mapping S : F28 →F28, designed to ensure
strong nonlinearity and to provide resistance against linear and differential cryptanalysis. The S-box is defined
by a 16 × 16 lookup table, in which each input byte is interpreted as a pair of hexadecimal digits specifying the
corresponding row and column indices. The table entries are represented in hexadecimal notation and arranged
in row-major order.

4.5
Complexity and success probability
The data complexity is two first-order structures, i.e., 2 · 28 = 29 chosen plaintexts and the same number
of 4-round encryptions. The offline phase first computes Y (t) = L−1(C(t)) for all ciphertexts, which costs 29
evaluations of L−1. The dominant step is the balanced test: per structure, for each of the 16 byte positions we
test 28 guesses, and each test processes 28 texts, resulting in 16·28 ·28 = 220 bytewise inverse S-box evaluations.
Using two structures, the dominant offline cost is therefore 221 bytewise inverse S-box evaluations, plus 29
evaluations of L−1. The memory requirement is about 2 · 28 · 16 ≈8 KB to store the Y -states.
For the correct key byte the balanced equation holds deterministically. For an incorrect guess, the balanced
test holds with probability about 2−8 per structure, so the expected number of surviving candidates per byte
is 28 · 2−8 = 1 after one structure and 28 · 2−16 = 2−8 after two structures, yielding unique recovery with high
probability.
5
Discussion and limitations
Role of the without pre-whitening setting. Our 3-round distinguisher is obtained by extending the known 2-round
first-order integral distinguisher with one additional backward round (one-round inversion). This construction
critically uses the reduced-round variant without initial key-whitening. In the standard Kuznyechik specification,
the unknown prewhitening key addition P ⊕K0 prevents us from generating the required preimage structure,
hence the backward extension is not directly applicable. We experimentally verified that with the same 28-
plaintext structure we do not obtain a 3-round integral distinguisher in the presence of prewhitening with key
independency.
Data requirements for higher-round distinguishers. Our analysis is restricted to first-order structures of size
28 and their backward-extended variants. To obtain distinguishers for substantially more rounds, one typically
needs much larger plaintext sets and higher-order properties. In particular, degree-based approaches (e.g., using
algebraic degree bounds) imply that integral properties may require structures of size as large as 2116 (or
larger) to appear at deeper rounds, as explicitly analyzed in the multiset-algebraic/degree-based line of work by
Biryukov et al. [9]. This is also reflected by division-property based searches: ElSheikh and Youssef [10] report
4-round integral distinguishers for Kuznyechik at data around 2120 and for 3 round it is 256. Consequently,
extending our distinguisher beyond three rounds in the standard model is expected to require prohibitive data
complexity.
Limits of extending key recovery beyond 4 rounds. We also attempted to extend our key-recovery attack beyond 4
rounds by adding one more round on the ciphertext side. For 5 rounds (still without prewhitening), after peeling
the last round via L−1 we obtain Y = L−1(C) = S(X4) ⊕K′
5 with K′
5 = L−1(K5), hence X4 = S−1(Y ⊕K′
5).
The next step required to reach the balanced boundary involves applying L−1 to X4, but Kuznyechik’s diffusion
is global: each byte of L−1(X4) depends on all 16 bytes of X4, and therefore on all 16 bytes of K′
5. As a result,
even a single-byte balanced test at the X3 boundary is not separable by key bytes and inherently involves the
full 2128 space of K′
5 (and additionally the relevant byte(s) of K′
4 if not already recovered). Thus, the time
complexity for extending the attack to 5 rounds is at least Ω(2128) basic operations, making it infeasible. We
therefore stop at 4 rounds.

Conclusion
We studied Kuznyechik under integral cryptanalysis in a reduced-round model without initial key-whitening.
Starting from a first-order 28 structure (one active byte), the round function exhibits the expected 2-round

bytewise-balanced property. Exploiting the absence of pre-whitening, we constructed a suitable preimage mul-
tiset via one-round inversion, which yields a 3-round bytewise-balanced distinguisher in this setting.
We then converted the 3-round balanced property into a 4-round key-recovery attack by partially inverting
the last round and testing transformed last-round key bytes independently using a balanced XOR condition.
The resulting attack has low data complexity (two 28 structures) and a small offline cost dominated by bytewise
