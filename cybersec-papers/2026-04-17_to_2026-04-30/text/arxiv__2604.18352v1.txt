Abstract
State-of-the-art Differentially Private (DP) synthetic data generators such as MST
and AIM are widely used, yet tightly auditing their privacy guarantees remains
challenging. We introduce a Gaussian Differential Privacy (GDP)-based auditing
framework that measures privacy via the full false-positive/false-negative tradeoff.
Applied to MST and AIM under worst-case settings, our method provides the
first tight audits in the strong-privacy regime. For (ϵ, δ) = (1, 10−2), we obtain
µemp ≈0.43 vs. implied µ = 0.45, showing a small theory-practice gap.
Our code is publicly available: https://github.com/sassoftware/dpmm.

Introduction
Privacy-preserving synthetic data, supported by formal guarantees such as Differential Pri-
vacy (DP) [11, 13], offers a promising approach for sharing sensitive tabular data while protecting
individual privacy. Under this paradigm, a generative model is trained on the sensitive data with
carefully calibrated noise so its outputs (e.g., model parameters/generated samples) are approximately
indistinguishable with respect to the inclusion/exclusion of any individual record (typically formalized
by the privacy parameters (ϵ, δ)). Then, the trained model can serve as a proxy for the original data,
enabling the release of either the model or synthetic datasets sampled from it.
Among the rich literature on DP generative models [9, 22], MST [28] and AIM [29] consistently
demonstrate strong privacy-utility tradeoffs [7, 15, 17, 41]. They have won a NIST competition [33],
been integrated into popular libraries [24, 35, 36], and supported a UK census data release [34]. Both
follow the select-measure-generate paradigm [26, 28]: selecting low-order marginals, measuring
them with Gaussian noise, and generating synthetic data preserving these marginals.
Given the importance and widespread use of MST and AIM, it is essential to empirically validate
their DP guarantees. This motivates DP auditing [2], which quantifies privacy leakage by framing
evaluation as a distinguishing game aligned with the DP definition, often realized via Membership
Inference Attacks (MIAs) [20, 39] (which assess the influence of a target record on the model). The
resulting leakage estimates are then compared to the claimed DP parameters to verify implementation
correctness [1, 31, 32, 40] or reveal potential violations [1, 6, 16, 19, 23].
Although prior work has investigated DP auditing for MST [1, 24], important gaps remain. First,
existing audits provide limited insight in the strong-privacy regime (e.g., ϵ = 1), yielding loose/null
results unless the implementation exhibits issues such as data domain leakage or floating-point
vulnerabilities. Second, prior studies report results for a single (ϵ, δ) setting rather than characterizing
the full privacy tradeoff, which can lead to incomplete or even misleading conclusions [18].
In this paper, we close these gaps by developing a Gaussian DP (GDP) [10] auditing framework
for DP synthetic data generators. Building on prior empirical audits [1], we adopt the GDP view,
which summarizes privacy via a single parameter µ capturing the full false-positive/negative tradeoff
(FPR/FNR) of a strong-adversary MIA. We apply this framework to MST/AIM in a worst-case setup.

MST and AIM follow the select-measure-generate paradigm [26, 28]. They use the Exponential
mechanism [12] to select informative marginals and the Gaussian mechanism [30] to measure counts.
Both start from one-way marginals; MST selects pairwise marginals forming a maximum spanning
tree, while AIM iteratively selects higher-order marginals based on estimated importance. Synthetic
datasets are generated to match the noisy marginals via a graphical-model inference [27].
Although privacy is specified as (ϵ, δ)-DP, both models internally convert this budget to a ρ-zCDP
guarantee using the Canonne et al. [5] conversion, selecting the smallest ρ that implies the target
(ϵ, δ) guarantee. Consistent with existing implementations [24, 25], we adopt this numerically stable
(though suboptimal [3, 38]) conversion in our experiments.

GDP Auditing Framework of MST and AIM
We adopt the empirical DP auditing framework of Annamalai et al. [1], which views privacy auditing
as a hypothesis test via a Membership Inference Attack (MIA). In short, a challenger trains many
independent instances of a randomized mechanism on two neighboring datasets and provides their
outputs to an adversary, who attempts to distinguish which dataset was used. The resulting false-
positive and false-negative rates define a point on the (α, β) tradeoff curve, which can be compared
to theoretical DP frontiers and converted to an empirical µ under µ-GDP. Next, we describe the
components of the framework and our specific instantiation.
MST and AIM. We study MST and AIM under a restricted configuration where only one-way
marginals are measured. The dependency graph is fixed and no higher-order marginals are selected,
so MST and AIM reduce to the same independent-marginal model (we relax this assumption in
App. A). The full budget is allocated to these measurements, reducing the model to (Gaussian) noisy
one-way marginals, which enables analysis in the µ-GDP framework. Also, we disable domain
compression to avoid additional sources of bias [14] and use the implementation in the dpmm
library [24], which, to our knowledge, has no known DP vulnerabilities/violations.
Privacy Parameterization. As mentioned, both models convert the input (ϵ, δ)-DP budget to ρ-zCDP.
Because only Gaussian mechanisms are used, this ρ directly maps to a µ-GDP guarantee, which we
treat as the theoretical target in our audits (we refer to it as implied µ).
Neighboring Datasets and Target Record. We use a worst-case construction: Dout contains 10
identical records [0, 0, 0], and Din adds one target record [1, 1, 1]. This maximizes the target’s
influence on all marginals and yields a stringent privacy test. These worst-case settings are consistent
with the DP definition and are often needed to obtain tight audits [1, 31, 32].
Threat Model. We consider a hybrid black/white-box adversary. Black-box features are derived from
synthetic data by evaluating all possible queries over the discrete domain, following the Querybased
MIA [21], while white-box features consist of the model’s internal noisy one-way marginal counts.
DP Distinguishing Game. We train 10,000 independent models (5,000 per Dout/Din) with (ϵ, δ) =
(1, 10−2). Each model releases a synthetic dataset of size 50 and noisy marginals. The adversary
receives balanced labeled outputs and splits them into 4,000 train, 2,000 validation, and 4,000 test
samples. They train an XGBoost classifier [8] and select a decision threshold τ ∗on validation to
maximize their advantage (defined by TPR−FPR). The test set is used only for final evaluation.
Statistical Estimation of µ. Based on the adversary’s predictions on Dtest, we compute the TPR
and FPR. We then use a joint Bayesian approach inspired by Zanella-Béguelin et al. [42] and derive a
lower bound µemp such that the corresponding µ-GDP region contains 90% posterior mass.
This should be interpreted as a Bayesian lower bound on privacy. As noted by Nasr et al. [32], such
bounds do not necessarily yield valid frequentist coverage, though they are often empirically tighter.

Experimental Results
In this section, we present the experimental evaluation of our GDP auditing framework on MST/AIM.
Full FPR–FNR Tradeoff Curve. In Fig. 1, we show the empirical FPR–FNR tradeoff of our attack
classifier across decision thresholds on the validation data, together with theoretical frontiers under
multiple DP notions. Expressing all guarantees in the µ-GDP framework enables a direct comparison
between empirical leakage and theory. We observe that the empirical curve (solid black line) closely
tracks the µ implied by the mechanism’s internal ρ-zCDP accounting (solid red line), indicating that
the implementation is consistent (and tight) with its privacy analysis.
In contrast, the direct (ϵ, δ)→µ conversion (dashed red line) yields a larger µ (weaker privacy) than
the µ implied by the (ϵ, δ)→ρ→µ accounting path used internally by MST/AIM. This gap arises
because the intermediate zCDP step relies on a conservative (though numerically stable) (ϵ, δ)→ρ
conversion [5]. As a result, it enforces slightly stronger privacy (smaller µ) than the direct GDP
interpretation, which may reduce the achievable utility for the same input (ϵ, δ).
Tight Audit. Next, in Fig. 2, we select the optimal decision threshold on validation data by maxi-
mizing empirical advantage, which exhibits smooth and stable behavior across thresholds. Applying
this fixed threshold to the held-out test data yields a tight lower-bound estimate (see Fig. 3, “Default”
configuration), with µemp ≈0.43 close to the implied µ = 0.45.
This establishes, to the best of our knowledge, the first tight audit of MST/AIM in the strong-privacy
regime (ϵ = 1), as prior work reports zero empirical estimates [1, 24]. A small theory-practice gap
remains, but it is substantially narrower than previously observed.
Ablation Study. Finally, in Fig. 3, we report an ablation study on the test data, where we vary one
audit component at a time: threshold selection criterion, lower-bound estimation, Dout size, classifier
choice, and threat model. We also include a naive white-box baseline that estimates µ from single
noisy marginal. From labeled in/out runs of this marginal, we compute a pooled variance and apply a
χ2 confidence bound to derive a lower bound on µ = ∆/σ (with ∆= 1) under Gaussian noise. The
“Default” configuration consistently yields the strongest (tightest) empirical lower-bound estimation.
Additionally, in App. A, we audit MST/AIM trained with higher-order marginals.
Notably, unstable threshold selection criteria (e.g., maximizing the estimated ˆµ on validation data)
can select overly small thresholds, leading to excessive FPR and collapsing the empirical lower bound
to zero. This explains why prior work reports ϵemp = 0 results in the strong-privacy regime [1, 24].

Conclusion
In this paper, we introduced a GDP-based auditing framework for MST and AIM that measures
privacy through the full false-positive/false-negative tradeoff, enabling the first tight empirical
audit in the strong-privacy regime. Beyond these two models, our framework readily extends to
other DP generative approaches based on the Gaussian mechanism. We hope that our work (and
the accompanying open-source implementation) will encourage researchers and practitioners to
systematically validate the privacy guarantees of newly proposed and deployed synthetic data models.
