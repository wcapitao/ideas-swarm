Abstract. Puncturable attribute-based encryption (PABE) not only sup-
ports fine-grained access control over encrypted data, but also enables
users to revoke the decryption capability for specific messages by punc-
turing tags, thereby achieving fine-grained forward security. It finds wide
applications in scenarios such as sharing government classified documents
and personal health records. However, existing PABE schemes only sup-
port tag-by-tag puncturing, where each puncturing operation is done
through key delegation, which causes the key size to grow with the num-
ber of punctured tags. This inefficiency makes PABE impractical in sce-
narios that require frequent puncturing or mass revocations. To address
this limitation, it is crucial to support batch puncturing of tags, i.e., the
decryption capability for messages associated with multiple tags can be
revoked simultaneously via a single puncture.
In this work, we construct a ciphertext-policy attribute-based encryp-
tion (CP-ABE) scheme for circuits with batch-puncturing. Notably, the
size of the punctured key in our scheme is independent of the number
of punctured tags, as well as the size and depth of the circuits. This is
achieved by leveraging the evasive learning with errors (LWE) and tensor
LWE assumptions. In addition, we observe that puncturable CP-ABE can
be re-stated by dual-policy ABE (DP-ABE) with key delegation, and gen-
eralize batch-puncturing CP-ABE to provide the first lattice-based con-
struction of DP-ABE for circuits. Moreover, inspired by the observation
of Agrawal and Yamada (Eurocrypt ’20), we introduce the puncturing
property into optimal broadcast encryption (BE), capturing a new prim-
itive called puncturable BE, which allows the receiver to securely erase
sensitive messages without communicating with the authority.

Introduction
Attribute-based encryption (ABE) [11,24,34] is a powerful cryptographic prim-
itive that supports fine-grained access control over encrypted data. Over the
past two decades, the research on ABE has focused on the two primary types:
ciphertext-policy ABE (CP-ABE) and key-policy ABE (KP-ABE). In a CP-ABE
scheme, a message µ is encrypted as a ciphertext ct corresponding to a function
(policy) f : X →Y that specifies which users have the ability to decrypt this
data. Each secret key skx contains an attribute vector x ∈X that describes the
user’s credentials. If f(x) = 0 ∈Y,1 then decryption using skx returns µ. Con-
versely, KP-ABE has an inverted structure, where the secret key skf corresponds
to a function f, and a message µ is encrypted as a ciphertext ct associated with
the attributes.
Since the primitive of ABE was introduced in the seminal work of [34], it
has attracted substantial attention within the research community, with differ-
ent constructions achieving different trade-offs between expressiveness, efficiency,
and security. In these studies, many constructions are based on bilinear groups,
which are vulnerable to attacks by quantum computers. To this end, the work of
Gorbunov, Vaikuntanathan, and Wee [23], along with that of Boneh et al. [12]
(henceforth BGG+) revealed new advances in ABE for circuits from learning with
errors (LWE). Building on this foundation, lattice-based ABE for circuits have
garnered considerable attention over the past decade, especially KP-ABE for the
richest function class [17, 18, 26, 42]. However, CP-ABE schemes have tradition-
ally relied on pairings to only support circuits in NC1 [11,16,28]. Until recently,
Wee [41] proposed the first lattice-based CP-ABE for circuits of a-priori bounded
polynomial depth based on new assumptions, namely evasive and tensor LWE.
Although these assumptions are strong, they have enabled some progress in the
long-stalled problem of constructing CP-ABE for the richest function class from
lattices. Additionally, Wee [43] presents almost-optimal CP-ABE for circuits from
succinct LWE, a falsifiable assumption following from evasive LWE.
ABE has found widespread applications in various domains such as cloud stor-
age systems [35], electronic medical records [5] and online social networks [9].
However, directly deploying ABE in these real-world scenarios raises several prac-
tical challenges. More specifically, as the communication lifespan of a system
increases, the long-term user keys pose an increasingly significant challenge. An
adversary may persistently collect ciphertexts over time and wait for an oppor-
tunity to compromise a user’s key (e.g., through malware extraction on mobile
devices, coercion by authoritarian entities, phishing, or even accidental key leak-
age via cloud backups). Once the key is leaked (either forced or accidentally,
which is considered inevitable in reality), all previously recorded ciphertexts
encrypted under the corresponding policies (or attributes) may be subject to
decryption, resulting in a complete loss of confidentiality. Unlike the situation in
identity-based encryption, where the impact of key leakage is limited to a single

To mitigate such risks, periodic key rotation has been widely regarded as
a good practice. Major cloud service providers such as Amazon2, Google3, and
Microsoft4 actively recommend it, and in some cases, it is even mandated by
regulatory standards [10]. Ideally, it is desired to ensure that messages sent prior
to the attack remain confidential even if an attacker steals a user’s current key.
To address these concerns, and inspired by Green and Miers [25], who utilized
non-monotonic KP-ABE to construct puncturable encryption (PE), Phuong et
al. [32] introduced puncturable ABE for any linear secret-sharing scheme (LSSS)-
realizable access structure. Puncturable ABE is similar to ordinary ABE, except
that each ciphertext is additionally associated with a set of tags. Moreover, it
introduces a puncture algorithm that, given the current key sk and a tag t′,
outputs a new (punctured) key skt′ that can decrypt all ciphertexts decryptable
by sk except those encrypted under the tag t′. Unlike traditional forward-secure
encryption [14], which revokes decryption capabilities for all ciphertexts within
a single time period at once, puncturable ABE allows users to precisely and in-
stantly revoke the decryption capability of a key for specific messages (or recipi-
ents or time periods) via the key puncturing process, achieving fine-grained for-
ward security. In other words, users can selectively achieve “burn-after-reading”
for certain important messages, rather than destroying all the messages.
However, following the initial PE structure [25], existing PE [36–38] and punc-
turable ABE [19,30,32] suffer from notable limitations: each puncture operation
can only handle a single tag, and the punctured key has a size of Ω(η), where η is
the number of punctured tags. This limitation becomes particularly problematic
in scenarios that require frequent puncturing or mass revocations, such as secure
handling of classified government information [44] and cloud-assisted personal
health records [30]. In such settings, where sensitive data are securely erased
via such tag-by-tag puncturing, the decryption cost and the key storage over-
head quickly become impractical. The situation is even worse for lattice-based
cryptosystems [19,38], where the punctured key size increases quadratically with
the number of punctured tags. Another troublesome limitation of [19,38] is that
their punctured key size is bounded by the pre-determined circuit depth, which
is due to the fact that the puncturing property is achieved by appropriate circuit
class setting. This means that if one trivially attempts to modify the circuit class
setting to puncture multiple tags simultaneously, a larger circuit depth must be
set at system initialization. As a result, even if only a single tag is punctured, an
additional cost is added to the punctured key, which is equivalent to the storage
overhead incurred when puncturing the maximum allowable number of tags in a

Our Contributions
In this work, the above question is answered affirmatively. Our contributions are
summarized as follows.
– We construct the first lattice-based batch-puncturing CP-ABE (BPCP-ABE)
scheme for circuits. In particular, our scheme enables revoking access to ci-
phertexts under multiple tags for a certain key via a single puncture opera-
tion, where the number of tags punctured each time can be freely determined
by users. Moreover, the size of the punctured key is independent of the num-
ber of punctured tags as well as the depth and size of circuits. To achieve
this, we introduce novel lattice techniques referred to as randomized dual-tier
keys and approximate relationship in homomorphic computation. We are op-
timistic that these techniques will find broader applications in the duality
of lattice-based ABE and beyond. Table 1 provides a detailed comparison of
our scheme with previous works on PE and puncturable ABE.

– We provide a security proof for our BPCP-ABE under the recently introduced
evasive LWE [39,41] assumption and the variant of tensor LWE assumption.
The latter is a consequence of our generalization of a Gaussian vector r to a
structured Gaussian matrix R in the tensor LWE assumption [41].
– Two natural generalizations of BPCP-ABE are presented: puncturable broad-
cast encryption (PBE) and dual-policy ABE (DP-ABE) [7,8] for circuits. PBE

Technical Overview
Below we first give a high-level technical overview for our BPCP-ABE. Our con-
struction starts by leveraging the techniques introduced in homomorphic compu-

We first review the BGG+ (KP-ABE) scheme proposed by Boneh et al. [12],
which together with [22] laid the basis for the homomorphic computation tech-

tensor LWE assumptions used to prove the security of this scheme. We general-

explain the extension of Wee’s scheme to our BPCP-ABE. After that, we provide
a high-level overview of the main techniques developed in our work, particularly
the randomized dual-tier keys and the approximate relationship within the ho-
momorphic computation for attributes and policies. Finally, by integrating the
prior techniques, we sketch our BPCP-ABE construction.

Conclusion
In this work, we present a lattice-based construction of BPCP-ABE for circuits,
where the size of the punctured key is independent of the number of punctured
tags, as well as the depth and size of the circuits. Our construction is proven
to be very selectively secure under the evasive and tensor LWE assumptions.
Moreover, we propose two natural generalizations of BPCP-ABE, named PBE
and DP-ABE for circuits. Looking ahead, we believe that our batch-puncturing
technique developed in this work can also be extended to lattice-based PE and
puncturable KP-ABE for circuits.
