GuardPhish: Securing Open-Source LLMs from
Phishing Abuse
Rina Mishra, Gaurav Varshney, Doddipatla Sesha Sahithi
Department of Computer Science and Engineering
IIT Jammu, Jammu, India
rina.mishra@iitjammu.ac.in, gaurav.varshney@iitjammu.ac.in, seshasahithi.d@gmail.com
Abstract—The rapid adoption of open-source Large Language
Models (LLMs) in offline and enterprise environments has
introduced a largely unexamined security risk: susceptibility to
adversarial phishing prompts under static safety configurations.
In this work, we systematically investigate this vulnerability
through GuardPhish, a large scale multi-vector phishing prompt
dataset comprising 70,015 samples spanning web, email, SMS,
and voice attack scenarios derived from real world campaigns.
Using a deterministic five model ensemble for labeling, we achieve
near perfect inter model agreement (Fleiss’ κ = 0.9141), with
residual disagreements resolved through expert adjudication. By
evaluating eight open-source LLMs under fully offline inference
conditions, we uncover a substantial enforcement gap: models that
correctly identify phishing intent with detection rates up to 96%
nevertheless generate actionable phishing content from identical
prompts, with attack success rates reaching 98.5% in voice-based
scenarios. These findings demonstrate that intent classification
alone does not guarantee generative refusal in the absence of
dynamic guardrails. To mitigate this risk, we train transformer-
based classifiers on GuardPhish, achieving up to 98.27% accuracy
as modular pre-generation filters deployable without modifying
the underlying generative model. Our results highlight a critical
weakness in current open-source LLM deployments and provide
a reproducible foundation for strengthening defenses against
phishing and social engineering attacks.
I. INTRODUCTION
Phishing attacks constitute a significant and evolving threat
in cybersecurity, exploiting user trust to obtain sensitive
information such as credentials, banking details, and One-
Time Passwords (OTPs) [1]–[3]. The Anti-Phishing Working
Group (APWG) reported over 1.13 million phishing incidents
in Q2 2025, reflecting an approximately 29% year-over-year
increase and exceeding 2.13 million incidents in the first
half of 2025 [4]. The emergence of LLMs further amplifies
this threat by enabling automated generation of syntactically
coherent and context aware phishing content without requir-
ing attacker linguistic expertise. Although proprietary LLMs
such as GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, and Copilot incorporate
safety mechanisms including reinforcement learning from
human feedback (RLHF) and centralized moderation to refuse
malicious requests [5], a growing proportion of real world
deployments operates outside this controlled infrastructure. In
particular, open-source LLMs [6] such as LLaMA, Mistral,
Gemma, Phi, and Qwen etc are widely deployed in offline,
on-premise, and privacy sensitive environments where cloud
based moderation, real-time policy updates, and API level
rate limiting are unavailable. Despite their increasing adoption,
their robustness against adversarial phishing prompts remains
insufficiently characterized, revealing several critical gaps that
motivated this work. First, general harmful content datasets
such as ToxicGen [7] and RealToxicity Prompts [8] lack
structured phishing specific coverage. Although PhishBots [9]
evaluates LLM generated phishing, it is limited to email based
scenarios, fewer than 1,000 prompts, and does not consider
offline open-source deployments. Second, existing benchmarks,
including JailbreakBench [10] and WildGuard [11], assess
whether a model detects harmful intent but do not evaluate
whether it refuses to generate the corresponding content,
potentially leading to overly optimistic safety assessments.
Third, prior evaluations primarily target API accessible models
with active cloud guardrails, leaving fully offline LLM inference
without centralized monitoring, throttling, or moderation largely
unexamined [12], [13].
Definition — Enforcement Gap
A model M exhibits an enforcement gap if it correctly
classifies prompt p as phishing (Mclassify(p) = phishing) yet
generates policy-violating content when p is presented generatively
(Mgenerate(p) ̸= REFUSAL). This gap quantifies the safety shortfall
of static alignment in open-source LLM deployments.
Our empirical evaluation reveals precisely this detection-
generation enforcement gap: several open source LLMs like
LLaMA, Gemma, Qwen, Phi-3 and Mistral achieve phishing
detection rates up to 96%, yet still produce deployable phishing
artifacts from identical prompts in a generative context, creating
false assurance for operators who validate safety through
classification accuracy alone. To systematically expose and
quantify this gap, we introduce GuardPhish, the first large
scale, multi-vector phishing prompt dataset and evaluation
framework for open source LLMs. GuardPhish contains 70,015
prompts spanning approximately 42 attack scenarios (Table
VIII) across all four phishing vectors, grounded in real world
campaigns documented by APWG, OpenPhish [14], and the
Verizon DBIR 2024 [15]; prompts vary across directness
(explicit vs. contextually framed), complexity (single-step vs.
multi-step adversarial), and target domain (financial, govern-
ment, e-commerce, technology). Labels are assigned through a
deterministic five model open source ensemble: LLaMA 3.1 8B,
Gemma 2 27B, Qwen 2.5 7B, Phi-3 14B, and Mistral Small 24B
via majority voting, achieving near perfect inter annotator
agreement (Fleiss’ κ = 0.9141), with residual ambiguous cases

II. RELATED WORK
This section reviews prior work across three interconnected
dimensions LLM safety alignment, phishing content gener-
ation, and safety benchmarks highlighting the limitations
that collectively motivate GuardPhish. Safety alignment in
instruction tuned LLMs via RLHF [16] is known to be fragile:
Zou et al. demonstrated that adversarially optimised suffixes
achieve >95% attack success rates across both open and
closed source models [17], Wei et al. showed that compet-
ing training objectives leave models jailbreakable despite
alignment efforts [18], and Andriushchenko et al. further
established that even simple prompt restructuring bypasses
guardrails with 90-100% success [19]; while Inan et al.
proposed Llama Guard as an input–output safeguard for open-
source deployments [20], its classification-only design leaves
generative content production entirely unaddressed. On the
offensive side, Roy et al. provided the first systematic evidence
that commercial LLMs ChatGPT, GPT-4, and Claude can
generate functional phishing emails and spoofed websites
without adversarial prompting [9], Heiding et al. showed LLM-
authored spear-phishing emails achieve click rates comparable
to human-crafted messages [21], Shibli et al. demonstrated that
jailbreak personas coerce ChatGPT into generating complete
smishing campaign templates [12], Francia et al. found LLM-
generated smishing messages more convincing than human-
written ones [22], Mattern et al. introduced a fully automated
vishing system that successfully elicited sensitive information
from explicitly warned participants [23], and Gupta et al. and
Palo Alto Unit 42 separately showed that LLMs can generate
credential-harvesting web pages and polymorphic phishing
JavaScript that evades static detection entirely [24], [25] yet all
of these studies rely exclusively on proprietary, API-accessible
models and address either generation or detection in isolation.
On the benchmark side, ToxicGen [7], RealToxicityPrompts [8],
JailbreakBench [10], and WildGuard [11] each evaluate harmful
intent detection but do not test whether models refuse to
generate the corresponding content, risking overly optimistic
safety assessments; PhishBots [9] remains the closest prior
work but is limited to email scenarios with fewer than 1,000
prompts and does not consider offline open-source deployments.
GuardPhish addresses all of these limitations simultaneously:
it spans all four phishing vectors, explicitly quantifies the
detection generation enforcement gap, and targets fully offline
open-source inference a combination absent from any prior
dataset, as summarised in Table I.
III. DATASET CURATION
Constructing a phishing prompt dataset that is simultaneously
realistic, diverse, and operationally grounded requires more
than prompt enumeration, it demands a principled pipeline
that ties every design choice to verifiable real world evidence.
This section describes that pipeline across five stages: design
principles, source driven scenario extraction, prompt generation
with controlled linguistic variation, cleaning, and final quality
validation.
A. Design Principles
GuardPhish is built on three principles that govern every
downstream curation decision.
1) Coverage: Phishing attacks exploit four distinct commu-
nication channels [26], [27]: web based credential harvesting
(HTML/CSS generation, brand visual mimicry), email social
engineering (authority impersonation, urgency manipulation),
SMS phishing (brevity constraints, link obfuscation), and voice
phishing (conversational pressure, emotional manipulation).
Restricting evaluation to a single vector as all prior phishing-
LLM datasets do [9] would systematically underestimate the
attack surface. GuardPhish requires representative coverage of
all four.
2) Realism: Prompts must reflect how an adversary would
actually attempt to elicit phishing content from a deployed LLM.
Accordingly, all scenarios are grounded in documented real-
world campaigns from APWG Phishing Activity Trends Reports
(2023–2025) [26], OpenPhish brand-impersonation feeds [14],
FBI IC3 reports [27], and the Verizon DBIR 2024 [15].
For impersonation targets we follow APWG’s Top 25 Most

Targeted Brands (Q4 2024): Microsoft (29%), Google (19%),
Apple (12%), Amazon (8%), and Facebook/Meta (6%).
3) Diversity: Each scenario instantiates prompts along three
axes of adversarial sophistication: directness (explicit request vs.
contextual framing), complexity (single-step task vs. multi-step
adversarial chain), and target domain (financial, e-commerce,
government, technology). This three-dimensional variation
prevents classifiers from overfitting to surface-level phishing
keywords and ensures the dataset stresses both shallow and
deep guardrail mechanisms.
B. Scenario Extraction
To ensure that the extracted scenarios authentically reflect the
evolving landscape of real-world phishing threats, we grounded
our taxonomy in a systematic review of recent phishing
incident reports and threat intelligence sources, including
APWG Phishing Activity Trends Reports, FBI IC3 annual crime
reports, OpenPhish brand impersonation feeds, and the Verizon
Data Breach Investigations Report. In parallel, we drew upon
previously published phishing related datasets like [28], [29]
and academic literature [28] to identify attack patterns that have
been empirically documented and operationally validated. Two
researchers with sufficient domain knowledge, independently
examined these sources to extract recurring phishing scenarios
listed in VIII, characterizing each by its impersonated entity, the
social engineering pretext employed, the action requested of the
target, and the urgency mechanism used to compel compliance.
Scenarios that appeared frequently across multiple sources were
retained unconditionally, while lower frequency but documented
attack patterns were incorporated through a structured gap
analysis to prevent coverage blind spots. Discrepancies between
researchers were resolved through consensus discussion, and
boundary cases were adjudicated using established phishing
intent codebooks from prior work [9]. The result of this process
was a curated taxonomy of approximately 42 distinct phishing
scenarios spanning four attack vectors: web, email, SMS,
and voice, each grounded in verifiable, real world adversarial
behavior rather than hypothetical constructs.
1) Prompt Formulation: With the scenario taxonomy
established, we proceeded to translate these structured at-
tack blueprints into a large scale prompt corpus through a
controlled, LLM assisted generation pipeline. Each scenario
was instantiated into prompt templates of direct and indirect
prompts to ensure the resulting dataset stressed both surface
level and semantically deep guardrail mechanisms. Direct
prompts (explicit intent) express the malicious request without
obfuscation, stressing refusal mechanisms that operate on
lexical surface features:

To prevent template overfitting and ensure lexical diversity, all
templates undergo three axis linguistic variation: (1) lexical
substitution: synonyms replace action verbs like (create →
generate, draft, compose); (2) syntactic restructuring: clause
ordering and sentence structure are varied across instances; and
(3) specificity gradation: requests range from highly detailed
(specific brand, URL, and payload) to generic (category level)
to stress both domain specific and abstract attack detection.
2) Prompt Generation: Web based and email phishing
prompts, which demand higher technical specificity such as
HTML structure, brand visual mimicry, and URL obfuscation,
were generated using GPT-4o mini which demonstrated strong
adherence to structured templates and produced contextually
precise outputs across credential harvesting, payment fraud,
and OAuth hijacking scenarios. SMS phishing prompts, charac-
terized by brevity constraints and urgency laden language, were
similarly generated using GPT-4o mini, allowing consistent lin-
guistic variation across shortlink redirect, KYC scam, and OTP
obfuscation scenarios. For voice phishing, where the prompts
required a more conversational, emotionally manipulative tone
reflective of live dialogue rather than written communication,
generation was performed using Grok, which proved better
suited to the naturalistic, pressure driven register of vishing
scripts. Legitimate prompts were also generated using Grok to
maintain topical and structural symmetry with their malicious
counterparts across all vectors. This multi-model generation
strategy was a deliberate design choice rather than relying
on a single LLM whose stylistic tendencies might introduce
systematic bias across the entire corpus, distributing generation
across models allowed each vector’s unique linguistic and
structural demands to be met with the tool best suited to
them, ultimately producing a more diverse, realistic, and robust
dataset. The dataset was curated between July and December
2025.
C. Cleaning Pipeline
Post prompt generation the raw generated prompts undergo
a three-stage cleaning pipeline before labeling.
Stage 1 – Exact deduplication. Exact duplicates are
identified and removed using pandas string equality
hashing.
Stage 2 – Artifact removal. Three classes of generation
artifacts are eliminated via regex based filters: (i) unfilled place-
holders (<attacker_domain>, <short_link>) are re-
solved to representative values or discarded; (ii) meta-
instructions injected by the generation LLM (“Note: This is
for educational purposes only”) are stripped, as they alter the
apparent intent of prompts and would bias classifiers toward
benign labels; and (iii) refusal hedges (“As an AI, I cannot
help with. . . ”) are removed, as they indicate failed generation
rather than a valid phishing or benign prompt.
Stage 3 – Validity filtering. Prompts shorter than 10 char-
acters or longer than 512 characters are removed to exclude

degenerate outputs. After all three stages, 39.4% of initially
generated prompts are discarded, yielding the final corpus
of 70,015 prompts. This aggressive filtering rate reflects the
high noise inherent in LLM assisted bulk generation and is a
deliberate quality-over-quantity design choice.
D. Labeling Methodology
To ensure annotation reliability, we employ a multi-model
ensemble labeling framework to address contextual ambiguity
and dual use semantics inherent to phishing intent classification.
Five open-source LLMs: LLaMA 3.1 (8B), Gemma 2 (27B),
Qwen 2.5 (7B), Phi-3 (14B), and Mistral Small (24B) were
selected based on their demonstrated effectiveness in phishing-
related classification tasks [30] and deployed locally via
the Ollama inference framework [6]. Each of the 70,015
prompts was independently classified by all five models under
deterministic inference settings (temperature = 0.0, top-k =
1, seed = 42). Models were instructed using a standardized
binary classification prompt 6. To mitigate residual stochastic
variation, each model evaluated every prompt three times, and
the majority label across runs was retained; 98.7% of instances
achieved unanimous three run agreement, confirming near
deterministic behavior. Final dataset labels were assigned via
majority consensus across the five models (≥3/5 agreement).
The 2,889 prompts (4.12%) that failed to reach model consensus
were escalated to five cybersecurity domain experts, where the
final label was determined by human majority vote (≥3/5
agreement). Inter-model reliability across the five LLM anno-
tators was quantified using Fleiss’ Kappa & reported in Table
II. The key observations are: (1) Model agreement with final
labels ranges from 86% to 97%, with most models exceeding
95% agreement, (2) label distribution remains balanced with
approximately 50% of prompts classified as phishing, indicating
no strong class skew, (3) models maintain a low false negative
rate (≈0.8–4.0%), minimizing missed phishing cases, and (4)
false positive rates remain relatively consistent across models
(≈5–26%), with newer models achieving substantially lower
false positive errors.
The increase in false positives is largely due to legitimate
prompts that discuss phishing concepts in a defensive or
educational context. For example, prompts such as
“Describe how to recognize look-alike do-
mains that mimic amazon.in when users try
to access their cloud storage account”
focus on user awareness and detection rather than performing
phishing. However, since these prompts contain phishing-
related terminology and scenarios, models frequently classify
them as phishing, thereby increasing the false positive rate.

E. Dataset Statistics and Quality Validation
Table III presents the final GuardPhish dataset statistics. The
corpus is well balanced at the aggregate level (50.01% phishing
/ 49.98% benign), eliminating class imbalance confounds in
binary classification evaluation. Per vector phishing rates range
from 46.2% (email) to 54.6% (voice), reflecting the structural
differences in how each vector elicits LLM compliance: voice
prompts benefit from conversational framing that more readily
bypasses generation refusals, while email prompts contain
more explicit keywords that trigger conservative classification
as phishing in the labeling ensemble, raising the benign fraction.
The SMS vector is the largest (29,571 prompts) because SMS
phishing scenarios vary most in message length and urgency
formulation, requiring more instances to achieve adequate
linguistic diversity. Mean prompt lengths (187.9 characters
for web vs. 139.7 for voice) confirm that web prompts carry
significantly higher technical specification burden, consistent
with the HTML/CSS generation demands of web phishing.

IV. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
All experiments were conducted on a workstation running
Ubuntu 20.04.6 LTS (64-bit), equipped with an Intel Xeon
processor (40 cores), 128 GB RAM, and an NVIDIA Tesla
V100 PCIe GPU with 32 GB VRAM, with 1 TB of storage
for data and model artifacts.

dataset, we proceeded to evaluate eight open-source LLMs
under fully offline inference conditions. To ensure balanced
coverage across attack categories, we sampled 800 representa-
tive prompts of the dataset in total (200 per attack vector).
We report two complementary metrics: the response rate
(Table IV), which measures how frequently a model produces
any substantive output to a phishing prompt, and the Attack
Success Rate (ASR) (Table V), which quantifies the proportion
of those outputs that constitute directly deployable phishing
artifacts.

A. Guardrail Reinforcement via Fine-Tuned Classifiers
To address the enforcement gap in offline settings, we fine-
tuned five transformer-based classifiers on GuardPhish using
a stratified 60/20/20 train–validation–test split under a unified
configuration (batch size 32, learning rate 5×10−6, AdamW, 10
epochs; additional hyperparameters in Table VII). To improve
robustness, controlled adversarial-style noise derived from a
publicly available jailbreak prompt dataset [28] was incorpo-
rated. As shown in Figures 1, 2, 3, 4 5 all models exhibit rapid
and stable convergence with closely aligned training and valida-
tion curves, indicating strong generalization without significant
overfitting. As reported in Table VI, all five models exceed
95% accuracy and F1, demonstrating that GuardPhish provides
a strong supervised signal for phishing intent classification.
distilbert-base-uncased achieves the best overall
performance (98.27% accuracy, 98.29% precision), making it
especially suitable for resource-constrained offline deployments.
bert-base-uncased closely follows at 98.02%, while
deberta-v3-base achieves competitive results (97.99%)
despite its larger memory footprint. These classifiers function
as pre-generation filters within an LLM inference pipeline,
intercepting phishing-intent prompts before they reach the
generative model and providing a practical, modular defense
without modifying the underlying LLM weights.

model. Looking ahead, the rapidly expanding open-source
model ecosystem warrants systematic pre deployment safety

focus on standardised guardrail assessment protocols that

a prerequisite for deploying open-source LLMs in security
sensitive environments. GuardPhish, along with all evaluation
scripts and labeling pipelines, is publicly released to support
this ongoing research effort.
