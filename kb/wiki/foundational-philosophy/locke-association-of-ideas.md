## TL;DR
Locke is the origin point of the modern phrase "association of ideas." In the chapter added to the 4th edition (1700) of his *Essay*, he argues that ideas not naturally allied can be welded in the mind by chance, custom, or education, and that these acquired connections explain a great deal of human reasoning, prejudice, and error.

## Key thesis
Locke distinguishes two ways ideas come to be linked: a **natural correspondence** (where ideas truly belong together by reason or evidence) and a **chance/custom connection** (where habit or circumstance has fused them). Most of what passes for reasoning is the second — and once formed these connections are nearly impossible to dissolve, which is why dogma and prejudice are so durable.

## Key concepts
- **association of ideas** — the welding together of ideas in the mind, whether or not their connection reflects reality.
- **natural vs. chance connection** — Locke's foundational distinction; the latter is the source of most error.
- **custom** — repeated co-occurrence as the engine of association.
- **tabula rasa** — Locke's image of the mind as initially blank, with ideas built up from sensation and reflection.

## Why it matters for ai-ideator
Locke is the philosophical license for treating concept-pairing as the basic mental operation. But Locke also issues the warning every ideation system must heed: arbitrary combinations form readily — the system must distinguish meaningful blends from spurious ones. His "natural correspondence vs. chance" distinction prefigures the novelty-vs-utility tradeoff at the center of LLM combinatorial creativity.

## Memorable passages
> "Some of our ideas have a natural correspondence and connexion with one another… Besides this there is another connexion of ideas wholly owing to chance or custom. Ideas, that in themselves are not at all of kin, come to be so united in some men's minds, that 'tis very hard to separate them." — *Essay* II.xxxiii.5
