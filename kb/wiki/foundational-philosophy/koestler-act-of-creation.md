## TL;DR
Every creative act — humour, scientific discovery, artistic invention — is an act of **bisociation**: the sudden interlocking of two previously unrelated "matrices of thought," each a self-consistent rule-governed frame, perceived simultaneously in a single situation. Possibly the single most important reference for an LLM ideation system that combines concepts.

## Key thesis
Routine thinking operates within a single matrix (associative routine). Creative thinking operates *across* two normally separate matrices simultaneously. The bisociative act has three domains: humour (the same situation read in two incongruous frames triggers laughter), science (two frames revealed to share a deep structure produce discovery), and art (two frames juxtaposed produce metaphor and aesthetic effect).

## Key concepts
- **bisociation** — Koestler's coinage; the simultaneous activity of two matrices on one situation.
- **matrices of thought** — self-consistent rule-governed frames within which routine reasoning happens.
- **associative routine** — within-matrix thinking; reliable but uncreative.
- **the Eureka act** — the moment when bisociation resolves into discovery.
- **the Jester / the Sage / the Artist** — Koestler's three domains where bisociation occurs.

## Why it matters for ai-ideator
Bisociation is **precisely** what an LLM ideation system attempts: cross-pollination between two domains/matrices that are normally kept separate. Koestler also gives:
- A concrete typology (humour / science / art) — useful for taxonomizing the type of business idea you want.
- Worked examples (Gutenberg's printing press = wine-press + coin-stamp; Archimedes' bath; Kepler's planetary geometry) — these translate directly into prompt patterns: "find a deep structural analogue from a distant matrix."
- The empirical claim that creative leaps feel sudden because both matrices were already prepared; the "Eureka" is the moment of overlap, not the construction.

## Memorable passages
> "I have coined the term 'bisociation' in order to make a distinction between the routine skills of thinking on a single 'plane,' as it were, and the creative act, which… always operates on more than one plane." — Book One, Ch. II
