## TL;DR
Creative thought is a Darwinian process: a generator produces variations **blindly** (uncorrelated with which will succeed), and a selection mechanism retains the variants that fit problem constraints. The most influential modern formal model of combinatorial creativity; the philosophical underpinning of generate-and-test architectures.

## Key thesis
Knowledge growth — in evolution, in trial-and-error learning, in scientific discovery — follows the same triadic pattern: (1) blind variation, (2) consistent selection criteria, (3) preservation/propagation of selected variants. "Blind" is technical: it means the variation generator is uncorrelated with the eventual success criterion, not random in the colloquial sense.

## Key concepts
- **blind variation** — the generator is not pre-tuned to produce solutions; it produces candidates without foresight.
- **selective retention** — a separate mechanism keeps the variants that fit.
- **BVSR** — the abbreviation for the whole framework.
- **evolutionary epistemology** — Campbell's broader program; knowledge as evolved.

## Why it matters for ai-ideator
BVSR underwrites the **generate-and-test architecture** of any LLM ideation system. It also sets a precise design standard: the generator should not be over-tuned to the evaluator (else there is no genuine variation), nor should the evaluator be too lax (else nothing is selected). The classic combinatorial-creativity tension. Cite Campbell when justifying why retrieval, generation, and evaluation should be *architecturally separated* with their own model/prompt configurations.

## Memorable passages
> "A blind-variation-and-selective-retention process is fundamental to all inductive achievements, to all genuine increases in knowledge, to all increases in fit of system to environment."
