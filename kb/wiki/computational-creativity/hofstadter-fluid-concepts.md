## TL;DR
Argues creativity, analogy, and high-level perception are the same cognitive mechanism: **fluid, context-sensitive activation of concepts**. Presents Copycat, Tabletop, and other small-domain models that demonstrate creativity via flexible analogy-making. The famous slogan: "analogy is the core of cognition."

## Key thesis
Creativity isn't about combining static concepts; it's about making concepts themselves flexible — letting a concept slip into a related one under contextual pressure. Copycat operates on letter-string analogies (e.g., "abc → abd; xyz → ?") and demonstrates that genuine creative behavior emerges from a parallel terraced scan over a network of concepts (the Slipnet) where activation spreads and reshapes the live concept set.

## Key concepts
- **Copycat** — Hofstadter & Mitchell's analogy-making program; the canonical demonstration.
- **fluid concepts** — concepts whose boundaries shift with context.
- **slippage** — the controlled drift from one concept to a related one (e.g., "rightmost" slipping to "leftmost" under reflection).
- **Slipnet** — the network of related concepts, with activation levels that change over time.
- **parallel terraced scan** — many partial perceptions explored in parallel, with successful ones reinforced.

## Why it matters for ai-ideator
Hofstadter's claim that "analogy is the core of cognition" is the precursor to all later combinatorial-creativity work. For an LLM business-idea generator, the operative implication is: **let concept boundaries be soft**. "Airbnb for X" should be able to slip to "fractional ownership of X" or "marketplace for X" when context demands. Hard concept tags hurt creativity; activation-spread retrieval helps.

## Memorable passages
> "Making variations on a theme is really the crux of creativity."
